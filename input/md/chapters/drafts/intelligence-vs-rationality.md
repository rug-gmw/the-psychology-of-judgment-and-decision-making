# Intelligence vs. Rationality

Learning goals:

- Explain why high intelligence does not guarantee good reasoning and decision-making.
- Define dysrationalia and describe how it arises from the distinction between cognitive capacity and thinking dispositions.
- Describe the Cognitive Reflection Test (CRT) and explain what it measures beyond standard intelligence tests.
- Define Actively Open-Minded Thinking (AOT) and explain how it predicts resistance to bias independently of intelligence.
- Relate the distinction between intelligence and rationality to the Bayesian framework of belief updating.

## When Smart People Think Poorly

Consider a university professor with an impressive publication record and a high IQ who nonetheless believes that homeopathic remedies cure cancer, or a successful lawyer who falls for a transparent financial scam. These are not fictional scenarios. Highly intelligent people regularly make poor judgments, hold irrational beliefs, and fall for the same cognitive traps as everyone else. If intelligence is supposed to help us think well, why does it so often fail to do so?

The answer, as we will see in this chapter, is that intelligence and rationality are not the same thing. Intelligence gives you the raw processing power to solve problems. Rationality is the tendency to actually use that power when it matters. You can have plenty of one and very little of the other. This distinction has deep implications for how we understand judgment and decision-making, because it means that knowing someone's IQ tells you surprisingly little about whether they will reason well in everyday life.

## Dysrationalia: Smart but Irrational

@stanovich1993 introduced the term dysrationalia to describe the condition of having adequate intelligence yet thinking and behaving irrationally. The term is deliberately modeled on words like dyslexia and dyscalculia, which describe specific difficulties in reading and math despite otherwise normal cognitive ability. Stanovich's argument was provocative: if we recognize that someone can have a normal IQ but struggle with reading, why not recognize that someone can have a normal (or even high) IQ but struggle with rational thinking?

The key insight behind dysrationalia is that standard IQ tests measure only a narrow slice of what it means to think well. IQ tests are good at measuring what Stanovich calls algorithmic-level processing: the raw computational power of your mind, such as working memory capacity, processing speed, and the ability to see abstract patterns. These are real and important cognitive abilities, and as discussed in the chapter on intelligence in the real world, they predict many life outcomes including academic success and job performance ([see Intelligence in the Real World](#intelligence-in-the-real-world)).

But IQ tests miss something crucial. They do not measure what Stanovich calls reflective-level thinking dispositions: the tendency to actually engage your cognitive power when it matters, to question your first impulse, to consider whether your intuitive answer might be wrong, and to seek out information that could change your mind. A person with high algorithmic-level ability but poor reflective-level dispositions is like a powerful car with a careless driver. The engine can go fast, but the driver does not bother to check the map.

Stanovich provided vivid examples of dysrationalia in action. People with high IQs who deny well-established historical events, who believe in paranormal phenomena despite overwhelming evidence against them, or who consistently make poor financial and medical decisions. These are not failures of intelligence in the traditional sense. These people can solve complex problems when asked to do so on a test. The failure is in the disposition to apply careful thinking to their own beliefs and choices in everyday life.

This idea connects directly to the dual-process framework discussed earlier in this book ([see Reason and Intuition](#reason-and-intuition)). System 1 generates quick, intuitive responses. System 2 can override those responses with more careful, deliberate analysis. But System 2 does not activate automatically. It requires effort, and more importantly, it requires the person to recognize that effort is needed. A high IQ means your System 2 is powerful. But dysrationalia means you rarely bother to use it.

## The Cognitive Reflection Test

If standard IQ tests miss the disposition to think carefully, how can we measure it? @frederick2005 proposed an elegantly simple tool: the Cognitive Reflection Test, or CRT. The test consists of only three questions, each designed so that an intuitive but wrong answer springs to mind immediately, and arriving at the correct answer requires you to notice that your gut feeling is wrong and override it.

The most famous item is the bat-and-ball problem: A bat and a ball cost 1.10 euros in total. The bat costs 1 euro more than the ball. How much does the ball cost? The intuitive answer, the one that jumps to mind almost immediately, is 10 cents. But this is wrong. If the ball costs 10 cents and the bat costs 1 euro more, the bat would cost 1.10 euros, making the total 1.20 euros. The correct answer is 5 cents.

The second item is: If it takes 5 machines 5 minutes to make 5 widgets, how long would it take 100 machines to make 100 widgets? The intuitive answer is 100 minutes, but the correct answer is 5 minutes. Each machine makes one widget in 5 minutes, so 100 machines working simultaneously produce 100 widgets in 5 minutes.

The third item is: In a lake, there is a patch of lily pads. Every day, the patch doubles in size. If it takes 48 days for the patch to cover the entire lake, how long would it take for the patch to cover half the lake? The intuitive answer is 24 days, but because the patch doubles each day, it covers half the lake just one day before it covers the whole lake: 47 days.

What makes the CRT special is that it does not measure whether you can solve difficult problems. The math involved is trivial. Any university student can verify that 5 cents is correct once they stop to check. The test measures whether you will stop to check. It captures the moment where System 2 should intervene to question the output of System 1, and it asks: does that intervention happen, or do you go with your gut?

Frederick found that CRT scores predicted decision-making behavior in ways that went beyond what standard intelligence tests could explain. People who scored higher on the CRT were more patient in choices between smaller immediate rewards and larger delayed rewards. They were also more willing to accept gambles with positive expected values rather than settling for smaller guaranteed amounts. In other words, they made decisions that were more aligned with what economic rationality would prescribe.

Perhaps most importantly for this book, CRT scores predict susceptibility to a range of cognitive biases, including anchoring, framing effects, and base-rate neglect ([see the chapters on these topics in the Uncertainty section](#anchoring-and-primacy)). Where IQ asks "can you solve this?", the CRT asks "will you bother to check your gut feeling?" These turn out to be very different questions with very different answers.

Frederick also found a notable gender difference on the CRT, with men scoring higher on average than women, even after controlling for mathematical ability. This difference is not well understood and should not be interpreted as a difference in intelligence. It may instead reflect differences in confidence, risk-taking in answering, or socialized approaches to problem-solving. The finding does highlight, however, that cognitive reflection is not simply another label for mathematical skill.

## Actively Open-Minded Thinking

The CRT captures one aspect of good thinking: the willingness to override an intuitive response. But rational thinking involves more than catching yourself on tricky math problems. It also involves how you handle beliefs, evidence, and disagreement. This is where the concept of Actively Open-Minded Thinking comes in.

@stanovich_toplak2023 define Actively Open-Minded Thinking, or AOT, as a thinking disposition characterized by willingness to consider alternative viewpoints, sensitivity to evidence that contradicts your current beliefs, and a habit of postponing conclusions until you have considered the issue from multiple angles. Where the CRT measures whether you override a specific intuitive error, AOT measures a broader pattern of intellectual humility and flexibility.

AOT is measured through a questionnaire rather than a performance test. People indicate how much they agree with statements that reflect open-minded thinking, such as whether they are willing to change their opinions when presented with good reasons, or whether they think it is important to consider perspectives that differ from their own. Over 25 years, the AOT scale has been refined to improve its accuracy and reduce biases, with the most recent version consisting of 13 items.

What makes AOT interesting is its predictive power. People who score higher on AOT are less likely to fall for superstitious thinking, less likely to believe in conspiracy theories, and less likely to accept pseudoscientific claims. These relationships hold even after accounting for intelligence. In other words, AOT captures something that IQ misses: a general orientation toward evidence-based reasoning that protects against a wide range of irrational beliefs.

This finding echoes the broader theme of this chapter. Consider two students with identical IQ scores who encounter a claim on social media that a common food additive causes a serious disease. The student low in AOT might accept the claim because it fits with a general suspicion of processed food, share it with friends, and resist changing their mind when a friend points out that the claim is based on a single poorly conducted study. The student high in AOT might pause, look for the original source, consider alternative explanations, and update their belief when they find that the scientific consensus contradicts the claim. Both students have the same computational power. They differ in whether they deploy it.

## Myside Bias: The Limit of Intelligence

One of the most striking findings in this area is the relationship, or rather the lack of relationship, between intelligence and myside bias. Myside bias is the tendency to evaluate evidence and generate arguments in a way that favors your own pre-existing position ([see the chapter on confirmation bias](#confirmation)). You might expect that smarter people would be better at recognizing when they are being one-sided. They are not.

@stanovich_etal2013 synthesized research showing that myside bias is largely independent of cognitive ability. In one line of studies, participants were asked to generate arguments both for and against controversial issues, such as whether the sale of human organs should be permitted. People consistently generated more and better arguments for their own side than for the opposing side. This is myside bias. But the degree of this bias was unrelated to intelligence. High-IQ participants were just as one-sided as low-IQ participants.

The same pattern emerged in causal reasoning tasks, where participants provided evidence for and against their own theories about social issues like criminal recidivism. It appeared in naturalistic judgment tasks, where participants evaluated real policy proposals. In one study, participants were more likely to support banning a dangerous German car in their own country than banning a dangerous domestic car in Germany. This nationalistic bias was equally strong among high- and low-intelligence individuals.

There is an important nuance here. When people are explicitly told to set aside their prior beliefs, as in formal logic tasks where the instructions say "assume the following premises are true regardless of what you believe," intelligence does help. Higher cognitive ability allows people to decouple their reasoning from their beliefs when instructed to do so. But in everyday life, no one taps you on the shoulder and says "please set aside your opinions now." Without that explicit instruction, intelligence provides no protection against myside bias.

This finding has a somewhat unsettling implication. It means that intelligence can actually make myside bias worse in some contexts, because smarter people are better at constructing arguments that support their pre-existing beliefs. They use their cognitive power not to find the truth, but to defend what they already think. This phenomenon is sometimes called motivated reasoning, and it suggests that cognitive ability is a tool that can be used well or poorly depending on the disposition of the person wielding it.

Interestingly, @stanovich_toplak2023 note a paradox in the AOT literature: while AOT scores predict performance on many rational thinking tasks, they do not reliably predict the avoidance of myside bias. This is surprising because being open to alternative viewpoints is the very thing that AOT is supposed to measure. The authors suggest that myside bias may be especially resistant to individual thinking dispositions because it involves deeply held convictions rooted in identity, ideology, and emotion. When your core beliefs are at stake, even a generally open-minded person may struggle to reason impartially.

## Implications for Judgment and Decision-Making

The distinction between intelligence and rationality has practical consequences for many topics covered in this book. Consider medical decision-making ([see Medical Decision-Making](#medical-decision-making)). A physician with a high IQ and extensive training can still anchor on an initial diagnosis and fail to revise it when new test results come in. The problem is not a lack of knowledge or processing power. It is a failure to engage the reflective disposition that would prompt the question: "Could my initial impression be wrong?"

Or consider legal decision-making ([see Legal Decision-Making](#legal-decision-making)). Judges and forensic experts are typically above average in intelligence, yet they show the same anchoring effects, confirmation biases, and framing effects as anyone else. Their expertise gives them more knowledge to draw on, but it does not automatically give them the disposition to question their own conclusions.

The good news is that thinking dispositions, unlike intelligence, may be more amenable to change. Intelligence is relatively stable across the lifespan and strongly influenced by genetics ([see What Is Intelligence?](#what-is-intelligence)). But dispositions like AOT reflect habits of mind that can, at least in principle, be cultivated through education and practice. Teaching people to ask "What would change my mind?" or "What evidence would I need to see to update my belief?" is a form of training in rational thinking that goes beyond what any IQ test measures.

This is also why the Cognitive Reflection Test, despite being only three items long, has become one of the most widely used measures in judgment and decision-making research. It captures, in a few seconds, something that standard intelligence tests miss entirely: the moment of hesitation where you notice that your intuitive answer might be wrong and decide to think again. That moment is the difference between intelligence and rationality.

## The Bayesian Lens

The distinction between intelligence and rationality maps naturally onto the Bayesian framework that runs through this book ([see Bayesian Reasoning](#bayesian-reasoning)). Bayesian reasoning requires you to start with a prior belief, consider new evidence and its reliability, and update your belief accordingly to form a posterior. This process demands both computational capacity and the willingness to carry it out.

Intelligence gives you the computational machinery to perform a Bayesian update. A person with high working memory can hold the prior and the evidence in mind simultaneously, weigh them appropriately, and compute a revised belief. But having this capacity is not the same as using it. A dysrational thinker has the processing power to integrate base rates, weigh evidence, and revise beliefs, but defaults to System 1 shortcuts instead. They take the intuitive posterior at face value rather than checking whether it properly accounts for the prior and the evidence.

The CRT captures exactly this distinction. Each CRT item presents a situation where your intuitive posterior, the answer that feels right, is wrong. Answering correctly requires you to notice the discrepancy and recalculate. People who score high on the CRT are, in effect, people who notice when their intuitive Bayesian update has gone wrong and are willing to redo the computation.

AOT goes a step further. It reflects not just the willingness to check your own calculations, but the habit of actively seeking out evidence that could change your posterior. A person high in AOT does not just wait for disconfirming evidence to appear; they go looking for it. In Bayesian terms, they actively search for data that has high diagnostic value, data that could shift their belief substantially in one direction or the other, rather than selectively gathering evidence that confirms what they already believe. Intelligence is the capacity to compute; rationality is the willingness to compute honestly.

## Summary

Intelligence and rationality are distinct. Standard IQ tests measure algorithmic-level processing, the raw computational power of the mind, but they miss reflective-level thinking dispositions, the tendency to actually deploy that power when it matters. Dysrationalia, a term coined by Stanovich, describes the condition of having adequate intelligence but thinking poorly due to a lack of these dispositions. The Cognitive Reflection Test measures one key aspect of this: the willingness to override an intuitive but incorrect response with a deliberate, correct one. CRT scores predict susceptibility to many cognitive biases above and beyond what IQ predicts. Actively Open-Minded Thinking captures a broader disposition toward intellectual humility, evidence-seeking, and willingness to revise beliefs. AOT predicts resistance to irrational beliefs independently of intelligence. However, neither intelligence nor AOT reliably protects against myside bias, the tendency to reason in favor of one's own position, especially when deeply held beliefs are at stake. Together, these findings show that good thinking requires not just a powerful mind, but the disposition to use it carefully and honestly.
