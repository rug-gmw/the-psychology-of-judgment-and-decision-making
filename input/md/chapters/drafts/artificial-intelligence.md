## Machines That Think Like Us

In 2023, a professor at a Dutch university used an AI chatbot to draft feedback on student essays. The feedback was fluent, detailed, and mostly accurate. Students found it helpful. But when one student asked the chatbot to explain a concept from a paper that did not exist, the chatbot confidently described the paper, invented an author, and summarized findings that had never been reported. The student, unsuspecting, cited the fake paper in a follow-up assignment. This episode captures something important about artificial intelligence: the same system that produces impressively human-like output can also fail in strikingly human-like ways. Throughout this book, we have examined how people judge, decide, and reason, and where these processes go wrong. In this final chapter, we turn to artificial intelligence, and to large language models (LLMs) in particular, because they serve as a revealing mirror for human cognition. Many of the biases, heuristics, and reasoning failures we have studied reappear in AI systems, and understanding why deepens our insight into both machines and minds.

## The Turing Test and Shifting Goalposts

In 1950, the mathematician Alan Turing proposed a simple test for machine intelligence. Rather than asking the unanswerable question "Can machines think?", he suggested an operational alternative: if a human interrogator, communicating by text with both a machine and a person, cannot reliably tell which is which, the machine should be considered intelligent [@turing1950]. This "imitation game," later called the Turing test, turned a philosophical debate into an empirical question. Turing predicted that by the year 2000, computers would be able to fool an average interrogator about 30 percent of the time after five minutes of conversation.

Modern LLMs have surpassed that prediction. Systems like GPT-4 and its successors can hold extended conversations, answer complex questions, write essays, tell jokes, and even discuss their own limitations in ways that many people cannot distinguish from human responses. By the original standard Turing proposed, these systems pass the test.

Yet many people remain unconvinced. When machines learned to hold conversations, critics said that real intelligence requires reasoning. When machines began to reason through multi-step problems, critics said that real intelligence requires understanding. When machines demonstrated flexible understanding across domains, critics said that real intelligence requires consciousness. The goalposts keep moving.

This pattern reveals something about how we think about intelligence. We tend to redefine it so that it always excludes whatever machines can currently do. This resembles the bias blind spot discussed in an earlier chapter: we are reluctant to accept that our own cognitive processes might operate on the same principles as a machine's pattern-matching ([see Heuristics and Biases](#heuristics-and-biases)). When a computer performs a task, we call it computation. When a person performs the same task, we call it thinking. The Turing test was designed to cut through exactly this kind of reasoning, but even a test specifically built to avoid it has not fully succeeded.

## Fast Patterns and Slow Reasoning

The distinction between System 1 and System 2 has been a recurring theme throughout this book ([see Reason and Intuition](#reason-and-intuition)). System 1 is fast, automatic, and pattern-based. System 2 is slow, deliberate, and rule-based. This distinction maps surprisingly well onto how LLMs operate.

When you type a question into a chatbot, the standard output resembles System 1 processing. The model generates its response by predicting, one word at a time, what is most likely to come next given all the text it has been trained on and the prompt you provided. This is associative pattern completion. The model does not pause to check its logic or verify its claims. It produces whatever response fits best with the patterns in its training data, much as your System 1 produces a quick intuitive answer without effortful reflection.

This works remarkably well for many tasks. If you ask an LLM to summarize a text, translate a sentence, or explain a well-known concept, the associative pattern-matching process produces fluent and accurate output. These are tasks where the most statistically likely response is also the correct one. But when you ask the model to solve a novel logic puzzle, perform multi-step arithmetic, or reason about an unfamiliar situation, straight pattern completion often fails, just as human intuition fails on problems that require careful deliberation, such as those on the Cognitive Reflection Test ([see Reason and Intuition](#reason-and-intuition)).

This is where chain-of-thought prompting becomes relevant. @wei_etal2022 demonstrated that when LLMs are prompted to show their reasoning step by step before arriving at a final answer, their performance on arithmetic, commonsense, and symbolic reasoning tasks improves dramatically. Rather than jumping directly from question to answer, the model generates intermediate steps: "First, I need to calculate X. Then I can use that to find Y. Therefore, the answer is Z." This technique, and the reasoning models that have been built around it, functions as a kind of System 2 for LLMs. It is slower and uses more computational resources, but it catches errors that the fast, associative process misses.

The analogy is not perfect. Human System 2 involves conscious effort and awareness; an LLM has neither. But the functional parallel is instructive. In both humans and LLMs, a fast pattern-based process handles routine tasks well, and a slower step-by-step process is needed for problems that require deliberation. And in both cases, the step-by-step process is more expensive: it takes more time for humans and more computation for machines.

## Two Kinds of Intelligence

Earlier in this book, we distinguished between fluid intelligence and crystallized intelligence ([see What Is Intelligence?](#what-is-intelligence)). Fluid intelligence is the ability to reason about novel problems, detect abstract patterns, and think flexibly in unfamiliar domains. Crystallized intelligence is accumulated knowledge: facts, vocabulary, procedures, and cultural information built up over a lifetime.

LLMs are, in essence, massive stores of crystallized intelligence. They have been trained on enormous amounts of human-generated text, and they can retrieve and recombine this knowledge with impressive flexibility. Ask an LLM to define a medical term, explain a historical event, compare philosophical positions, or describe the rules of a card game, and it will typically perform well. This is crystallized intelligence at a scale no individual human can match.

Where LLMs struggle is with fluid intelligence. Tasks that require genuinely novel reasoning, the kind where no amount of stored knowledge can substitute for figuring something out from scratch, remain difficult. A classic example is a logic puzzle with an unusual structure that does not resemble anything in the training data. Humans with high fluid intelligence can work through such problems by reasoning abstractly. LLMs, relying on pattern-matching from their training, often fail or produce answers that look plausible but are wrong.

Chain-of-thought prompting and reasoning models can be understood as attempts to add fluid intelligence on top of a crystallized knowledge base. By generating intermediate reasoning steps, the model moves beyond simple retrieval and engages in something closer to novel problem-solving. This mirrors the human relationship between the two forms of intelligence: crystallized intelligence depends on fluid intelligence having operated first. You can only store knowledge if you were once able to learn and reason about it.

But the developmental trajectory differs. Humans begin life with fluid intelligence and gradually accumulate crystallized knowledge over decades. LLMs begin with crystallized knowledge, absorbed from their training data, and are gradually acquiring more fluid capabilities through techniques like chain-of-thought reasoning and extended inference. Both humans and LLMs use both forms of intelligence, but they arrive at the combination from opposite directions.

## Inherited Biases

Where do biases in AI come from? The answer, to a significant degree, is that they come from us.

LLMs are trained on vast collections of human-generated text: books, articles, websites, forums, social media posts, and much more [@bommasani_etal2021]. This text reflects the full range of human thought, including its systematic errors. Stereotypes about gender, ethnicity, age, and occupation are embedded in the statistical patterns of language. Framing effects are built into how news is written. Cultural assumptions are woven into how stories are told. When an LLM learns to predict the most likely next word in a sequence, it absorbs all of these patterns, biases included.

As a result, LLMs replicate many of the classic biases studied in this book. They show anchoring effects: when a prompt contains a number, even an irrelevant one, the model's numerical estimates shift toward it ([see Anchoring and Primacy](#anchoring-and-primacy)). They show framing effects: the same scenario described in terms of gains produces different responses than when described in terms of losses ([see Gains and Losses](#gains-and-losses)). They show a form of confirmation bias known as sycophancy: when a user expresses a strong opinion, the model tends to agree, telling the user what they want to hear rather than what is accurate ([see Confirmation](#confirmation)). And they engage in representativeness-like pattern matching, associating people and situations with prototypes in ways that can perpetuate stereotypes ([see Representativeness](#representativeness)).

@weidinger_etal2021 provide a detailed taxonomy of the ethical and social risks that arise from these inherited biases. They document how LLMs can perpetuate harmful stereotypes, reinforce exclusionary norms, and produce discriminatory outputs, not because they were designed to do so, but because these patterns are embedded in the data they learned from. The biases are not bugs introduced by careless engineers. They are features of human language and culture, faithfully absorbed by systems designed to mirror that language and culture.

This replication is itself informative about the nature of cognitive biases. If anchoring, framing, and confirmation bias emerge from statistical regularities in human language, this suggests that these biases are not just quirks of biological neural hardware. They may be deeply embedded in how knowledge is structured and communicated. Any system that learns from human communication, whether a biological brain or a silicon model, may end up exhibiting similar patterns.

## When Machines Confabulate

One of the most discussed problems with LLMs is hallucination: the tendency to generate confident, detailed, and plausible-sounding output that is factually wrong. The chatbot that invented a fake academic paper in our opening example was hallucinating. It did not retrieve a real paper from a database. It generated what a real paper citation would look like based on patterns in its training data, and it did so with the same fluency and confidence it brings to accurate responses.

This parallels a well-known phenomenon in human cognition: confabulation. People confabulate when they produce explanations or memories that feel true and are delivered with full confidence but do not correspond to reality. This happens when schemas and mental models fill in gaps without verification ([see Mental Models](#mental-models)). A patient with memory damage might describe in convincing detail an event that never happened. A healthy person might confidently "remember" details of a scene that were never presented, because those details fit the schema activated by the context.

In both cases, the mechanism is the same: pattern completion without verification. The system, whether human or artificial, generates the most probable completion given the context. When the context aligns with reality, the output is accurate. When it does not, the output is a fabrication. And here is the troubling part: the fabrication often sounds just as convincing as the truth, sometimes even more so. The same process that generates good answers also generates compelling-sounding bad ones. There is no reliable internal signal that distinguishes "I am being accurate" from "I am making this up."

Humans have System 2 as a partial check on confabulation. You can pause, reflect, and ask yourself whether your memory is reliable or whether your explanation holds up under scrutiny. This check is imperfect, as we know from the many biases documented throughout this book, but it exists. Standard LLMs lack this verification mechanism entirely. They have no access to a database of ground truth against which to check their output. Reasoning models and retrieval-augmented systems are attempts to add such checks, much as human education and training aim to strengthen the habit of verification. But the fundamental vulnerability remains: any system that generates output by pattern completion is at risk of hallucination when the patterns lead away from the truth.

## Overconfident Machines

Closely related to hallucination is the problem of calibration. A well-calibrated system assigns high confidence to claims that are likely to be correct and low confidence to claims that might be wrong. As discussed earlier in this book, humans are often poorly calibrated, particularly in the direction of overconfidence ([see Overconfidence](#overconfidence)). People tend to be more certain than they should be, assigning confidence intervals that are too narrow and probability estimates that are too extreme.

LLMs show the same pattern. When asked to estimate their own certainty, models frequently express high confidence in answers that turn out to be wrong. Their stated confidence does not reliably track their actual accuracy. This is overconfidence in a form that mirrors the human version: the posterior distribution, if we think of it in Bayesian terms, is too narrow. The model acts as if it is more certain than the evidence warrants.

Improving calibration in AI is an active area of research, just as improving human calibration is a goal of debiasing interventions. Some approaches involve training models to express uncertainty more honestly, for example by saying "I am not sure" when the evidence is ambiguous. Others involve external calibration checks, where a separate system evaluates the reliability of the model's output. These efforts parallel the structured feedback and accountability mechanisms that help improve human calibration in professional settings.

The parallel suggests a deeper point. Overconfidence may not be a flaw specific to biological brains. It may be a natural consequence of any system that generates its best guess and presents it as output. Unless the system has a separate mechanism for evaluating and communicating uncertainty, the default will be to present responses with more confidence than they deserve.

## A Bayesian Lens on Artificial Intelligence

LLMs can be understood through the same Bayesian framework that runs through this book ([see Bayesian Reasoning](#bayesian-reasoning)). The training data serves as the prior: a vast body of knowledge and patterns that the model brings to any new problem. The prompt serves as the evidence: new information that should update the model's response. The output is the posterior: the most likely completion given the combination of prior knowledge and new evidence.

Seen this way, many of the problems we have discussed become specific forms of Bayesian failure. Biases in LLMs arise from biased priors. If the training data contains stereotypes, the model's prior will reflect those stereotypes, and its outputs will be skewed accordingly, just as human biases arise from biased experiential priors. Hallucination occurs when the prior dominates over the evidence. The model generates what fits its stored patterns rather than what the specific prompt requires, in the same way that human confirmation bias and belief perseverance arise when prior beliefs override new information ([see Belief Formation and Perseverance](#belief-formation-and-perseverance)). Calibration failures reflect posteriors that are too narrow: the model is too certain, not because it has strong evidence, but because it lacks a mechanism for representing the full range of its uncertainty.

Chain-of-thought reasoning improves performance because it makes the evidence-integration process more explicit. Instead of jumping from prior to posterior in a single step, the model works through the evidence piece by piece, much as deliberate Bayesian computation outperforms intuitive shortcuts in human reasoning. The resulting posterior is more sensitive to the actual structure of the problem and less dominated by surface-level pattern matching.

This Bayesian perspective also clarifies why AI and human cognition share so many failure modes. Both are systems that combine stored knowledge with new information to produce judgments. When the stored knowledge is biased, the judgments are biased. When the integration process is too fast or too shallow, errors creep in. The specific hardware differs, but the computational logic is the same.

## Summary

Large language models provide a useful mirror for understanding human judgment and decision-making. The Turing test, proposed over seventy years ago, has been functionally passed by modern AI systems, yet the continued reluctance to accept this reveals something about how people define and protect their intuitions about intelligence. LLM output resembles System 1 processing: fast, associative, and pattern-based. Chain-of-thought prompting and reasoning models add a System 2-like layer that improves performance on tasks requiring deliberation. LLMs excel at crystallized intelligence, drawing on vast stores of training data, but struggle more with the fluid intelligence needed for genuinely novel reasoning. The biases LLMs display, including anchoring, framing effects, and sycophancy, are largely inherited from the human-generated text they were trained on, suggesting that these biases are embedded in language and culture, not just in biological brains. LLM hallucination parallels human confabulation: both arise from pattern completion without verification, and both produce output that sounds equally confident whether it is right or wrong. Calibration failures in LLMs mirror human overconfidence, with models expressing more certainty than their accuracy warrants. From a Bayesian perspective, LLMs combine prior knowledge from training data with evidence from prompts to generate posterior outputs, and their failures map onto specific forms of Bayesian error: biased priors, prior-dominated posteriors, and overly narrow uncertainty estimates.
