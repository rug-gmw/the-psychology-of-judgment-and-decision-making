# Blame, Praise, and Responsibility

Learning goals:

- Distinguish between personal causality and impersonal causality.
- Explain the covariation principle and its three dimensions: consensus, distinctiveness, and consistency.
- Describe how different patterns of consensus, distinctiveness, and consistency lead to different causal attributions.
- Define the correspondence bias and explain why people over-attribute behavior to persons rather than situations.
- Connect causal attribution to the intentional stance and moral judgment.

## From Causes to People

Imagine you are cycling to the university on a rainy morning and a car splashes through a puddle, soaking you from head to toe. Your immediate reaction is probably not a calm analysis of road drainage and weather conditions. Instead, you think something like: "That driver is a terrible person." You attribute the event to the driver, to their character, to their lack of consideration. But what if the driver simply did not see the puddle? What if there was no way to avoid it? In that case, the soaking was caused by circumstances, not by any intention. The outcome for you is the same either way, yet the two explanations feel very different, because one of them points to a person as the cause.

This chapter is about what happens when the potential cause of an event is not a gust of wind or a mechanical failure but a human being. The previous chapter on [cues to causality](#cues-to-causality) introduced the general principles people use to infer cause and effect: temporal order, contiguity, covariation, and so on. Those principles apply broadly, whether you are figuring out why your laptop crashed or why your friend cancelled dinner plans. But when the potential cause is a person, a distinct set of processes kicks in. We move from asking "what caused this?" to asking "who caused this?" and, almost immediately, "who deserves blame or praise?"

This shift connects to the idea of stances introduced in [Mental Models](#mental-models). When we reason about physical objects, we tend to adopt the mechanical stance, thinking in terms of physical forces and mechanisms. When we reason about designed artifacts, we adopt the design stance, thinking in terms of function and purpose. But when we reason about people, we adopt the intentional stance, attributing behavior to beliefs, desires, and goals. The intentional stance is powerful because it allows us to predict and explain human behavior efficiently. It is also the gateway to moral judgment: once we decide that a person intended an outcome, we are ready to assign blame or praise, a topic explored further in [Morality](#morality).

## Personal and Impersonal Causality

Not every event that involves a person is caused by that person in a meaningful sense. A useful distinction is between personal causality and impersonal causality. Personal causality means that someone's intention was the driving force behind an action. Impersonal causality means that the action occurred without intention, driven instead by circumstances, chance, or mechanical forces [@young1995].

Consider a doctor who prescribes the wrong medication. If the doctor did this on purpose, perhaps to harm the patient, we have a clear case of personal causality. The doctor intended the outcome, and blame follows naturally. But if the doctor prescribed the wrong medication because the packaging of two drugs looked nearly identical, the cause is impersonal. The outcome is the same, a patient receives the wrong drug, but our reaction is fundamentally different. In the first case, we blame the doctor. In the second, we might blame the pharmaceutical company for confusing packaging, or we might see it as an unfortunate accident.

This distinction maps directly onto the stances from [Mental Models](#mental-models). When we perceive personal causality, we are using the intentional stance: we see the person as an agent with goals and beliefs, and we hold them responsible for acting on those goals. When we perceive impersonal causality, we shift toward the mechanical stance: the person was merely a link in a chain of physical or situational forces, not the origin of the event.

The distinction matters because blame and praise are reserved almost exclusively for cases of personal causality. We do not blame people for outcomes they did not intend, at least not in the same way. You might be annoyed at the driver who splashed you, but if you learn that the driver swerved to avoid hitting a child and had no choice but to go through the puddle, your annoyance is likely to dissolve. The outcome has not changed, but the cause has shifted from personal to impersonal, and with it, the basis for blame has disappeared.

## The Covariation Principle

How do we figure out whether a behavior is caused by the person, the situation, or some combination of the two? @kelley1973 proposed that people use the covariation principle: an effect is attributed to the cause with which it covaries over time. This idea was introduced in general terms in [Cues to Causality](#cues-to-causality), where covariation was discussed as one of several cues to causality. When applied to person perception, Kelley broke covariation down into three specific dimensions.

The first dimension is consensus: do other people respond the same way to the same stimulus? If everyone who takes a particular course finds it difficult, consensus is high. If only one student struggles while everyone else does fine, consensus is low.

The second dimension is distinctiveness: does this person respond this way only to this specific stimulus, or do they respond this way to everything? If a student struggles only in one particular course but does well in all others, distinctiveness is high. If the student struggles in every course, distinctiveness is low.

The third dimension is consistency: does this person respond the same way over time? If the student has struggled in this course every semester they have taken it, consistency is high. If the struggle is a one-time event, consistency is low.

Different combinations of these three dimensions lead to different attributions. Suppose you hear that your classmate Anna gave a very negative review of a restaurant. You want to understand why. If you learn that everyone who went to that restaurant also gave negative reviews (high consensus), that Anna usually enjoys restaurants and rarely complains (high distinctiveness), and that Anna has complained about this restaurant on multiple occasions (high consistency), you are likely to attribute her review to the restaurant itself. It must be a bad restaurant. This is a stimulus attribution.

Now change the pattern. If you learn that other people who went to the same restaurant actually enjoyed it (low consensus), that Anna complains about almost every restaurant she visits (low distinctiveness), and that she has been consistently negative about dining out for years (high consistency), you are likely to attribute her review to Anna herself. She is just a difficult person when it comes to food. This is a person attribution, and it opens the door to blame or praise depending on the context.

@cheng_novick1990 refined Kelley's model by showing that people do not simply match patterns to templates but instead compute something more like probabilistic contrasts. In their experiment, 91 participants received descriptions of events along with covariation information across persons, stimuli, and occasions. The researchers carefully varied the full pattern of information, not just the familiar consensus-distinctiveness-consistency configuration. They found that participants' attributions shifted systematically depending on the complete pattern of covariation. When the information was complete and unambiguous, participants made attributions that closely matched what a probabilistic contrast model would predict. The model compares the probability of an effect occurring in the presence of a potential cause to the probability of it occurring in the absence of that cause. If the effect is much more likely when a particular person is present, the person gets the attribution. If the effect is much more likely when a particular stimulus is present, the stimulus gets the attribution.

One important finding from this work was that the apparent biases identified in earlier research, such as a tendency to favor person attributions over stimulus attributions, largely disappeared when participants received complete information. This suggests that many so-called biases in causal attribution may stem not from faulty reasoning but from incomplete information. When people lack full covariation data, they fill in the gaps with assumptions, and these assumptions can skew attributions in predictable directions [@cheng_novick1990].

## The Correspondence Bias

Despite the sophistication of the covariation principle, people make a remarkably consistent error in everyday life: they over-attribute behavior to the person and under-attribute it to the situation. This tendency is known as the correspondence bias, because people assume a correspondence between what someone does and who they are. It has also been called the fundamental attribution error, a term that captures how pervasive and consequential the mistake is [@gilbert_malone1995].

One of the classic demonstrations of this bias comes from a study on essay writing. Participants read an essay that argued in favor of a particular political position. They were told that the writer had been assigned this position by a debate instructor, that the writer had no choice in the matter. Despite knowing this, participants still inferred that the writer personally believed what they had written. The situational constraint, being assigned a position, should have fully explained the essay's content. Yet participants treated the essay as a window into the writer's true beliefs.

This pattern shows up everywhere in daily life. A student fails an exam, and we think "they did not study hard enough" rather than "the exam was unusually difficult." A colleague is quiet during a meeting, and we think "they are shy" rather than "they are dealing with a family crisis." A friend cancels plans, and we think "they do not care about our friendship" rather than "something came up." In each case, the person's behavior is explained by the person's character, even when situational factors offer a perfectly adequate explanation.

@gilbert_malone1995 argued that the correspondence bias is not a single error but arises from four distinct mechanisms. The first is a lack of awareness: sometimes we simply do not know about the situational forces acting on a person. If you do not know that your colleague received bad news this morning, you cannot factor it into your explanation of their behavior. The second is unrealistic expectations: even when we know about situational pressures, we may underestimate how strongly they influence behavior. We might think "I would still be cheerful even after bad news," failing to appreciate the true power of the situation. The third is inflated categorization: knowing about a situation can actually make behavior seem more extreme. If you know that an interview topic is stressful, you might perceive the interviewee's nervousness as even more pronounced than it really is. The fourth, and perhaps most important, is incomplete correction. Research suggests that when we observe someone's behavior, we first make a quick, automatic inference about their character (a System 1 process, as discussed in [Reason and Intuition](#reason-and-intuition)) and then, if we have the time and mental energy, we adjust this inference by considering situational factors (a System 2 process). But this adjustment is often incomplete, especially when we are busy, distracted, or not motivated to think carefully.

This last mechanism is particularly revealing. It suggests that the correspondence bias is partly a problem of cognitive resources. When people are under cognitive load, for example when they are trying to remember a phone number while also forming an impression of someone, the bias gets worse. This fits with the broader framework of dual-process theory: our fast, automatic system jumps to a person attribution, and our slow, deliberate system sometimes fails to correct it.

The correspondence bias also has cultural dimensions. Research suggests that it is more pronounced in Western, individualistic cultures, where people are socialized to see individuals as autonomous agents responsible for their own outcomes. In more collectivist cultures, people may be somewhat more attuned to situational explanations, though the bias does not disappear entirely. For psychology students at a Dutch university, this is worth reflecting on: even in a relatively individualistic European culture, the tendency to see behavior as reflecting character rather than circumstances is deeply ingrained.

The consequences of this bias extend far beyond everyday misunderstandings. In legal settings, jurors may over-attribute a defendant's behavior to their character while underappreciating the situational pressures they faced, a topic explored in [Legal Decision-Making](#legal-decision-making). In medical contexts, a doctor might attribute a patient's non-compliance to laziness rather than to the complexity of the treatment regimen, as discussed in [Medical Decision-Making](#medical-decision-making). In political discourse, citizens may blame individuals for outcomes that are largely shaped by structural forces, which connects to the themes in [Populism and Social Media](#populism-and-social-media).

The correspondence bias is, in a sense, an over-application of the intentional stance. The intentional stance is a useful default for understanding human behavior: most of the time, people do act on their beliefs and desires, and inferring those beliefs and desires from behavior is efficient and often accurate. The problem arises when we apply this stance too rigidly, failing to switch to the mechanical stance when the situation calls for it. The driver who splashed you may have been careless, but they may also have been caught by circumstances beyond their control. The correspondence bias makes us lean too heavily toward the first explanation.

## A Bayesian Lens on Causal Attribution

The processes described in this chapter can be understood through the framework of Bayesian reasoning introduced in [Bayesian Reasoning](#bayesian-reasoning). Causal attribution is, at its core, a form of belief updating about the source of behavior. When you observe someone doing something, you start with a prior belief about whether the behavior reflects the person or the situation. You then update this belief based on evidence: information about consensus, distinctiveness, and consistency.

In this framework, the covariation principle describes the evidence. High consensus (everyone reacts the same way) is evidence for a stimulus attribution. Low consensus (only this person reacts this way) is evidence for a person attribution. Distinctiveness and consistency provide additional evidence that shifts the posterior belief toward or away from different causal explanations.

The correspondence bias, then, can be understood as an overly strong prior toward the intentional stance. People start with a strong default assumption that behavior reflects character, and they do not update sufficiently when situational evidence suggests otherwise. In Bayesian terms, the prior for person attributions is too high, and the weight given to situational evidence is too low. The result is a posterior belief that is systematically skewed toward blaming or praising the person, even when the situation deserves most of the explanatory weight.

This Bayesian perspective also helps explain the finding from @cheng_novick1990 that apparent biases diminish when information is complete. When people have access to full covariation data, they have strong evidence to work with, and this evidence can overcome even a biased prior. But when evidence is sparse or ambiguous, the prior dominates, and the default assumption that people are the cause of their own behavior goes uncorrected.

## Summary

When the potential cause of an event is a person rather than a physical force, a special set of attributional processes activates. The distinction between personal causality, where intention drives the action, and impersonal causality, where circumstances or chance are responsible, determines whether blame or praise is assigned. People assess these attributions using the covariation principle, evaluating consensus, distinctiveness, and consistency to determine whether a behavior should be attributed to the person, the stimulus, or the situation. When covariation information is complete, people are reasonably good at this task. However, in everyday life, information is often incomplete, and people show a persistent correspondence bias: they over-attribute behavior to the person and under-attribute it to the situation. This bias arises from multiple mechanisms, including lack of awareness of situational forces, unrealistic expectations about their influence, and incomplete correction of automatic person attributions. Understanding these processes is essential not only for social psychology but also for recognizing how blame and praise are assigned, sometimes fairly and sometimes not, in legal, medical, and everyday contexts.
