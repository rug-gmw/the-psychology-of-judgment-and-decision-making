Imagine you are planning a group project for one of your courses. You sit down with your teammates and estimate that it will take about two weeks to finish. You feel quite certain about this. Four weeks later, as you rush to submit the final version, you wonder how your estimate could have been so far off. This experience, familiar to almost every student, reflects not one but two forms of overconfidence operating at the same time: your estimate was too optimistic, and you were too sure about it. Understanding these two distinct distortions is the goal of this chapter.

## Two Faces of Overconfidence

Overconfidence is not a single bias. It is a family of biases that can be organized into two fundamentally different types, each corresponding to a different distortion of belief [@moore_healy2008]. The first is a self-favorable bias: a tendency to hold beliefs about oneself that are more positive than the evidence warrants. The second is overprecision: a tendency to be too certain about one's judgments, underestimating the range of possible outcomes. These two types are conceptually independent. You can be overly optimistic without being overly certain, and you can be overly certain without being overly optimistic. In practice, however, they often occur together.

Both forms of overconfidence can be understood as expressions of a broader tendency described in more detail in the chapter on [confirmation bias](#confirmation). When people form a belief, they tend to seek out and weigh more heavily the evidence that supports it, while discounting evidence that challenges it. In the case of overconfidence, this process is directed at beliefs about oneself and one's own judgments. But overconfidence also has a motivational dimension that goes beyond cold cognition: people are not merely failing to update their beliefs properly, they are driven to maintain a positive view of themselves.

## Self-Favorable Bias

The self-favorable bias refers to a systematic shift in beliefs about oneself in a positive direction. It shows up in several overlapping ways: overestimation, overplacement, and unrealistic optimism. Each of these is a different expression of the same underlying tendency, in which evidence of success or positive attributes is weighted more heavily than evidence of failure or shortcomings.

Overestimation is the simplest form. People overestimate their actual performance, their knowledge, or their degree of control over events. A classic demonstration comes from @langer1975, who studied the illusion of control. In one of her experiments, office workers participated in a lottery. Some chose their own ticket, while others were given a ticket at random. Later, they were asked how much they would sell their ticket for. People who had chosen their own ticket demanded more than eight dollars on average, while those who had been assigned a ticket were willing to sell for about two dollars. Choosing a ticket, of course, has no effect on the probability of winning a lottery. But the act of choosing introduced a feeling of control, inflating people's estimates of how likely they were to win.

Overplacement, often called the better-than-average effect, is the tendency to judge oneself as better than the typical person. Most people rate themselves as better-than-average drivers, better-than-average students, and better-than-average friends, which is statistically impossible for a majority to be. What makes this particularly interesting is that overplacement is not simply a fixed bias. It is sensitive to whether a trait is seen as desirable. @logg_etal2018 demonstrated this in a series of experiments. In one study, students were randomly assigned to read an article describing either introversion or extroversion as a key to success. When introversion was presented as desirable, students rated themselves as more introverted than average. When extroversion was presented as desirable, they rated themselves as more extroverted than average. This suggests that overplacement is partly driven by a motivation to see oneself in a positive light. However, @logg_etal2018 also found that this motivational effect was strongest when traits were defined vaguely. When specific, concrete criteria were used to measure a trait, motivation had much less influence on self-assessment. This indicates that ambiguity gives people room to define traits in self-serving ways.

Unrealistic optimism, sometimes called a positivity bias, is the tendency to believe that good things are more likely to happen to oneself than to others, and that bad things are less likely. @weinstein1980 documented this pattern in a study with college students who were asked to estimate how their chances of experiencing various life events compared to their classmates. Students consistently rated themselves as more likely than average to experience positive events, such as owning a home or living past 80, and less likely than average to experience negative events, such as getting divorced, developing a heart attack, or becoming an alcoholic. The bias was not uniform across all events: it was strongest for events that people perceived as controllable, and weakest for events associated with a clear stereotype of a typical victim. For example, students were less optimistic about avoiding lung cancer if they fit the stereotype of a smoker. Weinstein proposed that unrealistic optimism arises when people focus on their own protective actions while assuming others are less careful, creating an unfair comparison with a hypothetical "unlucky" peer rather than a realistic assessment of what others are actually doing.

All three of these patterns reflect the same underlying distortion. The person's belief about themselves is shifted in a favorable direction, as if confirming evidence (successes, positive traits, protective behaviors) is given more weight than disconfirming evidence (failures, shortcomings, risk factors).

## Overprecision

The second form of overconfidence is overprecision, and it is both the most pervasive and the least understood. Overprecision means that people are too certain about their judgments. They underestimate the range of possible outcomes, acting as if they know more than they actually do.

The standard way to measure overprecision is through confidence intervals. If you ask people to provide a range within which they are 90% confident the true answer falls, their ranges should capture the correct answer 90% of the time. In practice, the correct answer typically falls within these ranges only about 40 to 60% of the time [@lichtenstein_etal1982]. People's confidence intervals are far too narrow. To put this concretely: if you were asked to estimate the length of the Rhine River with a 90% confidence interval, you might say "between 800 and 1,200 kilometers." A well-calibrated person, however, would recognize the uncertainty in their knowledge and provide a much wider range, perhaps "between 500 and 1,800 kilometers." (The actual length is about 1,230 kilometers.) The temptation to give a narrow range comes from the feeling that one roughly knows the answer. But roughly knowing is not the same as precisely knowing, and people consistently confuse the two.

Overprecision differs from self-favorable bias in an important way. With self-favorable bias, the belief is shifted in a particular direction: toward a more positive self-view. With overprecision, the belief is not necessarily shifted in any direction. Instead, the range of possibilities considered is too narrow. The person may be correct on average, but they fail to account for how wrong they might be.

Why does overprecision occur? One explanation is that it too reflects a form of confirmation bias. Once you have formed an estimate, evidence that is consistent with that estimate comes to mind easily and is readily accepted, while evidence that would suggest you should widen your range is harder to generate and easier to dismiss. The result is a posterior belief that is too narrow: too much probability mass is concentrated around a single estimate, and too little is assigned to alternative outcomes.

## Overconfidence and Mental Health

If overconfidence is a bias, you might expect that less biased people would be better off. Curiously, the opposite appears to be true: a degree of self-favorable bias seems to be a marker of mental health.

@korn_etal2014 studied how people with major depressive disorder update their beliefs about future life events compared to healthy individuals. In their study, participants estimated their personal likelihood of experiencing 70 different adverse events, such as developing Alzheimer's disease or being robbed. They were then shown the average probability of each event for someone in a similar situation. After seeing this information, they re-estimated their own likelihood. Healthy participants showed an optimistic pattern: they adjusted their estimates more when the information was good news (the average probability was lower than they expected) than when it was bad news (the average probability was higher). In other words, healthy people selectively absorbed the positive information. Participants with major depression did not show this pattern. They updated their beliefs roughly equally for good and bad news. In fact, the more severe a person's depressive symptoms, the more their updating pattern shifted toward pessimism, weighting bad news more heavily than good news. The self-favorable bias, it seems, is not just a cognitive error. It is something that healthy minds do, and its absence is associated with depression.

This finding connects to earlier work on learned helplessness. @seligman_maier1967 conducted experiments in which dogs were exposed to electric shocks that they could not escape. Initially, the dogs struggled and tried to find a way out, which you might call an unrealistically optimistic response given the situation. But after repeated failure, they stopped trying altogether. When they were later placed in a new situation where escape was possible, they did not even attempt it. They had learned that their actions did not matter. Seligman and Maier saw this learned helplessness as a model for depression: the loss of the belief that one's actions can make a difference.

@alloy_abramson1979 took this line of reasoning further by studying depressed and non-depressed college students in a series of experiments involving a simple task: pressing a button and judging how much control they had over whether a light turned on. The actual degree of control was varied by the experimenters. When there was no real control, non-depressed students overestimated their influence, especially when the light turned on frequently or when they were winning money. Depressed students, by contrast, judged their control more accurately across all conditions. This pattern, sometimes called depressive realism, presents a paradox: the more accurate judges were the ones who were suffering.

Together, these findings suggest that a moderate degree of self-favorable bias is not just common but functional. A shifted belief about oneself, one that is more positive than the evidence strictly warrants, may serve as a psychological buffer that keeps people motivated and engaged. A perfectly calibrated person, who sees their abilities and prospects with complete accuracy, may lack the optimism needed to persist in the face of setbacks. This does not mean that more overconfidence is always better. Extreme overconfidence can lead to reckless decisions and painful failures. But a small, healthy dose appears to be part of how well-functioning minds work.

## The Planning Fallacy

The planning fallacy is a phenomenon in which people underestimate the time, cost, and risk of future actions while overestimating the benefits [@kahneman_tversky1979]. It is one of the clearest demonstrations of both forms of overconfidence operating simultaneously. The estimate is too optimistic (self-favorable bias), and it fails to account for the full range of things that could go wrong (overprecision).

@buehler_etal1994 studied this in a series of experiments with university students. In one study, psychology students predicted when they would finish their honors thesis. On average, students predicted they would finish far earlier than they actually did, with actual completion times averaging about 22 days longer than estimated. This happened even though students had extensive past experience with missing deadlines. In a follow-up study, students were asked to think aloud while making their predictions. The recordings revealed that students focused almost entirely on their plans for the future: how they would organize their work, when they would write each section, and so on. They rarely thought about past experiences with similar tasks. When they did recall past delays, they attributed them to unusual, one-time circumstances, such as a computer crashing or an unexpected family visit, rather than seeing them as part of a general pattern.

This is what Kahneman and Tversky called the inside view versus the outside view. The inside view focuses on the specific features of the current project: the plan, the timeline, the resources. It generates optimistic estimates because it naturally highlights what will go right. The outside view looks at the base rates: how long similar projects have taken in the past. It generates more realistic estimates because it incorporates the full range of actual outcomes, including all the delays and overruns that occurred for reasons nobody anticipated.

The planning fallacy is not limited to student assignments. @flyvbjerg2006 documented it on a much larger scale in public infrastructure projects. Analyzing data from hundreds of transportation projects worldwide, Flyvbjerg found average cost overruns of 44.7% for rail projects, 33.8% for bridges and tunnels, and 20.4% for roads. These overruns were not random: they were systematically skewed toward underestimation. Demand forecasts showed similar patterns, with rail passenger projections overestimated by an average of 51.4%. As a remedy, Flyvbjerg advocated for reference class forecasting: instead of relying on project-specific plans, forecasters should base their estimates on the actual outcomes of comparable past projects. This approach was adopted by the UK Department for Transport, which now requires cost estimates to include "optimism bias uplifts" based on historical data. For example, road projects require a 15% uplift to reach a 50% probability of staying within budget, while rail projects need a 40% uplift.

The planning fallacy illustrates both forms of overconfidence at once. The estimate itself is shifted in a favorable direction, reflecting the self-favorable bias. And the range of uncertainty around the estimate is too narrow, reflecting overprecision. A project manager who predicts that a bridge will cost 200 million euros, plus or minus 10 million, is making two errors: the 200 million is probably too low, and the 10-million range is almost certainly too tight. For further discussion of how statistical approaches can outperform intuitive prediction, see the chapter on [statistical vs. intuitive prediction](#statistical-vs-intuitive-prediction).

## The Dunning-Kruger Effect

The Dunning-Kruger effect describes a specific pattern of overconfidence tied to ability: the least competent individuals tend to show the greatest overconfidence, while highly competent individuals slightly underestimate their ability [@kruger_dunning1999].

Kruger and Dunning tested this across several domains, including logical reasoning, grammar, and the ability to recognize humor. In each study, participants completed a test and then estimated how well they had performed relative to their peers. The pattern was consistent. Participants who scored in the bottom quartile dramatically overestimated their performance. For example, in the humor study, those in the bottom quartile estimated their ability to recognize what is funny at the 62nd percentile, despite actually scoring at the 12th percentile. Meanwhile, those in the top quartile slightly underestimated their own standing, rating themselves lower than they actually were.

The explanation Kruger and Dunning proposed is metacognitive. The skills needed to perform well in a domain are the same skills needed to recognize good performance. If you lack the logical reasoning skills to solve a set of problems correctly, you also lack the skills to evaluate whether your answers are correct. This creates a double burden: poor performers not only make errors, but they are also unable to detect those errors. In a follow-up experiment, Kruger and Dunning trained participants in logical reasoning. After training, participants who had initially performed poorly became significantly better at assessing their own performance. Acquiring the relevant skills also gave them the metacognitive ability to recognize their earlier shortcomings.

The Dunning-Kruger effect combines both forms of overconfidence. Poor performers show a large self-favorable shift: they think they are doing much better than they are. And they show poor calibration: they have little awareness of their own uncertainty. Importantly, the mechanism here is different from the motivational account discussed earlier. In the case of unrealistic optimism, people are driven to maintain a positive self-image. In the Dunning-Kruger effect, the overconfidence arises from a metacognitive inability to recognize disconfirming evidence, not from a motivation to ignore it.

It is worth noting that the Dunning-Kruger effect has been debated. Some researchers have argued that part of the pattern can be explained as a statistical artifact: when people with low scores estimate their performance, their estimates tend to regress toward the mean, producing apparent overestimation even without any metacognitive deficit. However, the core finding, that poor performers are poorly calibrated and that improving their skills improves their self-assessment, has been replicated across many studies and domains.

## Expertise and Calibration

You might expect that experts, with their extensive experience, would be well-calibrated. Sometimes they are, but often they are not. The key factor is not the amount of experience but the quality of feedback that accompanies it.

@einhorn_hogarth1978 analyzed why people maintain high confidence in their judgments even when those judgments are inaccurate. They argued that the structure of most judgment tasks prevents people from learning about their errors. Consider a hiring manager who interviews candidates and selects the ones she judges to be the best. She then observes how these employees perform. If they do well, she takes it as confirmation of her judgment. But she never observes the performance of the candidates she rejected. Without this comparison, she cannot know whether her selections were actually better than the alternatives. This creates a one-sided feedback loop: you see the outcomes of your decisions but not the outcomes of the decisions you did not make. This asymmetry makes it easy to accumulate confidence over time without a corresponding increase in accuracy.

Einhorn and Hogarth also pointed out that several features of real-world tasks inflate the apparent accuracy of judgments. If a manager is highly selective, hiring only a small fraction of applicants, the positive hit rate (the proportion of hires who perform well) can be high even if the manager's judgment adds little value. Treatment effects further complicate the picture: hiring someone, training them, and giving them resources may improve their performance regardless of how well they were originally assessed. These factors create an illusion of validity, reinforcing confidence in one's own judgment.

There are exceptions. Weather forecasters, for example, tend to be well-calibrated. When a forecaster says there is a 70% chance of rain, it rains roughly 70% of the time [@lichtenstein_etal1982]. This is because weather forecasters receive clear, immediate, and unambiguous feedback after every prediction. They make many predictions, and the outcomes are easy to observe. Clinical psychologists, by contrast, tend to be poorly calibrated. Their predictions are about complex, long-term outcomes (Will this patient improve? Will this offender reoffend?), and the feedback they receive is delayed, ambiguous, and often incomplete.

The lesson is that experience alone does not cure overconfidence. Without clear and frequent feedback, more experience simply means more confidence, not better accuracy. This is one reason why people often prefer their own intuitive judgment over statistical models, even when models consistently outperform human experts. The confidence that comes with experience feels like evidence of skill, even when it is not. This theme is explored further in the chapter on [statistical vs. intuitive prediction](#statistical-vs-intuitive-prediction).

## A Bayesian Lens

Both forms of overconfidence can be understood as specific failures of [Bayesian reasoning](#bayesian-reasoning). A well-calibrated Bayesian reasoner maintains beliefs that are both unbiased (centered on the best available evidence) and appropriately wide (reflecting genuine uncertainty about outcomes).

The self-favorable bias corresponds to a shifted posterior. The person's belief about themselves is displaced in a positive direction, as if they were applying a biased prior that says "I am exceptional." Confirming evidence, such as past successes and positive traits, is given too much weight, while disconfirming evidence, such as failures and shortcomings, is discounted. The result is a posterior belief that is systematically more favorable than the evidence warrants.

Overprecision corresponds to a narrowed posterior. The person's belief is not necessarily shifted in any direction, but the probability mass is concentrated too tightly around a single estimate. Too little weight is given to outcomes far from the estimate, as if evidence that would widen the distribution is ignored. Once a person has formed an estimate, evidence consistent with that estimate is readily accepted, while evidence suggesting a wider range of possibilities is overlooked. This is [confirmation bias](#confirmation) applied to the precision of one's own beliefs.

The planning fallacy illustrates both distortions at once. When you estimate that your group project will take two weeks, your estimate is shifted toward optimism (the self-favorable bias), and your confidence interval around that estimate is too narrow (overprecision). A Bayesian approach would involve looking at the base rate, how long similar projects have actually taken, and maintaining a distribution of possible outcomes that reflects the full range of past experience. Reference class forecasting, as described by @flyvbjerg2006, is essentially an attempt to apply this outside-view reasoning: ground your prior in the actual distribution of outcomes for similar projects, rather than constructing an optimistic inside-view estimate.

## Summary

Overconfidence is not a single bias but a family of related distortions that can be organized into two types. The self-favorable bias is a shift in beliefs about oneself in a positive direction, and it manifests as overestimation of one's performance or control, overplacement of oneself relative to others, and unrealistic optimism about one's future. Overprecision is a narrowing of uncertainty, in which people are too confident that their estimates are close to the truth. Both types can be understood as expressions of confirmation bias applied to beliefs about the self, but the self-favorable bias also has a motivational component: a drive to maintain a positive self-image. A moderate degree of self-favorable bias appears to be associated with mental health, while its absence is linked to depression and learned helplessness. The planning fallacy demonstrates both forms of overconfidence at once: estimates of future projects are systematically too optimistic and too precise. The Dunning-Kruger effect shows that the least competent individuals are often the most overconfident, because they lack the metacognitive skills to recognize their own errors. Finally, expertise reduces overconfidence only when it is accompanied by clear and frequent feedback. Without such feedback, experience increases confidence without improving accuracy.
