# What Is Intelligence?

Learning goals:

- Explain why intelligence is best understood as a statistical concept rather than a verbal definition.
- Describe the g-factor and how it was discovered.
- Distinguish between the g-factor and s-factors.
- Explain the difference between fluid intelligence and crystallized intelligence.
- Describe how fluid and crystallized intelligence change across the lifespan.

Ask ten psychologists to define intelligence and you will get ten different answers. Some emphasize problem-solving, others learning speed, others the ability to adapt to new environments. This ambiguity sometimes leads people to a relativist position: if we cannot even define intelligence, maybe the concept is meaningless. But this conclusion confuses a verbal definition with a statistical one. We do not need a crisp sentence to make intelligence scientifically useful. We need it to behave consistently in data. And it does. Intelligence is one of the most robust and replicable findings in all of psychology. It may be hard to put into words, but it is easy to measure, and it predicts real-world outcomes with striking consistency.

## The g-Factor

Imagine we organize a mega-Olympics. We grab a hundred random people off the street and have them compete in every event: the 100 meters, the marathon, swimming, high jump, shot put, archery, gymnastics, and so on. What would we find? We would quickly notice that some people do well across the board. They are faster runners, better swimmers, and higher jumpers. Others consistently struggle. Of course there would be exceptions: someone might be a strong swimmer but a poor sprinter. But on the whole, performance across these very different events would be positively correlated. There is a general underlying factor we might call physical fitness that partly determines how well someone does in any athletic event.

@spearman1904 had the same insight about the mind. He noticed that children who scored well on one type of cognitive test tended to score well on others too. A child who excelled at distinguishing musical pitches also tended to do well in school, and a child who did well in Latin also tended to do well in mathematics. Spearman went further than just noting these correlations. He developed a statistical method to test whether a single underlying factor could explain the pattern of positive correlations across very different cognitive tasks. It could. He called this factor the g-factor, short for general intelligence.

In his original study, Spearman tested schoolchildren in an English village on sensory discrimination tasks, such as telling apart two slightly different weights or two slightly different musical tones, and compared their performance to teacher rankings of their intellectual ability. The correlation between general sensory discrimination and general intelligence was remarkably high, close to 1.00 after correcting for measurement error. This was a bold claim: that a single general factor runs through all cognitive abilities, from the most basic perceptual tasks to the most complex academic reasoning. Over a century of subsequent research has confirmed the core finding. People who do well on one cognitive task tend to do well on others, and the g-factor captures this shared variance.

What does this mean in practice? It means that the specific tasks in an IQ test matter less than you might think. Whether we test someone with vocabulary questions, spatial puzzles, or mental arithmetic, we are always partly measuring the same underlying ability. Different IQ tests, despite using very different tasks, tend to agree remarkably well on who scores high and who scores low. This is because most cognitive tasks tap into the g-factor to some degree. In applied and organizational psychology, the g-factor is often referred to as general mental ability (GMA). This is the same construct with a different label, used especially when discussing how intelligence predicts job performance and academic success, a topic we return to in [a later chapter](#intelligence-in-the-real-world).

## Specific Abilities

The physical-fitness analogy also makes clear that general ability is not the whole story. A steady hand matters for archery but not for sprinting. Long legs help in the high jump but not in weightlifting. Similarly, some cognitive abilities are task-specific. Language skill matters for vocabulary tests but not for visual puzzles. Musical talent helps in distinguishing pitches but not in solving algebra problems. Spearman called these s-factors, short for specific intelligence.

Some tasks are almost purely g-loaded, meaning that performance on them is mostly determined by the g-factor. Matrix puzzles, where you must identify the pattern in a grid of abstract shapes and select the missing piece, are the best example. These tasks require working memory, flexible recombination of information, and active problem-solving, all of which are central to general intelligence. Other tasks, like recognizing melodies or drawing from memory, depend more heavily on specific factors. The balance between g and s varies from task to task, but the g-factor is always present to some degree. This is why matrix puzzles are often considered the purest measure of intelligence: they tap strongly into g and only weakly into any specific ability.

## Fluid and Crystallized Intelligence

@cattell1963 refined Spearman's framework by proposing that the g-factor is not a single, uniform ability but can be split into two distinct components: fluid intelligence and crystallized intelligence.

Fluid intelligence is the capacity to flexibly manipulate information in working memory to solve novel problems. It is what you rely on when you encounter something you have never seen before and need to figure it out on the spot. Think of a student in their first statistics class staring at a proof they have never encountered, trying to work through the logic step by step. Or think of solving a Sudoku puzzle: there is nothing to memorize, no facts to retrieve. You simply need to hold multiple constraints in mind and work through the possibilities. Matrix puzzles, the same tasks that load strongly on the g-factor, are a near-pure measure of fluid intelligence.

Crystallized intelligence is the accumulated body of knowledge, skills, and facts stored in long-term memory. It is what you rely on when you already know the answer because you have learned it before. A medical student who has memorized the symptoms of hundreds of diseases, a chess player who recognizes a familiar opening, or a Dutch student who knows that "gezellig" has no direct English translation are all drawing on crystallized intelligence. Vocabulary tests, general knowledge quizzes, and reading comprehension tasks all tap into crystallized intelligence.

Cattell tested this distinction in a study of 277 seventh- and eighth-grade students. He gave them a battery of cognitive tests, including verbal, spatial, reasoning, and number tasks, along with culture-fair tests that used abstract shapes and patterns. He then used factor analysis to see whether one general factor or two would better explain the data. The results clearly supported two distinct factors. The first, identified as fluid intelligence, loaded most strongly on the culture-fair tests and on spatial reasoning. The second, identified as crystallized intelligence, loaded primarily on verbal, reasoning, and number tasks that reflected culturally acquired knowledge. The two factors were correlated with each other (about +.47), which makes sense: crystallized intelligence is partly the result of applying fluid intelligence over time. The smarter you are in the fluid sense, the more you tend to learn and remember.

The most important practical difference between fluid and crystallized intelligence lies in how they change across the lifespan. Fluid intelligence peaks in early adulthood, roughly in the late teens or early twenties, and then gradually declines. This is why learning a new language, picking up a new programming language, or mastering an unfamiliar board game feels harder at 50 than at 20. The raw computational power of the brain slowly diminishes. Crystallized intelligence, by contrast, continues to grow well into middle age and remains relatively stable into old age. An experienced doctor in their sixties may be slower to learn a new diagnostic tool, but they have a vast library of cases, patterns, and knowledge that a younger doctor simply cannot match.

This explains a pattern that many people notice intuitively. With experience, tasks that once required fluid intelligence can be solved through crystallized intelligence. A beginning chess player must calculate every possible move (fluid intelligence). A grandmaster simply recognizes the position and knows the right response (crystallized intelligence). A first-year psychology student struggles to understand a research paper because every concept is new. A professor reads the same paper effortlessly, because decades of accumulated knowledge fill in the gaps. In both cases, the task has not changed, but the balance of fluid and crystallized intelligence needed to perform it has shifted.

This also explains why culture-fair tests, which use abstract shapes and patterns rather than words or facts, are considered a purer measure of fluid intelligence. Because they minimize the role of culturally acquired knowledge, they come closer to measuring raw problem-solving ability. This makes them especially useful when comparing people from different cultural or educational backgrounds.

The distinction between fluid and crystallized intelligence also connects to [the discussion of dual-process theory](#reason-and-intuition). Fluid intelligence, with its reliance on effortful, flexible reasoning, maps onto System 2 processing. Crystallized intelligence, with its reliance on stored knowledge and pattern recognition, maps onto the kind of fast, associative retrieval characteristic of System 1. Similarly, the distinction matters for understanding [why intelligence and rationality can come apart](#intelligence-vs-rationality): a person may have ample fluid intelligence but lack the thinking dispositions or accumulated knowledge needed to reason well in everyday life.

## A Bayesian Lens

The fluid-crystallized distinction maps neatly onto Bayesian inference, the framework for rational belief updating that runs throughout this book (see [Bayesian Reasoning](#bayesian-reasoning)). Crystallized intelligence is your stock of priors: the rich body of knowledge, patterns, and expectations accumulated over a lifetime. Fluid intelligence is the engine that processes new evidence and computes the posterior, flexibly integrating new information with what you already know. A person with strong priors but a weakening engine (that is, high crystallized intelligence but declining fluid intelligence) can still perform well in familiar domains, because their priors do most of the work. But they will struggle when the environment changes and priors alone are not enough. This is exactly what we observe with aging: experienced professionals compensate for slower updating with richer priors. A young doctor and an older doctor may arrive at the same diagnosis, but through different routes. The young doctor reasons through the evidence from scratch. The older doctor recognizes the pattern immediately.

## Summary

Intelligence is difficult to define in words, but it is well defined statistically. The g-factor, first identified by @spearman1904, is a general ability that underlies performance across all cognitive tasks. It is one of the most robust findings in psychology. People who score high on one cognitive task tend to score high on others, and this shared variance is what the g-factor captures. Alongside the g-factor, specific abilities (s-factors) contribute to performance on particular tasks. @cattell1963 further refined this picture by distinguishing between fluid intelligence, the capacity for flexible problem-solving with novel information, and crystallized intelligence, the accumulated knowledge stored in long-term memory. Fluid intelligence peaks in early adulthood and declines with age, while crystallized intelligence continues to grow throughout life. Together, these concepts provide a foundation for understanding how cognitive ability shapes judgment and decision-making.
