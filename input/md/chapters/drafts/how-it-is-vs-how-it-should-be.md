# How It Is vs. How It Should Be

Learning goals:

- You can explain the difference between descriptive, normative, and prescriptive models of judgment and decision-making.
- You can give examples of each type of model.
- You can explain why prescriptive models are often a practical compromise between descriptive and normative models.
- You understand why people sometimes resist normative models, using effective altruism and QALYs as examples.
- You can describe how the distinction between these three model types organizes the field of judgment and decision-making.

## Three Ways to Think About Decisions

Imagine you are choosing what to eat for lunch. A food scientist could study what you actually pick and why. A nutritionist could tell you what you should eat to be as healthy as possible. And a public health official could redesign the cafeteria layout so that healthy options are easier to grab. These three perspectives, studying what people do, defining what is ideal, and finding practical ways to improve, correspond to the three types of models that organize the field of judgment and decision-making: descriptive, normative, and prescriptive models.

This distinction matters because it prevents a common confusion. When researchers show that people make systematic errors in reasoning, they are comparing what people actually do (a descriptive observation) against some standard of what they should do (a normative benchmark). Without being clear about both, it is impossible to say whether something counts as an error at all. And once we know where people go wrong, we need a third type of thinking to figure out how to help, which is where prescriptive models come in.

## Descriptive Models

Descriptive models describe how people actually judge and decide, without evaluating whether those judgments are good or bad. The goal is accuracy: does the model capture what people really do?

The vast majority of models in the field of judgment and decision-making are descriptive. For example, dual-process theory, discussed in the chapter on [Reason and Intuition](#reason-and-intuition), describes the interplay between fast, intuitive thinking and slow, deliberate thinking. It does not say that one system is better than the other; it simply characterizes how the two systems operate. Similarly, prospect theory, which is covered in [Gains and Losses](#gains-and-losses), describes how people evaluate outcomes relative to a reference point, treating losses as more painful than equivalent gains are pleasant. Prospect theory does not claim that people should be loss averse. It simply documents that they are.

Descriptive models also include the many heuristics that people rely on, such as the [availability heuristic](#availability), [representativeness](#representativeness), and [anchoring](#anchoring-and-primacy). Each of these describes a mental shortcut that people use to make judgments under uncertainty. Whether these shortcuts lead to good or bad outcomes depends on the situation. The heuristic itself is simply a description of how people think.

A classic early contribution to descriptive modeling came from @simon1955, who argued that people do not behave like the perfectly rational agents assumed by traditional economics. Instead, they operate within cognitive and environmental constraints, simplifying complex problems to make them manageable. Simon called this bounded rationality: people are rational, but only within the limits of their information, time, and cognitive capacity. Rather than evaluating every possible option to find the best one, people often satisfice, choosing the first option that meets a minimum threshold of acceptability. A student looking for an apartment in Groningen, for example, does not inspect every available room in the city. Instead, they set some minimum requirements for rent, location, and size, and take the first room that meets those requirements. This is not irrational; it is a practical response to the impossibility of evaluating all alternatives. Simon's work was both descriptive, capturing how people actually make decisions, and, as we will see, it also carried normative and prescriptive implications.

## Normative Models

Normative models specify how people should judge and decide if they want to be logically consistent and achieve their goals as effectively as possible. These models assume unlimited time, information, and cognitive capacity. They serve as benchmarks against which actual behavior can be evaluated.

The most prominent normative model for decision-making under uncertainty is expected utility theory. The idea is straightforward: when choosing between options, you should multiply the value (utility) of each possible outcome by its probability, and then choose the option with the highest expected utility. If you are deciding whether to bike or take the bus to campus, and there is a 40% chance of rain, expected utility theory says you should weigh the costs and benefits of each option, taking into account the probability of getting wet. This framework is covered in more detail in [Gains and Losses](#gains-and-losses).

For belief updating, the normative standard is Bayes' theorem, discussed at length in [Bayesian Reasoning](#bayesian-reasoning). Bayes' theorem specifies exactly how you should revise your beliefs when you encounter new evidence. If you initially think there is a 10% chance that your headache is caused by dehydration, and you then learn that you drank very little water today, Bayes' theorem tells you precisely how much to increase that estimate, given how diagnostic the evidence is.

These normative models are powerful because they are mathematically precise. They leave no room for ambiguity about what the "right" answer is, given certain assumptions. But they are also unrealistic as descriptions of human behavior. No one actually computes expected utilities when deciding whether to bike or take the bus. No one applies Bayes' theorem in their head when updating beliefs about what is causing their headache. This is exactly why the distinction between normative and descriptive models matters: the gap between the two is where biases and errors live.

## Prescriptive Models

Prescriptive models bridge the gap between how people actually decide and how they ideally should. They offer practical recommendations that take human limitations into account. Where normative models say what is optimal in theory, prescriptive models say what is achievable in practice.

Consider hand hygiene in hospitals. The normative model is clear: healthcare workers should wash their hands thoroughly with soap and water between every patient interaction. Descriptively, however, compliance with this standard is far from perfect, because handwashing takes time, sinks are not always nearby, and busy professionals cut corners. A prescriptive solution is to install hand sanitizer dispensers at every bedside. This does not achieve the normative ideal of full handwashing, but it dramatically improves hygiene in practice by making the desired behavior easier.

Another example comes from medical statistics. The normative way to reason about diagnostic test results involves applying Bayes' theorem to conditional probabilities. Descriptively, both patients and physicians struggle with this. They misinterpret what it means when a test comes back positive, often dramatically overestimating the probability of disease. A prescriptive solution, discussed in [Bayesian Reasoning](#bayesian-reasoning), is to present the same statistical information using natural frequencies instead of probabilities. Rather than saying "the sensitivity of the test is 90% and the base rate of the disease is 1%," you can say "out of 1,000 people, 10 have the disease, and of those 10, 9 will test positive; of the 990 who do not have the disease, about 99 will also test positive." This format makes it much easier for people to see that a positive test result does not mean a 90% chance of disease. The underlying math is the same, but the way information is presented changes how well people reason.

Prescriptive models also include nudges: small changes in how choices are presented that steer people toward better decisions without restricting their freedom. Default options, such as automatically enrolling employees in a pension plan unless they opt out, are a classic example. These interventions, discussed in [Steering Other People's Choices](#steering-other-peoples-choices), work precisely because they exploit descriptive knowledge about human behavior (people tend to stick with defaults) to move people closer to normatively better outcomes (saving for retirement).

## Resistance to Normative Models

If normative models define the best way to make decisions, you might expect people to welcome them. In practice, the opposite often happens. People frequently resist normative approaches, finding them cold, unnatural, or even offensive. This resistance is not random; it arises precisely because normative models conflict with the heuristics and biases that shape everyday judgment.

Effective altruism illustrates this tension well. Effective altruism is a movement that tries to answer a simple question: how can we do the most good with the resources we have? It applies a version of expected utility theory to charitable giving. Rather than donating to whichever cause tugs at your heartstrings, effective altruism recommends calculating where your money will have the largest impact. Organizations like GiveWell evaluate charities by estimating the cost per life saved or the cost per quality-adjusted life year (QALY) gained. A QALY is a measure that combines the quality and quantity of life: one year of perfect health equals one QALY, and one year of life with a serious disability might equal, say, 0.5 QALYs. By this logic, a medical intervention that gives 10 people an extra year at 0.8 QALYs each produces 8 QALYs, and you can compare this with other interventions to decide where resources should go.

This is, in principle, a straightforward application of normative reasoning to an important real-world problem. Yet many people find it deeply uncomfortable. One reason is that QALYs seem to place a numerical value on human life, which conflicts with the intuition that every life is equally and infinitely valuable. Another reason is that effective altruism often recommends donating to causes that are geographically and psychologically distant, such as distributing malaria bed nets in sub-Saharan Africa, rather than helping a local homeless shelter. This clashes with our natural tendency to care more about identifiable, nearby individuals than about anonymous, distant ones, a bias discussed in [Risk Perception](#risk-perception) under the identifiable victim effect.

There is also a deeper issue. Normative models strip away the emotional and social dimensions of decision-making. When you donate to a friend's fundraiser, you are not just trying to maximize global welfare; you are also maintaining a relationship, expressing solidarity, and responding to a specific person's story. These motivations are real and important to people, even if they are irrelevant from the perspective of expected utility theory. The result is that approaches like effective altruism are sometimes perceived as cold and calculating, not because they are wrong in any logical sense, but because they conflict with the intuitive, affect-driven processes that normally guide moral judgment. As discussed in the chapter on [Morality](#morality), moral judgments often arise from fast intuitions rather than careful reasoning, and people experience discomfort when a normative framework asks them to override those intuitions.

This does not mean that normative models are useless or that resistance to them is always justified. It means that prescriptive models must take this resistance seriously. If you want people to actually make better decisions, it is not enough to tell them what the optimal choice is. You also need to understand why they are drawn to suboptimal choices, and design interventions that work with human psychology rather than against it.

## How the Three Models Organize the Field

The distinction between descriptive, normative, and prescriptive models is not just an abstract classification. It organizes the entire field of judgment and decision-making.

Most of the chapters in this book are primarily descriptive. They document the heuristics people use, the biases that result, and the conditions under which these biases are more or less pronounced. Chapters on [availability](#availability), [representativeness](#representativeness), [anchoring](#anchoring-and-primacy), [overconfidence](#overconfidence), [confirmation](#confirmation), and many others describe systematic patterns in how people think, without claiming that people should think this way.

The normative benchmark in this book is usually Bayesian reasoning, supplemented by expected utility theory for decisions under uncertainty. These frameworks define what it means to reason well, and they provide the yardstick against which biases are measured. Without a normative standard, the concept of bias has no meaning; a bias is, by definition, a systematic departure from some norm.

Prescriptive insights appear throughout the book as well, especially in applied chapters. The chapter on [Medical Decision-Making](#medical-decision-making) discusses how decision support systems can help physicians avoid diagnostic errors. The chapter on [Steering Other People's Choices](#steering-other-peoples-choices) explores how choice architecture can improve decisions. And the chapter on [Statistical vs. Intuitive Prediction](#statistical-vs-intuitive-prediction) makes the case for replacing clinical intuition with statistical models in high-stakes settings like criminal justice and hiring.

@simon1955 captured the essential insight that links these three perspectives. By showing that human rationality is bounded, he made clear why descriptive and normative models diverge. People do not fail to be rational because they are foolish. They fall short because the world is complex and the mind has limits. Once you accept this, the role of prescriptive models becomes obvious: they are tools for helping bounded minds make better decisions in a complex world.

## A Bayesian Lens

Bayesian updating provides the normative backbone for much of this book. The basic idea is that you start with a prior belief, encounter evidence, and update your belief accordingly to form a posterior belief. Bayes' theorem specifies exactly how this should be done, taking into account both the strength of your prior and the reliability of the evidence.

Descriptive models document the many ways in which people deviate from this standard. People neglect base rates, overweight vivid evidence, stick with their initial beliefs in the face of contradictory data, and are overconfident in their estimates. Each of these biases represents a specific departure from Bayesian updating: a prior that is ignored, evidence that is given too much or too little weight, or a posterior that is too narrow or too extreme.

Prescriptive models, in turn, often aim to nudge people closer to Bayesian rationality. Presenting information as natural frequencies rather than conditional probabilities helps people approximate Bayesian reasoning without doing the math. Structured decision-making tools force people to consider base rates. Checklists prevent premature anchoring on a single hypothesis. In each case, the prescriptive intervention works by reducing the gap between how people actually update their beliefs and how Bayes' theorem says they should.

## Summary

The field of judgment and decision-making is organized around three types of models. Descriptive models capture how people actually think and decide. Normative models define how people should think and decide to be logically consistent and achieve their goals. Prescriptive models offer practical ways to close the gap between the two. Most of the research in this field is descriptive, documenting the heuristics and biases that characterize human judgment. The primary normative standard is Bayesian reasoning, supplemented by expected utility theory. Prescriptive interventions, such as natural frequencies, nudges, and decision support systems, use descriptive knowledge about human psychology to help people make better decisions. People sometimes resist normative models, as illustrated by reactions to effective altruism and QALYs, because these models conflict with the intuitive and emotional processes that normally guide judgment. Understanding this resistance is itself an important part of building effective prescriptive tools.
