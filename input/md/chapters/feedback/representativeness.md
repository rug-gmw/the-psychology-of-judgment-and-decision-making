### Confirmation-bias connection is plausible but should be framed carefully
> “The representativeness heuristic can also be understood as a form of confirmation bias…”

This is defensible, but it is more an interpretive connection than a standard canonical claim. I would soften slightly:
- “can be related to”
- “shares a family resemblance with”
- “involves a confirmation-like failure to consider disconfirming category information”

### Randomness section: gambler’s fallacy and hot hand need one nuance
You write:
> “The flip side of the gambler's fallacy is the hot-hand belief.”

This is broadly right, but the relationship is not simply “flip side.” The gambler’s fallacy often arises when people expect negative recency in random sequences; hot-hand belief reflects perceived positive recency in human performance. Better wording:
- “A related error, though in the opposite direction, is the hot-hand belief.”

### Minor issue in Bayesian lens
> “people's stereotype of what random sequences should look like acts as a rigid prior about randomness.”

This is a bit slippery in Bayesian terms. Earlier in the outline, the randomness item is framed as a prototype/stereotype of randomness, not necessarily as a prior in the formal sense. Calling it a “prior” may confuse readers, because the error is also about a mistaken generative model of randomness, not just a bad prior probability.

**Suggestion:** revise to:
- “people rely on an incorrect mental model of what random sequences should look like”
or
- “they impose a prototype of randomness that misguides their judgments”

That would be more precise.

### Possible omission: base-rate neglect as the fundamental mechanism could be signposted more strongly
The chapter says this, but the structure could emphasize it more explicitly. The outline treats all later sections as consequences. A sentence at the start of each consequence section could remind the reader:
- “Because people rely on representativeness instead of base rates…”

## 5. Style

### Overall style is plain and readable
The language is generally clean, accessible, and appropriate for a textbook.

### A few filler or overemphatic phrases can be trimmed
Examples:
> “As you will see”

> “without hesitation”

> “The results were striking.”

> “One of the most surprising consequences…”

> “This chapter is about…”

These are not wrong, but they add a slightly conversational tone that could be tightened in a textbook chapter.

### Some sentences are longer than necessary
For example:
> “@barhillel1980 argued that people neglect base rates not because they cannot understand them, but because they perceive them as less relevant than the specific evidence at hand.”

This is clear, but a split would improve readability:
- “Bar-Hillel argued that people do not neglect base rates because they cannot understand them. They neglect them because the specific evidence feels more relevant to the case at hand.”

### “What might be called an averaging heuristic”
> “One way to understand the underlying reasoning is through what might be called an averaging heuristic.”

The outline presents averaging heuristic as an explanation, so you can state it more directly:
- “One proposed explanation is an averaging heuristic.”

### Be careful with “entirely”
> “People based their predictions entirely on resemblance…”

> “The representativeness heuristic amounts to basing your judgment almost entirely on the likelihood…”

The first is too strong. Better:
- “primarily”
- “to a large extent”
because in many such tasks people underweight rather than literally ignore base rates.

### Consistency in naming
You alternate among:
- “representativeness heuristic”
- “representativeness”
- “similarity”
- “prototype”
This is fine, but keep the distinctions clear. In some places “representativeness” means the heuristic; elsewhere it means the match between stimulus and prototype. A little more consistency would help.
