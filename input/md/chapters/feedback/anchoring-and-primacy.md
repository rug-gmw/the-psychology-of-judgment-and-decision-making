## 1. Structure

### Overall flow is strong
The chapter has a clear progression:

1. intuitive example  
2. classic studies  
3. mechanism  
4. boundary conditions  
5. extension to primacy  
6. applications  
7. debiasing  
8. Bayesian lens  
9. summary

That largely matches the outline and reads naturally.

### Structural issue: the mechanism section overcommits too early
> “For years, the dominant explanation was that people start with the anchor and adjust away from it… @strack_mussweiler1997 proposed a different explanation. They argued that anchoring works through selective accessibility.”

This is a good transition, but the chapter then treats selective accessibility as *the* explanation. Since later primacy material returns to anchoring-and-adjustment and belief-adjustment models, the structure creates tension: is anchoring best understood as selective accessibility, insufficient adjustment, or both?

**Suggestion:** signal earlier that multiple mechanisms may operate in different tasks. For example:
- numerical estimation often shows selective accessibility,
- sequential impression updating can also be modeled as belief adjustment.

That would make the transition into primacy smoother.

### Structural issue: cross-modal anchoring interrupts the build toward primacy
Current order:
- mechanism
- absurd anchors
- cross-modal anchoring
- primacy

This works, but **cross-modal anchoring** feels like a side branch before the chapter’s major conceptual move from numbers to impressions.

**Suggestion:** consider moving “Anchoring Across the Senses” to *after* primacy or integrating it as a short subsection under boundary conditions. That would preserve the main conceptual arc:
**numerical anchoring → mechanism → extreme anchors → primacy → applications → special cases like cross-modal anchoring**.

### Structural issue: applications section is slightly overloaded
> “The same principle applies in salary negotiations…”  
> “Anchoring also affects medical diagnosis…”  
> “Similarly, anchoring influences legal sentencing…”

This section piles together several domains quickly. Real estate is discussed in detail, then advertising/salary/medicine/law are mentioned briefly. That creates an imbalance.

**Suggestion:** either
- keep this section focused on **real estate + one brief paragraph on other domains**, or
- add a subheading like **“Other applications”** so the compression feels intentional.

## 2. Duplication

### Repeated explanation of the core effect
Several passages restate the same idea in near-identical terms:

> “The initial number acts as a starting point that pulls the final estimate toward it.”  
> “This is the anchoring effect: our judgments are pulled toward an initial value…”  
> “The anchor makes certain information more available, and that information biases the estimate.”  
> “Anchoring is the tendency for an initial value to pull subsequent estimates toward it…”

Some repetition is useful pedagogically, but here it becomes slightly redundant.

**Suggestion:** tighten repeated formulations, especially in:
- opening section
- “Classic Demonstrations”
- summary

### Repetition of “even irrelevant anchors matter”
This point appears multiple times:

> “even when that value should have no influence”  
> “even when the anchor is clearly irrelevant”  
> “Even when people are explicitly warned that the anchor is irrelevant”  
> “even absurd anchors influence estimates”

These are related but overlapping. Consider distinguishing them more clearly:
- **irrelevant anchors**
- **implausible anchors**
- **warned-about anchors**

That would reduce redundancy while preserving the important boundary-condition message.

### Repetition in Bayesian section
> “the first piece of information… forms a strong prior”  
> “the anchor creates a prior that is sticky”  
> “people cling to the prior”

These all make the same point. One of these formulations can be removed.

## 3. Concreteness

This chapter is generally concrete and example-rich. Still, a few places would benefit from more specificity.

### Add one concrete study or example on legal anchoring
> “Similarly, anchoring influences legal sentencing: a prosecutor's recommended sentence or a plaintiff's damage request serves as an anchor…”

This is plausible and useful, but unlike real estate, it is not anchored to a named study or specific finding. Since the chapter outline mentions sentencing applications, this is a good place for one.

**Suggestion:** briefly cite a concrete sentencing or damages study and give one result. Even a one-sentence illustration would help.

### The “shared representation of magnitude” paragraph is slightly abstract
> “Numbers, physical lengths, durations, temperatures, and weights are all partly represented in overlapping neural systems.”

This risks sounding more settled than it is, and it is somewhat abstract for a textbook at this level.

**Suggestion:** either soften it:
- “may reflect partly overlapping representations of magnitude”
or add a concrete hook:
- “for example, people often map quantity onto spatial metaphors such as ‘high’ prices or ‘long’ waits.”

### The Bayesian section would benefit from a very concrete mini-example
> “Crucially, the order in which evidence arrives should not matter.”

That is correct, but abstract. A one-line illustration would sharpen it:
- “If a person is described as both generous and rude, the final impression should not depend on which adjective came first.”

### Primacy section could use a clearer contrast with recency
> “Whether primacy or recency effects emerge depends on several factors…”

This is accurate, but still abstract.

**Suggestion:** add a concrete example:
- “for example, if jurors evaluate each witness as testimony unfolds, recent evidence may carry more weight than if they hear all testimony and only then give a final judgment.”

### Missing or underemphasized: the definition’s explicit link to availability
The outline defines anchoring as:
> “The anchor is highly available … making anchor-consistent information more accessible in memory…”

The draft does make this connection later, but not in the initial definition.

> “This is the anchoring effect: our judgments are pulled toward an initial value…”

This is fine, but slightly incomplete relative to the outline.

**Suggestion:** build the availability link into the first definition, not just the mechanism section.

### Potential overstatement about the dominant explanation
> “For years, the dominant explanation was that people start with the anchor and adjust away from it…”

This is broadly fair, but then the chapter implies selective accessibility has superseded it. In the literature, mechanisms differ by task and context. The text should be a bit more cautious.

**Suggestion:** revise to:
- “One influential explanation…”
- “A later and highly influential account…”

### Potential overstatement in cross-modal anchoring
> “The explanation proposed by @oppenheimer_etal2008 is that the brain has a shared representation of magnitude.”

This sounds stronger than the outline, which says:
> “This may reflect the brain’s shared representation of magnitude…”

**Suggestion:** preserve that caution. The current wording risks presenting a debated interpretation as settled fact.

### Possible issue in the Hogarth & Einhorn summary
> “In general, primacy effects are strongest when information is simple, the sequence is long, and people give their judgment only at the end.”

This may be too simplified. Hogarth and Einhorn’s model is more conditional than that sentence suggests, and in some settings end-of-sequence responses can also produce recency. If you keep it, qualify it.

**Suggestion:** use softer language:
- “Under some common conditions…”
- “Their model predicts primacy in some sequential-evaluation settings, but recency in others…”

### Missing outline point: “experts are less likely to report using the listing price”
You cover this well:
> “agents were significantly less likely than students to mention the listing price.”

Good.

### Missing outline point: “informing people about anchoring does not attenuate it”
You cover this directly:
> “Simply telling people about anchoring does not reduce it.”

Good.

## 5. Style

### Plain and readable overall
The prose is clear and accessible. It avoids heavy jargon and uses examples well.

### A few places are too emphatic or rhetorical for textbook style
> “A random number nearly doubled the estimate.”  
> “Both groups were wildly off…”  
> “The results were striking…”  
> “This finding has an unsettling implication…”

These phrases are lively, but they lean slightly journalistic.

**Suggestion:** tone them down a little for consistency with the rest of the book:
- “substantially increased”
- “both groups substantially underestimated the true value”
- “the results suggested”
- “this implies”

### Some filler phrases could be trimmed
> “If anchoring only affected trivial estimates about rivers and historical figures, it would be an interesting curiosity but little more.”

This is stylistically fine, but somewhat conversational and expendable.

> “From a practical standpoint, the study suggests that…”

Can be shortened to:
- “Practically, the study suggests…”

### Repeated use of “This is consistent with…”
That phrase appears several times and becomes formulaic.

Examples:
> “This is consistent with the selective accessibility mechanism…”  
> “This fits with the anchoring account…”

**Suggestion:** vary with:
- “This supports…”
- “This pattern is compatible with…”
- “This interpretation aligns with…”

### Slight jargon creep
> “anchor-consistent information”  
> “diagnostic value”  
> “posterior belief”

These are appropriate in moderation, but where they occur, make sure surrounding prose stays simple. The Bayesian section especially gets more technical than the rest of the chapter.

## Passage-specific revision suggestions

### 1. Opening definition
> “This is the anchoring effect: our judgments are pulled toward an initial value, even when that value should have no influence…”

**Revise to include the mechanism hinted in the outline:**  
“Anchoring is the tendency to start from an initial value or impression and adjust insufficiently from it. The anchor becomes highly available in memory, making anchor-consistent information easier to retrieve and biasing the final judgment.”

### 2. Mechanism section
> “@strack_mussweiler1997 proposed a different explanation.”

**Revise for balance:**  
“@strack_mussweiler1997 proposed a later and highly influential explanation…”

### 3. Cross-modal section
> “The explanation proposed by @oppenheimer_etal2008 is that the brain has a shared representation of magnitude.”

**Revise for caution:**  
“One interpretation offered by @oppenheimer_etal2008 is that anchoring may rely on partly shared representations of magnitude across domains…”

### 4. Primacy section
> “This pattern is consistent with an anchoring-and-adjustment process…”

This is plausible, but because the chapter has just emphasized selective accessibility, the reader may wonder whether these are competing accounts.

**Revise to integrate the perspectives:**  
“This pattern is consistent with the broader idea that early information disproportionately shapes later judgment, whether described as anchoring-and-adjustment or as a form of sequential belief updating.”

### 5. Hogarth & Einhorn paragraph
> “In general, primacy effects are strongest when information is simple, the sequence is long, and people give their judgment only at the end.”

**Revise for accuracy:**  
“Their model predicts that both primacy and recency can occur, depending on task structure. Under some conditions, early information dominates; under others, later information does.”

### 6. Applications section
> “The same principle applies in salary negotiations…”

This is fine, but it reads like an unsupported aside.

**Suggestion:** either add a citation/example or trim it.

### 7. Debiasing section
> “While this strategy has some empirical support…”

This is vague.

**Suggestion:** either name the strategy more concretely and cite support, or remove “some empirical support” and present it as a plausible implication rather than an established intervention.

### 8. Bayesian lens
> “Whether the listing price is €66,000 or €84,000, your estimate of the property's true value should converge on the same number once you have inspected the house and reviewed the market data.”

This is a good example. Consider moving it earlier in the paragraph and pairing it with the Asch example for symmetry.

## Bottom line

This is a strong draft: clear, engaging, and closely aligned with the outline. The main improvements I would recommend are:

1. **Clarify the relationship between selective accessibility and anchoring-and-adjustment** so the chapter does not imply a single settled mechanism.
2. **Qualify a few overstatements**, especially about cross-modal anchoring and Hogarth & Einhorn.
3. **Reduce mild repetition** of the core definition and “irrelevant anchor” point.
4. **Add one concrete legal/sentencing study** and, if possible, one more concrete debiasing reference.
5. **Tighten style slightly** by toning down journalistic phrases.

With those changes, the chapter would be both more precise and more cohesive.
