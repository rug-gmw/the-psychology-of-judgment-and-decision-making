## 1. Structure

### Overall
The chapter is well organized and broadly follows the outline. The sequence from core tension → paradigms → mechanisms → synthesis → Bayesian lens works. The opening kitchen example is effective and concrete.

That said, there are a few structural issues:

### A. The chapter drifts from “cooperation” into “fairness/moral punishment” without always signaling the shift
For example:

> “The ultimatum game shows that people reject unfair offers even at a personal cost, revealing a willingness to enforce fairness norms.”

This is true, but ultimatum-game behavior is not exactly the same phenomenon as cooperation in a social dilemma. It belongs in the chapter, but it would help to explicitly mark it as a related paradigm about **norm enforcement**, not direct cooperative contribution.

**Suggested fix:** Add a transition before the ultimatum section such as:
- “Not all cooperation research examines direct contribution to a common good. Some paradigms instead ask how people enforce norms of fairness, which is one route by which cooperation becomes stable.”

### B. “What Sustains Cooperation” partly repeats earlier conclusions instead of advancing the argument
This section reads more like an extended summary than a new conceptual step:

> “Across all of these games, a few themes emerge consistently. First, people cooperate far more than the narrow predictions of game theory suggest…”

Because the same point has already been made after the prisoner’s dilemma, public goods game, framing section, and intuitive cooperation section, this can feel redundant.

**Suggested fix:** Either:
- shorten this section substantially and make it explicitly integrative (“Three mechanisms sustain cooperation: expectations, norm enforcement, and intuitive default responses”), or
- fold parts of it into the final summary.

### C. The Bayesian lens comes too late to shape the chapter
The Bayesian section is good, but because it appears only near the end, it feels appended rather than integrated.

> “Strategic interaction requires maintaining and updating beliefs about other people.”

This is one of the chapter’s central conceptual insights. It would work better if lightly foreshadowed earlier, especially in the prisoner’s dilemma or trust game.

**Suggested fix:** Add one sentence earlier, e.g. in the prisoner's dilemma or trust game:
- “Even in a one-shot game, players are not just choosing an action; they are acting on beliefs about what the other person is likely to do.”

### D. Missing explicit bridge from tragedy of the commons to the lab paradigms
The chapter moves from commons problems to prisoner’s dilemma smoothly enough, but the bridge could be sharper.

> “Changing the outcome requires changing the structure, whether through regulation, communication, or the kinds of psychological mechanisms we will examine next.”

This is slightly vague. The next sections are mostly *experimental games*, not directly “psychological mechanisms.”

**Suggested fix:** Replace with:
- “Researchers study these tensions in simplified experimental games that isolate trust, reciprocity, fairness, and punishment.”

## 2. Duplication

There is moderate repetition of the same core claims.

### A. Repetition of “people cooperate more than game theory predicts”
This idea appears many times:

> “What these experiments consistently show is that people cooperate far more than pure self-interest would predict…”

> “Game theory predicts universal defection, yet real people cooperate at substantial rates.”

> “These deviations from theoretical predictions are not noise…”

> “Together, these findings paint a picture of human nature that is more cooperative than traditional economic models assume…”

The repetition is not wrong, but it becomes predictable.

**Suggested fix:** State this strongly once early, then vary the emphasis:
- one section can focus on **cooperation rates**
- another on **fairness**
- another on **norm enforcement**
- another on **context sensitivity**

### B. Repetition of the “emotion overrides calculation” explanation
Examples:

> “This feeling overrides the cold calculation…”

> “people punished because free riding made them angry, not because they calculated…”

> “moral behavior arises from intuitive, affect-driven processes…”

This is a central theme, but the wording is repetitive and slightly overstates a dichotomy. In several paradigms, behavior likely reflects both preference structure and affect, not simply “emotion versus reason.”

**Suggested fix:** Vary and soften:
- “suggests that people value fairness intrinsically”
- “indicates that emotional reactions are part of the mechanism”
- “shows that utility is not exhausted by material payoff”

### C. Duplication between “What Sustains Cooperation” and “Summary”
The summary repeats the chapter almost section by section. That is normal, but because “What Sustains Cooperation” already functions as a summary, the final “Summary” is too repetitive.

**Suggested fix:** Make “What Sustains Cooperation” more conceptual and reduce the end summary to 1 short paragraph.

## 3. Concreteness

The chapter is generally concrete and much stronger than many textbook drafts on this dimension. Still, a few places remain abstract or would benefit from tighter empirical grounding.

### A. “System 1” and “System 2” explanation in the commons section could use a more concrete behavioral example
> “The benefit of exploiting the resource is immediate and tangible, the kind of concrete outcome that System 1 processes easily. The cost, by contrast, is distributed across many people and stretched over time…”

This is clear but still abstract.

**Suggested fix:** Add a specific example:
- “For a fisher, today’s larger catch is visible immediately; the depletion of the stock is gradual, uncertain, and shared across many others.”

### B. The chapter mentions climate change but does not illustrate how cooperation dilemmas scale up
> “It appears when fishers overexploit a shared fishing ground, when commuters each choose to drive rather than cycle, and when countries delay cutting carbon emissions.”

A brief elaboration on one of these would make the opening stronger.

**Suggested fix:** One sentence:
- “A single country bears the immediate political cost of reducing emissions, while the climate benefit is delayed and spread globally.”

### C. The public goods game section would benefit from a very explicit worked example
The arithmetic is good, but a simple mini-table or one more sentence would help students.

> “Contributing 1 euro to the pool returns only 0.40 euros to the contributor…”

**Suggested fix:** Add:
- “So if you keep your 1 euro, you are personally better off by 0.60 euros, even though the group as a whole loses 0.60 euros in total gains.”

### D. The “warm glow” phrase appears without explanation
> “the warm glow of cooperating with others…”

This is understandable, but in a textbook it might be worth either naming it as an informal phrase or connecting it to a more precise concept.

**Suggested fix:** Either remove it or gloss it:
- “the positive affect people often report after acting cooperatively”

### E. The Bayesian section could use one numeric toy example
Right now it stays verbal:

> “If you send money in a trust game and your partner returns a fair share, your belief that they are trustworthy increases.”

**Suggested fix:** Add a simple example:
- “For example, you might begin by thinking there is a 50% chance a new partner is trustworthy. After one generous return, that estimate may rise; after repeated defections, it falls.”

## 4. Accuracy and coverage relative to the outline

### A. The chapter largely covers the outline faithfully
Included and well represented:
- core tension
- tragedy of the commons
- prisoner’s dilemma
- ultimatum game
- dictator game
- trust game
- public goods game
- altruistic punishment
- framing effects on cooperation
- intuitive cooperation
- Bayesian lens

### B. One key outline phrase is underdeveloped: “social norms”
The opening says cooperation draws on “social norms,” but there is no dedicated treatment of norms beyond scattered mentions.

> “Understanding why requires drawing on moral intuitions…, framing effects…, and the interplay between fast intuition and slow deliberation…”

**What’s missing:** a more explicit statement that norms operate through expectations about what others do and what others approve of.

**Suggested fix:** In the trust, public goods, or framing sections, add a brief distinction:
- “Social norms matter in two ways: descriptive norms tell us what others typically do, and injunctive norms tell us what others approve or disapprove of.”

### C. The chapter does not really explain “game theory provides the formal framework”
The text uses the phrase, but beyond dominant strategy and Nash equilibrium, there is little explanation of why these paradigms are game-theoretic rather than just social experiments.

**Suggested fix:** Add one sentence early:
- “Game theory models these situations by specifying each player’s options, payoffs, and the way one person’s best choice depends on what others do.”

### D. “Intuitive cooperation” is presented too strongly given the state of the literature
> “These findings suggest that cooperation is the default, intuitive response, while defection requires deliberate override.”

This is too definitive for a textbook, especially because the social heuristics account and time-pressure findings have been debated and not always replicated cleanly.

**Suggested fix:** Qualify the claim:
- “These findings were initially interpreted as evidence that cooperation can be an intuitive default in many contexts, though the robustness and boundary conditions of this effect remain debated.”

Similarly:

> “the evidence suggests that people are fundamentally cooperative and must reason their way into selfishness.”

This overstates the evidence and is more rhetorical than textbook-accurate.

**Suggested fix:** Replace with:
- “the evidence suggests that many people bring cooperative heuristics into one-shot interactions, even when strict self-interest would favor defection.”

### E. The Hardin section could use a caution that “tragedy” is not inevitable under all institutions
> “The problem is not that people are selfish. The problem is that the structure of the situation makes selfishness the dominant strategy.”

This is useful, but Hardin’s framing has been heavily criticized because communities often successfully govern commons. Since the chapter already emphasizes structure, it would be more accurate to acknowledge that commons can be managed.

**Suggested fix:** Add one sentence:
- “Later research showed that shared resources are not always doomed: under the right institutions, monitoring, and norms, communities can manage commons successfully.”

You need not expand fully, but this prevents oversimplification.

### F. The statement about punishment being “purely altruistic” is too absolute
> “Punishment was purely altruistic in the sense that it cost the punisher and could not generate any future personal benefit.”

In the reshuffled design, it could not generate future benefit **through repeated interaction with the same person**, but punishment may still satisfy emotion, signal identity, or sustain general expectations. “Purely altruistic” is too strong.

**Suggested fix:** Revise to:
- “Punishment was costly and could not be justified by direct strategic gain from future interaction with the same target.”

### G. Minor accuracy/style issue with percentages from the prisoner study
You write:

> “behavioral studies show substantial cooperation (~55% among German prisoners...)” in the outline, but the draft gives a more nuanced result by condition.

The draft is better than the outline here. However, because the percentages differ by design, it would help to clarify that the 56% figure applies to the simultaneous condition only.

### H. Missing explicit mention of “prisoner’s dilemma” as a model of one-shot interaction
This matters because later “intuitive cooperation” depends on the mismatch between one-shot lab settings and repeated real life.

**Suggested fix:** In the prisoner's dilemma section:
- “In the one-shot version, players cannot build reputation or retaliate later, making cooperation especially puzzling from a narrow self-interest perspective.”

## 5. Style

Overall the prose is clear and readable. It is more polished than a typical first draft. The main style issues are occasional overstatement, mild rhetorical inflation, and a few filler phrases.

### A. Some sentences are too emphatic for a textbook
Examples:

> “This is a striking result…”

> “The results were dramatic.”

> “This is one of the clearest demonstrations in this book…”

These are not wrong, but the tone is slightly promotional.

**Suggested fix:** Use flatter academic prose:
- “This result is notable…”
- “The effect was large…”
- “This illustrates especially clearly…”

### B. Avoid false contrasts between “pure self-interest” and “real people”
Example:

> “The gap between the prediction and the behavior tells us that moral intuitions about fairness and reciprocity are powerful enough to override pure self-interest.”

This implies that game-theoretic prediction is psychologically natural and anything else is an override. Better to say that the standard model assumes payoffs limited to own material outcomes.

**Suggested fix:** Replace with:
- “The gap suggests that people value fairness and reciprocity in addition to their own immediate material payoff.”

This is plainer and theoretically cleaner.

### C. Some filler phrases can be trimmed
Examples:

> “ever since”

> “The story goes like this.”

> “But this is not what happens.”

> “This is where altruistic punishment comes in.”

These are readable but conversational filler.

**Suggested fix:** Tighten:
- “Hardin described…”
- “In the standard story…”
- “Empirical results differ.”
- “A key mechanism is altruistic punishment.”

### D. Cross-chapter references are sometimes woven awkwardly into sentences
Example:

> “This connects directly to the idea that moral judgments are driven by fast, automatic feelings rather than slow, calculated reasoning ([Morality](#morality)).”

This is fine once or twice, but repeated parenthetical links can interrupt flow.

**Suggested fix:** Reserve cross-links for end-of-paragraph sentences or “see also” formulations.

### E. Consistency of terminology
Mostly consistent, but note:
- “free ride” / “free rider’s payoff” / “free riding” are all fine, but keep usage parallel.
- “System 1 default” is repeated often; consider alternating with “intuitive default” only if the technical term has already been established.

## Example of a useful revision target

Current passage:

> “The evidence suggests that people are fundamentally cooperative and must reason their way into selfishness.”

Suggested revision:

> “The evidence instead suggests that many people bring cooperative habits and expectations into social interactions. In one-shot settings, these intuitions can promote cooperation even when narrow material self-interest would favor defection.”

This keeps the main point while making it more accurate and less rhetorical.
