## 1. Structure

### Strong overall arc, but one section feels bolted on
The chapter mostly moves logically:

1. broad motivation  
2. framework theories  
3. stances  
4. schemas  
5. scripts  
6. artifacts vs. natural kinds  
7. errors/misapplication  
8. Bayesian lens  
9. summary

That is a sensible progression from broad to specific and back to integration.

### Problem: “Framework theories” and “stances” are not clearly related early enough
You write:

> “Within these framework theories, people adopt different explanatory stances depending on the situation.”

This risks implying that stances are simply nested inside framework theories. But in the outline, the relation is looser: framework theories are domain-level background knowledge, while stances are modes of construal. The chapter should explain more explicitly whether stances are:
- tools used within framework theories,
- alternative levels of explanation,
- or overlapping but distinct constructs.

A short bridge sentence would help, e.g.:
“Framework theories specify broad domains of knowledge; stances are the explanatory mode a person adopts when interpreting a specific entity or event.”

### “When Stances Go Wrong” comes a bit late and duplicates earlier material
The discussion of cross-domain misapplication already appears in “Framework Theories”:

> “Problems arise when knowledge from one framework is inappropriately applied to another.”

Then it returns in:

> “When a stance is applied inappropriately, it can lead to systematic errors in reasoning.”

These sections are closely related and could be better integrated. Either:
- move “When Stances Go Wrong” directly after “Stances,” or
- keep it later but make it explicitly a synthesis of framework-confusion + stance-misapplication + schema effects.

Right now it feels like a second pass over a point the reader already got.

### Transition into artifacts/natural kinds could be sharper
You write:

> “One of the most fundamental ways that stances, schemas, and scripts interact can be seen in how people reason about two broad categories of things: artifacts and natural kinds.”

This is serviceable, but “scripts” do not actually play much role in the section that follows. Since the section is mainly about stance activation, revise to avoid overpromising:
- “One important illustration of stance activation can be seen in how people reason about artifacts and natural kinds.”

### Bayesian section is appropriate, but the “zero probability” phrasing is too strong
Structurally, ending with the Bayesian lens is good. But this sentence overstates things:

> “...effectively assigning zero probability to explanations from the wrong domain.”

That sounds too rigid. People do consider cross-domain explanations, especially in superstition, anthropomorphism, and metaphor. Better:
- “strongly down-weighting”
- “making such explanations less available or less plausible”

## 2. Duplication

### Repetition of “different kinds of background knowledge”
Opening example:

> “...each draws on a different kind of background knowledge: knowledge about animal minds, about how machines work, and about physical forces.”

Later in framework theories:

> “Three core domains stand out: naive physics, naive psychology, and naive biology.”

And again in the explanation of each framework. This is fine conceptually, but the opening and the framework section partly duplicate one another. Consider tightening the opening so it introduces the idea without previewing all three domains in full.

### Repetition of “different stances lead to different explanations”
You say versions of this several times:

> “The best stance is the one that makes the most sense of the situation.”

> “The stance you adopt is not merely an abstract explanatory choice. It shapes the entire causal narrative you construct...”

> “The key insight is that the type of thing you encounter triggers a default stance...”

These are all good points, but they partly restate the same claim. You could compress one of them and use the saved space for a concrete study or stronger example.

### Duplication between “Framework Theories” and “When Stances Go Wrong”
Examples of misplaced-domain reasoning appear in both sections:
- storm is angry
- illness caused by thoughts
- anthropomorphism
- conspiracy-style intentional explanations
- just-world/design-like teleology

This material should either be consolidated or clearly differentiated:
- framework confusions = applying knowledge from one domain to another
- stance errors = choosing the wrong explanatory mode in a particular case

Right now they blur together.

## 3. Concreteness

This is the main place the chapter could improve.

### Add at least one specific developmental study to support framework theories
This passage is too general:

> “Infants as young as three to four months show surprise when objects appear to pass through solid barriers or vanish without explanation.”

This should cite or briefly describe a specific violation-of-expectation paradigm. Even one sentence would make it more concrete:
- e.g. infants look longer when a screen appears to pass through a solid object hidden behind it.

Likewise:

> “By age three or four, children understand that people act on the basis of what they believe, even when those beliefs are false.”

This needs a concrete false-belief example (Sally–Anne task) rather than a paraphrase alone. Readers benefit from seeing the canonical task.

### The animated-shapes example needs a citation or brief identification
You write:

> “In a famous study, people watched a short animation of geometric shapes...”

This is good, but it should be named more concretely, e.g. Heider and Simmel. As written, it sounds familiar but underdocumented.

### “Scripts” would benefit from a classic empirical demonstration
The section is clear, but abstractly descriptive:

> “Violations of scripts are noticed and remembered...”

Add one concrete memory study or even a simple textbook-style experiment:
- participants recall script-inconsistent actions especially when they disrupt the sequence,
- or they falsely remember script-consistent actions that never occurred.

That would deepen the reader’s understanding of how scripts affect memory.

### “Not everyone has the same schema content...” needs a stronger example
You write:

> “If your schema for ‘university professor’ includes traits like absent-minded and socially awkward...”

This is readable, but a more research-based example might be stronger. For instance, schema effects in eyewitness memory, classroom interpretation, or doctor–patient interaction would make the point less anecdotal.

### “Artifacts and natural kinds” is the section most in need of evidence
You claim:

> “Even young children distinguish between these two categories and reason about them differently...”

This is central to the section but remains unsupported. Add one concrete finding:
- children treat artifact categories as function-based,
- natural kinds as essence-based,
- or a transformation task showing differing judgments.

### “Inner essence” arrives abruptly
You write:

> “For natural kinds, identity is tied to an inner essence.”

This introduces essentialism without explanation. Either define it briefly or avoid the term. As written, it sounds like a technical concept entering without setup.

## 4. Accuracy and coverage of the outline

### Covered well
The draft does cover the major outline elements:
- framework theories
- three stances
- schemas
- scripts
- artifacts vs. natural kinds
- Bayesian lens

### Missing or underdeveloped: “schemas and scripts operate within stances”
The outline emphasizes:

> “Schemas and scripts operate within stances. The stance you adopt determines which schemas are activated...”

The draft implies this, but does not fully demonstrate it. The schema section reads as if schemas are generic structures independent of stance. You should add a clearer example showing the same event interpreted differently under different stances.

For example:
- A dog scratching at a door:
  - intentional stance: it wants to go outside
  - mechanical stance: claws contacting wood
  - biological framework: a response to discomfort or conditioning
This would show layered activation more directly.

### Missing: “The adopted stance influences the causal narrative one constructs, which in turn shapes moral judgment”
You mention this, but only briefly:

> “This connection between stances and moral reasoning is explored further in the chapter on blame, praise, and responsibility.”

That is accurate, but underdeveloped relative to the outline. A short example would help:
- mechanical framing of an accident reduces blame,
- intentional framing increases blame.

### Underrepresented: framework confusion causing “errors and superstitions”
You mention superstition and misconceptions, so the point is present. But there is no concrete example of superstitious reasoning besides anthropomorphism. One sharper case would help:
- illness as punishment,
- “nature is trying to restore balance,”
- “the universe sent me a sign.”

### Slight overstatement about living things and the intentional stance
You write:

> “Living things, by contrast, trigger the intentional stance.”

This is too broad. The outline says:
- artifacts → design stance
- living things → intentional stance  
but in practice this is more natural for animals and people than for plants. Since you list plants as natural kinds earlier, this sentence may mislead. Consider:
- “Animals and people strongly invite the intentional stance”
- “living things, especially animals and humans...”

### Potential misrepresentation: “A broken chair is still a chair because it was designed to be sat on”
This is a useful illustration, but it simplifies a contested issue. Function and intended design matter, yes, but readers may infer that current function never matters for artifact concepts. You might soften:
- “often still judged to be a chair because of its intended design”

### Missing direct mention of culture-specific scripts causing standstill
You cover culture-specific scripts and mention disruption, so this is mostly present. Still, the outline’s phrase “especially when they bring the script to a standstill” could be mirrored more directly. You hint at it, but a stronger example would help.

## 5. Style

Overall, the prose is clear and readable. A few style issues stand out.

### Some sentences are unnecessarily padded
For example:

> “This chapter is about the mental models that make such everyday understanding possible.”

Fine.

But then:

> “Before you ever encounter a specific situation, you carry with you layers of background knowledge that determine how you interpret new information, what you find surprising, and what explanations you find satisfying.”

This is clear, but several nearby sentences make similar points. The introduction could be tightened by about 15–20%.

### Avoid soft intensifiers and filler
Examples:
> “without any effort”
> “remarkably early”
> “remarkably powerful”
> “deep organizing principle”
> “fundamentally change”
> “at its core”

These are not wrong, but they accumulate. The chapter would feel more textbook-like if it relied a bit less on rhetorical emphasis.

### Some terminology could be more consistent
You alternate between:
- “mechanical stance”
- “physical stance”

and
- “design stance”
- “functional stance”

Since the outline uses “mechanical (physical)” and “design (functional),” introduce both labels once, then choose one consistently thereafter. I’d suggest “mechanical stance” and “design stance” as primary terms.

### A few phrases verge on conversational overreach
For example:

> “The intentional stance is so deeply ingrained that it activates even when the ‘agents’ are nothing more than moving shapes.”

Readable, but slightly dramatic. More neutral:
- “People readily apply the intentional stance even to simple moving shapes.”

### “The best stance” sounds too normative
You write:

> “The best stance is the one that makes the most sense of the situation.”

This is understandable, but “best” is vague. Better:
- “The most useful stance is the one that yields the most accurate and efficient explanation in context.”

## 6. Cross-links

This is the weakest technical aspect because several links point to sections that are not in this chapter and are not formatted in a stable textbook way.

### Problem: internal anchor links are used for other chapters
Examples:

> “[how we assign blame and praise](#blame-praise-and-responsibility)”

> “[how we assess risk](#risk-perception)”

> “[conspiracy theories](#conspiracy-theories)”

> “[stereotypes operate](#representativeness)”

> “[confirmation bias](#confirmation)”

> “[how priors and evidence combine in judgment](#bayesian-reasoning)”

These anchors will not work unless those headings exist on the same page. Since they refer to other chapters, they should use the book’s cross-reference format, such as:
- “see Chapter X, ‘Blame, Praise, and Responsibility’”
- “as discussed in Chapter Y on Risk Perception”
- or whatever cross-reference style the book uses.

### One cross-link is conceptually good but should be made more specific
This one works conceptually:

> “This is also relevant to understanding [conspiracy theories](#conspiracy-theories), where intentional explanations are applied to events that may have purely mechanical or coincidental causes.”

Good cross-link idea, but use formal chapter reference formatting.

### Some cross-links are a bit stretched
For example:

> “This is one of the mechanisms through which [stereotypes operate](#representativeness)...”

Representativeness is related, but this chapter is talking specifically about schema-guided social expectations. A cross-link to a chapter on categorization or stereotyping would be better; if that does not exist, keep the link but make the relation explicit:
- “This resembles the prototype-based reasoning discussed in the chapter on Representativeness.”

### Missing obvious cross-link
The Bayesian section should probably also cross-link to the earlier Bayesian chapter in a more explicit way:
- not just “priors and evidence combine in judgment,”
- but “see Chapter __, ‘Bayesian Reasoning,’ for the formal framework.”

## Specific revision suggestions by passage

### 1. Clarify framework theories vs. stances
Current:

> “Within these framework theories, people adopt different explanatory stances depending on the situation.”

Suggested change:
“Framework theories provide broad domain knowledge about what kinds of entities and causes exist; stances are the explanatory modes people adopt when interpreting a particular entity or event.”

Why: Clarifies relation instead of implying simple nesting.

### 2. Add concrete false-belief task
Current:

> “By age three or four, children understand that people act on the basis of what they believe, even when those beliefs are false.”

Suggested addition:
“Classic false-belief tasks such as the Sally–Anne task show this: children predict that Sally will search where she last saw the object, not where it really is.”

Why: Makes the claim concrete.

### 3. Strengthen schema section with study detail
Current:

> “A classic demonstration of how schemas work comes from @bransford_johnson1972.”

Suggested addition:
Briefly explain the “washing clothes” title manipulation and the key result numerically if possible.

Why: This is your strongest empirical anchor; make it work harder.

### 4. Remove unsupported mention of scripts in artifact section intro
Current:

> “One of the most fundamental ways that stances, schemas, and scripts interact can be seen...”

Suggested change:
“One important way to see stance activation in action is to compare reasoning about artifacts and natural kinds.”

Why: Scripts are not actually central in the section.

### 5. Soften “zero probability”
Current:

> “...effectively assigning zero probability to explanations from the wrong domain.”

Suggested change:
“...strongly down-weighting explanations from the wrong domain.”

Why: More psychologically realistic.

### 6. Replace broken internal links
Current:

> “[conspiracy theories](#conspiracy-theories)”

Suggested change:
“(see Chapter __, ‘Conspiracy Theories’)”

Why: Current format likely breaks.

## Bottom line

This is a solid draft: clear, coherent, and well aligned with the chapter’s core ideas. The main improvements needed are:

1. **Clarify conceptual relations** between framework theories, stances, schemas, and scripts.  
2. **Reduce repetition** around cross-domain misapplication and “stances shape explanation.”  
3. **Add more concrete studies**—especially for infant physics, false belief, artifact/natural-kind reasoning, and script memory.  
4. **Fix cross-references** so they point to chapters rather than nonexistent local anchors.  
5. **Tighten a few overgeneralized or overstated claims**, especially about “zero probability,” “living things trigger the intentional stance,” and artifact identity.

With those revisions, the chapter would be much stronger as a textbook chapter rather than a well-written overview.
