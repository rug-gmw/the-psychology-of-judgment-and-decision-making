## Overall assessment

This is a strong draft: clear, readable, and faithful to the chapter’s central theme that the future self is psychologically distant. The chapter has a good teaching structure, and the prose is mostly plain. The main revisions I’d suggest are:

- tighten the chapter around the two-part structure announced at the start: **affective forecasting** and **temporal discounting**
- trim a few repeated formulations of the core idea
- add one or two places where the text needs more concrete study detail or a sharper example
- adjust a few claims that currently overstate the evidence
- simplify or soften the Bayesian section, which currently feels a bit forced in places

Below I organize comments under your requested headings.

---

## 1. Structure

### 1.1 Strong opening, but the middle becomes slightly list-like
The chapter opens with a clear roadmap:

> “This chapter is about two related problems people have with their future selves. The first is a prediction problem… The second is a motivation problem…”

That works well. But after that, the affective forecasting section becomes a sequence of subtopics—impact bias, focalism, immune neglect, emotional evanescence, projection bias, hot-cold empathy gap, disability paradox, peak-end rule, changing tastes—without enough explicit signposting of how each one fits.

### Suggested fix
Add short orienting transitions at the start of each subsection. For example:

- after “Affective Forecasting,” add a sentence like:  
  **“The rest of this first half explains why these forecasts go wrong: people overestimate impact, focus too narrowly on the target event, project their current state into the future, and misremember what experiences are like.”**

- before “The Peak-End Rule,” clarify that this is not just another forecasting bias but a complication involving remembered experience:  
  **“A final source of error is that people do not evaluate experiences by their total duration, so the experience they later remember is not the one they originally tried to forecast.”**

Right now the transition into peak-end is a little abrupt.

---

### 1.2 “Predicting Changing Tastes” is interesting but feels slightly tacked on
> “## Predicting Changing Tastes”

This section is relevant, but structurally it comes late, after the chapter has already developed a coherent arc and after temporal discounting has been introduced. It feels like an extra example rather than a necessary part of the argument.

### Options
- **Option A:** move it earlier, directly after “Projecting the Present onto the Future,” since it fits naturally with projection bias.
- **Option B:** keep it where it is but shorten it and explicitly frame it as a brief extension:
  **“The same difficulty applies not only to future emotions but also to future preferences.”**

I would lean toward Option A.

---

### 1.3 Temporal discounting section could use one more bridge from affective forecasting
You write:

> “While affective forecasting is about mispredicting future feelings, temporal discounting is a different kind of problem.”

That distinction is good and important. But the chapter outline emphasizes that these are **distinct but related**, and that relation should be made more explicit right at the transition.

### Suggested revision
Add a linking sentence such as:

**“These are different failures—one cognitive, one motivational—but both arise because the future self is hard to represent vividly.”**

You say this later, but bringing it forward would improve flow.

---

### 1.4 The Bayesian section is placed well, but it interrupts the narrative tone
> “## A Bayesian Lens”

It makes sense near the end, especially given the book’s framing. But compared with the concrete earlier sections, this one becomes noticeably more abstract. It reads more like a thematic overlay than an explanatory section.

### Suggested fix
Shorten it by about one-third and keep only the clearest mappings. In particular, the temporal discounting paragraph is structurally weaker than the others because it stretches the Bayesian metaphor.

## 2. Duplication

### 2.1 Repetition of the “future self as a stranger” idea
This is the core thesis, so some repetition is useful. But several passages restate it in very similar language.

Examples:

> “Your future self is psychologically distant from you, almost like a stranger.”

> “It is a matter of not caring enough about the person who will receive the later outcome: your future self.”

> “When the future self feels like a stranger, it is easy to prioritize the present.”

> “The idea that the future self feels like a stranger is not just a metaphor.”

> “Both affective forecasting errors and temporal discounting share a common root: the psychological distance between who you are now and who you will become.”

### Suggested fix
Keep the metaphor strongly in:
- the introduction
- the Hershfield section
- the summary

Then trim some intermediate restatements. For example, in “Temporal Discounting,” you could cut:

> “It is a matter of not caring enough about the person who will receive the later outcome: your future self.”

because the next paragraph already makes the same point more concretely.

---

### 2.2 Repetition of “people are better at valence than intensity/duration”
This is stated in the learning goals, introduction, affective forecasting section, and summary. That’s fine pedagogically, but one statement in the body would be enough.

### Suggested fix
Keep the full distinction in the learning goals and first body definition; in the summary, shorten.

---

### 2.3 Repetition around projection bias and current self as model
You write:

> “When you try to predict how you will feel next month, you use your present self as a model for your future self.”

and later:

> “The healthy person projects their current state onto their future sick self…”

and later again:

> “People relied heavily on their current feelings as an anchor for predicting the future…”

These are related but overlapping formulations. The later sections would be tighter if they referred back rather than re-explaining the same mechanism each time.

### Suggested revision
In the disability and taste sections, use shorter callback phrases like:
**“This is projection bias in a high-stakes domain.”**  
or  
**“Again, current experience serves as a misleading model for future experience.”**

## 3. Concreteness

### 3.1 “Psychological immune system” would benefit from one concrete illustration
> “People have what Wilson and Gilbert call a psychological immune system: a set of coping mechanisms including rationalization, self-affirmation, and dissonance reduction…”

This is clear, but still somewhat abstract.

### Suggested fix
Add a quick concrete example:
**“A student who fails an exam may initially feel devastated, but within days may start telling themselves that the exam was unusually unfair, that one grade does not define their ability, or that the setback will motivate better study habits.”**

That would make the mechanism vivid.

---

### 3.2 The hot-cold empathy gap could use one classic study or stronger empirical anchor
> “When you are not sexually aroused, you underestimate how arousal would affect your choices.”

This is memorable, but the section mostly relies on examples rather than evidence. Since the chapter outline explicitly cites Loewenstein and highlights practical implications, a brief mention of a specific experiment would strengthen it.

### Suggested fix
Add a sentence describing a representative finding, for example that men in an aroused state reported greater willingness to engage in risky sexual behaviors than they had predicted in a non-aroused state. Even a brief study sketch would make the section feel less anecdotal.

---

### 3.3 “Temporal discounting” could use a more specific experimental pattern
> “People consistently prefer smaller rewards now over larger rewards later.”

This is accurate, but generic. A textbook reader would benefit from one concrete pattern showing discounting’s shape.

### Suggested fix
Add a standard contrast such as:
**“Many people prefer \$50 today to \$60 in a month, yet prefer \$60 in 13 months to \$50 in 12 months. The one-month wait is the same in both cases, but immediate rewards exert a disproportionate pull.”**

That would also nicely connect to present bias if you want to hint at it without expanding too far.

---

### 3.4 “There is also evidence that temporal discounting decreases with age” needs either support or softening
> “There is also evidence that temporal discounting decreases with age.”

As written, this is somewhat bare. Since the next sentence interprets it theoretically, readers need either a citation/study or a softer phrasing.

### Suggested fix
Either add a citation or rewrite as:
**“Some studies suggest that temporal discounting becomes less steep with age…”**

---

### 3.5 The Bayesian section needs a concrete example
> “Projection bias can be understood as using the wrong prior.”

This is a nice idea, but the abstraction may lose students.

### Suggested fix
Immediately follow with a miniature example:
**“If you shop while hungry, your current hunger serves as the prior. You then predict wanting large meals all week, even though your future state will usually be less hungry.”**

That would make the mapping more intuitive.

### 4.2 One outline point is underdeveloped: emotional evanescence
The outline names:

> “Emotional evanescence (duration): Emotions fade more quickly than anticipated.”

In the draft, this appears briefly:

> “The speed at which emotions fade is sometimes called emotional evanescence.”

This is accurate, but it is given much less weight than the outline implies. It currently reads like a passing label.

### Suggested fix
Either:
- fold it explicitly into impact bias as the duration component, or
- give it a separate short paragraph with one concrete example:
  **“Students expect rejection, embarrassment, or disappointment to dominate their mood for weeks, but daily routines and new events quickly compete for attention.”**

---

### 4.3 The chapter outline’s link to theory of mind is present but could be slightly clearer
The outline says:

> “This connects to theory of mind and the curse of knowledge…”

Your draft says:

> “This connects to the broader difficulty of perspective-taking…”

That’s fine, but “theory of mind” itself never appears. Since the outline foregrounds that connection, I would mention the term once.

### Suggested fix
Revise to:
**“This connects to perspective-taking and theory of mind more broadly…”**

---

### 4.4 Possible overstatement in the Eastwick description
> “The results were clear: participants significantly overestimated how upset they would be immediately after the breakup. Interestingly, they were reasonably accurate about the trajectory of recovery.”

This may be broadly fair, but “reasonably accurate” risks sounding stronger than the evidence warrants unless the study is being summarized very carefully.

### Suggested fix
Soften slightly:
**“They were more accurate about the direction of recovery than about the initial intensity of distress.”**

That is safer.

---

### 4.5 Possible overstatement in the peak-end rule
> “when people evaluate a past experience, they base their judgment on an average of the peak intensity … and the end, largely ignoring the total duration.”

This is the classic shorthand, but “average” sounds too precise and “largely ignoring” can sound stronger than the evidence supports across all contexts.

### Suggested fix
Use more standard cautious language:
**“people’s retrospective evaluations are strongly influenced by the peak and the end, with duration often underweighted.”**

That preserves the point without overstating.

---

### 4.6 The Bayesian framing of temporal discounting is not quite right
> “Temporal discounting can be reframed as implicitly assigning low prior probability to the future self's preferences mattering.”

This is clever, but conceptually awkward. Temporal discounting is usually about **subjective value over time**, not about the probability that future preferences matter. Bayesian language fits forecasting errors better than intertemporal valuation.

### Suggested fix
Either drop this sentence or recast it in less strictly Bayesian terms:
**“From a Bayesian perspective, vivid representations of the future self may change the inputs to valuation by making future outcomes feel more concrete and personally relevant.”**

That is looser but more defensible.

---

### 4.7 “This is not the same as a cognitive error” is too absolute
> “This is not the same as a cognitive error. It is a motivational pattern.”

The distinction is useful, but too sharp. Temporal discounting can involve both valuation and cognition, and some models treat it as a systematic decision bias rather than purely motivational.

### Suggested fix
Replace with:
**“This is less about mispredicting what will happen than about how strongly future outcomes are weighted in present choice.”**

That preserves the contrast without oversimplifying.
