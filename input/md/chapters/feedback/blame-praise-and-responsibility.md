## Overall assessment

This is a strong draft: it is clear, readable, and tracks the outline closely. The opening example works well, and the chapter successfully ties attribution to intentional stance and moral judgment. The main improvements needed are:

- tighten a few repeated ideas,
- sharpen the distinction between **blame/praise** and **person/situation attribution**,
- add one or two concrete classic studies,
- trim some unsupported expansions beyond the outline,
- make cross-links more consistent.

Below I organize feedback by the six requested criteria.

---

## 1. Structure

### 1.1 Strong opening, but the chapter could signal its roadmap earlier
> “This chapter is about what happens when the potential cause of an event is not a gust of wind or a mechanical failure but a human being.”

Good transition from the previous chapter. However, the next paragraph moves immediately into stances, then the next section introduces personal/impersonal causality, then covariation. The logic would be even clearer if you explicitly previewed the chapter’s three steps:  
1) intention and responsibility,  
2) covariation-based attribution,  
3) the tendency to over-attribute to persons.

Suggested revision after the opening example:
- “This chapter proceeds in three steps. First, it distinguishes personal from impersonal causality. Second, it explains how people use covariation information to infer whether behavior reflects the person or the situation. Third, it examines the correspondence bias: our tendency to over-attribute behavior to the person.”

That would help orient readers.

### 1.2 Transition into the covariation section is slightly abrupt
> “How do we figure out whether a behavior is caused by the person, the situation, or some combination of the two?”

This is a good question, but the prior section has emphasized **intention** and **blame**, whereas covariation is more about **attributional inference** generally. Add one bridging sentence explaining that before assigning responsibility, people first infer what kind of cause is involved.

Suggested change:
- “Before assigning blame or praise, people first need to decide what kind of cause they are dealing with: something about the person, something about the stimulus, or something about the surrounding circumstances.”

### 1.3 The Cheng & Novick discussion interrupts the textbook flow a bit
> “@cheng_novick1990 refined Kelley's model by showing that people do not simply match patterns to templates but instead compute something more like probabilistic contrasts.”

This is interesting, but the paragraph is more detailed and methodological than the surrounding exposition. It slightly disrupts the chapter’s pacing, especially because Cheng & Novick is not one of the core concepts named in the chapter title or learning goals.

Consider either:
- shortening this to 2–3 sentences, or
- moving it into a brief boxed aside (“Research note”) if your format allows.

For example:
- “Later work by Cheng and Novick showed that people’s attributions can often be modeled as probabilistic contrasts rather than simple rule matching. Importantly, when people are given complete covariation information, many apparent attribution errors shrink.”

That preserves the point without slowing the chapter.

### 1.4 The chapter ends clearly, but the summary could more directly mirror the learning goals
The summary is accurate, but it would be stronger if each sentence mapped more explicitly onto the listed learning goals. In particular, the learning goals mention:
- patterns of consensus/distinctiveness/consistency,
- correspondence bias,
- intentional stance and moral judgment.

The summary does cover these, but only briefly. Consider one explicit sentence linking intention to moral responsibility:
- “Because moral judgment depends heavily on perceived intention, attribution is often the first step in deciding whether someone deserves blame or praise.”

## 2. Duplication

### 2.1 The same “same outcome, different intention” point appears multiple times
You make the same conceptual point repeatedly:

> “The outcome for you is the same either way, yet the two explanations feel very different...”  
> “The outcome is the same, a patient receives the wrong drug, but our reaction is fundamentally different.”  
> “The outcome has not changed, but the cause has shifted from personal to impersonal...”

This is not wrong, but it is repetitive. Keep one strong statement, probably in the doctor example, and trim the rest.

### 2.2 Repetition of intentional vs mechanical stance
This linkage appears in three places:

> “When we reason about people, we adopt the intentional stance...”  
> “This distinction maps directly onto the stances from [Mental Models](#mental-models).”  
> “The correspondence bias is, in a sense, an over-application of the intentional stance.”

The idea is central, so it should recur, but currently it is restated almost fully each time. Consider:
- introducing it fully once in “From Causes to People,”
- briefly invoking it later without re-explaining it.

For example, in “Personal and Impersonal Causality,” you could shorten:
- “This distinction maps onto the intentional-versus-mechanical stance discussed in [Mental Models](#mental-models).”

### 2.3 Repetition of “over-attribute person, under-attribute situation”
This appears several times in closely similar wording:

> “they over-attribute behavior to the person and under-attribute it to the situation”  
> “We systematically over-blame people and under-blame situations”  
> “People start with a strong default assumption that behavior reflects character...”

Again, this is the chapter’s core point, but one or two instances are enough. The Bayesian section especially repeats rather than extends. You might compress the Bayesian section and make it add something more concrete, such as a simple attribution example framed as prior/evidence/posterior.

## 3. Concreteness

### 3.1 Add the classic Jones & Harris essay study by name and with a little more specificity
> “One of the classic demonstrations of this bias comes from a study on essay writing.”

This needs to be more concrete. Name the study and briefly specify the manipulation. Even if you do not include a full citation in prose, readers benefit from recognizable landmarks.

Suggested revision:
- “In a classic study by Jones and Harris, participants read pro- or anti-Castro essays and were told that some writers had been assigned their position. Even when the essay stance was assigned, readers still inferred that the writer privately held the expressed view.”

That is much more memorable than “a study on essay writing.”

### 3.2 The Cheng & Novick section could use less procedural detail and more intuitive example
> “In their experiment, 91 participants received descriptions of events along with covariation information across persons, stimuli, and occasions.”

This is concrete in a technical sense, but not pedagogically concrete. The sample size is less useful here than a worked example. Instead of “91 participants…”, illustrate probabilistic contrast with a simple scenario:

- “Suppose only Anna dislikes this restaurant, but Anna also dislikes most restaurants. That pattern points to Anna. Suppose many people dislike this restaurant, while Anna usually likes restaurants. That pattern points to the restaurant.”

That would better support understanding.

### 3.3 The paragraph on the four mechanisms could use a specific study example
> “Research suggests that when we observe someone's behavior, we first make a quick, automatic inference about their character...”

This would benefit from one concrete demonstration. For instance, the Gilbert, Pelham, and Krull cognitive-load work is exactly what you allude to. A simple one-sentence example would help:
- “In studies where participants formed impressions while simultaneously memorizing a string of digits, they were less able to correct for situational constraints and showed stronger dispositional inferences.”

That makes the System 1/System 2 interpretation feel earned.

### 3.4 “Cultural dimensions” is too abstract and somewhat unsupported here
> “Research suggests that it is more pronounced in Western, individualistic cultures...”

This is plausible, but currently generic. Either add a concrete comparative example/study or cut this paragraph. As written, it feels bolted on and beyond the chapter outline, which does not mention culture.

If retained, make it specific and cautious:
- “Some cross-cultural studies have found stronger dispositional attributions in North American samples and relatively greater attention to situational context in East Asian samples, though both tendencies appear in both groups.”

### 3.5 Bayesian section would benefit from one toy example
> “When you observe someone doing something, you start with a prior belief...”

This is clear but abstract. A one-line example would help:
- “If a usually punctual student misses one seminar, a strong prior that they are reliable should make you favor a situational explanation; repeated absences would then shift the posterior toward a person-based explanation.”

That would concretize the Bayesian framing.

## 4. Accuracy and coverage of the outline

### 4.1 The chapter broadly covers the outline well
It does include:
- transition from general causality to people,
- intentional stance link,
- personal vs impersonal causality,
- covariation principle,
- consensus/distinctiveness/consistency,
- correspondence bias/fundamental attribution error,
- Bayesian lens.

So coverage is solid.

### 4.2 But the treatment of blame/praise is slightly narrower than the outline suggests
The title is **“Blame, Praise, and Responsibility,”** but praise is mostly mentioned in passing:

> “who deserves blame or praise?”  
> “blame or praise are reserved almost exclusively for cases of personal causality”  
> “opens the door to blame or praise depending on the context.”

Most examples are about blame. Add at least one clear praise example parallel to the blame examples.

For instance:
- A student helps a struggling classmate because they genuinely want to help versus because they were required to do so.
- A surgeon saves a patient through skillful action versus through lucky timing.

This would better justify the title and the learning goal:
> “Connect causal attribution to the intentional stance and moral judgment.”

### 4.3 “We assign blame or praise only when we perceive personal causality” is too strong
This comes from the outline:
> “We assign blame or praise only when we perceive personal causality...”

And in the draft:
> “The distinction matters because blame and praise are reserved almost exclusively for cases of personal causality.”

Be careful here. People do often blame negligence, recklessness, or foreseeable carelessness even without harmful intent. Praiseworthiness can also involve effort and duty, not only intention in the narrow sense of intending the outcome.

Suggested softening:
- “Blame and praise depend heavily on perceived agency and intention, even though people may also assign responsibility for negligent or careless outcomes.”

This is especially important because the current formulation risks conflating **intentionality** with **responsibility** too completely.

### 4.4 The doctor example risks oversimplifying responsibility
> “But if the doctor prescribed the wrong medication because the packaging of two drugs looked nearly identical, the cause is impersonal.”

That is too absolute. In many real cases, responsibility is shared: the packaging may be a situational cause, but the doctor may still bear some professional responsibility depending on context. Since the chapter is about attribution, acknowledging mixed causality would improve accuracy.

Suggested revision:
- “...the error may be attributed more to situational factors, such as confusing packaging, though observers may still assign the doctor some responsibility.”

### 4.5 The chapter introduces material not in the outline without enough support
The biggest additions are:
- the four mechanisms from Gilbert & Malone,
- cultural differences.

These are not necessarily inappropriate, but they expand beyond the outline. The Gilbert & Malone mechanisms are useful because they deepen the bias explanation. The culture paragraph is less essential and may be expendable.

### 4.6 Missing explicit mention of responsibility as distinct from causality
The title includes **responsibility**, but the chapter mostly discusses causality and blame. A brief sentence distinguishing them would improve fidelity to the title:
- “Causing an outcome is not identical to being responsible for it: responsibility depends not only on causal contribution but also on intention, foresight, control, and alternatives.”

Even one sentence like this would add conceptual precision.

## 5. Style

### 5.1 Overall style is plain and readable
The prose is accessible and mostly free of jargon overload. This is a strength.

### 5.2 Some sentences are wordier than necessary
For example:

> “This chapter is about what happens when the potential cause of an event is not a gust of wind or a mechanical failure but a human being.”

This is fine, but could be tighter:
- “This chapter examines causal reasoning when the potential cause is a person rather than a physical event or mechanical failure.”

Another example:
> “The intentional stance is powerful because it allows us to predict and explain human behavior efficiently.”

Could be:
- “The intentional stance helps us predict behavior by interpreting actions in terms of beliefs, desires, and goals.”

### 5.3 Avoid conversational filler that slightly weakens precision
> “and so on”  
> “just a difficult person”  
> “This is worth reflecting on”

These are not major problems, but a textbook chapter usually benefits from slightly more disciplined phrasing.

For instance:
- replace “and so on” with one more specific cue or omit it,
- replace “just a difficult person” with “a dispositionally critical diner,”
- remove “this is worth reflecting on” unless the chapter consistently uses pedagogical asides.

### 5.4 Be careful with “remarkably consistent error” and similar evaluative phrasing
> “people make a remarkably consistent error in everyday life”

This is rhetorically strong but a bit dramatic. A more neutral textbook tone might be:
- “people show a robust tendency in everyday life”

### 5.5 Terminology is mostly consistent, but “stimulus attribution” may need a quick gloss
You use:
- person attribution
- stimulus attribution
- situation

These are related but not identical, and readers may blur them. In the Kelley framework, “stimulus attribution” refers specifically to something about the target/stimulus, whereas “situation” is broader. A clarifying sentence would help:
- “In Kelley’s framework, the alternative to a person attribution is often a stimulus attribution—something about the object or target of the behavior—though in everyday discussion this is often folded into the broader category of situational causes.”

That would improve precision.

## Most important revisions to make

If you want to improve the chapter efficiently, I would prioritize these five changes:

1. **Add one praise example**, not just blame examples.
2. **Soften the claim that blame/praise require intention**, to allow negligence and mixed responsibility.
3. **Make the essay-study example more concrete** by naming the classic paradigm.
4. **Trim repetition** around “same outcome, different intention” and “over-attribute person, under-attribute situation.”
5. **Either cut or support the culture paragraph** with a concrete example.

## Example of a key passage to revise

Current:
> “The distinction matters because blame and praise are reserved almost exclusively for cases of personal causality. We do not blame people for outcomes they did not intend, at least not in the same way.”

Suggested:
- “The distinction matters because blame and praise depend heavily on perceived agency and intention. Harmful outcomes produced on purpose invite stronger blame than the same outcomes produced accidentally, although people may still assign some responsibility for negligence or carelessness.”

This preserves the chapter’s main point while improving accuracy.

A solid, teachable draft that is already close to chapter-ready. Its biggest strengths are clarity, accessibility, and good integration with the book’s broader themes. The main work left is refinement: reduce repetition, sharpen conceptual precision around responsibility and intention, and add one or two more concrete study examples.
