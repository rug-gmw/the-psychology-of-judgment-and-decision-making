## Overall assessment

This is a strong draft: clear, accessible, and well aligned with the book’s larger framework. The central distinction between **descriptive**, **normative**, and **prescriptive** models comes through clearly, and the examples are generally well chosen. The main revisions I would recommend are:

- tighten some repeated formulations,
- sharpen a few claims that are currently too broad,
- add one or two concrete study-based examples,
- make the Bayesian “normative benchmark” language more precise,
- and smooth the transition between the general framework and the “resistance to normative models” section.

Below I organize feedback by the six requested categories.

---

## 1. Structure

### 1.1 Strong overall flow, but one transition feels abrupt
The chapter largely moves in a logical order:

1. introduce the three model types,
2. define each,
3. discuss resistance to normative models,
4. explain how the framework organizes the field,
5. close with a Bayesian lens.

That said, the move into **“Resistance to Normative Models”** is slightly abrupt. Up to that point, the chapter reads as a taxonomy; then it suddenly shifts into moral controversy.

> “## Resistance to Normative Models  
> If normative models define the best way to make decisions, you might expect people to welcome them. In practice, the opposite often happens.”

This is not wrong, but it needs a clearer bridge. Consider adding one sentence at the end of **Prescriptive Models** such as:  
**“But moving people toward a normative ideal is not only a technical problem. It is also a psychological and moral one, because people often resist being told that their intuitive judgments are suboptimal.”**

That would prepare the reader for the effective altruism/QALY discussion.

---

### 1.2 “How the Three Models Organize the Field” partly repeats earlier material
This section is conceptually useful, but parts of it restate things already established.

> “Most of the chapters in this book are primarily descriptive. They document the heuristics people use, the biases that result…”

and

> “The normative benchmark in this book is usually Bayesian reasoning, supplemented by expected utility theory…”

and

> “Prescriptive insights appear throughout the book as well…”

These are good points, but they mostly summarize what has already been said. To make the section feel less repetitive, consider making it more explicitly meta-structural: explain **how the rest of the book maps onto the framework**. For example, you could organize the section as:

- Foundations: introduces the normative benchmark and descriptive architecture,
- middle chapters: show recurring descriptive deviations,
- applied chapters: emphasize prescriptive tools.

That would make the section feel like a roadmap rather than a recap.

---

### 1.3 “A Bayesian Lens” might work better earlier or integrated into normative/prescriptive sections
Right now, the Bayesian lens comes near the end, after the chapter has already repeatedly identified Bayes as the normative standard.

> “## A Bayesian Lens  
> Bayesian updating provides the normative backbone for much of this book.”

This is true, but by this point it feels slightly belated. Two options:

- **Option A:** move this section earlier, perhaps immediately after **Normative Models**, since Bayes is the main normative standard for belief updating.
- **Option B:** keep it here, but shorten it and frame it explicitly as a book-wide preview:  
  **“Throughout this book, many apparent biases can be understood as departures from Bayesian updating.”**

As written, the section is good, but slightly redundant with earlier paragraphs on Bayes.

---

## 2. Duplication

### 2.1 Repetition of the same descriptive/normative/prescriptive formula
Several passages restate the same triad in nearly identical language.

For example:

> “Descriptive models describe how people actually judge and decide…”

then later:

> “Normative models specify how people should judge and decide…”

then later:

> “Prescriptive models bridge the gap between how people actually decide and how they ideally should.”

and in the summary:

> “Descriptive models capture how people actually think and decide. Normative models define how people should think and decide…”

This is pedagogically useful once, but repeated too often. You can trim by varying the function of later sections: once the definitions are established, later references should build on them rather than restate them.

---

### 2.2 Repeated claim that biases are deviations from a normative benchmark
This idea appears multiple times:

> “When researchers show that people make systematic errors in reasoning, they are comparing what people actually do … against some standard of what they should do…”

> “This is exactly why the distinction between normative and descriptive models matters: the gap between the two is where biases and errors live.”

> “Without a normative standard, the concept of bias has no meaning…”

> “Each of these biases represents a specific departure from Bayesian updating…”

This is a core point, but four versions are more than you need. I would keep the first strong statement early in the chapter and one later Bayesian restatement, but cut or compress the others.

---

### 2.3 Repetition of Bayes + natural frequencies example
The positive-test / natural-frequencies example is used effectively in **Prescriptive Models**, then revisited in **A Bayesian Lens**, and implied elsewhere.

> “A prescriptive solution … is to present the same statistical information using natural frequencies instead of probabilities.”

then later:

> “Presenting information as natural frequencies rather than conditional probabilities helps people approximate Bayesian reasoning…”

That second mention is fine, but brief is enough. The full explanation should appear only once.

---

## 3. Concreteness

### 3.1 Add at least one canonical study for each model type
The chapter is clear, but somewhat example-heavy in everyday terms and light on landmark empirical studies. Because this is a textbook, one or two canonical studies would strengthen it.

#### Descriptive models
You mention prospect theory and heuristics, but not the classic experimental evidence.

> “Similarly, prospect theory… describes how people evaluate outcomes relative to a reference point…”

Consider adding a brief reference to a standard finding, such as the **Asian disease problem** or a classic certainty-effect choice pattern. That would concretize “descriptive model” with evidence rather than only definition.

#### Prescriptive models
The natural-frequencies example would be much stronger if tied to a specific finding, e.g. Gigerenzer’s work showing improved Bayesian reasoning when probabilities are translated into frequency formats.

> “This format makes it much easier for people to see that a positive test result does not mean a 90% chance of disease.”

This is true, but would benefit from:  
**“In studies, physicians and patients often reason much more accurately when the same information is presented as counts out of 1,000 rather than as abstract conditional probabilities.”**

#### Resistance to normative models
The effective altruism/QALY section is conceptually good but would benefit from one more concrete contrast:
- local child in need vs. many statistically anonymous beneficiaries,
- or one identified patient vs. a larger but faceless health benefit.

That would connect to the identifiable victim effect more vividly.

---

### 3.2 Some abstract claims need examples
A few sentences are too general.

> “Normative models strip away the emotional and social dimensions of decision-making.”

This is too sweeping. Better:  
**“Normative models often abstract away from emotional and social motives unless those motives are explicitly included in the utility function.”**

As written, it sounds like a universal claim about all normative models; in fact, many normative models can incorporate social preferences if those are part of the person’s goals.

---

### 3.3 The Bayesian section would benefit from one explicit “departure” example
> “People neglect base rates, overweight vivid evidence, stick with their initial beliefs in the face of contradictory data…”

A quick concrete illustration would help: e.g., “a rare disease test,” “the Linda problem,” or “media coverage of shark attacks.” That would make the “Bayesian lens” less abstract.

---

## 4. Accuracy

### 4.1 Good coverage of the outline overall
The draft covers all the main outline points:

- descriptive models,
- normative models,
- prescriptive models,
- practical compromise,
- resistance to normative models via effective altruism and QALYs,
- Bayesian lens.

So in broad terms, coverage is faithful.

---

### 4.2 One claim about dual-process theory is too strong / slightly misleading
> “It does not say that one system is better than the other; it simply characterizes how the two systems operate.”

This is a bit too clean. Many dual-process discussions do imply that System 2 is more normatively reliable in certain contexts, even if the theory itself is primarily descriptive. Also, some versions of dual-process theory are partly explanatory rather than merely descriptive. I would soften this:

**Suggested revision:**  
“Dual-process models are primarily descriptive: they characterize the interplay between fast, intuitive processes and slower, more deliberate ones, even though some applications also evaluate which process is more reliable in a given context.”

---

### 4.3 The statement about normative models assuming unlimited resources is overstated
> “These models assume unlimited time, information, and cognitive capacity.”

That fits some classical normative models, but it is too broad as a definition of “normative.” Normative models specify what one **ought** to do relative to some criterion; they do not always require unlimited information or capacity. For example, bounded-optimal or resource-rational approaches are also normative in a broader sense.

Because this book foregrounds classical Bayesian and expected-utility norms, the sentence is understandable, but it should be narrowed:

**Suggested revision:**  
“Classical normative models often abstract away from human limits in time, information, and cognitive capacity.”

---

### 4.4 The expected utility section is a little too simplified
> “The idea is straightforward: when choosing between options, you should multiply the value (utility) of each possible outcome by its probability…”

Fine for an introductory chapter, but note that utility is not simply “value” in the everyday sense; it is subjective utility, not objective payoff. Consider a small clarification:
**“subjective utility”** or **“the decision-maker’s utility”**.

Otherwise students may later be confused when the book distinguishes objective outcomes from utility.

---

### 4.5 Bayes example is intuitive but not fully specified
> “If you initially think there is a 10% chance that your headache is caused by dehydration, and you then learn that you drank very little water today, Bayes' theorem tells you precisely how much to increase that estimate…”

Strictly speaking, only if you know how likely that evidence is under dehydration versus other causes. Since this chapter is about standards, precision matters.

Suggested revision:  
**“…Bayes’ theorem tells you how to revise that estimate, given how much more likely that evidence is if dehydration is the cause than if it is not.”**

---

### 4.6 “No one applies Bayes' theorem in their head” is too absolute
> “No one applies Bayes' theorem in their head when updating beliefs…”

Too categorical. Better:
**“People rarely update by explicitly computing Bayes’ theorem.”**

---

### 4.7 QALY explanation should be handled carefully
> “one year of life with a serious disability might equal, say, 0.5 QALYs.”

This is serviceable, but because QALYs are ethically contentious, the wording should avoid implying that disability straightforwardly equals a lower human worth. Better to stress that QALYs quantify **health states for policy comparison**, not the value of persons.

Suggested revision:  
**“In QALY-based analyses, a year lived in less-than-full health may be assigned a weight below 1 for the purpose of comparing health interventions.”**

This is more precise and less likely to alienate readers.

---

### 4.8 Effective altruism sentence slightly overstates equivalence
> “It applies a version of expected utility theory to charitable giving.”

Reasonable shorthand, but “essentially a version of expected utility theory” may be too strong if used elsewhere. Effective altruism is influenced by consequentialist and cost-effectiveness reasoning, but it is not reducible to a formal decision theory. I’d soften to:
**“It is strongly informed by consequentialist and expected-value reasoning.”**

---

### 4.9 Missing a clearer statement that prescriptive models are not always “nudges”
The chapter gives good prescriptive examples, but because two of the three examples are intervention-focused (hand sanitizer, natural frequencies, defaults), readers may infer that prescriptive models are always external redesigns. It may help to note that they also include **decision aids, checklists, training, and rules of thumb**.

You partly do this in the Bayesian lens section (“checklists,” “structured decision-making tools”), but a sentence earlier in the prescriptive section would help.

---

## 5. Style

### 5.1 Plain and readable overall
The prose is clear and textbook-appropriate. Most sentences are concise and direct.

---

### 5.2 A few phrases are slightly inflated or repetitive
#### Example:
> “These normative models are powerful because they are mathematically precise. They leave no room for ambiguity about what the ‘right’ answer is, given certain assumptions.”

This is mostly fine, but “powerful” + “leave no room for ambiguity” is slightly emphatic. A plainer version:
**“These models are useful because they specify, given a set of assumptions, what the correct answer should be.”**

#### Example:
> “This is exactly why the distinction between normative and descriptive models matters: the gap between the two is where biases and errors live.”

“where biases and errors live” is vivid but a bit colloquial relative to the surrounding style. Consider:
**“…because biases and errors are identified by comparing actual judgments with a normative benchmark.”**

---

### 5.3 Some filler transitions can be tightened
#### Example:
> “This distinction matters because it prevents a common confusion.”

Good sentence, but the next lines already explain the confusion. Could be streamlined.

#### Example:
> “The idea is straightforward…”

This phrase is common in explanatory prose, but often removable.

---

### 5.4 Minor wording issue
> “Effective altruim aims to be a rational way…”

This typo appears in the outline, not the chapter draft. The chapter draft uses “Effective altruism,” which is correct.

---

### 5.5 Watch consistency in “should”
At times “should” means:
- logical consistency,
- goal achievement,
- moral desirability,
- or public-health best practice.

That can blur normative standards. For example:

> “Normative models specify how people should judge and decide if they want to be logically consistent and achieve their goals as effectively as possible.”

Good. But later:

> “The normative model is clear: healthcare workers should wash their hands thoroughly…”

Here “normative” shifts from formal decision theory to policy/medical best practice. The example works, but it would help to flag that “normative” can refer more broadly to **the relevant standard of correctness in a domain**, not only formal rationality.

## Specific suggested revisions by passage

### Passage 1
> “Simon's work was both descriptive, capturing how people actually make decisions, and, as we will see, it also carried normative and prescriptive implications.”

Good point, but vague. Add one clause specifying those implications:
**“…because once rationality is viewed as bounded, good decision procedures must be adapted to human limitations rather than modeled on unattainable ideals.”**

---

### Passage 2
> “These models assume unlimited time, information, and cognitive capacity.”

Revise to:
**“Classical normative models often abstract away from limits in time, information, and cognitive capacity.”**

---

### Passage 3
> “No one actually computes expected utilities…”

Revise to:
**“People rarely compute expected utilities or Bayes’ theorem explicitly in everyday life.”**

---

### Passage 4
> “Normative models strip away the emotional and social dimensions of decision-making.”

Revise to:
**“Normative models often omit emotional and social motives unless those motives are explicitly built into the objective being optimized.”**

---

### Passage 5
> “One reason is that QALYs seem to place a numerical value on human life…”

Revise to reduce ethical friction and improve precision:
**“One reason is that QALYs translate health outcomes into a common numerical metric, which many people experience as uncomfortably impersonal.”**

---

### Passage 6
> “Without a normative standard, the concept of bias has no meaning…”

This is a strong and useful claim, but a bit absolute. You might soften slightly:
**“Without some normative standard, it is difficult to say why a recurring judgment pattern should count as a bias rather than merely a difference in behavior.”**

## What is missing or could be added

### 1. A direct mention of “descriptive, normative, prescriptive” as a recurring distinction across the whole book
You imply this, but a short framing sentence early on would help:
**“This three-part distinction will recur throughout the book: descriptive chapters document patterns of judgment, normative chapters provide standards of correctness, and prescriptive discussions ask how to improve real decisions.”**

### 2. A brief note that normative standards are conditional on goals and assumptions
This is important philosophically and pedagogically. Otherwise students may read “normative” as universally correct in every sense.

Suggested insertion in the normative section:
**“Importantly, normative recommendations are always relative to some goal and set of assumptions: the best choice for maximizing expected value may not be the best choice for preserving fairness, loyalty, or peace of mind.”**

This would also prepare the reader for the resistance section.

### 3. A named study or researcher besides Simon
The chapter currently cites only Simon explicitly. Since the book appears to use references in-text, one additional canonical citation would improve anchoring:
- Kahneman & Tversky for prospect theory,
- Gigerenzer for natural frequencies,
- or a classic framing study.

---

## Bottom line

The draft is strong and mostly faithful to the outline. The biggest improvements would come from:

1. **tightening repeated definitions**,  
2. **softening a few overgeneralized claims**,  
3. **adding one or two concrete study-based examples**, and  
4. **smoothing the transition into the effective altruism/QALY discussion**.

With those revisions, the chapter would work very well as a conceptual map for the rest of the book.
