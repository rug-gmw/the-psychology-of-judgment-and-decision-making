## Overall assessment

This is a strong draft: the core distinction between **self-favorable bias** and **overprecision** is clear, the chapter is readable, and the examples are generally well chosen. The biggest strengths are the opening vignette, the repeated return to the “two forms” framework, and the clean treatment of planning fallacy as a case where both distortions operate together.

The main revisions I would recommend are:

- tighten a few repetitive explanations of the two-form distinction,
- add one or two more concrete empirical details where claims are currently general,
- make sure the chapter fully reflects the outline’s emphasis on **overconfidence as shifted vs. narrowed posterior** throughout,
- be slightly more careful in a few places where the wording overstates debated findings,
- standardize cross-links and formatting.

---

## 1. Structure

### Strong opening and overall arc, but some sections repeat the same conceptual setup

> “Overconfidence is not a single bias. It is a family of biases that can be organized into two fundamentally different types…”

This is a good start. However, the same point is restated in very similar terms in:
- the introduction,
- “Two Faces of Overconfidence,”
- “Overprecision,”
- “A Bayesian Lens,”
- and the summary.

That repetition makes the chapter feel slightly circular rather than cumulative. Consider trimming the early setup so that later sections can do new work.

### Transition into mental health is a bit abrupt

> “If overconfidence is a bias, you might expect that less biased people would be better off. Curiously, the opposite appears to be true…”

This is a sensible transition, but the section itself feels somewhat detached from the previous material because it shifts from cognitive form to clinical significance without a clear bridge. Add one sentence tying this specifically to **self-favorable bias**, not overconfidence in general. For example: “This asymmetry matters because not all forms of overconfidence have the same psychological function; the self-favorable form may help sustain agency and motivation.”

### Dunning-Kruger comes slightly late relative to the conceptual distinction

The current order is:
1. two forms
2. self-favorable bias
3. overprecision
4. mental health
5. planning fallacy
6. Dunning-Kruger
7. expertise

This works, but **Dunning-Kruger** might fit better immediately after **overprecision** or after **self-favorable bias + overprecision**, since it is fundamentally about miscalibration and metacognition. Right now, the mental-health section interrupts the conceptual arc before returning to classic overconfidence effects.

A possible cleaner order:
1. Two faces
2. Self-favorable bias
3. Overprecision
4. Dunning-Kruger
5. Planning fallacy
6. Expertise and calibration
7. Overconfidence and mental health
8. Bayesian lens

That said, if you keep the current order, add a stronger transition after mental health back to judgment under uncertainty.

### The Bayesian lens is useful but partly redundant

> “Both forms of overconfidence can be understood as specific failures of Bayesian reasoning…”

This section is conceptually appropriate, but it largely repeats earlier explanations rather than sharpening them. Consider making it shorter and more schematic, perhaps explicitly mapping:
- self-favorable bias → **biased posterior mean/location**
- overprecision → **posterior variance too small**
- planning fallacy → **both wrong center and wrong spread**

That would make the Bayesian section add precision rather than summary.

## 2. Duplication

### Repeated explanation of the two forms

You explain the same distinction multiple times in almost the same words:

> “your estimate was too optimistic, and you were too sure about it.”

> “The estimate is too optimistic (self-favorable bias), and it fails to account for the full range of things that could go wrong (overprecision).”

> “The estimate itself is shifted in a favorable direction… And the range of uncertainty around the estimate is too narrow…”

> “your estimate is shifted toward optimism… and your confidence interval around that estimate is too narrow…”

One or two of these are enough. Keep the introduction and one later reprise in the planning-fallacy section; trim the others.

### Confirmation-bias framing is repeated too often

You return several times to:

> “Both forms of overconfidence can be understood as expressions of… [confirmation bias](#confirmation).”

> “Why does overprecision occur? One explanation is that it too reflects a form of confirmation bias.”

> “This is [confirmation bias](#confirmation) applied to the precision of one’s own beliefs.”

The chapter outline does want this link, but in the draft it starts to feel over-insisted. State the connection clearly once near the beginning, then only reintroduce it where it adds something new, such as in the Bayesian lens or in the self-favorable bias section.

### Repeated “clear and frequent feedback” point

The expertise section says this well, but the same exact conclusion appears again at the end of the section and in the summary:

> “Without clear and frequent feedback, more experience simply means more confidence, not better accuracy.”

> “Finally, expertise reduces overconfidence only when it is accompanied by clear and frequent feedback.”

This is minor, but one version can be shortened.

## 3. Concreteness

### Overprecision could use one classic study or task beyond confidence intervals

> “The standard way to measure overprecision is through confidence intervals.”

This is fine, but the section would benefit from a more specific study example, not just the broad Lichtenstein et al. result. Right now it reads a bit textbook-general. You could briefly mention a standard calibration paradigm: general-knowledge questions, confidence judgments, and the mismatch between stated confidence and actual accuracy.

### The mental-health section is clear but risks remaining too abstract in the first paragraph

> “Healthy participants showed an optimistic pattern…”

This is understandable, but a concrete mini-example would help. For instance: “If someone initially thought their chance of being robbed was 40% and then learned the average was 20%, healthy participants tended to revise downward more than they revised upward when the news was worse than expected.” That would make the updating asymmetry more intuitive.

### Planning fallacy is strong, but one concrete everyday example could complement the thesis study

You open with a group project, then move quickly to thesis completion and infrastructure. It may help to return explicitly to a familiar example before scaling up:

- student assignment,
- software release,
- home renovation,
- moving house.

That would create a better ladder from everyday judgment to institutional consequences.

### Expertise section would benefit from one more domain-specific contrast

> “Weather forecasters, for example, tend to be well-calibrated…”

Good. But:

> “Clinical psychologists, by contrast, tend to be poorly calibrated.”

This is plausible, but it would be stronger with a concrete prediction task or a link to the actuarial-clinical literature. For example: predictions of violence, treatment outcome, or recidivism. As written, the contrast is slightly too broad.

## 4. Accuracy and coverage relative to the outline

### Mostly faithful, but a few outline points are underemphasized or softened

#### Good coverage
The draft clearly covers:
- self-favorable bias,
- overestimation,
- overplacement,
- unrealistic optimism,
- overprecision,
- mental health,
- planning fallacy,
- Dunning-Kruger,
- expertise/feedback,
- Bayesian framing.

#### Missing or underdeveloped: explicit mention of “posterior shifted” vs. “posterior narrowed” earlier and more consistently
The outline makes this distinction central. The draft introduces it, but after the opening it often switches into ordinary prose (“too optimistic,” “too certain”) rather than keeping the formal distinction visible. I would preserve the plain wording but more consistently pair it with the book’s Bayesian terminology.

For example, in “Self-Favorable Bias,” you could add a sentence like:
- “In Bayesian terms, the center of the posterior is displaced in a self-flattering direction.”

And in “Overprecision”:
- “Here the problem is not the center of the estimate but its spread.”

#### Dunning-Kruger section slightly overstates the underestimation by high performers
> “while highly competent individuals slightly underestimate their ability”

This is in the outline, but the literature is more nuanced. High performers often appear less extreme partly because they assume tasks that are easy for them are easier for others as well. If you keep the sentence, hedge it a bit:
- “high performers often rate themselves less exceptionally than their performance warrants.”

#### Statistical-artifact caveat should be a bit more precise
> “Some researchers have argued that part of the pattern can be explained as a statistical artifact…”

Good to include, but the next sentence:

> “However, the core finding, that poor performers are poorly calibrated and that improving their skills improves their self-assessment, has been replicated across many studies and domains.”

This may be too confident for a contested area. Consider softening:
- “However, there is still substantial evidence that low performers are often poorly calibrated, even if some headline versions of the effect have been overstated.”

That would be more accurate and less categorical.

#### “Most pervasive and least understood” needs attribution or softening
> “The second form of overconfidence is overprecision, and it is both the most pervasive and the least understood.”

This comes from the outline, but in textbook prose it reads as an unsupported global claim. Suggest:
- “Many researchers regard overprecision as the most pervasive form of overconfidence, and one of the least well explained.”

#### Learned helplessness wording needs a touch more care
> “Initially, the dogs struggled and tried to find a way out, which you might call an unrealistically optimistic response…”

This is interpretively interesting, and it reflects the outline, but it risks anthropomorphic overreach. Better:
- “Initially, the dogs continued to act as if escape might still be possible—behavior that can be interpreted as persistence in the face of negative evidence.”

That is more careful.

#### One notable omission: calibration as a defined concept
Calibration appears in the learning goals and in several sections, but it is never explicitly defined. Add a brief definition early in “Overprecision” or “Expertise and Calibration”:
- “Calibration refers to the match between confidence and accuracy.”

That would help students.

## 5. Style

### Generally plain and readable, but some phrases recur too often

Phrases repeated frequently:
- “in a positive direction”
- “the full range of things that could go wrong”
- “evidence that is consistent with…”
- “disconfirming evidence”
- “appropriately wide”
- “clear and frequent feedback”

Varying sentence structure is fine, but here the issue is not lack of variation; rather, some formulations are used enough times to sound formulaic. Trim rather than synonymize.

### A few sentences are longer than needed

For example:

> “This suggests that overplacement is partly driven by a motivation to see oneself in a positive light. However, @logg_etal2018 also found that this motivational effect was strongest when traits were defined vaguely.”

This is clear, but throughout the chapter you often use a two-sentence pattern where one tighter sentence would do. The prose is already accessible; it can be made slightly crisper.

### Avoid slightly inflated phrasing

> “goes beyond cold cognition”

This is understandable, but “cold cognition” may feel vague or jargony here. “goes beyond a simple information-processing error” would be plainer.

> “the self-favorable bias, it seems, is not just a cognitive error. It is something that healthy minds do…”

This is readable but a little conversational and imprecise. Consider:
- “This suggests that self-favorable updating is not merely an error; it may also serve a motivational function.”

### Be consistent in hyphenation and capitalization

You use:
- “better-than-average effect”
- “outside-view”
- “self-favorable bias”

These are mostly consistent, but check whether the book style guide prefers “outside view” without hyphen except as a modifier. Also standardize “Bayesian lens”/“A Bayesian Lens” with other chapters’ conventions.

## 6. Cross-links

### Appropriate in substance, but formatting consistency should be checked

These are all sensible:
- [confirmation bias](#confirmation)
- [statistical vs. intuitive prediction](#statistical-vs-intuitive-prediction)
- [Bayesian reasoning](#bayesian-reasoning)

However, there are two issues.

### Possible anchor mismatch with chapter titles
You link to:

> “[confirmation bias](#confirmation)”

In the condensed outline, the chapter is titled **Confirmation**. If the actual heading in the book is “Confirmation,” then linking the phrase “confirmation bias” to `#confirmation` is fine. Just make sure the anchor format is consistent with your publishing system.

Likewise:

> “[statistical vs. intuitive prediction](#statistical-vs-intuitive-prediction)”

This appears correct if the target chapter heading is exactly that.

> “[Bayesian reasoning](#bayesian-reasoning)”

Also appropriate.

### Cross-link placement is slightly repetitive
You reference the statistical prediction chapter twice:

> “For further discussion of how statistical approaches can outperform intuitive prediction…”

and later

> “This theme is explored further in the chapter on [statistical vs. intuitive prediction](#statistical-vs-intuitive-prediction).”

One cross-link is enough. Keep the second one in the expertise section; remove the earlier one in planning fallacy unless you want that section specifically to foreshadow later material.

## Specific passage-level suggestions

### 1. Clarify independence of the two forms
> “These two types are conceptually independent. You can be overly optimistic without being overly certain, and you can be overly certain without being overly optimistic.”

Good point, but it would benefit from a quick example:
- overly optimistic but uncertain: “I think my startup will probably succeed, though I admit I may be wrong.”
- overly certain but not optimistic: “I’m sure sales will fall next quarter.”

Without examples, the claim is clear but abstract.

### 2. Define calibration
> “The standard way to measure overprecision is through confidence intervals.”

Add:
- “More generally, calibration refers to how closely confidence matches accuracy.”

This would help students and aligns with the outline.

### 3. Tighten the Rhine River example
> “A well-calibrated person, however, would recognize the uncertainty in their knowledge and provide a much wider range…”

This works, but the specific numbers risk making the “well-calibrated” range look arbitrary. Better to stress the principle rather than invent a contrast range, unless the example is sourced.

### 4. Soften the mental-health generalization
> “a degree of self-favorable bias seems to be a marker of mental health.”

This is the right direction, but a bit too global. Suggest:
- “a moderate self-favorable bias often accompanies psychological well-being.”

That avoids implying a simple diagnostic marker.

### 5. Tighten the Seligman paragraph
> “which you might call an unrealistically optimistic response given the situation.”

As noted above, this is too interpretive. Rephrase more cautiously.

### 6. Improve the bridge in planning fallacy
> “This is what Kahneman and Tversky called the inside view versus the outside view.”

Very good conceptually, but you should explicitly connect this to earlier chapters:
- “As discussed in the Bayesian Reasoning chapter, the outside view relies on base rates from similar cases.”

That would make the cross-chapter integration stronger than the current generic Bayesian link.

### 7. Dunning-Kruger percentile example needs caution
> “those in the bottom quartile estimated their ability… at the 62nd percentile”

This is memorable, but because this literature is debated, I would present the number as an example from the original study, not as the settled benchmark:
- “In the original humor study…”

### 8. Expertise section could mention selection effects more clearly
> “If a manager is highly selective, hiring only a small fraction of applicants, the positive hit rate… can be high even if the manager's judgment adds little value.”

This is good but dense. Consider spelling out the logic:
- if most rejected candidates would also have done well, the manager’s apparent success is misleading.

### 9. Bayesian lens repeats too much prose from earlier sections
> “Confirming evidence, such as past successes and positive traits, is given too much weight…”

This repeats almost verbatim from earlier. Condense and make it more formal:
- “In Bayesian terms, self-favorable bias misplaces the posterior mean; overprecision understates posterior variance.”

That would give the section a distinct role.

## Recommended priorities for revision

### High priority
1. **Trim repetition** of the “two forms” explanation and confirmation-bias framing.
2. **Define calibration** explicitly.
3. **Soften a few strong claims** about Dunning-Kruger, overprecision being “most pervasive,” and self-favorable bias as a “marker” of mental health.
4. **Improve transitions**, especially into and out of the mental-health section.
5. **Condense the Bayesian lens** so it adds precision rather than restating the chapter.

### Medium priority
6. Add one more **concrete study/task example** for overprecision.
7. Add a sentence linking planning fallacy more explicitly to **base rates/outside view** from Bayesian reasoning.
8. Reduce one of the two cross-links to **statistical vs. intuitive prediction**.

### Low priority
9. Standardize minor style choices and hyphenation.
10. Consider reordering Dunning-Kruger and mental health if you want a cleaner conceptual progression.

Overall, this is a solid and teachable chapter. The main work now is refinement: make it slightly leaner, slightly more precise, and slightly more explicit about the empirical and Bayesian structure already present in the outline.
