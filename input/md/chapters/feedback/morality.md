## 1. Structure

### Overall flow is strong, but two transitions could be improved

The chapter has a clear arc:

1. moral judgment as a kind of belief  
2. rationalist models  
3. intuitionist models  
4. content of intuitions via Moral Foundations Theory  
5. bodily/emotional roots  
6. affect/somatic mechanisms  
7. trolley cases  
8. omission bias  
9. Bayesian lens

That sequence mostly works well. The main structural issue is that the chapter moves from **theoretical models** to **evolutionary roots** a bit abruptly.

### Awkward transition: from Moral Foundations Theory to evolution

> “## The Evolutionary Roots of Moral Feeling  
> If moral judgments are driven by gut feelings, where do those feelings come from?”

This is a reasonable question, but the transition would be smoother if you explicitly signaled that you are moving from **what intuitions contain** to **where intuitions come from**. As written, it feels like a new mini-chapter starts.

**Suggested fix:** add a bridge sentence at the end of the previous section, e.g.:
- “Moral Foundations Theory describes the contents of moral intuitions, but it does not by itself explain where those intuitions originate. To answer that question, we turn to emotion, embodiment, and evolution.”

### The “evolution” section currently mixes two different jobs

> “One answer comes from evolutionary biology… Another part of the answer comes from the psychology of emotion.”

This section combines:
- evolutionary origins of morality
- Jamesian emotion / bodily feeling
- hunger and misattribution

These are related, but they are not the same level of explanation. Evolutionary arguments explain **why** moral emotions may exist; James, affect-as-information, and somatic markers explain **how** feelings influence judgments in the moment.

**Suggested fix:** either
- split into two sections:  
  **The Evolution of Morality** and **Emotion, the Body, and Moral Judgment**, or
- tighten the first section so it stays on evolution/custom/species continuity, then use a clear transition into the emotion section.

This would also better match the outline, which treats “The evolution of morality” and “Emotion as self-perception and evolutionary roots” as distinct points.

### Missing explicit section on morality vs convention as its own topic

The outline gives this topic notable weight:

> “The notion that there is a distinction between morality and cultural conventions may be relatively new…”

In the draft, this appears briefly in the opening and then partly reappears in the social interactionist section. That is fine, but it may deserve either:
- a short dedicated subsection, or
- a more explicit callback later.

Right now, the point feels introduced and then partially absorbed into later material.

## 2. Duplication

Several ideas are repeated more than necessary.

### Repetition of “moral judgment = quick intuition, reasons after the fact”

This core idea appears in similar language in multiple places:

> “did the judgment arrive instantly, as a gut feeling, with the reasons coming only afterward…”

> “moral judgments come first, arriving as fast, automatic gut feelings. Reasoning comes afterward…”

> “suggesting that the judgment came first and the reasoning second.”

> “with reasoning serving mainly to justify judgments after the fact.”

This repetition is conceptually central, but the phrasing could be varied less and condensed more. After the intuitionist section, later sections can assume this point rather than restating it.

### Repetition of “bodily feelings guide moral judgment”

This idea recurs across:
- “The Evolutionary Roots of Moral Feeling”
- “Affect as Information and Somatic Markers”
- “Trolley Problems and the Body”
- “A Bayesian Lens”
- “Summary”

For example:

> “When you encounter a moral violation, your body responds first…”

> “The emotional body plays a necessary role.”

> “The difference between the two dilemmas is somatic.”

> “Those feelings can be amplified or dampened…”

> “The somatic marker hypothesis suggests that these priors are literally embodied.”

This is not wrong, but the chapter starts to sound like it is proving the same point repeatedly. You could reduce duplication by making each section do a more distinct job:
- **James / hangry** = bodily states shape affective interpretation
- **Affect-as-information** = psychological mechanism
- **Somatic markers** = neurobiological implementation
- **Trolley** = application to moral dilemmas
- **Omission bias** = one downstream moral pattern

### Repetition between “Bayesian Lens” and “Summary”

The summary restates the Bayesian paragraph quite closely:

> “From a Bayesian perspective, moral intuitions function as strong, embodied priors…”

This is fine in a summary, but you can trim some repeated wording like “formed early,” “resistant to updating,” “embodied priors,” which already appeared almost verbatim in the Bayesian section.

## 3. Concreteness

Overall, the chapter is already fairly concrete. The strongest parts are the Heinz dilemma, Julie-and-Mark scenario, capuchin monkey example, Iowa Gambling Task, trolley/footbridge, and vaccination/end-of-life examples.

Still, a few places remain too abstract or could use sharper empirical grounding.

### The opening claim about moral beliefs resisting updating is too abstract

> “Moral judgments are beliefs about whether something is right or wrong. In many ways, they follow the same principles as other beliefs: they form quickly, they resist updating…”

This is plausible, but here it reads as an assertion. Since the chapter outline frames morality as belief formation, it would help to anchor this with a concrete phenomenon already in the chapter.

**Suggested fix:** briefly preview moral dumbfounding or belief perseverance:
- “Like other beliefs, moral judgments can be hard to revise; one sign is moral dumbfounding, where people maintain a firm judgment even after their stated reasons are undermined.”

### “The distinction between morality and cultural convention may be relatively recent” needs more support

> “@hallpike2004 argued that many cultures, and many people within our own culture, do not sharply separate…”

This is an interesting claim but remains abstract and potentially overstated without an example.

**Suggested fix:** add a concrete example of a norm treated as morally mandatory in one community but as mere etiquette in another. Even a brief example would help:
- forms of dress
- food taboos
- purity rules
- respect rituals

### Social interactionist model could use a citation/study anchor

> “Research has shown that children as young as five or six can distinguish between moral violations and social conventions.”

This is a classic and important claim. It would benefit from naming a specific paradigm or researcher, especially because this section is otherwise a bit thinner than the Haidt material.

**Suggested fix:** briefly describe the standard finding:
- children judge hitting as wrong even if a teacher permits it, but judge dress-code violations as dependent on rules

You already imply this, but explicitly framing it as a study result would make the section more textbook-like.

### The MFT section could use one sentence clarifying that the foundations are not the same as political opinions

> “The results showed a consistent pattern…”

As written, the section risks sounding like liberals have two morals and conservatives have five, a common misreading.

**Suggested fix:** clarify:
- both groups endorse all five to some degree; the difference is in relative weighting

### The phrase “Morality, understood as cooperation, exists at all levels of biological organization” from the outline is not really represented

The draft instead says:

> “The capacity for empathy, retribution, and fairness is not uniquely human.”

This is safer and better. I would **not** add the “all levels of biological organization” phrase from the outline literally; it is too sweeping. But if the outline intends a broader continuity claim, you could add one sentence connecting morality to cooperation:
- “At minimum, these findings suggest that some building blocks of human morality evolved out of older systems for cooperation and social regulation.”

### Somatic marker claim may need a cautionary note

> “This demonstrates that rational analysis alone is not enough. The emotional body plays a necessary role.”

This is a strong claim. Given debates around the Iowa Gambling Task and the somatic marker hypothesis, the wording should be slightly softened.

**Suggested fix:**  
- “This supports the view that emotional signals often play a necessary role in adaptive decision-making.”  
or  
- “This is consistent with the view that intact decision-making depends on emotional as well as analytic processes.”

### “The difference between the two dilemmas is somatic” is too categorical

> “The difference between the two dilemmas is somatic.”

That overstates the evidence. The literature points to differences in personal force, directness, contact, emotional salience, and cognitive control, not only bodily feeling.

**Suggested fix:**  
- “A major part of the difference appears to be somatic and emotional…”  
or  
- “One influential interpretation is that pushing triggers a stronger visceral response than flipping a switch.”

### Omission bias section would benefit from one empirical study reference

> “One consequence of the somatic-marker account of morality is omission bias…”

The examples are good, but a study reference would make this less essayistic and more textbook-grounded. A classic vaccination or action/inaction paradigm would help.

## 4. Accuracy

### Strong coverage overall, but a few outline points are missing, blurred, or slightly overstated

#### A. Missing explicit treatment of **moral judgment as belief formation** beyond the opening
The opening introduces this nicely, but the chapter does not really return to it except in the Bayesian section.

The outline emphasizes:
> “formed quickly, resistant to updating, shaped by System 1 and System 2”

The draft strongly covers System 1 vs System 2, but gives less attention to moral judgment as part of the broader psychology of belief. A short link to belief perseverance, Spinozan acceptance, or identity-protective updating would strengthen fidelity to the outline.

#### B. The evolution/convention point is underdeveloped
The outline explicitly says:
> “Many cultures … do not make a distinction between ‘this is what we generally do here’ and ‘this is what we should do because it is morally right’.”

The draft mentions this, but the significance is not fully unpacked. You might explain more clearly that the morality/convention distinction often treated as psychologically basic may itself be culturally variable.

#### C. The chapter outline says:
> “These two frameworks describe the same core insight … but from different perspectives.”

The draft says:
> “Two frameworks describe this relationship…”

But it does not quite make explicit enough that **affect-as-information** is the psychological principle and **somatic markers** the neurobiological implementation of a similar idea. That distinction should be made sharper.

#### D. Missing explicit mention of **moral dumbfounding as evidence against rationalist models**
The draft does present dumbfounding well, but it could more directly say why it matters theoretically:
- if people cannot state reasons yet maintain confidence, that challenges the idea that conscious reasoning generated the judgment.

#### E. Kohlberg section is accurate, but Stage 6 should be handled cautiously
> “In Stage 6, people reason from universal ethical principles…”

This is standard textbook language, but many treatments note that Stage 6 was more theoretically posited than robustly observed. If this book aims for strong empirical precision, add a clause:
- “Kohlberg treated Stage 6 as the highest form of moral reasoning, though evidence for a distinct final stage has been mixed.”

#### F. The social interactionist model label may be too vague
The outline uses “social interactionist model,” but many readers will expect Turiel’s domain theory. If that is what you mean, naming it would increase precision.

#### G. Potential overstatement on Greene et al.
> “Brain regions associated with emotional processing… were significantly more active…”

Fine in broad terms, but avoid implying a simple one-to-one mapping between “emotion areas” and moral intuition. Better to say “regions commonly associated with emotional processing.”

#### H. Missing **Kohlberg’s stages** in relation to answer vs reason classification
The outline explicitly notes:
> “Responses classified by reasons given, not the answer itself.”

The draft does state this:

> “Kohlberg was not interested in whether people said yes or no. He was interested in the reasons they gave.”

Good—this is accurate and should stay.

#### I. Omission bias is correctly included, but the tie to **omission bias in moral judgment more generally** could be expanded
The outline places omission bias as a key concept in morality. The draft covers it briefly. That is sufficient, but because it appears late and in a short section, it feels slightly tacked on.

## 5. Style

Overall, the style is clear, plain, and accessible. It reads well for an undergraduate audience. A few phrases should be tightened.

### Strong stylistic choices
- Good use of concrete scenarios
- Definitions are usually clear before elaboration
- Tone is explanatory without being too casual

### Passages to tighten or simplify

#### Slightly overdramatic / rhetorical
> “This question is at the heart of the psychology of morality.”

Fine, but generic textbook filler. Could simply be cut.

#### Slightly repetitive emphasis
> “Morality, it turns out, is not just one component of the self. For most people, it is the core of the self.”

This is readable, but slightly dramatic. Since the preceding sentence already makes the point, consider trimming to one sentence.

#### Possible overstatement
> “This finding helps explain why political disagreements about moral issues can feel so intractable.”

Good, but a bit broad. Consider:
- “This finding helps explain one reason political disagreements can feel intractable.”

#### Jargon without enough payoff
> “The wag-the-dog illusion”  
> “The wag-the-other-dog's-tail illusion”

These are in the outline and should remain, but the second term is cumbersome. The draft explains them well, but students may find them memorable only if visually separated or boxed. In prose, they are a bit clunky.

#### Slightly inflated wording
> “The affect-as-information heuristic and the somatic marker hypothesis converge in one of the most discussed thought experiments in moral psychology…”

Could be shorter:
- “These ideas can be illustrated with the trolley problem.”

#### Too categorical
> “Together, these findings paint a clear picture.”

Given replication debates around some disgust-priming findings, this is too confident.

**Suggested fix:**  
- “Together, these findings suggest that incidental bodily states can influence moral judgment.”

#### Consistency issue: “gut feelings,” “bodily feelings,” “somatic signals,” “emotional body”
These all point to the same idea, but there are many near-synonyms. Pick a more stable vocabulary:
- “intuitions” for the psychological level
- “affective/bodily signals” for the mechanistic level

That will reduce stylistic drift.
