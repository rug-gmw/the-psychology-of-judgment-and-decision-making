## 1. Structure

### Overall flow is strong, but one section feels slightly bolted on
The chapter mostly follows the outline well: definition → positive vs. negative testing → 2-4-6 → goal-based explanation → Bayesian lens. That is a clean progression.

The section that feels least integrated is:

> “## The Consistency Fallacy”

**Suggestion:** add a bridge sentence at the start, such as:
- “Confirmation bias is not limited to how we *search* for evidence; it also affects how we *evaluate* evidence once we encounter it.”

That would make the section feel like an extension of confirmation bias rather than a new mini-topic.

### “Confirmation Bias in Everyday Life” comes a bit late
You already use concrete examples throughout, so the separate section:

2. as a brief concluding paragraph before the Bayesian lens.

Right now it slightly interrupts the theoretical progression from “Consistency Fallacy” to “Bayesian Lens.”

**Suggestion:** either shorten this section substantially or retitle it as “Why It Matters” and make it explicitly synthetic.

### Minor transition issue
Between the 2-4-6 task and “Why Positive Testing Persists,” the transition is good in content but could be more explicit.

> “What makes the 2-4-6 task especially revealing ... This shows that the pull toward positive testing is a basic cognitive default...”

Then:

> “If disconfirmatory evidence is so informative, why do people so rarely seek it?”

This works, but a one-line bridge would help:
- “But cognitive default is only part of the story.”

This is not terrible, but it is somewhat redundant because the second section largely restates the first example in reverse.

**Suggestion:** keep it once, then refer back briefly:
- “Using the manager example, a negative test would involve looking for counterexamples...”

The chapter needs this point, but three full formulations is a bit much.

**Suggestion:** make the early statement the main explanation, and later sections refer back more briefly:

> “Even a perfectly Bayesian agent might choose not to seek disconfirmatory evidence...”

because the point has already been developed fully. One concise paragraph is enough.

## 3. Concreteness

### Good use of examples overall
The chapter is already fairly concrete. The opening city example, coffee example, manager example, and 2-4-6 task all help.

### Add a concrete example for “asymmetric updating”
This passage is conceptually clear but abstract:

> “Confirmation bias violates this principle through asymmetric updating. When people encounter evidence that fits their belief, they update upward readily. When they encounter evidence that contradicts it, they discount it...”

A short concrete illustration would help. For example:
- A student thinks they are bad at math, treats one poor quiz as decisive, but dismisses a strong test score as a fluke.
- Or a voter sees one news story supporting their candidate as highly revealing, but treats a scandal as media bias.

This would make the Bayesian point more tangible.

### “Consistency fallacy” could use a sharper explanation of why the neuroscience is irrelevant
This passage is useful:

> “Crucially, some explanations included neuroscience information... that experts confirmed added no logical support...”

But readers may still wonder *why* it adds no support.

**Suggestion:** briefly specify that all psychological processes involve the brain, so merely naming a brain area does not distinguish between competing explanations. That would connect directly to the earlier discussion of diagnostic evidence.

### The Popper section could use a clearer example of falsifiability
> “Popper argued that what makes a hypothesis scientific is not that it can be confirmed, but that it can in principle be proven wrong.”

This is accurate but abstract for some students.

**Suggestion:** add one brief example:
- “For example, ‘all metals expand when heated’ is falsifiable because a single counterexample would refute it; ‘hidden forces always explain the outcome somehow’ is not.”

### “Everyday life” section could use one deeper study or phenomenon
The section lists domains:

> “In medicine... In criminal investigations... In science itself...”

These are good illustrations, but because they are all quick mentions, the passage reads somewhat generic.

**Suggestion:** choose one and make it concrete, perhaps with a specific pattern such as:
- doctors anchoring on an initial diagnosis and then ordering tests mainly to confirm it,
- police interpreting ambiguous interview behavior as suspicious once a suspect is identified.

That would make the practical relevance more vivid.

## 4. Accuracy and coverage of the outline

### The chapter covers the outline very faithfully
You hit all major points:
- definition of confirmation/myside bias
- confirmatory evidence and positive test strategy
- disconfirmatory evidence and negative test strategy
- goal-based explanation via exploration-exploitation tradeoff
- Wason’s 2-4-6 task
- consistency fallacy / irrelevant neuroscience example
- Bayesian asymmetric updating

### One concept from the outline is underdeveloped: falsifiability
The outline explicitly flags falsifiability as a key concept. The draft mentions Popper, but only briefly.

> “This idea connects to a principle from the philosophy of science associated with Karl Popper: falsifiability.”

This is accurate, but because falsifiability is one of the chapter’s key concepts, it deserves slightly more explicit treatment. Right now students may not leave with a durable definition.

**Suggestion:** define it more crisply:
- “A claim is falsifiable if there is some possible observation that would show it to be wrong.”

### Slightly too strong wording here
> “A single piece of disconfirmatory evidence can be enormously powerful.”

This is generally right, but it depends on the hypothesis. For universal claims (“all swans are white”), yes; for probabilistic claims (“older employees are less likely to adopt new technology”), a single exception does not refute the hypothesis.

You partly handle this by using “can be,” but elsewhere the chapter risks overstating the asymmetry.

**Suggestion:** qualify the point earlier:
- “For strict universal claims, a single counterexample can be decisive; for probabilistic claims, disconfirming cases are still often more informative than additional confirming ones.”

That distinction would improve precision.

### The use of “consistency fallacy” may need caution
The outline includes “Consistency fallacy,” but this is not a universally standard textbook term in the way “confirmation bias” or “positive test strategy” is. The draft handles it as:

> “This finding illustrates what we might call the consistency fallacy...”

That phrasing is actually good because it signals modesty. Keep that caution. Do not present it as a settled canonical label unless the wider book has already defined it elsewhere.

### Minor issue with the Wason numbers
> “Out of 29 participants, only 6 identified the correct rule on their first attempt.”

This may be correct for one report/version, but because textbook readers often generalize from landmark findings, you may want to avoid overprecision unless you are certain of the source details used in your bibliography.

**Safer phrasing:** “Only a minority of participants identified the correct rule.”

## 5. Style

### Plain and readable overall
The prose is strong, clear, and accessible. It suits a textbook well.

### Some sentences are slightly inflated
For example:

> “This self-reinforcing cycle is one reason why confirmation bias is so difficult to correct: with each round of biased updating, the person becomes more confident, which makes future disconfirmatory evidence seem even less plausible.”

Clear, but a little heavy. Could be tightened to:
- “Biased updating creates a self-reinforcing cycle: increasing confidence makes later counterevidence easier to dismiss.”

### A few phrases feel more rhetorical than textbook-like
> “the informational equivalent of a lab coat that makes someone look like a doctor without telling you whether they actually know medicine.”

Memorable, but slightly flourish-heavy relative to the surrounding style. If the chapter aims for a consistently plain style, consider simplifying.

Possible replacement:
- “The extra details increase the appearance of scientific support without increasing actual diagnostic value.”

### Remove filler phrasing
> “This tendency has a name: confirmation bias.”

This is fine in trade writing, but in a textbook it is slightly conversational. Not a problem, but if you want a more uniform tone:
- “This tendency is called confirmation bias.”

### Some terminology could be standardized
You alternate among:
- “disconfirmatory”
- “disconfirming”

and
- “confirmation bias”
- “myside bias”

That is acceptable, but it may be smoother to choose one main form and note the alternatives once.

For instance:
- use “disconfirming evidence” throughout unless you prefer the more formal “disconfirmatory evidence.”

### Avoid needless restatement
> “People with no neuroscience training rated bad explanations as significantly more satisfying... Students enrolled in a neuroscience course showed the same pattern... Only neuroscience experts were immune...”

This is good evidence reporting, but “more satisfying, more complete, more convincing” piles near-synonyms.

**Suggestion:** choose one or two:
- “more satisfying and more convincing.”
