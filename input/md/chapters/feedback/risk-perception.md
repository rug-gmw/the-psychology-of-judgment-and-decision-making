## Overall assessment

This is a strong draft: the chapter is clear, coherent, and broadly faithful to the outline. It does a good job connecting risk perception to earlier material on affect, availability, and Bayesian reasoning, and the examples are memorable. The main issues are:

- a few places where the text goes beyond the outline and risks overclaiming,
- some duplication between sections,
- a couple of study descriptions that should be tightened or made more concrete,
- cross-links that are inconsistent with the book’s likely chapter structure,
- a few wording choices that are more rhetorical than textbook-plain.

Below I organize comments by the six requested categories.

---

## 1. Structure

### 1.1 Strong overall flow, but the opening example may distract
> “Every year, thousands of students in the Netherlands cycle to university without a helmet. Most of them would never consider riding a motorcycle without one.”

This is vivid, but it may not cleanly illustrate the chapter’s core distinction. The reader may start wondering whether the relevant difference is social norm, vehicle type, speed, legal context, or actual risk per kilometer, rather than perceived risk. Since the chapter outline begins with **voluntary vs. involuntary risk**, consider opening with that distinction directly, using an example like skiing vs. living near a chemical plant, which maps more tightly onto the literature.

**Suggested change:** Replace or shorten the Netherlands example and transition more directly into the “voluntary vs. involuntary” asymmetry.

---

### 1.2 Transition from “rare risks are ignored” to “dread risk” works, but could be more explicit
> “The neglect of rare risks breaks down dramatically when a hazard triggers a particular emotional response: dread.”

This is a good pivot. Still, the previous section presents neglect of rare risks as a broad pattern, and this sentence introduces dread as an exception without explicitly saying that these are two parts of the same general story: **rare risks are usually neglected unless affect makes them salient**.

**Suggested change:** Add one bridging sentence at the end of “When Rare Risks Are Ignored,” e.g.:
> “The major exception is when a rare hazard is emotionally loaded. In those cases, the same low probability can be treated as intolerably high.”

---

### 1.3 The “Dread in Action” section is well placed
No structural problem here. Moving from concept to the 9/11 and Madrid cases is effective.

---

### 1.4 Scope insensitivity and identifiable victim effect are logically connected, but the link could be signposted more clearly
> “A related phenomenon is the identifiable victim effect.”

This is correct, but the connection would be stronger if you explicitly say that both are consequences of affective rather than numerical evaluation.

**Suggested change:** Add a sentence like:
> “If scope insensitivity shows that feelings do not scale well with numbers, the identifiable victim effect shows what feelings do scale to: vivid individuals.”

---

### 1.5 Climate change section fits the outline, but arrives a bit abruptly
The move from risk communication to climate change is reasonable, but currently it feels like a new mini-chapter.

> “If dread can make a rare risk feel overwhelming, the absence of dread can make a massive risk feel abstract and unimportant.”

This is a good opening sentence. I would make the transition more explicit by tying climate change to the earlier contrast between **vivid episodic risks** and **abstract statistical risks**.

---

### 1.6 Summary is effective but slightly overfull
The summary repeats nearly every major point in full sentences. Since the chapter is already clear, this could be trimmed to emphasize only the key patterns.

---

## 2. Duplication

### 2.1 “Risk is about how it feels” is repeated several times
You state the same core idea in very similar language in multiple places:

> “Risk perception is not primarily about numbers. It is about how a hazard feels.”

> “This asymmetry reveals something fundamental about how risk perception works.”

> “Together, these patterns paint a consistent picture: risk perception is driven by affect...”

These are all useful, but the first two sections in particular repeat the same conceptual point before adding much new information.

**Suggested change:** Keep the claim in the introduction, then make later sections more specific rather than re-stating the general thesis.

---

### 2.2 The description of dread repeats the same features more than once
You list dread as:
> “catastrophic, uncontrollable, and unfairly distributed”

Then later:
> “The characteristics that increase dread include newness, poor understanding, unobservability, involuntariness, and catastrophic potential.”

And earlier in the Slovic paragraph:
> “uncontrollable, catastrophic, and inequitable” / “novel, unobservable, and poorly understood”

This is all accurate, but somewhat repetitive.

**Suggested change:** Consolidate the list once, ideally when introducing Slovic’s psychometric dimensions, then refer back to it more briefly later.

---

### 2.3 Scope insensitivity is explained twice in nearly the same terms
> “The emotional response was triggered by the category, endangered pandas, and did not scale with the number of pandas at stake.”

Later:
> “The reason is that their emotional response is generated for the category, endangered birds, rather than scaled to the quantity.”

This second sentence mostly repeats the first.

**Suggested change:** Keep one explanation and use the later passage to deepen it, for example by connecting it more directly to policy implications or the affect heuristic.

---

### 2.4 Bayesian section re-describes earlier examples without adding much
The Bayesian section is useful, but parts of it simply restate prior sections in new vocabulary.

> “Dread risk... reflects a distortion in how evidence is weighted.”
> “Scope insensitivity means that the posterior does not scale...”
> “the identifiable victim effect shows that narrative evidence... updates beliefs... more powerfully than statistical evidence...”

That is the right idea, but it currently reads like a relabeling exercise.

**Suggested change:** Either shorten this section or make it more precise by distinguishing:
- distortion of priors,
- distortion of likelihood weighting,
- failure to aggregate sample size,
- substitution of affect for evidence.

That would add analytical value rather than repetition.

## 3. Concreteness

### 3.1 “When Rare Risks Are Ignored” needs a supporting study or more concrete evidence
> “Most of the time, people treat extremely low probabilities as if they were zero.”

This is plausible and central to the outline, but here it is asserted without a citation, study, or concrete demonstration. Since the rest of the chapter is evidence-rich, this section stands out as under-supported.

**Suggested change:** Add either:
- a classic finding on neglect of low-probability events in everyday judgment, or
- a concrete illustration, such as low uptake of preventative actions against household or environmental hazards.

Right now, this section feels more like informed commentary than textbook exposition.

---

### 3.2 The “smoke detector” example may be misleading
> “People may fail to take simple precautions, such as installing a smoke detector... because the probability of harm on any given day is vanishingly small.”

This is not the best example of neglect of rare risks, because smoke detector adoption is strongly shaped by law, housing standards, income, and landlord responsibility. It may invite objections unrelated to the psychological point.

**Suggested change:** Use examples more clearly driven by individual underreaction to low-probability hazards, such as backup data storage, radon testing, or emergency kit preparation.

---

### 3.3 Scope insensitivity section could benefit from naming the migratory-birds study directly
> “Classic studies have found that people's willingness to pay is nearly identical for saving 2,000, 20,000, or 200,000 migratory birds.”

This is exactly the kind of concrete result that helps, but it currently appears without citation or enough context. Since it is one of the outline’s key examples, it should be briefly anchored to a specific study.

**Suggested change:** Name the study or at least note that this result comes from contingent valuation research on environmental harms.

---

### 3.4 The identifiable victim section is concrete, but perhaps too detailed on one study and not enough on the basic phenomenon
The Small and Loewenstein discussion is interesting, but the reader may need the more standard version of the effect first: named child vs. statistics about many children.

> “The only difference between conditions was whether the recipient had already been selected...”

This is clever, but it is not the most intuitive first example of the identifiable victim effect.

**Suggested change:** Start with the classic form of the effect—name, face, and story increase giving—then use the determined/undetermined study as a more refined demonstration.

---

### 3.5 Climate section could use one concrete communication example
> “simulations of rising sea levels in one's own neighborhood”

Good. Consider adding a specific illustration—e.g., flood maps, local wildfire smoke days, or heat alerts—to make the point less abstract.

## 4. Accuracy and coverage of the outline

### 4.1 Coverage is mostly faithful
The draft includes all major outline points:
- voluntary vs. involuntary risk,
- neglect of rare risks,
- dread risk,
- 9/11 driving substitution,
- Madrid contrast,
- scope insensitivity,
- identifiable victim effect,
- relative vs. absolute risk,
- natural frequencies,
- climate change and affect,
- Bayesian lens.

So in terms of **coverage**, the chapter is successful.

---

### 4.2 “Type 1 / Type 2” from the outline has been replaced by “emotional vs analytical,” which is mostly fine, but slightly inconsistent
The outline says:
> “Abstract statistics about global warming influence Type 2 reasoning but fail to trigger the Type 1 emotional response needed to motivate action.”

The draft instead uses:
> “the information engages analytical reasoning but fails to generate the affect needed to change behavior.”

That is clearer stylistically, but since the book seems to use **System 1 / System 2** terminology consistently, you may want to keep that vocabulary here for consistency with earlier chapters.

---

### 4.3 Small possible overstatement about cycling vs motorcycle risk
> “The objective risk of a head injury per kilometer traveled may not differ as dramatically as one might expect...”

This claim needs support and may not be true enough to use casually. Motorcycle risk is generally much higher on many injury metrics. Since the point is about perceived risk, you do not need this empirical aside.

**Suggested change:** Remove “may not differ as dramatically as one might expect.”

---

### 4.4 The account of Slovic should be a bit more precise
> “@slovic1987 identified dread as the single most important factor distinguishing hazards...”

This is slightly too strong. The psychometric paradigm often identifies **dread** and **unknown risk** as major dimensions, not simply one single dominant factor in all contexts.

**Suggested change:** Rephrase to:
> “In psychometric studies of risk perception, dread emerged as one of the major dimensions distinguishing hazards...”

---

### 4.5 The statement about public perceptions “incorporate legitimate concerns” is fair but could be framed more carefully
> “As Slovic argues, public risk perceptions incorporate legitimate concerns, such as fairness and controllability, that technical risk assessments often ignore.”

This is substantively reasonable, but it edges from description into endorsement. In a textbook chapter, I would present it slightly more neutrally.

**Suggested change:**  
> “As Slovic argued, public risk judgments often reflect concerns—such as fairness, controllability, and catastrophic potential—that are not captured by simple mortality statistics.”

---

### 4.6 Potential issue with “This is scope insensitivity taken to its logical extreme”
> “This is scope insensitivity taken to its logical extreme...”

The identifiable victim effect is related to scope insensitivity, but not reducible to it. It also involves vividness, empathy, and singularity. Calling it simply “scope insensitivity taken to its logical extreme” oversimplifies the relationship.

**Suggested change:**  
> “This is closely related to scope insensitivity, but it also reflects the special power of vivid, concrete individuals to trigger empathy.”

---

### 4.7 “Deliberation does not increase sympathy for the millions; it reduces sympathy for the one.”
This is rhetorically strong, but too categorical. Analytical processing can reduce affective giving in some paradigms, but the sentence sounds like a general law.

**Suggested change:** soften to:
> “In these studies, deliberation did not increase concern for the many; instead, it weakened the emotional pull of the single identified victim.”

---

### 4.8 Bayesian interpretation is useful, but “likelihood ratio” may be too strong or too technical without explanation
> “functions as an inflated likelihood ratio”

This is accurate in spirit, but if the chapter uses Bayesian language, it should be introduced carefully. Otherwise it feels imported from a formal framework without enough scaffolding.

**Suggested change:** Either explain the term briefly or replace it with:
> “is treated as stronger evidence than it really is.”

## 5. Style

### 5.1 Generally clear and readable
The prose is strong overall: plain, well paced, and accessible.

---

### 5.2 Remove occasional rhetorical flourishes that sound more like essay writing than textbook writing
Examples:

> “It is three orders of magnitude.”

> “The consequences of dread risk are not merely theoretical. They can be measured in lives lost.”

> “This is scope insensitivity taken to its logical extreme.”

> “But perhaps the most troubling finding in this area...”

These lines are engaging, but a few of them lean dramatic. In a textbook, a slightly more neutral tone would fit better.

---

### 5.3 Avoid inconsistent terminology: “Type 1/Type 2” vs “System 1/System 2”
The condensed outline uses **System 1** and **System 2**, but the chapter outline uses **Type 1** and **Type 2** once. The draft mostly avoids both except indirectly.

**Suggested change:** Standardize on whatever the book uses elsewhere—probably **System 1/System 2**.

---

### 5.4 Tighten filler phrases
A few sentences could be trimmed:

> “This is not a small difference in tolerance. It is three orders of magnitude.”

Could simply be:
> “This is a very large difference in tolerance—about three orders of magnitude.”

> “The mechanism is straightforward...”

Often removable.

> “This finding presents a troubling dilemma...”

Can be shortened unless you want the rhetorical emphasis.

---

### 5.5 “calm public and a panicked one” is a bit journalistic
> “the way risk information is communicated can make the difference between a calm public and a panicked one.”

This sounds media-like rather than textbook-like.

**Suggested change:**  
> “the way risk information is communicated can strongly shape public reactions.”

## Specific revisions I would prioritize

1. **Strengthen “When Rare Risks Are Ignored” with evidence or a concrete study.**
2. **Tighten repetition** around “risk is about feeling,” dread features, and the category-vs-quantity explanation.
3. **Refine the identifiable victim section** so the basic phenomenon appears before the more technical Small & Loewenstein design.
4. **Soften a few overstatements**, especially:
   - “single most important factor,”
   - “scope insensitivity taken to its logical extreme,”
   - “Deliberation does not increase sympathy for the millions...”
5. **Standardize cross-links and terminology** with the rest of the book.
6. **Trim rhetorical phrasing** to keep the tone consistently textbook-like.

## Bottom line

This is a solid chapter draft with good examples and good conceptual integration. The main work now is editorial: reduce repetition, sharpen a few claims, support the “rare risks are ignored” section more concretely, and standardize style and cross-references. The underlying structure and content are strong.
