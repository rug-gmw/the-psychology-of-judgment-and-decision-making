# A Probabilistic Contrast Model of Causal Induction

Cheng, P. W., & Novick, L. R.

__This article challenges the notion that human causal reasoning is inherently biased, proposing instead a probabilistic contrast model that explains apparent biases as rational responses to incomplete information. The authors argue that previous models failed to account for unspecified data patterns and demonstrate through experiments that their model accurately predicts causal attributions.__

The article begins by reviewing existing models of causal attribution, particularly Kelley’s (1967) ANOVA model, which posits that people act as "intuitive scientists" by assessing covariation between potential causes and effects. However, subsequent research identified systematic deviations from this model, including biases against using consensus information and a tendency to attribute effects to persons rather than stimuli or circumstances. Cheng and Novick contend that these apparent biases may stem from incomplete information in experimental designs rather than irrational reasoning processes.

The authors introduce their probabilistic contrast model, which extends Kelley’s ANOVA analogy by computing contrasts between the probability of an effect occurring in the presence versus absence of potential causal factors. Unlike previous models, this approach accounts for both main effects and interactions (conjunctive causes) and distinguishes between facilitatory and inhibitory factors. The model assumes that people estimate and compare proportions rather than performing complex statistical computations.

To test their model, Cheng and Novick conducted an experiment with 91 participants who were presented with 15 causal attribution problems. Each problem described a target event and provided information about its occurrence across different combinations of persons, stimuli, and occasions. The problems were designed to systematically vary the pattern of information while keeping configurational information constant, allowing the authors to assess whether apparent biases could be explained by the probabilistic contrast model.

The results strongly supported the probabilistic contrast model. For problems sharing the same configuration of consensus, distinctiveness, and consistency information, participants' causal attributions varied systematically depending on the complete pattern of covariation across all dimensions. The model accurately predicted these shifts in attribution, including interaction attributions that previous models could not explain. On average, 61% of participants' responses across all problems matched the predictions of the probabilistic contrast model, with even higher percentages for specific configurations.

The authors compared their model against competing models, including the template model (Orvis et al., 1975), the deterministic covariation principle (Jaspars et al., 1983; Försterling, 1989), and the abnormal conditions focus model (Hilton & Slugoski, 1986). The probabilistic contrast model outperformed all alternatives, particularly in accounting for attributions that previous models considered "biases" or deviations from normative reasoning. A replication study further confirmed these findings.

Cheng and Novick also addressed the issue of biases in causal attribution. They found no evidence for a bias toward person attributions or against using consensus information when information was complete and counterbalanced across dimensions. Regression analyses showed that consensus, distinctiveness, and consistency information each accounted for significant variance in their respective main-effect attributions, with no bias against consensus information.

The discussion emphasizes the generality of the probabilistic contrast model, which can be applied to any set of dimensions and accounts for the distinction between causes and enabling conditions. The authors address potential criticisms of their methodology, including the artificiality of their materials and the possibility of knowledge retrieval rather than inference. They argue that their results demonstrate the effectiveness of their manipulation and that the systematic variation in attributions supports the validity of their model.

In conclusion, Cheng and Novick propose that causal induction is an unbiased process when the relevant set of information is considered. Apparent biases in causal attribution can be explained as rational responses to incomplete information, and the probabilistic contrast model provides a normative framework for understanding how people compute causal inferences based on covariation.
