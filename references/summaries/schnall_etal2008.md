# Disgust as Embodied Moral Judgment

Schnall, S., Haidt, J., Clore, G. L., & Jordan, A. H.

__This article explores how incidental feelings of disgust influence moral judgments, demonstrating that disgust increases the severity of moral condemnation. The authors find that this effect is particularly strong for individuals who are highly attuned to their bodily sensations, and that disgust uniquely shapes moral judgments compared to other negative emotions like sadness.__

The study challenges the traditional philosophical emphasis on reason in moral decision-making by highlighting the role of affective, embodied responses. Drawing on the social intuitionist model and the affect-as-information framework, the authors argue that moral judgments are often driven by quick, gut-level emotional reactions rather than deliberate reasoning. Disgust, in particular, is hypothesized to play a significant role in moral condemnation due to its evolutionary roots in avoiding contamination and its extension into social contexts. The article presents four experiments that test whether extraneous disgust—induced through various methods such as exposure to bad smells, disgusting environments, recalling disgusting experiences, or watching disgusting videos—affects moral judgments.

In **Experiment 1**, participants exposed to a foul odor (a "fart spray") made harsher moral judgments compared to those in a neutral environment, regardless of whether the moral scenarios involved inherently disgusting actions. This suggested that disgust's influence on moral judgment is not limited to cases where the action itself is physically repulsive. **Experiment 2** introduced individual differences in sensitivity to bodily sensations, measured by the Private Body Consciousness (PBC) scale. Participants who were highly sensitive to their bodily cues (high-PBC) made more severe moral judgments when seated in a disgusting environment, while low-PBC participants were unaffected. This finding underscored the embodied nature of disgust, as only those attuned to their physical reactions were influenced by the manipulation. The experiment also tested whether disgust spilled over into nonmoral judgments, such as public policy preferences or activity choices, but found no evidence of such effects, indicating that disgust's influence is specific to moral evaluations.

**Experiment 3** replicated the findings of Experiment 2 using a different disgust induction method: participants wrote about a physically disgusting experience. Again, high-PBC participants in the disgust condition made harsher moral judgments, while low-PBC participants showed no effect. This experiment confirmed that the results were not dependent on the specific disgust manipulation used. **Experiment 4** addressed whether the effect was unique to disgust or could be attributed to any negative emotion. Participants watched film clips designed to induce either disgust, sadness, or a neutral state. The results showed that disgust, but not sadness, increased the severity of moral judgments, particularly for high-PBC participants. This demonstrated that the effect is specific to disgust and not merely a consequence of negative affect.

A meta-analysis combining data from Experiments 2-4 confirmed the robustness of the findings: disgust consistently increased moral condemnation for high-PBC participants across different experimental manipulations. The authors conclude that disgust serves as an embodied moral intuition, guiding judgments even when it is irrelevant to the action being evaluated. They argue that while affective intuitions are often useful in moral decision-making, they can also lead people astray when the feelings are extraneous or misattributed. The study highlights the importance of understanding the psychological mechanisms underlying moral judgments, particularly the role of embodied emotions like disgust, and suggests that awareness of these influences may help individuals make more reasoned moral decisions.
