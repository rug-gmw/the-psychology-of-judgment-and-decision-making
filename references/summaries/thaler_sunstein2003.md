# Libertarian Paternalism

Sunstein, C. R., & Thaler, R. H.

__This article introduces the concept of "libertarian paternalism," arguing that policymakers can and should design choice architectures that nudge individuals toward better decisions without restricting their freedom of choice.__

The article by Cass Sunstein and Richard Thaler challenges the traditional economic view that paternalism is inherently undesirable and an oxymoron when paired with libertarianism. The authors argue that the anti-paternalistic stance held by many economists is based on flawed assumptions and misconceptions. Specifically, they contest the belief that individuals always make choices in their best interest, asserting that this claim is empirically testable and often false. They introduce the idea that in many situations, some form of choice architecture must be established by either private or public actors, which inevitably influences people's decisions.

The authors illustrate their argument with practical examples, such as a company cafeteria director arranging food to influence healthier eating choices. They propose that while some arrangements might be seen as paternalistic, they are preferable to random or malicious arrangements. The key insight is that since some form of paternalism is unavoidable, the focus should shift from whether to be paternalistic to how to design paternalistic interventions that respect individual freedom.

Sunstein and Thaler delve into the irrationality of human choices, citing extensive research in behavioral economics that shows people often make suboptimal decisions due to cognitive biases, heuristics, and lack of self-control. They provide examples from real-world scenarios, such as poor dietary habits leading to obesity and suboptimal investment choices in retirement savings plans, to underscore the prevalence of these issues.

The article discusses the inevitability of paternalism, particularly through the selection of default options. They highlight how default settings, such as automatic enrollment in 401(k) plans, significantly influence behavior due to status quo bias. The authors argue that these defaults are a form of libertarian paternalism because they guide individuals toward beneficial outcomes without coercing them.

Sunstein and Thaler propose methods for choosing among paternalistic options, including cost-benefit analysis and indirect proxies for welfare, such as market-mimicking strategies and minimizing opt-outs. They introduce the "Save More Tomorrow" program as an example of successful libertarian paternalism, where employees commit to increasing their savings rates in the future, leading to substantial improvements in savings behavior.

In conclusion, the authors advocate for libertarian paternalism as a pragmatic approach to policy design. They argue that by carefully structuring choices, institutions can promote individual welfare while preserving freedom of choice. The goal is to avoid arbitrary or harmful effects and to create systems that genuinely enhance people's well-being.
