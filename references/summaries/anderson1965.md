# Primacy Effects in Personality Impression Formation Using a Generalized Order Effect Paradigm

Anderson, N. H.

__This study investigates how the order of personality trait adjectives influences impression formation, demonstrating a linear primacy effect—where earlier adjectives have greater impact than later ones. Using a novel experimental design, Anderson shows that impression judgments can be modeled as a weighted average of trait values, with weights decreasing linearly across presentation order. The findings challenge prior interpretations of primacy effects, such as shifts in meaning, and instead support models emphasizing attentional or integrative processes.__

The article explores the phenomenon of primacy effects in impression formation, where the order in which personality traits are presented affects how individuals judge a hypothetical person. Building on earlier work by Asch (1946), Anderson seeks to quantify the influence of adjective position by introducing a "generalized order effect paradigm." In this design, subjects hear sequences of high-value (H) or low-value (L) adjectives, with a block of three opposing adjectives (e.g., LLL in a sequence of Hs) inserted at varying positions. The study tests whether the impact of these interpolated adjectives diminishes as they appear later in the sequence, thereby revealing the relative weight of early versus late information.

Two experiments were conducted, each involving female psychology students who rated their liking for hypothetical individuals described by adjective sets. In Experiment I, subjects judged sets of six or nine adjectives, with the interpolated block (e.g., LLL or HHH) placed at all possible positions within the sequence. The results showed that the influence of the interpolated adjectives decreased linearly with their position, producing straight-line interpolation curves that confirmed a primacy effect. Experiment II replicated these findings with a refined design, where each subject judged the same adjectives in all possible orders, improving data reliability. Statistical analyses confirmed the robustness of the linear primacy effect, ruling out alternative explanations tied to variability in adjective sets or subject differences.

Anderson interprets the results through three theoretical lenses. First, the **weighted-average model** posits that impressions are formed by averaging the scale values of adjectives, with weights assigned to each adjective that decrease linearly with their position. This model successfully predicts the straight-line interpolation curves observed in the data. Second, the **proportional-change (linear) model**, originally developed for opinion change, describes impression formation as a step-by-step process where each new adjective adjusts the existing impression proportionally. Anderson demonstrates that this model is mathematically equivalent to the weighted-average model under the observed conditions, offering a dynamic account of how impressions evolve over time. Third, the **shift-of-meaning interpretation**, proposed by Asch, suggests that early adjectives establish a "directed impression" that alters the perceived meaning of later traits. While this explanation aligns with qualitative observations of primacy, Anderson argues that it struggles to account for the linear patterns in the data and lacks empirical support from related studies.

The discussion highlights the advantages of the weighted-average and proportional-change models, which attribute primacy to diminishing attention or integration weights for later adjectives. Supporting evidence comes from studies where primacy effects disappear or reverse when subjects are required to recall adjectives or respond cumulatively after each trait, suggesting that attentional processes play a key role. In contrast, the shift-of-meaning interpretation faces challenges, as primacy effects have been observed in tasks (e.g., judging meals) where contextual shifts in meaning seem implausible. Anderson concludes that while meaning shifts may occur in some cases, they are not the primary driver of primacy effects in impression formation. Instead, the findings underscore the importance of serial integration processes, where the weight of information declines as new details are processed. The study thus advances a quantitative framework for understanding how order shapes social judgments.
