# Why U.S. Test Scores Are in a 'Generation-Long Decline'

Miller, C. C., Paris, F., & Mervosh, S.

__U.S. student test scores have declined across income, racial, and geographic lines over the past decade, with the steepest drops among low-achieving students, signaling a systemic crisis in education beyond pandemic disruptions.__

This *New York Times* analysis reveals a troubling, decade-long decline in U.S. student achievement, based on district-level test score data from the Educational Opportunity Project at Stanford. The findings show that reading scores fell in 83% of districts between 2015 and 2025, while math scores declined in 70%, affecting students across socioeconomic, racial, and geographic divides. The decline predates the pandemic, with reading scores dropping as sharply between 2017 and 2019 as they did during COVID-19, and continued to fall through 2024. Though scores inched upward in 2025, the recovery has been insufficient to offset earlier losses, particularly for the lowest-performing students.

The data, which covers 68% of U.S. school districts, underscores a "learning recession" that began in the mid-2010s, coinciding with two major shifts: the easing of federal accountability under the No Child Left Behind Act (replaced in 2015) and the rise of smartphones and personalized school devices. While the pandemic exacerbated the decline—especially in low-income districts—experts argue the roots of the problem run deeper. Chronic absenteeism, mental health struggles, and reduced academic expectations have all played a role. Even affluent districts, often assumed to be insulated from such trends, have seen declines, with over half reporting lower scores than a decade ago.

Researchers point to the end of No Child Left Behind as a potential factor, noting that the law’s strict accountability measures correlated with rising test scores, particularly for disadvantaged students. However, its replacement reduced pressure on schools, and the bipartisan urgency around education reform has waned. Globally, similar declines suggest broader forces at work, including the pervasive influence of digital devices. Nearly half of U.S. teenagers now report being online "almost constantly," and educators observe that screen time has eroded focus, displaced reading, and led to a simplified curriculum. Fewer students read for pleasure, and schools increasingly assign shorter texts or excerpts rather than full books.

Despite the bleak national picture, some districts have bucked the trend. Compton Unified, a historically low-performing district in California, now matches the national average in math and reading, thanks to aggressive attendance initiatives, in-school tutoring, and data-driven interventions. Washington, D.C., has also seen gains, attributed to high-dosage tutoring, the adoption of the "science of reading" (a phonics-based approach), and financial incentives for high-performing teachers in struggling schools. Yet such success stories remain exceptions. Experts warn that without urgent, sustained action—such as stricter accountability, reduced screen time, and targeted support for struggling students—the decline could have long-term consequences for the nation’s educational and economic future. The article concludes with a sobering observation: the pandemic may have accelerated the crisis, but it did not create it.
