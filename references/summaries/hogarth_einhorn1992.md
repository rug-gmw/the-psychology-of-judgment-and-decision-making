# Order Effects in Belief Updating: The Belief-Adjustment Model

Hogarth, R. M., & Einhorn, H. J.

__This article introduces the belief-adjustment model, a theory explaining how order effects (primacy, recency, or none) emerge in belief updating based on task characteristics and information-processing strategies. The model accounts for diverse experimental findings and predicts novel order effects under previously untested conditions.__

The article presents a comprehensive theory of belief updating that explains how and why order effects—such as primacy (early information having greater influence) or recency (later information being more impactful)—occur in human judgment. The authors begin by reviewing the existing literature on order effects, noting inconsistencies in findings across different domains like social cognition, jury decision-making, and probabilistic inference. They identify key task variables that influence order effects: the complexity of evidence items, the length of the evidence series, and the response mode (Step-by-Step, SbS, or End-of-Sequence, EoS). Their analysis reveals that primacy effects are more common in simple, long tasks with EoS response modes, while recency effects dominate in complex or short tasks, particularly with SbS response modes.

To address these patterns, the authors propose the **belief-adjustment model**, a general anchoring-and-adjustment framework. The model posits that belief updating occurs sequentially, with current beliefs serving as an anchor that is adjusted based on new evidence. The model incorporates three critical subprocesses: (1) **encoding**, where evidence is interpreted relative to either a constant reference point (evaluation mode) or the preceding anchor (estimation mode); (2) **processing**, which can be either Step-by-Step (SbS) or End-of-Sequence (EoS); and (3) **adjustment**, where the weight given to new evidence depends on its sign and the current anchor's strength. The authors specify conditions under which each subprocess is likely to be used, such as task complexity and response mode, and derive predictions for order effects under various combinations of these factors.

The model's predictions are validated in two ways: first, by demonstrating its consistency with existing experimental data, and second, through a series of five original experiments designed to test novel predictions. These experiments focus on short series of complex evidence items, a condition where prior research is sparse. The results confirm the model's predictions: no order effects for consistent evidence (all positive or all negative) and recency effects for mixed evidence (both positive and negative). The experiments also provide support for the model's underlying assumptions, such as the contrast effect, where the impact of new evidence depends on the current anchor's strength.

The discussion highlights the model's implications for understanding belief updating and order effects. The authors emphasize the distinction between evaluation and estimation modes of encoding, which explains why some tasks produce "averaging" data while others yield "adding" data. They also discuss the role of processing mode (SbS vs. EoS) and how cognitive limitations influence strategy selection. The article concludes by advocating for a **procedural theory of judgment**, where complex behavior arises from the interaction of simple psychological processes with environmental demands. This approach offers a unifying framework for understanding the sensitivity of judgment to task variables, bridging the gap between cognitive limitations and the apparent chaos of human decision-making.
