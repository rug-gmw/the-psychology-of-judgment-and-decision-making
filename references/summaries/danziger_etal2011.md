# Extraneous Factors in Judicial Decisions

Danziger, S., Levav, J., & Avnaim-Pesso, L.

__This study challenges the legal formalist view that judicial rulings are based solely on laws and facts, demonstrating instead that extraneous factors—such as the timing of food breaks—can significantly influence parole decisions. Judges were far more likely to grant favorable rulings at the start of a decision session, with approval rates dropping sharply before rebounding after a meal.__

The article empirically tests the legal realist caricature that "justice is what the judge ate for breakfast" by analyzing 1,112 parole decisions made by eight experienced Israeli judges over a 10-month period. The authors investigate whether sequential decision-making depletes judges' mental resources, leading them to default to simpler, status-quo outcomes—such as denying parole—as fatigue sets in. The study focuses on the role of two daily food breaks, which segment the judges' work into three distinct "decision sessions." The data reveal a striking pattern: favorable rulings began at approximately 65% at the start of each session but declined steadily to nearly zero by the session's end, only to spike again after a break. This effect persisted even after controlling for legally relevant factors such as crime severity, prior incarcerations, and rehabilitation program availability.

The methods involved a meticulous analysis of judicial rulings, including the ordinal position of each case within a session, the timing of food breaks, and key legal and demographic variables. The authors employed logistic regression models with judge-specific fixed effects to isolate the impact of decision fatigue, ruling out alternative explanations such as case ordering or judicial quotas. Their findings suggest that the act of making repeated decisions—rather than merely the passage of time—drives the observed depletion effect. For instance, favorable rulings took significantly longer to deliberate and produced longer written verdicts, implying that judges default to rejection when mentally fatigued. The study also addressed potential confounds, such as whether case order was manipulated to favor certain prisoners before breaks, but found no evidence of such bias.

The discussion situates these results within broader debates about judicial decision-making, highlighting how even experienced judges are susceptible to psychological biases. While the study cannot definitively isolate whether rest, mood improvement, or glucose replenishment drives the post-break rebound in favorable rulings, it underscores the vulnerability of expert decision-makers to extraneous influences. The authors speculate that similar patterns may emerge in other high-stakes sequential decisions, such as legislative votes or medical diagnoses, reinforcing the idea that human judgment is inherently context-dependent. Ultimately, the research bolsters the legal realist argument that the law is indeterminate, as situational factors—like a judge’s meal schedule—can sway outcomes in cases with identical legal merits.
