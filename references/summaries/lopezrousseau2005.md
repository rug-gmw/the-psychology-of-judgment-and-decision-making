# Avoiding the Death Risk of Avoiding a Dread Risk: The Aftermath of March 11 in Spain

López-Rousseau, A.

__This study examines whether Spaniards, like Americans after 9/11, avoided train travel due to terrorism fears and inadvertently increased fatal car accidents. Unlike the U.S., Spain saw no rise in highway deaths, suggesting cultural and sociopolitical factors mitigated the "dread risk" effect.__

After the September 11, 2001, terrorist attacks in the U.S., many Americans avoided flying, opting to drive instead—a decision that paradoxically increased fatal car accidents, as driving is statistically riskier than air travel. Gigerenzer (2004) confirmed this phenomenon, estimating that approximately 350 additional traffic deaths occurred in the three months following 9/11 due to this behavioral shift. The present study investigates whether a similar pattern emerged in Spain after the March 11, 2004 (M-11), train bombings in Madrid, which killed around 200 people. Given that train travel in Spain is significantly safer than car travel, the study tests whether Spaniards, driven by fear, reduced train ridership and increased highway traffic, thereby raising the risk of fatal accidents.

To replicate Gigerenzer’s (2004) methodology, the study analyzed three key conditions in the months following M-11: (1) a decrease in train travel, (2) an increase in highway traffic, and (3) a rise in fatal highway accidents. Data from Spanish railway, highway, and traffic authorities were examined using interannual variation—a measure comparing current trends to those of the same period in previous years—to account for seasonal and long-term patterns. The results revealed that while train travel did decline in March and April 2004 (by 4% and 6%, respectively), this reduction was smaller and shorter-lived than the U.S. post-9/11 drop in air travel. Crucially, however, highway traffic did not increase; instead, it decreased by 1% in March and 3% in April. Consequently, fatal highway accidents also declined by 8% in March and 11% in April, contrary to the U.S. experience. These findings suggest that Spaniards did not substitute train travel with car travel, thereby avoiding the unintended consequence of increased traffic fatalities.

The discussion highlights key differences between the U.S. and Spanish responses. While both populations initially avoided the "dread risk" of terrorism, Spaniards did not face the same fatal trade-off. Several factors may explain this divergence. Psychologically, the M-11 attacks, though devastating, had fewer fatalities than 9/11, potentially reducing their impact. Culturally, Spain has a weaker car-centric culture than the U.S., and alternative transportation modes (e.g., buses, carpooling) may have absorbed the shift away from trains. Sociopolitically, Spain’s long history of terrorism—including decades of domestic attacks—may have fostered greater resilience, leading to a more measured response. For instance, trains continued running immediately after M-11, and many Spaniards used them to attend anti-terrorism demonstrations the following day. This suggests that, unlike Americans, Spaniards may have perceived terrorism as a calculated rather than an existential risk, mitigating the fear-driven behavioral changes observed in the U.S.

In sum, the study demonstrates that while avoiding dread risks is a universal reaction, the unintended consequences of such avoidance are not inevitable. Cultural, psychological, and sociopolitical contexts shape how populations respond to terrorism, influencing whether they inadvertently expose themselves to greater risks. The findings underscore the importance of considering these factors in risk communication and policy design to prevent counterproductive safety behaviors.
