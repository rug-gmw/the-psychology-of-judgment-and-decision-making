# Risk, Ambiguity, and the Savage Axioms

Ellsberg, D.

__This article challenges the universality of the Savage axioms by demonstrating that people often violate them in situations of ambiguity—where probabilities are uncertain rather than precisely known. Ellsberg argues that ambiguity, distinct from risk, leads to systematic deviations from expected utility theory, proposing an alternative decision rule that accounts for confidence in probability estimates.__

The article begins by revisiting Frank Knight’s distinction between *risk* (measurable uncertainty) and *uncertainty* (unmeasurable ambiguity), a dichotomy often dismissed by economists who assume individuals behave as if they assign numerical probabilities even in ambiguous situations. Ellsberg questions this assumption, asking whether people’s choices in ambiguous contexts can truly be described by subjective probabilities. He critiques the Savage axioms—particularly the *Sure-thing Principle*—which posit that rational decision-makers should act as if they assign consistent probabilities to events, regardless of the ambiguity of their information. While these axioms appear normatively compelling in many cases, Ellsberg argues that they fail to capture how people actually behave when faced with deep uncertainty.

To illustrate this, Ellsberg presents a series of thought experiments involving urns with unknown or partially known distributions of colored balls. In one example, subjects are asked to bet on drawing a red ball from either an urn with a known 50-50 distribution (Urn II) or an urn with an unknown ratio of red and black balls (Urn I). Most people prefer betting on Urn II, even when the known probability is slightly less favorable (e.g., 49-51), violating the Savage axioms. This preference persists even when small samples are drawn from Urn I, suggesting that ambiguity—not just ignorance—drives the behavior. Ellsberg further demonstrates that such choices cannot be reconciled with any consistent probability assignment, as they lead to logical contradictions (e.g., preferring to bet on red over black while also preferring to bet against red over black).

Ellsberg then explores why people deviate from the axioms in ambiguous situations. He introduces the concept of *ambiguity* as a distinct dimension of uncertainty, separate from the desirability of outcomes or the relative likelihood of events. Ambiguity arises when information is scant, unreliable, or conflicting, undermining confidence in any single probability estimate. Unlike "complete ignorance," where no information exists, or "risk," where probabilities are known, ambiguity describes a state where multiple plausible probability distributions exist, but none can be confidently selected. Ellsberg argues that in such cases, people may prioritize *security*—the worst plausible outcome—over expected utility, leading them to favor options whose payoffs are less sensitive to ambiguity.

To formalize this behavior, Ellsberg proposes a decision rule that combines an estimated probability distribution (reflecting best guesses) with a "security level" (the worst plausible outcome under ambiguity). The rule weights these two factors by a parameter *ρ*, representing the decision-maker’s confidence in their probability estimates. When *ρ* is high (low ambiguity), the rule approximates expected utility maximization; when *ρ* is low (high ambiguity), it biases choices toward options with higher security levels. This model explains why people might prefer "known risks" (e.g., Urn II) over ambiguous gambles (e.g., Urn I), even at the cost of lower expected payoffs. It also accounts for the observed shift in behavior as ambiguity decreases—for instance, when sample information reduces uncertainty about Urn I’s contents.

Ellsberg acknowledges that this decision rule violates the Savage axioms, as it implies that subjective probabilities depend on payoffs (e.g., a conservative person "distorts" their best estimates to emphasize worse outcomes). However, he argues that such behavior is neither irrational nor erratic. Instead, it reflects a deliberate, if conservative, strategy for navigating ambiguity. He challenges the normative supremacy of the Savage axioms, suggesting that their applicability may be limited to situations where ambiguity is low. For highly ambiguous contexts, alternative decision rules—like his proposed model—may better describe and prescribe behavior.

The article concludes by questioning whether people who violate the axioms are "foolish." Ellsberg argues that empirical research is too nascent to dismiss their behavior as irrational, especially given that many sophisticated decision-makers (including Savage himself) admit to such violations upon reflection. He calls for further study of ambiguity’s role in decision-making, suggesting that the Savage axioms may need to be qualified or supplemented to account for real-world uncertainty. Ultimately, Ellsberg’s work lays the foundation for the "Ellsberg paradox," demonstrating that ambiguity aversion is a robust phenomenon with profound implications for economic theory and behavioral science.
