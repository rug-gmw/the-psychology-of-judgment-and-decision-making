# Dual-Process Theories of Higher Cognition: Advancing the Debate

Evans, J. St. B. T., & Stanovich, K. E.

__This article defends dual-process theories of cognition against recent critiques, arguing that while some criticisms apply to oversimplified versions of the theory, a refined distinction between autonomous (Type 1) and reflective (Type 2) processes remains empirically and theoretically well-supported.__

The article systematically addresses and rebuts five major criticisms of dual-process theories, which propose that human cognition operates through two distinct types of processes: fast, intuitive, and autonomous (Type 1) versus slow, deliberative, and reflective (Type 2). The authors begin by acknowledging that early formulations of dual-process theories suffered from vague definitions, inconsistent attribute clusters, and overly broad assumptions about the alignment of features such as speed, consciousness, and normativity with either Type 1 or Type 2 processing. They clarify that many critiques target a "received view" of dual-process theories—a straw-man version that assumes all proposed attributes (e.g., fast/slow, conscious/unconscious, biased/normative) must co-occur perfectly. Instead, the authors argue that only a few defining features are necessary to distinguish the two types of processing: Type 1 processes are autonomous, requiring minimal working memory, while Type 2 processes involve cognitive decoupling (the ability to simulate hypothetical scenarios) and heavily load on working memory.

The introduction traces the historical development of dual-process theories across cognitive and social psychology, noting their independent origins in reasoning, judgment, decision-making, and social cognition. The authors emphasize that while dual-process theories have proliferated under various labels (e.g., implicit/explicit, heuristic/analytic), critics often conflate distinct theories and overlook recent refinements. They highlight their own evolving perspectives, including the abandonment of the "System 1/System 2" terminology in favor of "Type 1/Type 2" to avoid implying that these processes map onto singular, unified brain systems. Instead, Type 1 processing encompasses a diverse set of autonomous systems (e.g., modular, habitual, or automated processes), while Type 2 processing is characterized by its reliance on working memory and hypothetical thinking.

The core of the article addresses five key criticisms. First, the authors concede that the proliferation of dual-process theories has led to vague and overlapping definitions but argue that this does not invalidate the fundamental distinction. They note that many critiques mistakenly assume all dual-process theories share identical assumptions, whereas contemporary theories (including their own) have moved beyond simplistic dichotomies. Second, they tackle the "attribute clustering" problem, where critics argue that the proposed features of Type 1 and Type 2 processing (e.g., fast/slow, conscious/unconscious) do not consistently align. The authors respond that only a subset of features—autonomy for Type 1 and cognitive decoupling/working memory engagement for Type 2—are defining, while others are merely correlated under specific conditions. For example, they reject the fallacy that Type 1 processing is always biased or that Type 2 processing is always normative, citing evidence that both types can produce correct or incorrect responses depending on context.

Third, the article distinguishes between "types" and "modes" of processing, clarifying that modes (e.g., cognitive styles or thinking dispositions) vary continuously within Type 2 processing and should not be confused with the discrete Type 1/Type 2 distinction. They illustrate this with Stanovich’s tripartite model of mind, which separates autonomous processes (Type 1), algorithmic processing (Type 2), and reflective control (a higher-order aspect of Type 2). Fourth, the authors address the claim that single-process accounts can explain dual-process phenomena, such as rule conflict in reasoning tasks. They argue that while all cognitive processes can be described as rule-based, this does not negate the qualitative differences between autonomous and reflective processing. For instance, default-interventionist theories (which they endorse) posit that Type 1 processes generate rapid, intuitive responses that may be overridden by slower, reflective Type 2 processing—a dynamic that cannot be reduced to mere rule conflict.

Finally, the article counters the criticism that the evidence for dual-process theories is weak or ambiguous. The authors present converging evidence from experimental manipulations, neuroscience, and individual differences research. Experimentally, manipulations such as time pressure or working memory loads selectively impair Type 2 processing while leaving Type 1 intact, as seen in tasks like the Wason selection task or belief-bias syllogisms. Neuroscientific studies show that belief-based and logic-based responses activate distinct brain regions, with conflict detection (anterior cingulate cortex) and override (prefrontal cortex) supporting the default-interventionist model. Psychometric studies reveal that Type 2 processing correlates strongly with cognitive ability (e.g., fluid intelligence), whereas Type 1 processing does not, further validating the distinction. The authors also address evolutionary perspectives, arguing that while Type 1 processes may be evolutionarily older and shared with other animals, Type 2 processing—particularly cognitive decoupling—is uniquely human and enables abstract, hypothetical thought.

The article concludes by proposing a refined definition of Type 1 and Type 2 processing. Type 1 processes are defined by their autonomy and minimal working memory demands, encompassing a heterogeneous set of systems (e.g., modular, habitual, or associative). Type 2 processes are defined by their engagement of working memory and cognitive decoupling, enabling hypothetical thinking and mental simulation. The authors advocate for default-interventionist models, where Type 1 processes generate default responses that may be overridden by Type 2 processing when necessary. They emphasize that dual-process theories are not static but evolving, with ongoing refinements driven by empirical evidence and critical debate. Ultimately, they argue that the dual-process distinction remains a robust and necessary framework for understanding human cognition, despite the valid critiques of earlier, oversimplified versions.
