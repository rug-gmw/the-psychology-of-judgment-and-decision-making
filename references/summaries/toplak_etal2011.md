# The Cognitive Reflection Test as a predictor of performance on heuristics-and-biases tasks

Toplak, M. E., West, R. F., Stanovich, K. E.

__This study demonstrates that the Cognitive Reflection Test (CRT) is a stronger predictor of rational thinking and performance on heuristics-and-biases tasks than measures of cognitive ability, executive functioning, or thinking dispositions. The CRT uniquely captures the tendency to override intuitive but incorrect responses, highlighting its role in assessing cognitive miserliness.__

The article investigates the predictive power of the Cognitive Reflection Test (CRT) in relation to performance on a wide range of heuristics-and-biases tasks, which assess rational thinking. The CRT, introduced by Frederick (2005), consists of three problems designed to measure an individual's ability to override an intuitive but incorrect response in favor of a more reflective and correct one. The authors argue that the CRT is uniquely positioned to predict rational thinking because it captures the tendency toward "miserly processing"—a cognitive shortcut that often leads to errors in judgment and decision-making.

The study involved 346 participants who completed a battery of tasks, including the CRT, measures of cognitive ability (using the Wechsler Abbreviated Scale of Intelligence), executive functioning tasks (such as the Trailmaking Test, Stroop task, and Paced Auditory Serial Addition Test), and self-report measures of thinking dispositions (e.g., actively open-minded thinking, superstitious thinking, and consideration of future consequences). Additionally, participants completed 15 classic heuristics-and-biases tasks and syllogistic reasoning problems to assess their rational thinking abilities.

The results revealed that the CRT was the strongest predictor of performance on the rational-thinking composite score, which combined heuristics-and-biases tasks and syllogistic reasoning. While the CRT showed moderate correlations with cognitive ability measures, regression analyses demonstrated that it accounted for substantial unique variance in rational thinking, even after controlling for cognitive ability, executive functioning, and thinking dispositions. This suggests that the CRT measures aspects of rational thinking that are not fully captured by traditional intelligence tests or other cognitive measures.

The authors propose that the CRT's predictive power stems from its ability to assess the tendency toward cognitive miserliness—a reluctance to engage in effortful, reflective processing. Unlike traditional insight problems, which often leave participants struggling to find any solution, the CRT presents problems where an intuitive but incorrect answer is readily available. Overriding this initial response requires both cognitive ability and a disposition toward reflective thinking, which the CRT effectively captures. The study concludes that the CRT is a particularly potent measure of rational thinking because it directly assesses performance in situations where cognitive miserliness is likely to lead to errors. This makes it a valuable tool for understanding individual differences in rational thought beyond what is measured by intelligence tests alone.
