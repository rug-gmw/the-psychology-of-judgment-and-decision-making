# Affect, Culture, and Morality, or Is It Wrong to Eat Your Dog?

Haidt, J., Koller, S. H., & Dias, M. G.

__This study challenges the universality of harm-based morality, demonstrating that cultural and socioeconomic factors shape moral judgments about harmless yet offensive actions. Brazilian and U.S. participants, particularly those from lower socioeconomic backgrounds, often moralized actions like flag desecration or incest, while elite university students viewed them as personal or conventional matters.__

The article explores whether morality is universally rooted in harm, rights, and justice—or if cultural norms and emotions expand its domain. The authors contrast cognitive-developmental theories (e.g., Turiel, Kohlberg), which argue morality is harm-centered, with cultural psychology perspectives (e.g., Shweder), which propose morality varies across cultures and may include non-harm-based codes like community (duty, respect) and divinity (purity, sanctity). To test this, the researchers presented 360 Brazilian and U.S. adults and children from high and low socioeconomic status (SES) with stories depicting harmless but offensive actions (e.g., eating a dead dog, incest, flag desecration). Participants were asked whether these acts were morally wrong, universally prohibited, or permissible, alongside probes about harm and emotional reactions.

Results revealed stark cultural and SES differences. High-SES U.S. college students predominantly adopted a permissive stance, viewing the actions as personal or conventional matters, consistent with a harm-based morality. In contrast, most other participants—especially low-SES Brazilians—moralized the actions, judging them as universally wrong and warranting punishment, even when no harm was perceived. Affective reactions (e.g., disgust, disrespect) better predicted moral judgments in these groups than appraisals of harm. Children mirrored adult patterns but were more likely to moralize actions, despite procedural adjustments to encourage conventional reasoning. The study also found that the distinction between moral and conventional domains was weaker in Brazil, particularly among low-SES groups, challenging the universality of Turiel’s domain theory.

The discussion emphasizes the role of culture and emotion in shaping morality. The authors propose an expanded model where moral judgment arises from both harm appraisals and affective reactions, with culture mediating their interplay. They argue that harm-based morality may be a Western, upper-middle-class phenomenon, while other cultures integrate community and divinity codes. The findings underscore the need for cross-culturally valid models of moral judgment that account for variability in how affect and norms influence moral reasoning.
