# An Essay Towards Solving a Problem in the Doctrine of Chances

Bayes, T.

__This foundational work introduces Bayes’ Theorem, providing a mathematical framework to infer the probability of an event based on prior knowledge of conditions related to it. It establishes the principles of inverse probability and lays the groundwork for modern Bayesian statistics.__

Thomas Bayes’ *An Essay Towards Solving a Problem in the Doctrine of Chances* presents a groundbreaking approach to probability theory by addressing the problem of determining the likelihood of an unknown event’s probability given observed outcomes. The essay begins with precise definitions of key probabilistic concepts, such as inconsistent and contrary events, probability as a ratio of expectations, and the independence of events. Bayes then derives several propositions that formalize relationships between probabilities, including the additive nature of probabilities for inconsistent events (Proposition 1) and the multiplicative rule for the joint occurrence of subsequent events (Proposition 3). These foundational results culminate in Proposition 5, which articulates the core of what would later be known as Bayes’ Theorem: the probability of a prior event given the occurrence of a subsequent event is proportional to the joint probability of both events divided by the probability of the subsequent event.

The latter half of the essay shifts to a geometric and experimental framework to illustrate these principles. Bayes introduces a thought experiment involving a square table and balls thrown onto it, where the position of a line drawn based on the first ball’s resting place determines the probability of a second event (M) in subsequent trials. Through Lemmas 1 and 2, he demonstrates that the probability of the event M in a single trial corresponds to the ratio of a segment of the table’s base to its total length. Proposition 8 extends this idea, showing that the probability of observing a specific number of successes (p) and failures (q) in n trials, combined with the position of the initial line, can be represented as an area under a curve defined by a binomial expansion. This geometric interpretation allows Bayes to derive Proposition 9, which provides a method to infer the unknown probability of an event after observing its outcomes in multiple trials. The essay concludes with a scholium arguing that this rule applies even when nothing is known about the event’s probability beforehand, as the uniform distribution of possible outcomes aligns with the principle of indifference.

Bayes’ work is remarkable for its blend of rigorous mathematical reasoning and philosophical insight, bridging the gap between observed data and underlying probabilities. While the original essay omits some of the more elaborate geometric derivations, its core contributions—particularly the inversion of probabilities to update beliefs in light of new evidence—remain central to Bayesian inference and have profoundly influenced statistics, machine learning, and decision theory.
