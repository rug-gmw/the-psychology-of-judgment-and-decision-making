# Morality and the Social Instincts: Continuity with the Other Primates

de Waal, F. B. M.

__This article challenges the "Veneer Theory," which posits that human morality is a thin cultural overlay masking an inherently selfish nature. Instead, de Waal argues that morality is a natural outgrowth of social instincts shared with other primates, rooted in empathy, reciprocity, and fairness. Drawing on evolutionary biology, neuroscience, and primate behavior, he demonstrates that moral tendencies are deeply embedded in human nature rather than a recent, artificial invention.__

The article begins by critiquing Veneer Theory, which traces its origins to Thomas Henry Huxley’s assertion that humans are fundamentally selfish and must exert great effort to achieve morality. This perspective, de Waal argues, is theoretically flawed and empirically unsupported. It fails to explain how humans alone could break from their biological nature or what motivates moral behavior worldwide. In contrast, Charles Darwin’s view—that morality emerges naturally from social instincts—aligns with modern evolutionary theories, including kin selection, reciprocal altruism, and fairness principles. Neuroscience further undermines Veneer Theory by showing that moral judgments activate ancient emotional brain centers, not just the neocortex, suggesting continuity with other animals.

De Waal then explores empirical evidence from primate studies to illustrate this continuity. He highlights behaviors such as empathy, reciprocity, and conflict resolution in chimpanzees, bonobos, and capuchin monkeys, which mirror foundational aspects of human morality. For instance, chimpanzees exhibit consolation behavior, comforting distressed individuals after conflicts, while capuchin monkeys demonstrate a sense of fairness by rejecting unequal rewards. These examples suggest that moral capacities like sympathy and reciprocity are not unique to humans but are deeply rooted in our evolutionary history. De Waal also references historical and philosophical perspectives, including those of Mencius and Edward Westermarck, who emphasized the role of emotions and social instincts in shaping morality.

The article further delves into the mechanisms of empathy, proposing a "Russian Doll Model" where basic emotional contagion forms the core, with more complex cognitive layers built upon it. This model explains how primates, particularly great apes, can exhibit targeted helping behaviors, such as a bonobo aiding a bird or a gorilla rescuing a child. These actions require an understanding of another’s specific needs, a capacity that may be linked to self-awareness, as seen in mirror self-recognition studies. De Waal also discusses reciprocity and fairness, showing how chimpanzees remember and repay favors, while capuchin monkeys react negatively to perceived inequity, suggesting an early form of fairness.

Ultimately, de Waal argues that morality is not a cultural imposition but an extension of primate social instincts. He rejects the notion that humans are inherently selfish, instead positioning morality as a natural product of evolution, shaped by the need for cooperation and social harmony. This perspective aligns with Darwin’s view of morality as a logical outgrowth of social instincts, offering a more integrated and empirically grounded understanding of human nature. The article concludes by emphasizing that moral behavior is not a veneer but a fundamental aspect of our biology, shared with other social animals.
