# Hindsight ≠ Foresight: The Effect of Outcome Knowledge on Judgment Under Uncertainty

Baruch Fischhoff

This article demonstrates that knowing an outcome's result distorts retrospective judgments, making events seem more predictable than they were, while people remain largely unaware of this bias. The study reveals how hindsight inflates perceived inevitability and impairs learning from past experiences.

The article explores the psychological divergence between hindsight and foresight, focusing on how outcome knowledge alters perceptions of past events. Fischhoff introduces the concept of "creeping determinism"—the tendency to perceive reported outcomes as inevitable once they are known—contrasting it with philosophical determinism. The study tests two core hypotheses: first, that reporting an outcome increases its perceived likelihood, and second, that people are unaware of how outcome knowledge shapes their judgments. The research is motivated by the broader question of how individuals learn (or fail to learn) from history, given that historical judgment is often clouded by knowledge of how events unfolded.

Three experiments form the empirical backbone of the study. In Experiment 1, participants were divided into "Before" and "After" groups, with the latter receiving information about the "true" outcome of historical or clinical events. The "After" groups consistently assigned higher probabilities to the reported outcomes, regardless of whether the outcomes were accurate, confirming the creeping determinism effect. Additionally, outcome knowledge altered participants' judgments of the relevance of specific details in the event descriptions, suggesting that hindsight reshapes how evidence is interpreted. Experiment 2 tested whether individuals could ignore outcome knowledge when instructed to do so. Participants who knew the outcome struggled to reconstruct how they would have judged the event without that knowledge, indicating a lack of awareness of hindsight's influence. Experiment 3 extended this finding by showing that people also overestimate how much others—who lack outcome knowledge—would have predicted the event accurately. Together, these experiments reveal a systematic bias: outcome knowledge not only distorts individual judgments but also leads to an overestimation of others' foresight.

The discussion situates these findings within broader psychological and historical contexts, drawing parallels to phenomena like the "law of small numbers" and defensive attribution. Fischhoff argues that hindsight bias stems from an unconscious assimilation of outcome knowledge, which makes events appear more coherent and predictable in retrospect. This bias is exacerbated by cognitive heuristics such as representativeness and availability, which lead individuals to overestimate the likelihood of known outcomes. The article also explores structural explanations, such as the tendency to anchor judgments in hindsight and adjust insufficiently when reconstructing foresight.

The implications of these findings are far-reaching. Hindsight bias can lead to unfair evaluations of decision-makers, particularly in high-stakes contexts like policy or military strategy, where surprises are inevitable. By making past events seem more predictable than they were, hindsight undermines the ability to learn from mistakes and adapt to uncertainty. Fischhoff concludes that recognizing and mitigating this bias is essential for improving judgment, decision-making, and historical understanding. The study underscores the challenges of learning from the past when the past itself is viewed through the distorting lens of hindsight.
