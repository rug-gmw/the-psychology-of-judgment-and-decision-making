# Defaults and Donation Decisions

Johnson, E. J., & Goldstein, D. G.

__This article argues that organ donation rates are heavily influenced by default policies—such as opt-in or opt-out systems—because people’s preferences are often constructed rather than pre-existing. The authors demonstrate that presumed consent (opt-out) significantly increases donation rates compared to explicit consent (opt-in), offering a low-cost policy solution to the organ shortage crisis.__

The article challenges the assumption that people have fixed preferences about organ donation, instead proposing that decisions are often constructed in response to how the question is framed. The authors, Johnson and Goldstein, contend that the persistent shortage of donated organs stems not from deeply held opposition but from the way donation requests are structured, particularly through default options. They argue that defaults shape behavior by acting as implicit recommendations, reducing the effort required to make a decision, and leveraging psychological biases like loss aversion, where the perceived costs of changing the status quo outweigh potential benefits.

To support their claims, the authors first review evidence from behavioral research showing that preferences are malleable and influenced by framing. For example, they cite a classic study where patients, physicians, and students chose between surgery and radiation therapy for lung cancer based on whether outcomes were presented as survival or mortality rates—demonstrating how the same information can yield different decisions depending on its presentation. Defaults, they argue, operate similarly by anchoring choices in the absence of strong prior preferences. This is illustrated through real-world examples, such as auto insurance policies in New Jersey and Pennsylvania, where default assignments led to vastly different uptake rates, and retirement savings plans, where a 3% default contribution dramatically increased participation.

The core of the article focuses on organ donation, where the authors conduct both experimental and cross-national analyses. In an online experiment with 161 participants, they found that donation rates were nearly twice as high under an opt-out default compared to an opt-in default, with the opt-out condition mirroring a neutral choice scenario. Extending this to real-world data, they compare European countries with opt-in and opt-out policies, revealing that presumed consent countries have significantly higher effective consent rates—often exceeding 90%—while opt-in countries lag far behind, sometimes below 30%. Even after controlling for factors like transplant infrastructure, education, and religion, regression analyses show that opt-out policies lead to a 16–56% increase in actual donation rates.

The authors acknowledge the ethical and practical trade-offs of default policies, such as the risk of misclassifying non-donors as donors or the potential for public backlash. However, they argue that the benefits—measured in lives saved—outweigh these costs, especially given that most people support donation in principle but fail to act without a nudge. They also contrast defaults with mandated choice policies, noting that forcing decisions can lead to avoidance, as seen in Virginia where nearly a quarter of people refused to state a preference. The article concludes that defaults offer a cost-effective, scalable solution to the organ shortage, emphasizing that small changes in how questions are framed can have outsized effects on behavior.
