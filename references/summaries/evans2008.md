# Dual-Processing Accounts of Reasoning, Judgment, and Social Cognition

Evans, J. St. B. T.

__This article reviews dual-process theories in cognitive and social psychology, which propose two distinct modes of thinking: fast, automatic, and unconscious (System 1) versus slow, deliberative, and conscious (System 2). It evaluates whether these theories can be unified under a single "generic dual-system" framework and explores their applications across reasoning, judgment, and social cognition.__

Dual-process theories have gained prominence in psychology as a means of explaining human cognition through two distinct modes of processing: System 1, characterized by rapid, automatic, and unconscious operations, and System 2, defined by slow, effortful, and conscious deliberation. Jonathan Evans critically examines the coherence of these theories, questioning whether they can be consolidated into a single, overarching "generic dual-system" model. The article begins by outlining the core attributes commonly ascribed to Systems 1 and 2, such as their associations with consciousness, evolutionary origins, functional characteristics, and individual differences. For instance, System 1 is often linked to unconscious, implicit, and high-capacity processes, while System 2 is tied to conscious, explicit, and capacity-limited reasoning. However, Evans highlights inconsistencies in these attributions, particularly when considering the evolutionary age of System 1 processes, which may not uniformly align with the idea of a single, ancient cognitive system.

The review then delves into the applications of dual-process theories across three key domains: reasoning, judgment and decision-making, and social cognition. In reasoning, Evans discusses how heuristic processes (System 1) often compete with or override analytic reasoning (System 2), leading to biases such as belief bias in syllogistic reasoning or matching bias in the Wason selection task. Evidence from studies on time pressure, working memory load, and individual differences in cognitive ability supports the distinction between these two systems, with higher cognitive capacity often correlating with more effective System 2 intervention. In judgment and decision-making, dual-process theories explain phenomena like probability judgments, where intuitive heuristics (System 1) may lead to biases, but deliberative reasoning (System 2) can correct these errors. Evans also notes the value of System 1 in expert decision-making, where rapid, intuitive responses often prove more effective than slow, analytic deliberation.

In social cognition, dual-process theories have been particularly influential, with models like the heuristic-systematic model and cognitive-experiential self-theory (CEST) distinguishing between automatic, implicit processes and controlled, explicit ones. These theories explore how implicit attitudes, stereotypes, and social judgments operate outside conscious awareness, often conflicting with explicit beliefs. Evans highlights the role of social cognitive neuroscience in identifying distinct neurological systems underlying these processes, such as the X-system (reflexive, automatic) and C-system (reflective, controlled). Despite the empirical support for dual-process theories, Evans argues that the notion of a unified dual-system framework is oversimplified. He suggests that the field might benefit from distinguishing between "type 1" (automatic, unconscious) and "type 2" (effortful, conscious) processes without assuming a rigid two-system architecture. The article concludes by advocating for a more nuanced understanding of dual-process theories, recognizing the diversity of implicit processes and the potential for hybrid models that integrate both parallel-competitive and default-interventionist mechanisms.
