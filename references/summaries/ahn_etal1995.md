# The Role of Covariation Versus Mechanism Information in Causal Attribution

Ahn, W., Kalish, C. W., Medin, D. L., & Gelman, S. A.

__This article challenges traditional covariation-based models of causal attribution, demonstrating through a series of experiments that people prefer mechanism-based explanations and seek information about underlying causal processes rather than statistical correlations.__

The study investigates how individuals determine the causes of events, contrasting two theoretical approaches: the covariation approach, which emphasizes statistical relationships between factors and outcomes, and the mechanism approach, which focuses on identifying underlying causal processes. The authors argue that while covariation models (such as Kelley’s ANOVA model and Cheng and Novick’s probabilistic contrast model) have dominated psychological research on causal attribution, they may not reflect how people naturally reason about causes. Instead, the authors propose that people prioritize understanding *how* an event occurred through mechanism-based explanations, which provide a deeper, more explanatory account of causality.

The introduction outlines the historical context of causal attribution research, highlighting the prominence of covariation models. These models suggest that people identify causes by analyzing patterns of co-occurrence between potential causal factors and effects. However, the authors question whether people *spontaneously* use covariation information when attributing causality, or if they instead rely on knowledge of underlying mechanisms. They note that while covariation models describe how people *use* given information, they do not address what information people *seek* when forming causal judgments. This distinction is critical, as the authors argue that information-seeking behavior provides a clearer window into natural causal reasoning processes.

The article then contrasts the covariation and mechanism approaches across three dimensions. First, the two approaches differ in the *types of vocabularies* they employ. Covariation models operate at the same level of abstraction as the event description, identifying causal factors (e.g., "John caused the accident") without explaining *how* the factor led to the outcome. In contrast, mechanism-based explanations introduce new entities or processes (e.g., "John was drunk, impairing his driving ability") that are not explicitly stated in the event description. Second, the approaches differ in their assumptions about *causal necessity*. Covariation models treat causality as an inductive generalization based on observed regularities, whereas mechanism models assume a deterministic relationship where causes necessitate their effects. Third, the mechanism approach is *content-specific*, meaning that the process of causal attribution depends on the particular details of the event, while the covariation approach is more general and syntactic, treating events as abstract variables.

To test these ideas, the authors conducted four experiments examining what types of information people seek and use when attributing causality. In Experiments 1 and 2, participants were presented with event descriptions (e.g., "John had a car accident last night") and asked to generate questions they would need answered to determine the cause. The results showed that participants overwhelmingly sought mechanism-based information (e.g., "Was John drunk?") rather than covariation information (e.g., "Has John had other accidents?"). This pattern held even for "no-schema" events, where participants had little prior knowledge, and for nonsense events (e.g., "The fep mimbled the wug"), where participants still attempted to apply mechanism-based reasoning. Additionally, participants' explanations for events were predominantly mechanism-based, even when instructed to focus on covariation.

Experiment 3 further explored whether providing covariation information would influence participants' information-seeking behavior. Participants were given varying amounts of covariation data (none, partial, or complete) and asked to generate additional questions or explanations. While the proportion of covariation questions increased when covariation information was provided, most participants still favored mechanism-based questions and explanations. Even when given complete covariation information, participants' explanations rarely aligned with the predictions of covariation models, suggesting that covariation information alone is insufficient to override the preference for mechanism-based reasoning.

Experiment 4 addressed a potential counterargument: that mechanism-based explanations are merely a proxy for covariation information. The authors tested whether mechanism statements (e.g., "John was drunk") and covariation statements (e.g., "John is more likely to have accidents") had equivalent effects on causal attributions. Participants rated the strength of causal attributions for factors supported by mechanism, covariation, or both types of information. The results showed that mechanism information had a significantly stronger influence on causal judgments than covariation information, even when the two conveyed equivalent statistical relationships. This finding demonstrates that mechanism-based explanations provide something beyond mere covariation—likely a sense of causal necessity and explanatory depth.

The discussion synthesizes the findings, arguing that people prioritize mechanism-based reasoning in causal attribution. While covariation information may play a role in certain contexts (e.g., as a heuristic for generating hypotheses or when direct mechanism information is unavailable), it is not the primary driver of causal reasoning. The authors draw parallels between the mechanism-covariation distinction and the theory-similarity distinction in categorization research, suggesting that both mechanism and theory provide explanatory coherence that covariation and similarity alone cannot. They also highlight the pragmatic and functional advantages of mechanism-based explanations, such as their ability to support counterfactual reasoning and predictions about novel situations.

In conclusion, the article challenges the dominance of covariation models in causal attribution research and presents compelling evidence that people naturally seek and prefer mechanism-based explanations. The findings have broad implications for understanding how people reason about causality, suggesting that theories of causal attribution must account for the role of underlying mechanisms in addition to statistical patterns.
