# Resource-rational analysis: understanding human cognition as the optimal use of limited computational resources

Lieder, F., & Griffiths, T. L.

__This article introduces *resource-rational analysis*, a cognitive modeling framework that integrates rational principles with cognitive constraints to explain human cognition. By formalizing the optimal use of limited computational resources, the authors reconcile seemingly irrational cognitive biases with the mind’s adaptive efficiency, offering a unifying approach to understanding perception, decision-making, memory, and reasoning.__

The article begins by addressing a fundamental challenge in cognitive science: the ill-posed problem of inferring cognitive mechanisms from limited behavioral data, where infinitely many mechanisms can produce the same observations. Traditional approaches, such as cognitive architectures and rational analysis, provide complementary constraints—architectural limits versus functional optimality—but neither alone suffices to explain the full spectrum of human cognition. Resource-rational analysis bridges this gap by combining the strengths of both, assuming that cognitive mechanisms are optimally adapted to both environmental demands and the brain’s computational constraints. This framework extends Marr’s levels of analysis, deriving algorithmic-level models from computational-level theories while incorporating realistic cognitive limitations.

The authors trace the historical role of rationality in cognitive science, noting how classic models—such as expected utility theory, logic, and probability theory—have struggled to account for systematic deviations like cognitive biases. While some biases may reflect adaptive heuristics (e.g., satisficing or ecological rationality), others stem from cognitive constraints, such as limited attention, memory, or processing speed. Resource-rational analysis formalizes this intuition by defining bounded optimality: the best possible cognitive strategy given finite resources. The framework posits that evolution, learning, and development shape heuristics to approximate this ideal, trading off accuracy for efficiency in a principled way.

The article reviews case studies across cognitive domains to demonstrate the framework’s explanatory power. In decision-making, resource-rational models account for biases like anchoring, probability distortion, and risk aversion by showing how they emerge from optimal trade-offs between speed, accuracy, and effort. For example, satisficing (choosing the first "good enough" option) is reinterpreted as a bounded-optimal solution to costly information search. Similarly, in perception, biases like the overrepresentation of extreme events or perceptual illusions arise from the efficient allocation of limited neural resources. The framework also sheds light on memory, attention, and reasoning, explaining phenomena like working memory capacity limits or the framing problem as resource-rational adaptations.

A key contribution of resource-rational analysis is its potential to connect psychology with neuroscience and artificial intelligence. By grounding cognitive models in biophysical constraints (e.g., metabolic costs of neural activity), the framework links algorithmic-level theories to neural implementation. Conversely, insights from AI—such as bounded optimality in machine learning—can inspire new hypotheses about human cognition. The article also revisits the debate on human rationality, arguing that many "biases" may reflect resource-rational strategies rather than irrationality. This perspective suggests redefining cognitive biases as deviations from resource-rationality, not classical norms.

The authors acknowledge challenges, including the difficulty of measuring cognitive constraints, the risk of overfitting models to data, and the need to scale the framework to real-world complexity. They propose future directions, such as developing methods to automatically discover resource-rational heuristics and applying the framework to improve human decision-making through cognitive training or prosthetics. Ultimately, resource-rational analysis offers a principled, integrative approach to reverse-engineering the mind, explaining both its remarkable efficiency and its apparent flaws as products of the same adaptive process.
