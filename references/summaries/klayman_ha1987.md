# Confirmation, Disconfirmation, and Information in Hypothesis Testing

Klayman, J., & Ha, Y.-W.

__This article challenges the notion of a universal "confirmation bias" in hypothesis testing, arguing that many behaviors labeled as such are better understood as manifestations of a general positive test strategy (+test strategy). The authors demonstrate that this strategy can be effective under common real-world conditions, though it may lead to errors in specific contexts.__

The article explores the nature of hypothesis testing in both scientific and everyday reasoning, critiquing the prevailing view that people are prone to a deleterious "confirmation bias" that leads them to test cases most likely to verify rather than falsify their beliefs. Klayman and Ha propose that many phenomena labeled as confirmation bias are better explained by a general *positive test strategy*, where individuals tend to test cases expected to have the property of interest (+Htests) or known to have that property (+Ttests). This strategy is not inherently flawed; rather, its effectiveness depends on the relationship between the hypothesized set (H) and the target set (T).

The authors begin by distinguishing between two senses of "seeking disconfirmation": testing cases predicted not to have the target property and testing cases most likely to falsify the hypothesis. They argue that the latter is the more normatively appropriate goal, but the optimal strategy for achieving it varies with task characteristics. Using Wason’s (1960) rule-discovery task as a foundation, the authors analyze different possible relationships between H and T (e.g., embedded, overlapping, surrounding) and demonstrate that the +test strategy can be effective in many situations, particularly when the target phenomenon is rare (a common real-world scenario). For example, when the base rate of the target phenomenon (p(t)) is low and the hypothesis roughly matches this rate (p(h) ≈ p(t)), +Htests are more likely to yield falsifications than -Htests.

The article extends this analysis to probabilistic environments, where hypotheses are not expected to be perfectly accurate. Here, both verification and falsification provide valuable but ambiguous information, and the +test strategy remains useful under conditions where the probability of falsification (z) exceeds irreducible error rates (ε). The authors also apply their framework to diverse hypothesis-testing tasks, including concept identification, the four-card problem, intuitive personality testing, learning from outcome feedback, and judgments of contingency. In each case, they show how the +test strategy can explain observed behaviors and how its effectiveness depends on task-specific factors. For instance, in the four-card problem, the +test strategy leads to suboptimal choices because the task structure requires testing for falsifications of sufficiency, not necessity. However, in contingency judgment tasks, the +test strategy aligns well with how people naturally weigh evidence.

Klayman and Ha conclude that the +test strategy serves as a general default heuristic, useful in the absence of task-specific information or under cognitive constraints. While it can lead to errors in certain contexts (e.g., Wason’s task), it is often effective in real-world situations where the target phenomenon is rare and hypotheses are roughly accurate. The article emphasizes the importance of understanding the interaction between testing strategies and task characteristics, rather than assuming that a single strategy (e.g., seeking disconfirmation) is universally optimal. This integrative approach provides a framework for analyzing both the successes and failures of human hypothesis testing across diverse domains.
