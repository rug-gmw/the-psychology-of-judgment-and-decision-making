# Advances in Prospect Theory: Cumulative Representation of Uncertainty

Tversky, A., Kahneman, D.

__This article introduces cumulative prospect theory, an extension of original prospect theory that applies cumulative decision weights to both gains and losses, accommodating uncertain and risky prospects with any number of outcomes while preserving key features like loss aversion and diminishing sensitivity.__

The article presents a significant advancement in prospect theory, originally developed by Kahneman and Tversky in 1979, which challenged the dominance of expected utility theory by demonstrating systematic violations of its principles in human decision-making. The authors, Amos Tversky and Daniel Kahneman, propose cumulative prospect theory as a refined model that addresses limitations of the original theory, particularly its inability to handle prospects with more than two outcomes and its occasional violation of stochastic dominance. The new model employs cumulative rather than separable decision weights, applying distinct weighting functions to gains and losses, and extends its applicability to both risky and uncertain prospects.

The introduction outlines five major phenomena that violate expected utility theory: framing effects, nonlinear preferences, source dependence, risk seeking, and loss aversion. These phenomena serve as benchmarks for any adequate descriptive theory of choice. The authors argue that cumulative prospect theory can explain these anomalies through its value and weighting functions. The value function is concave for gains and convex for losses, reflecting diminishing sensitivity, and steeper for losses than gains, reflecting loss aversion. The weighting functions, which transform probabilities nonlinearly, overweight small probabilities and underweight moderate to high probabilities, contributing to the observed fourfold pattern of risk attitudes: risk aversion for gains and risk seeking for losses of high probability, and risk seeking for gains and risk aversion for losses of low probability.

The theoretical framework of cumulative prospect theory is detailed in Section 1. The model distinguishes two phases in decision-making: framing, where the decision-maker constructs a representation of the decision problem, and valuation, where the value of each prospect is assessed. The valuation process involves a value function and decision weights derived from capacities, which are nonadditive set functions generalizing probabilities. The cumulative functional transforms the entire cumulative distribution function rather than individual probabilities, ensuring stochastic dominance and extending the theory to prospects with any number of outcomes. The authors illustrate the model with examples and discuss its relationship to previous work, including rank-dependent utility models and the original prospect theory, highlighting how cumulative prospect theory resolves issues like violations of stochastic dominance without requiring an editing phase.

Section 1.3 elaborates on the qualitative properties of the value and weighting functions. The value function’s S-shape—concave for gains and convex for losses—reflects diminishing sensitivity, while its steeper slope for losses embodies loss aversion. The weighting functions are hypothesized to have an inverse S-shape, concave near zero and convex near one, explaining phenomena like the certainty effect and the common consequence effect. The authors present experimental evidence, including a study with money managers and Stanford students, demonstrating violations of the independence axiom, which further supports the nonlinearity of the weighting functions.

Section 2 describes an experiment designed to test the predictions of cumulative prospect theory. Twenty-five subjects participated in three sessions, making choices between risky prospects and sure outcomes to estimate certainty equivalents. The results confirmed the fourfold pattern of risk attitudes: risk aversion for high-probability gains, risk seeking for high-probability losses, risk seeking for low-probability gains, and risk aversion for low-probability losses. The data also supported the hypothesis of preference homogeneity, where scaling outcomes by a constant proportionally scales their cash equivalents. The authors fit the data to a two-part power value function and weighting functions, estimating parameters that aligned with the theory’s predictions, such as pronounced loss aversion and the inverse S-shape of the weighting functions.

The discussion addresses the role of monetary incentives, noting that high stakes do not eliminate the observed patterns of risk attitudes, as demonstrated by studies with substantial payoffs. The authors argue that cumulative prospect theory provides a unified framework for understanding choice under both risk and uncertainty, though they acknowledge its limitations, such as potential sensitivity to the formulation of prospects and the number of outcomes. The appendix presents an axiomatic analysis of cumulative prospect theory, formalizing its key properties, including comonotonic independence and sign-comonotonic tradeoff consistency, which distinguish it from expected utility theory and cumulative utility theory.

Overall, the article advances a robust descriptive theory of decision-making that accounts for a wide range of empirical phenomena, offering a more accurate alternative to expected utility theory while maintaining mathematical rigor and generality.
