# Judgment of Contingency in Depressed and Nondepressed Students: Sadder but Wiser?

Alloy, L. B., Abramson, L. Y.

__This seminal study investigates how depressed and nondepressed individuals perceive the relationship between their actions and outcomes. The core finding is that nondepressed individuals often overestimate their control over positive outcomes and underestimate it over negative outcomes, while depressed individuals tend to judge contingencies more accurately.__

The article explores the concept of contingency in learning theory, emphasizing that both animals and humans are sensitive to the relationships between stimuli and outcomes. The authors distinguish between contingency and temporal contiguity, arguing that contingency is essential for true learning. They review literature on learned helplessness, which posits that exposure to uncontrollable events leads to motivational, cognitive, and emotional deficits, akin to depression in humans. The study aims to empirically test how subjective judgments of contingency align with objective contingencies, particularly focusing on differences between depressed and nondepressed individuals.

Four experiments were conducted with college students categorized as depressed or nondepressed based on their scores on the Beck Depression Inventory (BDI) and the Multiple Affect Adjective Check List (MAACL). In Experiment 1, students judged the degree of control they had over a green light onset in scenarios with varying degrees of actual contingency. Both depressed and nondepressed students accurately judged the degree of control, suggesting that subjective representations of contingency can mirror objective contingencies under certain conditions.

Experiment 2 examined judgments in noncontingent scenarios with differing frequencies of the desired outcome. Nondepressed students overestimated their control when the outcome was frequent but were accurate when it was infrequent. Depressed students, however, accurately judged the lack of contingency in both scenarios. This finding indicates that nondepressed individuals may rely on invalid heuristics, such as the frequency of outcomes, when judging contingencies.

Experiment 3 manipulated the valence of outcomes (winning or losing money) while keeping the frequency of outcomes constant. Nondepressed students showed an illusion of control when winning money but not when losing it, whereas depressed students remained accurate in both conditions. This suggests that the valence of outcomes significantly influences nondepressed individuals' judgments of control.

Experiment 4 further explored the impact of outcome valence in contingent scenarios. Nondepressed students underestimated their control when losing money but were accurate when winning, while depressed students consistently judged the degree of control accurately. This experiment highlights that nondepressed individuals' judgments are influenced by both the valence and the type of response (active vs. passive) associated with the outcome.

The discussion integrates findings from cognitive psychology, social psychology, and animal learning to contextualize the results. The authors evaluate the learned helplessness theory, suggesting that while it may not fully explain the cognitive distortions observed, a revised version focusing on motivational deficits could be more accurate. They also propose a motivational account derived from social psychology, where nondepressed individuals' self-serving biases lead to illusions of control to maintain or enhance self-esteem.

The article concludes by discussing the broader implications of the findings, including the potential for "sadder but wiser" effects in depressed individuals, the challenges of studying "appetitive helplessness," and the need for learning theories to account for the discrepancies between subjective and objective contingencies. The results underscore the complexity of human judgment and the influence of emotional and motivational factors on perceptions of control.
