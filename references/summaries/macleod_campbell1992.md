# Memory Accessibility and Probability Judgments: An Experimental Evaluation of the Availability Heuristic

MacLeod, C., Campbell, L.

__This study empirically validates Tversky and Kahneman’s availability heuristic by demonstrating that experimentally induced moods influence both memory accessibility and probability judgments, with stronger recall facilitation for mood-congruent events predicting higher perceived likelihood of similar future events.__

The article investigates the availability heuristic, a cognitive shortcut where individuals estimate the likelihood of future events based on how easily relevant instances come to mind. The authors, Colin MacLeod and Lynlee Campbell, argue that while previous research has shown correlations between memory accessibility and probability judgments, few studies have experimentally manipulated memory accessibility to test its causal influence on probability estimates. The study addresses three key predictions derived from the availability heuristic hypothesis: (1) a negative correlation between recall latency and perceived probability, (2) experimental manipulations of memory accessibility should alter probability judgments, and (3) the magnitude of changes in recall latency should correlate with changes in probability judgments.

The researchers conducted an experiment with 24 undergraduate students, using the Velten mood-induction procedure to manipulate participants' emotional states. Participants were exposed to either positive or negative self-descriptive statements to induce happy or sad moods, respectively. They were then presented with descriptions of positive and negative real-life events and asked to recall specific instances of these events while their recall latency was measured. Following recall, participants rated the likelihood of experiencing similar events in the next six months. The study found a significant negative correlation between recall latency and perceived probability, supporting the first prediction. The mood-induction procedure effectively altered participants' moods and selectively facilitated recall for mood-congruent events, with faster recall latencies for positive events in a happy mood and for negative events in a sad mood, confirming the second prediction. Most critically, the magnitude of changes in recall latency was directly correlated with changes in probability judgments, even when controlling for the magnitude of mood change, thus supporting the third prediction.

The results provide robust evidence for the availability heuristic, demonstrating that experimentally induced changes in memory accessibility directly influence probability judgments. The authors discuss the implications of their findings, noting that while previous studies have struggled to establish a causal link between memory accessibility and judgment, their use of recall latency as a measure of accessibility and the mood-induction procedure allowed for a clearer demonstration of this relationship. They also address potential criticisms, such as the transparency of the Velten procedure, and argue that demand characteristics are unlikely to account for the observed effects. The study concludes that the availability heuristic plays a significant role in probability judgments, with memory accessibility serving as a key mediator in how individuals assess the likelihood of future events. Future research is encouraged to explore the boundaries of this heuristic and its applicability to different types of judgments and memory measures.
