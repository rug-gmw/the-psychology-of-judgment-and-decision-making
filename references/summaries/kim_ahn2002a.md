# Clinical Psychologists’ Theory-Based Representations of Mental Disorders Predict Their Diagnostic Reasoning and Memory

Kim, N. S., & Ahn, W.

__This study demonstrates that clinical psychologists and trainees rely on personal causal theories of mental disorders—rather than the atheoretical symptom checklists prescribed by the DSM-IV—when diagnosing patients, recalling symptoms, and assessing symptom importance. Across five experiments, clinicians consistently weighted causally central symptoms more heavily in diagnostic reasoning, even when their theories diverged from peers or DSM guidelines.__

The article investigates how clinical psychologists and trainees conceptualize and diagnose mental disorders, challenging the assumption that they strictly adhere to the atheoretical symptom checklists outlined in the *Diagnostic and Statistical Manual of Mental Disorders (DSM-IV)*. Drawing on the theory-based model of categorization—which posits that concepts are organized around causal explanations rather than mere feature lists—the authors conducted five experiments to explore whether clinicians' personal theories about symptom relationships influence their diagnostic reasoning, memory, and symptom weighting.

The introduction establishes the theoretical framework, contrasting the DSM-IV’s atheoretical approach with the theory-based view of categorization. The DSM-IV, introduced in 1980, was designed to improve diagnostic reliability by providing symptom checklists without prescribing causal explanations. However, the theory-based model suggests that humans naturally seek causal explanations to organize information, raising the question of whether clinicians can—or do—suppress their intuitive theories when using the DSM. The authors hypothesize that clinicians' representations of mental disorders are structured around causal relationships between symptoms, and that these theories systematically influence their diagnostic judgments and memory.

The methods employed in the experiments were designed to measure clinicians' theories and their impact on reasoning. In Experiment 1, clinical psychologists and graduate students drew causal diagrams linking symptoms of five Axis I disorders (e.g., anorexia nervosa, schizophrenia) and rated the likelihood of diagnosing hypothetical patients with varying symptom profiles. The results revealed that participants were more likely to diagnose patients with causally central symptoms (those causing other symptoms in their theories) than those with peripheral or isolated symptoms, even when the number of DSM-IV diagnostic criteria was held constant. Additionally, participants recalled causally central symptoms more accurately, and their ratings of symptom centrality correlated with the causal centrality of those symptoms in their theories. These findings held for both experts and trainees, with no significant expertise effects observed.

Experiment 2 expanded on these results by allowing participants to draw any type of relational link between symptoms, not just causal ones. Despite this broader instruction, nearly all links drawn were causal or implied causal, reinforcing the centrality of causality in clinicians' theories. Participants again rated hypothetical patients with causally central symptoms as more typical of the disorder and were more likely to falsely recognize causally central symptoms in a memory task. The study also found significant agreement among clinicians' theories, suggesting that their causal models are not entirely idiosyncratic. Experiment 3 demonstrated that laypeople’s judgments of symptom importance aligned with clinicians' theories, indicating that these theories may reflect broader cultural knowledge.

Experiment 4 extended the investigation to personality disorders, which are known for lower diagnostic reliability. Even for schizotypal personality disorder—a disorder with little consensus among clinicians—participants relied on their personal theories to weight symptoms. Familiarity with the disorder emerged as a moderator: clinicians showed stronger theory-based effects for familiar disorders, whereas trainees exhibited the effect regardless of familiarity. Finally, Experiment 5 confirmed that laypeople’s symptom weightings for personality disorders also aligned with clinicians' theories, further supporting the idea that these theories are grounded in shared cultural knowledge.

The discussion synthesizes the findings, emphasizing that clinicians' diagnostic reasoning and memory are systematically influenced by their causal theories, despite the DSM-IV’s atheoretical guidelines. The authors argue that this theory-based reasoning is not inherently flawed, provided the theories themselves are valid. However, the lack of expertise effects suggests that clinicians may not refine their theories with experience, potentially due to the DSM’s atheoretical emphasis. The study also highlights the broader implications for conceptual thought, demonstrating that causal theories play a critical role in categorization, even in domains where atheoretical guidelines are prescribed.

The article concludes by addressing the relationship between these findings and the DSM system, cautioning that the study is descriptive rather than prescriptive. While the results suggest that clinicians naturally rely on theories, the authors do not advocate for revising the DSM without a clear, empirically validated theoretical framework. Instead, they propose that these findings could inform future research on the etiology of mental disorders, offering hypotheses grounded in clinicians' shared theories. The study underscores the importance of understanding how clinicians think, not just how they are instructed to think, and raises questions about the interplay between theory-based and similarity-based reasoning in clinical practice.
