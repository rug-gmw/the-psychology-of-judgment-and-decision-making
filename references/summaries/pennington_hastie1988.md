# Explanation-Based Decision Making: Effects of Memory Structure on Judgment

Pennington, N., Hastie, R.

__This article presents empirical support for an explanation-based model of decision making, specifically applied to juror decisions. The authors demonstrate that jurors spontaneously construct narrative stories to interpret evidence, and these stories directly influence verdict choices, perceived importance of evidence, and confidence in decisions.__

The article introduces an explanation-based decision-making model, arguing that individuals evaluate complex evidence by constructing causal explanations—mental representations that integrate facts with prior knowledge. In the context of juror decisions, this model is termed the *Story Model*, where jurors organize trial evidence into coherent narratives structured around causal and intentional relationships. The authors propose three key claims: (1) jurors spontaneously construct and store these explanatory stories in memory, (2) the chosen verdict is determined by the constructed story, and (3) the coherence of the story influences confidence in the decision. The research focuses on criminal trials, where evidence is often fragmented, temporally disordered, and interdependent, making narrative construction a natural strategy for comprehension.

The article details two experiments designed to test these claims. *Experiment 1* examined whether jurors spontaneously construct story-like representations of evidence without explicit prompting. Using a recognition memory task, the authors found that subjects were more likely to correctly recognize evidence items that aligned with their chosen verdict’s story and falsely recognize plausible but unpresented story-consistent events. Additionally, the perceived importance of evidence items was predicted by their role in the story, particularly their causal connectedness and placement within the story’s central episode. These results suggest that jurors actively construct and rely on narrative explanations during decision making, rather than merely aggregating evidence in a piecemeal fashion.

*Experiment 2* manipulated the order of evidence presentation to test whether story coherence causally influences verdict decisions. Subjects were more likely to convict when prosecution evidence was presented in a coherent story order and defense evidence was disordered, and vice versa. Story order also affected perceptions of evidence strength and confidence in decisions, with greater coherence leading to higher confidence. However, confidence was highest when both prosecution and defense stories were coherent, suggesting that clarity of competing narratives also plays a role in decision certainty. These findings challenge traditional decision-making models, such as Bayesian or linear integration models, which do not account for the role of narrative structure in evidence evaluation.

The discussion emphasizes the generality of the explanation-based model beyond legal decisions, proposing its applicability to other complex domains like medicine, diplomacy, and engineering, where decision makers must integrate fragmented information into causal explanations. The authors argue that while algebraic models may suffice for simpler, independent evidence, narrative-based reasoning is essential for tasks involving interdependent, incomplete, or ambiguous information. The article concludes that the Story Model provides a robust framework for understanding how jurors—and potentially other decision makers—interpret evidence, make choices, and assess confidence in their judgments.
