# Decision-making Biases in Children and Early Adolescents: Exploratory Studies

Baron, J., Granato, L., Spranca, M., & Teubal, E.

__This study investigates decision-making biases in children and early adolescents, revealing that many fail to consider probabilities, precedent-setting effects, or frequency information in their choices. While some biases like omission bias and sunk-cost effects were observed, age-related improvements were inconsistent, suggesting that formal education may not adequately address these cognitive gaps.__

The article presents three exploratory studies examining decision-making biases in children and adolescents, focusing on whether they consider key factors like probabilities, consequences, and precedent-setting in their choices. The research was motivated by the observation that most studies on decision-making biases have focused on adults, leaving a gap in understanding how these biases develop in younger populations. The authors hypothesized that biases might either decrease with age (as children learn to avoid overgeneralizations), remain stable (if schooling does not address these skills), or even increase (if biases stem from learned heuristics that children later unlearn).

Study 1 compared two groups of children aged 7 to 15: a gifted group and an underprivileged group. Participants were presented with vignettes testing their attention to precedent-setting, probability, frequency, sunk costs, omission bias, and actively open-minded thinking. Results showed that children often neglected precedent-setting and probability considerations, with few age-related improvements. The gifted group performed better on some items (e.g., probability dominance and frequency) but worse on omission bias, suggesting that cognitive ability alone does not uniformly reduce biases. Notably, most children failed to spontaneously consider the long-term implications of their choices, such as habit formation or the precedent set by allowing exceptions.

Study 2 expanded the investigation to younger children (ages 5 to 12) in a private school, focusing on actively open-minded thinking, precedent setting, sunk costs, and omission bias. The study also examined "myside bias," where children erroneously classified arguments as supporting their preexisting views. While myside bias was consistently observed, age effects were minimal, indicating that even older children struggled to evaluate arguments objectively. Precedent-setting and sunk-cost effects were also prevalent, with no clear developmental trends. Omission bias, however, appeared less pronounced in this sample, possibly due to the school's emphasis on moral responsibility.

Study 3 further explored omission bias in second and seventh graders, using additional scenarios to test whether children distinguished between harmful acts and omissions. Unlike the previous studies, this one found a significant age effect: older children were more likely to judge acts and omissions as equally wrong, often justifying their responses by referencing equal intentions. Younger children, by contrast, tended to view acts as worse than omissions and justified their judgments based on outcomes rather than intentions.

The general discussion highlights the persistence of decision-making biases into early adolescence, suggesting that current educational practices may not adequately address these cognitive gaps. The authors argue that many children lack the skills to consider probabilities, frequencies, or precedent-setting effects—skills critical for making informed decisions about health, safety, and public policy. While some biases, like omission bias, showed age-related improvements, others remained stable, indicating that formal education may not inherently foster these abilities. The findings underscore the need for targeted instruction to help children develop more rational decision-making strategies.
