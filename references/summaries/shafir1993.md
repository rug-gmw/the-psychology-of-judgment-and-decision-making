# Choosing versus rejecting: Why some options are both better and worse than others

Shafir, E.

__This article demonstrates that people’s preferences depend on whether they are choosing or rejecting options, with positive features weighted more heavily in choosing and negative features in rejecting. This "compatibility principle" leads to counterintuitive results where an "enriched" option (with more pros and cons) is both chosen and rejected more often than an "impoverished" one.__

The article challenges classical decision theory’s assumption of stable, well-defined preferences by showing how preferences are constructed during decision-making. The author, Eldar Shafir, introduces the *compatibility principle*, which posits that the weight of an option’s attributes depends on their alignment with the decision task. For example, positive features (pros) are more salient when choosing, while negative features (cons) dominate when rejecting. This principle builds on prior work in judgment and decision-making, such as Tversky and Kahneman’s framing effects and Slovic et al.’s scale compatibility, which showed that attributes matching the response scale (e.g., monetary payoffs in pricing gambles) are weighted more heavily.

Shafir draws an analogy to Tversky’s contrast model of similarity, where common features loom larger in similarity judgments and distinctive features in dissimilarity judgments. Similarly, in decision-making, the act of choosing emphasizes pros, while rejecting emphasizes cons. This leads to the prediction that an "enriched" option—one with more pros *and* more cons—can be both chosen over and rejected in favor of an "impoverished" option with fewer pros and cons. The article tests this prediction through a series of experiments involving binary and nonbinary choices, such as awarding custody in a divorce case, selecting vacation spots, or choosing between lotteries.

In one key experiment, subjects were asked to award or deny custody to one of two parents. Parent B, the enriched option, had compelling reasons for both awarding (e.g., high income, close relationship with the child) and denying custody (e.g., health problems, frequent travel). As predicted, Parent B was both awarded custody (64%) and denied custody (55%) more often than Parent A, the impoverished option. Similar patterns emerged in other scenarios, such as choosing between vacation spots or ice-cream flavors, where the enriched option’s pros dominated in choosing and its cons in rejecting.

The article also explores whether these effects persist in nonbinary choices. For example, when subjects were asked to choose one lottery from three or reject two, the enriched lottery was both chosen and rejected more often than expected. The framing of the task—whether it was perceived as choosing or rejecting—also influenced outcomes. For instance, asking subjects to choose five out of six lotteries was often reframed as rejecting one, favoring impoverished options, while rejecting five was reframed as choosing one, favoring enriched options.

Shafir addresses alternative explanations, such as confusion or anticipated regret, and finds that the compatibility principle best accounts for the data. He also notes that people tend to frame decisions as choices rather than rejections, which may bias them toward focusing on pros. This has real-world implications, such as in medical decision-making, where doctors may emphasize treatment benefits while courts focus on risks, or in politics, where voters may prioritize avoiding bad candidates over selecting good ones.

The article concludes by discussing the broader implications of these findings for rational choice theory, which assumes procedure invariance (i.e., equivalent methods of eliciting preferences should yield the same results). The observed discrepancies between choosing and rejecting violate this principle, suggesting that preferences are not fixed but constructed in the moment. Shafir argues that this lability of preferences raises questions about how to assess "true" preferences and highlights the importance of considering both pros and cons in decision-making. The findings also suggest that enriched options—those with more reasons for and against them—may be overrepresented in both winning and losing positions, depending on the decision frame.
