# Stages of Moral Development as a Basis for Moral Education

Kohlberg, L.

__This article argues that moral education should be grounded in universal, developmentally sequenced stages of moral reasoning rather than cultural relativism or arbitrary virtue inculcation. Kohlberg presents empirical evidence for his six-stage model of moral development, critiques alternative approaches like the "hidden curriculum," and proposes a cognitive-developmental framework for fostering principled moral judgment in schools.__

Lawrence Kohlberg’s article challenges prevailing approaches to moral education by advocating for a developmental, philosophically grounded framework. He begins by critiquing the "equal-time" perspective in moral education, which often reduces the discussion to cultural relativism or superficial consensus. Kohlberg argues that only two disciplines—developmental social psychology and moral philosophy—offer meaningful generalizations about moral education. He laments the fragmentation of these fields in recent decades, contrasting it with the unified approaches of earlier thinkers like Dewey and Durkheim, who integrated psychological and philosophical insights into their theories of moral development.

Kohlberg then critiques two dominant but flawed approaches to moral education: the "hidden curriculum" and the "bag of virtues." The hidden curriculum, he explains, refers to the implicit moral lessons conveyed through classroom management and institutional norms. While some sociologists, like Durkheim, justify this approach as necessary for socializing children into societal values, Kohlberg argues that it often reduces morality to conformity and collective discipline, as exemplified by Soviet moral education practices. He contends that such approaches prioritize loyalty to the group over individual moral reasoning, leading to morally arbitrary outcomes. Similarly, the "bag of virtues" approach, which seeks to inculcate specific moral traits like honesty or politeness, fails because it ignores the developmental and cognitive dimensions of moral judgment. Kohlberg illustrates this with an example of a teacher who moralizes about politeness without addressing deeper issues of human dignity.

Central to Kohlberg’s argument is the rejection of ethical relativism, the idea that moral values are culturally determined and arbitrary. He presents empirical evidence from cross-cultural studies to demonstrate that moral development follows a universal sequence of stages, each representing a more differentiated and integrated form of moral reasoning. These stages, outlined in Appendix 1, range from preconventional morality (based on punishment and reward) to conventional morality (based on social norms and authority) and finally to post-conventional or principled morality (based on universal ethical principles like justice). Kohlberg’s research shows that while the rate and endpoint of development vary across cultures, the sequence of stages is invariant, suggesting that moral reasoning is not merely a product of cultural conditioning but reflects deeper cognitive and structural processes.

Kohlberg’s cognitive-developmental theory posits that moral development is driven by the child’s active engagement with moral dilemmas and opportunities for role-taking. Unlike theories that emphasize internalization of external norms, Kohlberg argues that moral growth occurs through cognitive conflict and the restructuring of moral understanding. He highlights the role of role-taking—empathizing with others’ perspectives—as a key mechanism for moral development. This process is not merely emotional but involves cognitive differentiation and integration, leading to increasingly universal and principled moral judgments. For example, children at higher stages of development recognize the value of human life as independent of social status or instrumental utility, reflecting a more mature and autonomous moral perspective.

Philosophically, Kohlberg aligns his theory with formalist traditions in moral philosophy, such as those of Kant and Hare, which emphasize the universal and prescriptive nature of moral judgments. He argues that only the highest stages of moral reasoning (stages 5 and 6) embody these formal characteristics, as they are based on principles of justice and respect for human rights rather than conformity to authority or social norms. Stage 6, in particular, represents a fully autonomous and principled morality, where individuals act based on self-chosen ethical principles that are universalizable and reversible. Kohlberg contends that justice is the foundational moral principle, as it resolves conflicts between competing claims in a way that respects the equal dignity of all individuals.

Kohlberg addresses the "naturalistic fallacy"—the idea that one cannot derive an "ought" from an "is"—by arguing that his developmental model provides a bridge between descriptive psychology and normative ethics. While science cannot prescribe moral ideals, it can identify universal patterns of moral development that align with philosophically defensible principles. He suggests that educators should aim to stimulate moral development by exposing children to moral dilemmas and higher-stage reasoning, thereby fostering their capacity for principled moral judgment. This approach respects the child’s autonomy and avoids the pitfalls of indoctrination, as it encourages children to engage with moral issues on their own terms.

The article also explores the relationship between moral judgment and moral action, arguing that principled moral reasoning correlates with more ethical behavior. Kohlberg presents studies showing that individuals at higher stages of moral development are less likely to cheat, more likely to resist unjust authority, and more inclined to engage in civil disobedience when necessary. However, he acknowledges that moral education must also address the broader moral atmosphere of the school, encouraging participation and justice in real-life contexts. He advocates for schools that foster democratic decision-making and respect for individual rights, as these environments provide the necessary conditions for moral growth.

In conclusion, Kohlberg’s article offers a comprehensive vision for moral education that integrates psychological research, philosophical analysis, and practical pedagogy. By focusing on the development of principled moral reasoning, he provides a framework that transcends cultural relativism and arbitrary virtue inculcation, offering a universal and developmentally grounded approach to fostering moral maturity in children.
