# A Review of the FBI's Handling of the Brandon Mayfield Case

U.S. Department of Justice, Office of the Inspector General

__This report examines the FBI’s misidentification of Brandon Mayfield as a suspect in the 2004 Madrid train bombings, analyzing the causes of the fingerprint error, the role of Mayfield’s religion, the use of the Patriot Act, and the broader implications for forensic and investigative practices.__

The Office of the Inspector General (OIG) conducted a comprehensive review of the FBI’s erroneous identification of Brandon Mayfield, an Oregon attorney, as the source of a fingerprint linked to the 2004 Madrid train bombings. The misidentification led to Mayfield’s arrest as a material witness, invasive surveillance, and a two-week detention before Spanish authorities identified the true source of the print—an Algerian national. The report explores the technical and procedural failures in the FBI Laboratory’s fingerprint analysis, the influence of Mayfield’s Muslim faith on the investigation, and the legal and operational consequences of the FBI’s reliance on the USA PATRIOT Act.

The investigation began after the Spanish National Police (SNP) recovered fingerprints from a bag of detonators connected to the Madrid attacks and requested assistance from the FBI Laboratory. Using the Integrated Automated Fingerprint Identification System (IAFIS), the FBI generated a list of potential matches, ultimately identifying Mayfield’s fingerprint as a match to Latent Fingerprint Number 17 (LFP 17). This conclusion was verified by multiple FBI examiners, despite the absence of any direct evidence linking Mayfield to the bombings. The FBI’s confidence in the identification persisted even after the SNP reported a "negativo" result on April 13, 2004, and only reversed its position after the SNP definitively matched the print to Ouhnane Daoud. The OIG’s investigation sought to determine the root causes of the misidentification, assess the FBI’s response to the error, and evaluate whether Mayfield’s religion or the Patriot Act influenced the case.

The OIG identified several critical flaws in the FBI’s fingerprint examination process. First, the unusual similarity between Mayfield’s and Daoud’s fingerprints—particularly in the arrangement of minutiae points—misled examiners into overlooking subtle but significant differences. Second, the examiners engaged in "circular reasoning," interpreting ambiguous features in LFP 17 to align with Mayfield’s known prints rather than objectively assessing the latent print. Third, the FBI placed undue reliance on "Level 3" details—extremely small features like pores and ridge edges—which are highly variable and unreliable for identification. Fourth, the examiners failed to adequately explain discrepancies between LFP 17 and Mayfield’s prints, violating the FBI’s "one discrepancy rule," which requires a valid explanation for any difference in appearance. Finally, the FBI’s overconfidence in its own expertise and the IAFIS system prevented it from seriously reconsidering the identification after the SNP’s negative report. The OIG also dismissed several explanations offered by the FBI, such as the quality of the digital image or lack of access to the original evidence, finding them insufficient to justify the error.

Beyond the technical failures, the OIG examined whether Mayfield’s Muslim faith influenced the investigation. While the FBI examiners were unaware of Mayfield’s religion at the time of the initial identification, the OIG concluded that his faith, along with his representation of a convicted terrorist and his contacts with suspected extremists, may have contributed to the FBI’s reluctance to revisit the identification after the SNP’s doubts emerged. The report also assessed the FBI’s use of the Patriot Act, particularly its provisions allowing expanded surveillance and information sharing. Although the FBI likely would have obtained Foreign Intelligence Surveillance Act (FISA) authorization even without the Patriot Act, the law’s removal of the "wall" between intelligence and criminal investigations facilitated broader dissemination of information about Mayfield, amplifying the consequences of the misidentification.

The OIG’s review of the FBI’s field investigation found no evidence that Mayfield’s religion was the primary factor in his targeting, but it did note that his faith was considered a "mildly corroborating factor" by some investigators. The affidavits submitted to the court in support of the material witness and search warrants contained inaccuracies and ambiguous language, particularly regarding the SNP’s position on the fingerprint match. The report also addressed Mayfield’s conditions of confinement, which, while not unlawful, included communication failures that led to unnecessary risks, such as the inadvertent disclosure of his alias to the media.

In response to the error, the FBI Laboratory implemented several reforms, including revised verification procedures, expanded documentation requirements, and efforts to develop more objective identification standards. The OIG endorsed many of these changes but recommended additional measures, such as stricter criteria for using Level 3 details, blind verification processes, and a review of prior cases involving single latent prints. The report concluded that while no intentional misconduct occurred, systemic issues in the FBI Laboratory and investigative practices contributed to the misidentification, underscoring the need for ongoing reforms to prevent similar errors in the future.
