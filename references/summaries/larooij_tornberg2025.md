# Can We Fix Social Media? Testing Prosocial Interventions using Generative Social Simulation

Larooij, M., Törnberg, P.

__This study uses generative social simulation—combining large language models with agent-based modeling—to test whether platform design interventions can mitigate social media’s core dysfunctions, such as polarization and attention inequality. Despite testing six proposed solutions, the authors find only modest improvements, suggesting these issues may be structurally embedded in how engagement and network growth interact.__

The article introduces a novel methodological approach to studying social media’s societal harms, particularly its role in fostering polarization, elite influence concentration, and the amplification of divisive content. Traditional research has struggled to evaluate counterfactual scenarios—such as how discourse might change under alternative algorithms—due to limited access to platform data. To address this, the authors employ *generative social simulation*, embedding large language models (LLMs) within agent-based models (ABMs) to create a synthetic yet socially rich platform. This method allows them to model both structural network dynamics and culturally nuanced interactions, offering a controlled environment to test interventions that cannot be implemented in real-world settings.

The study constructs a minimal social media platform where LLM-powered agents—each assigned distinct personas based on real-world demographic and ideological data—can post, repost, and follow others. Despite its simplicity, the model reliably reproduces three well-documented pathologies of social media: (1) partisan echo chambers, where users predominantly follow and engage with like-minded others; (2) extreme concentration of visibility, with a small elite capturing most attention; and (3) a "social media prism" that disproportionately amplifies politically extreme voices. These outcomes emerge not from complex algorithms but from the feedback loop between reactive engagement (e.g., reposting emotionally charged content) and network formation, where visibility begets further visibility.

The authors then test six platform-level interventions designed to promote prosocial outcomes, including chronological feeds, algorithms that downplay dominant voices, boosting out-partisan content, prioritizing "bridging" attributes (e.g., empathy and reasoning), and hiding engagement metrics or user biographies. The results are sobering: while some interventions yield modest improvements—such as chronological feeds reducing attention inequality or bridging attributes slightly weakening partisan amplification—none fully disrupt the underlying mechanisms. Worse, some interventions exacerbate problems they aim to solve, such as chronological feeds increasing the correlation between political extremism and influence. The findings suggest that these dysfunctions are deeply rooted in the architecture of social media, particularly the self-reinforcing cycle where engagement-driven visibility shapes network growth.

The discussion emphasizes that the study’s minimal model reveals how structural and cultural dynamics interact to produce harmful outcomes, even in the absence of engagement-optimized algorithms. The authors argue that meaningful reform may require rethinking the foundational design of social media platforms, rather than relying on incremental tweaks. They also highlight the potential of generative social simulation as a tool for social scientific inquiry, while acknowledging its limitations, including computational constraints, challenges in validation, and the "black box" nature of LLM-based agents. The work opens avenues for further research into how prosocial design can coexist with user engagement and satisfaction, as well as the broader implications of using AI-driven simulations to study complex social systems.
