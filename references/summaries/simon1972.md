# Theories of Bounded Rationality

Simon, H. A.

__This article explores Herbert Simon’s concept of bounded rationality, arguing that human decision-making is constrained by cognitive limits, incomplete information, and environmental complexity, leading to satisficing rather than optimizing behavior.__

Herbert Simon’s *Theories of Bounded Rationality* challenges classical economic and decision-making models that assume perfect rationality, where actors maximize utility or profit with complete information and unlimited computational capacity. Instead, Simon argues that real-world decision-making is shaped by inherent cognitive and environmental constraints, forcing individuals and organizations to adopt simplified strategies. The article begins by distinguishing between normative and descriptive theories of rationality, emphasizing that while classical models prescribe how decisions *should* be made, bounded rationality seeks to describe how they *are* made in practice. Simon blurs the line between individual and organizational rationality, noting that both face similar limitations—goal conflicts, incomplete information, and computational complexity—though organizations may compound these challenges through coordination and communication barriers.

The core of Simon’s argument lies in his critique of the classical theory of the firm, which assumes profit maximization under perfect knowledge of demand and cost functions. He demonstrates how this model breaks down when faced with uncertainty, incomplete information about alternatives, or computational infeasibility. For instance, in chess—a microcosm for real-world decision-making—game theory’s optimal strategy requires evaluating an astronomically large number of possible moves (on the order of 10¹²⁰), rendering it practically useless. Instead, chess players rely on heuristics, satisficing by generating a small subset of plausible moves, evaluating them dynamically, and selecting the first satisfactory option rather than the absolute best. This approach, Simon argues, is not a flaw but a necessary adaptation to bounded rationality, where aspiration levels guide search until a "good enough" solution is found.

The article extends this framework to design and management science, where bounded rationality similarly shapes problem-solving. In design, whether of chess strategies or engineering projects, alternatives are not given but must be discovered through iterative search, often in simplified "planning spaces" that abstract away complexity. Management science, too, grapples with these limits; while techniques like linear programming optimize within constrained models, real-world problems—such as warehouse location—often require heuristic programs that build solutions incrementally, balancing computational feasibility with flexibility. Simon highlights how satisficing and optimizing are not mutually exclusive but exist on a spectrum, with satisficing often proving more practical in complex, uncertain environments. The conclusion underscores the growing toolkit of decision-making models that acknowledge human limitations, offering more tractable ways to navigate the gap between bounded capabilities and the daunting complexity of real-world problems.
