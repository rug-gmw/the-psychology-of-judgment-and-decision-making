# When Choice is Demotivating: Can One Desire Too Much of a Good Thing?

Iyengar, S. S., & Lepper, M. R.

__This article challenges the assumption that more choices always lead to greater motivation and satisfaction, demonstrating through three experiments that an excess of options can actually reduce engagement, performance, and subsequent contentment.__

The study by Sheena Iyengar and Mark Lepper investigates a counterintuitive phenomenon: while psychological and economic theories often assume that greater choice enhances motivation and well-being, their research reveals that an overabundance of options can have the opposite effect. The authors begin by outlining the prevailing belief in modern society that "more is better," citing examples from consumer markets to life decisions, where an expansive array of choices is presumed to empower individuals. They acknowledge the robust body of research supporting the benefits of choice, such as increased intrinsic motivation, perceived control, and life satisfaction, but question whether these findings hold when the number of options becomes overwhelming.

The article presents three experiments conducted in both field and laboratory settings to test the "choice overload hypothesis," which posits that while extensive choices may initially seem appealing, they can ultimately demotivate individuals. In the first study, shoppers at an upscale grocery store were more likely to stop at a tasting booth displaying 24 varieties of jam than one with only 6, yet they were far less likely to purchase jam after sampling from the larger selection. This pattern suggested that while extensive choices attracted initial interest, they hindered follow-through and satisfaction.

The second study extended these findings to an educational context, where college students were given the option to write an extra-credit essay from either 6 or 30 potential topics. Students in the limited-choice condition were more likely to complete the assignment and produced higher-quality essays, reinforcing the idea that fewer options can enhance both motivation and performance. The authors addressed potential confounds, such as prior student performance and awareness of additional options, to ensure the robustness of their results.

The third study moved to a controlled laboratory setting, where participants chose chocolates from either a limited (6 options) or extensive (30 options) array. While those in the extensive-choice condition reported greater initial enjoyment of the decision-making process, they also found it more difficult and frustrating. After sampling their chosen chocolates, they expressed less satisfaction and more regret than those in the limited-choice group. Additionally, participants in the extensive-choice condition were less likely to opt for chocolates over cash as compensation, mirroring the purchasing behavior observed in the first study. The inclusion of a no-choice control group further highlighted that the demotivating effects of extensive choices were not merely due to a lack of choice but rather an excess of it.

The authors explore potential mechanisms underlying choice overload, such as the use of simplifying heuristics in complex decision-making or heightened feelings of responsibility and regret when faced with too many options. While their data did not support the hypothesis that individuals in extensive-choice contexts were more likely to "satisfice" (settle for a "good enough" option), they found strong evidence that such contexts increased both enjoyment and frustration, leading to greater dissatisfaction and regret. The discussion broadens to consider the implications of choice overload in high-stakes decisions, such as medical treatments or financial investments, where the perceived costs of making the "wrong" choice may amplify demotivation.

The article concludes by reflecting on the broader societal implications of choice overload, noting that even as individuals gain more freedom, they may become increasingly dependent on institutions or experts to navigate overwhelming options. The authors suggest that future research should examine how factors like expertise, the identity of the choice recipient, and the affective dimensions of decision-making influence the experience of choice overload. Ultimately, their findings challenge the assumption that unlimited choice is universally beneficial, offering a nuanced perspective on the psychological costs of abundance.
