# Flynn Effect and Its Reversal Are Both Environmentally Caused

Bratsberg, B., & Rogeberg, O.

__This study demonstrates that both the historical rise in IQ scores (the Flynn effect) and its recent reversal in Norway are driven entirely by environmental factors, not genetic changes or family composition shifts.__

The article investigates the causes behind the Flynn effect—the observed increase in population IQ scores throughout the 20th century—and its subsequent reversal in several Western countries. Using comprehensive administrative data from Norway, covering male birth cohorts from 1962 to 1991, the authors analyze cognitive ability scores from military conscription tests. Their central question is whether the Flynn effect and its reversal can be explained by within-family variation, which would rule out genetic or between-family environmental explanations such as dysgenic fertility or immigration. The study categorizes existing hypotheses about the Flynn effect into those that allow for within-family variation and those that do not, with the latter including prominent explanations like genetic decline due to lower fertility among high-IQ individuals.

The authors employ a fixed-effects model to estimate within-family trends in IQ scores, controlling for factors shared by siblings, such as parental education and family size. Their results show that within-family variation fully recovers the observed Flynn effect, its turning point around 1975, and the subsequent decline in IQ scores. This finding is robust even after accounting for selection bias in conscription testing, which disproportionately excluded lower-IQ individuals in later cohorts. The study uses a Bayesian model to correct for this bias, confirming that the within-family estimates align closely with the population-level trends. The authors conclude that the Flynn effect and its reversal are driven by environmental factors that vary within families, such as changes in education, nutrition, health, or media exposure, rather than genetic or compositional shifts.

The discussion emphasizes the implications of these findings for existing theories. The study effectively rules out dysgenic fertility and immigration as primary causes of the negative Flynn effect, as these factors would not produce within-family variation. The authors also note that the magnitude of the IQ decline is too large to be explained by genetic changes alone, further undermining the dysgenic hypothesis. While the study cannot pinpoint the specific environmental causes, it highlights the need to focus on factors that affect siblings differently, such as differences in schooling, media consumption, or health over time. The results challenge the conclusions of recent reviews that attribute the reversal of the Flynn effect primarily to genetic or immigration-related factors, instead pointing to a more complex interplay of environmental influences.
