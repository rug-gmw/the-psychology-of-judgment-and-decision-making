# The Group as a Polarizer of Attitudes

Moscovici, S., & Zavalloni, M.

__This study challenges the long-held assumption that groups moderate individual attitudes, demonstrating instead that group discussions lead to more extreme positions—a phenomenon termed "polarization effect." The authors argue that this shift stems from normative commitment rather than mere averaging or risk-taking behaviors, extending the effect beyond risk-related decisions to broader attitudinal domains.__

The article begins by critiquing the prevailing view in social psychology that group interactions tend to produce convergence or moderation of individual opinions, as seen in classic studies by Allport (1924) and Sherif (1935). These studies suggested that groups average out extreme positions, fostering compromise and social cohesion. However, Stoner’s (1961) discovery of the "risky shift"—where groups adopt riskier decisions than individuals—challenged this assumption, raising questions about whether polarization is limited to risk-taking or reflects a broader group dynamic. Moscovici and Zavalloni seek to resolve this tension by investigating whether group discussions polarize attitudes more generally, not just in risk-related contexts.

The authors review two dominant models explaining the risky shift: the *social facilitation model*, which attributes the shift to diffusion of responsibility in groups, and the *normative model*, which posits that individuals adjust their positions to align with culturally valued traits (e.g., risk-taking). However, neither model fully accounts for the phenomenon. For instance, experiments by Bateson (1966) and Flanders and Thistlethwaite (1967) showed that individuals, even without group discussion, exhibit risky shifts when familiarizing themselves with decision dilemmas or preparing arguments. This suggests that the shift is not solely a product of group interaction but may stem from deeper cognitive and motivational processes. Moscovici and Zavalloni propose an alternative explanation: *normative commitment*. They argue that when individuals engage deeply with an issue—whether through group discussion, argument preparation, or familiarization—they become more invested in their positions, making certain dimensions of the issue more salient. This commitment, rather than emotional fusion or mere information exchange, drives polarization. The direction of the shift depends on the dominant values or attitudes in the group or society, which act as selective filters for the arguments considered.

To test this hypothesis, the authors conducted three experiments with 140 male secondary school students in Paris. In Experiments I and II, groups discussed attitudes toward General de Gaulle and Americans, respectively, using Likert-type scales. In Experiment III, groups judged the favorability of statements about de Gaulle on a Thurstone-like scale. In all cases, subjects first rated items individually, then discussed them to reach a group consensus, and finally rated them individually again. The results consistently showed that group consensus ratings were significantly more extreme than the average of pre-discussion individual ratings. This polarization effect persisted in post-consensus individual ratings, suggesting that subjects internalized the group’s more extreme positions. The effect was stronger for opinion scales (where subjects expressed personal attitudes) than for judgment scales (where they evaluated statements objectively), supporting the idea that normative commitment drives polarization. Additionally, polarization was more pronounced in groups with greater initial disagreement, indicating that conflict and cognitive effort amplify the effect. The authors also found that groups used fewer response categories than individuals, further reflecting a sharpening of positions.

The discussion situates these findings within broader theoretical debates. Moscovici and Zavalloni argue that polarization is not a pathological deviation but a fundamental aspect of group dynamics, particularly when issues are meaningful or ego-involving. They challenge the assumption that groups inherently moderate attitudes, proposing instead that polarization is a norm when individuals are committed to their positions. The study extends the risky shift phenomenon to non-risk domains, suggesting that normative commitment—rooted in affective involvement, cognitive effort, and certainty—is the key mechanism. The authors conclude by calling for further research to disentangle the components of commitment and to explore how group interactions radicalize attitudes in real-world settings, such as social movements. Their work reframes group decision-making as a process that can both moderate and amplify individual positions, depending on the context and depth of engagement.
