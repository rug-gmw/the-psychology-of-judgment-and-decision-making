# Bayesian Models of Cognition

Griffiths, T. L., Kemp, C., & Tenenbaum, J. B.

__This chapter presents Bayesian models of cognition as a framework for understanding how humans make inductive inferences under uncertainty, integrating probabilistic reasoning with structured knowledge representations.__

A comprehensive summary of this chapter begins by introducing Bayesian models of cognition as a principled approach to understanding human learning and inference. The authors argue that many cognitive processes can be viewed as problems of induction—how to generalize from limited, noisy data to form rich, abstract models of the world. Bayesian inference provides a normative framework for addressing these problems, specifying how rational agents should update their beliefs in light of new evidence. The chapter highlights the increasing prominence of Bayesian models across various domains of cognitive science, including perception, language acquisition, causal reasoning, and social cognition, illustrating how these models can capture the complexity of human thought.

The core of the chapter is structured around key concepts and techniques in Bayesian modeling. It starts with the basics of Bayesian inference, explaining Bayes' rule and its application to hypothesis testing, parameter estimation, and model selection. The authors use simple examples, such as coin flipping, to illustrate how Bayesian methods combine prior knowledge with observed data to compute posterior probabilities. They emphasize the distinction between parameter estimation (e.g., inferring the bias of a coin) and model selection (e.g., deciding whether a coin is fair or biased), showing how Bayesian approaches naturally handle both tasks while accounting for uncertainty and complexity.

The chapter then introduces graphical models, a powerful tool for representing and reasoning about probabilistic dependencies among variables. Directed graphical models, or Bayesian networks, are particularly emphasized for their ability to encode causal relationships and support efficient inference. The authors demonstrate how these models can be applied to problems like causal induction, where learners infer causal structures from observed contingencies. They present a detailed case study on elemental causal induction, comparing Bayesian models to traditional psychological models like ΔP and causal power. The Bayesian approach, particularly the "causal support" model, is shown to provide a superior fit to human judgments by integrating prior knowledge about causal mechanisms with observed data.

Next, the chapter explores hierarchical Bayesian models, which extend the Bayesian framework to address the acquisition of prior knowledge itself. These models allow learners to infer higher-level abstractions (e.g., the parameters of a prior distribution or the structure of a domain) from lower-level data. The authors illustrate this with examples from property induction and feature variability learning, showing how hierarchical models can explain how humans acquire abstract knowledge, such as the shape bias in categorization or taxonomic structures for biological kinds. They argue that hierarchical Bayesian models provide a unified account of how prior knowledge is both used and learned, bridging the gap between innate and learned constraints on cognition.

The chapter also introduces Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) methods as a general-purpose tool for approximate Bayesian inference in complex models. MCMC methods, such as Gibbs sampling, enable researchers to sample from posterior distributions that are otherwise intractable, making it possible to apply Bayesian models to real-world cognitive phenomena. The authors demonstrate the use of MCMC in inferring semantic representations from text corpora, using topic models as an example. Topic models represent word meanings in terms of probabilistic distributions over latent topics, and MCMC allows these topics to be learned from large document collections. This example highlights the flexibility of Bayesian models in capturing structured representations of meaning and their potential to outperform traditional semantic models like Latent Semantic Analysis.

Finally, the chapter concludes by reflecting on the broader implications of Bayesian models for cognitive science. The authors argue that Bayesian models offer a unique synthesis of structured knowledge representations and statistical learning, providing a framework for understanding how humans combine prior knowledge with observed data to make inductive inferences. They emphasize the potential of these models to address long-standing debates in cognitive science, such as the tension between symbolic and statistical approaches or the role of innate versus learned knowledge. The chapter closes with a call for further research to explore the limits and possibilities of Bayesian models in capturing the richness of human cognition.
