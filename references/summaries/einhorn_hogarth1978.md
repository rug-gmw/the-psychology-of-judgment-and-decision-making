# Confidence in Judgment: Persistence of the Illusion of Validity

Einhorn, H. J., Hogarth, R. M.

__This article explores why people maintain high confidence in their judgments despite evidence of their fallibility, arguing that task structure, feedback mechanisms, and cognitive biases prevent individuals from recognizing their errors.__

The article investigates the paradox between the documented fallibility of human judgment and the unwavering confidence individuals often exhibit in their decision-making abilities. The authors, Hillel J. Einhorn and Robin M. Hogarth, begin by reviewing extensive research demonstrating the limitations of both expert and non-expert judgment across various domains, including clinical prediction, probability estimation, and decision-making. Despite these findings, people consistently display overconfidence in their judgments, a phenomenon the authors term the "illusion of validity." This contradiction raises critical questions about how individuals learn from experience and why they fail to adjust their confidence in light of evidence suggesting their judgments are flawed.

To address this puzzle, the authors develop a theoretical framework that examines the interplay between judgment, action, and feedback within specific task environments. They argue that the structure of judgmental tasks—where decisions lead to actions, and outcomes are observed only for those actions—creates a feedback loop that reinforces confidence. Specifically, individuals typically receive outcome feedback only for the actions they take, not for the alternatives they reject. This selective exposure to outcomes makes it difficult to gather disconfirming evidence, as people rarely observe the consequences of actions they did not choose. For example, a manager who hires candidates based on their perceived potential will only see the performance of those hired, not those rejected, thereby limiting their ability to assess the accuracy of their judgments comprehensively.

The article delves into the cognitive processes underlying this phenomenon, particularly the tendency to focus on confirming evidence while ignoring disconfirming information. Drawing on research by Wason (1960, 1968) and others, the authors highlight how people struggle to test hypotheses by seeking disconfirming evidence, even when trained in scientific reasoning. This difficulty is exacerbated in real-world judgment tasks, where ethical or practical constraints often prevent individuals from systematically testing their hypotheses. For instance, a clinician cannot ethically withhold treatment from a patient judged to need it or provide treatment to one judged not to need it simply to test the accuracy of their diagnosis. The authors replicate Wason’s experiments with statisticians, demonstrating that even individuals trained in hypothesis testing often fail to seek disconfirming evidence when evaluating predictive claims.

A central component of the article is the development of a formal model to explain how confidence in judgment is learned and maintained. The model posits that confidence is shaped by the frequency and reinforcing value of positive and negative feedback. Positive feedback (e.g., successful outcomes) is weighted more heavily than negative feedback, leading individuals to overestimate their judgmental accuracy. The model also incorporates the effects of experience, showing that confidence tends to increase with the number of decisions made, even if judgmental accuracy does not improve. This creates a partial-reinforcement schedule where confidence is slow to develop but highly resistant to extinction, even in the face of contradictory evidence.

The authors further explore the factors influencing the "positive hit rate"—the proportion of correct predictions—through a simulation study. They identify four key variables: judgmental ability (the correlation between judgments and outcomes), the selection ratio (the proportion of cases selected for action), the base rate (the overall probability of success), and treatment effects (the impact of actions on outcomes). The simulation reveals that even when judgmental ability is low, high positive hit rates can occur due to favorable combinations of selection ratios, base rates, and treatment effects. For example, in situations where the selection ratio is low (e.g., highly selective hiring processes), the positive hit rate can be high even if the correlation between judgments and outcomes is weak. Treatment effects, such as the placebo effect in medicine or the Hawthorne effect in psychology, further inflate positive hit rates by improving outcomes for those selected, thereby reinforcing the illusion of validity.

The discussion section synthesizes these findings to address four key issues: the conditions for learning from experience, the nature of outcome feedback, the circumstances under which confidence in judgment may be low, and strategies for improving learning from experience. The authors argue that learning from experience is hindered by the lack of disconfirming evidence, unawareness of environmental effects (e.g., regression to the mean, base rates, and treatment effects), and reliance on unaided memory for coding and retrieving outcome information. They suggest that formal training in experimental design, the use of environmental models, and systematic record-keeping (e.g., maintaining a "box score" of outcomes) could mitigate these issues. However, they acknowledge that overcoming these cognitive biases is challenging, even for individuals with scientific training.

In conclusion, the article provides a compelling explanation for the persistence of the illusion of validity. By highlighting the structural and cognitive barriers to learning from experience, the authors demonstrate why people remain confident in their judgments despite evidence of their fallibility. The work underscores the importance of understanding the interplay between task environments, feedback mechanisms, and cognitive processes in shaping judgment and decision-making. Ultimately, the article raises a provocative question: if individuals believe they can learn from experience, is it possible for them to learn that they cannot?
