# Effect of Self-Relevance of an Event on Hindsight Bias: The Foreseeability of a Layoff

Mark, M. M., & Mellor, S.

__This study examines how the self-relevance of a negative event—specifically, a job layoff—influences hindsight bias, finding that individuals directly affected by the layoff reported less foreseeability than those less impacted, suggesting self-relevance reduces hindsight bias.__

The article investigates the conflicting predictions about how the self-relevance of an event affects hindsight bias, defined as the tendency to overestimate the predictability of an outcome once it is known. Previous research presented two opposing hypotheses: that self-relevance increases hindsight bias due to a need for predictability and sense-making, or that it decreases hindsight bias through self-serving biases and memory cues. To test these predictions, the authors studied retrospective judgments about the foreseeability of layoffs among three groups: laid-off workers, survivors of the layoffs (those not laid off), and community members with no direct connection to the layoffs.

The study was conducted in a northeastern industrial area of the United States, involving 285 laid-off union members, 355 survivors, and 255 community members. Participants were asked to rate how foreseeable the layoffs were on a three-point scale, with higher scores indicating greater perceived foreseeability (and thus more hindsight bias). The authors employed multiple analytical approaches to control for potential preexisting differences between groups, including analysis of variance (ANOVA), analysis of covariance (ANCOVA), and the regression-discontinuity design—a quasi-experimental method that leverages known assignment mechanisms to strengthen causal inferences.

The results consistently showed that community members reported the highest foreseeability of layoffs, followed by survivors, with laid-off workers reporting the least foreseeability. This pattern held across all analyses, including the regression-discontinuity design, which revealed a significant discontinuity at the seniority cutoff point for layoffs, indicating that laid-off workers exhibited less hindsight bias than survivors. The findings suggest that self-relevance reduces hindsight bias, likely due to a self-serving bias where individuals deny the predictability of negative outcomes to avoid blame or protect self-esteem. Supplementary analyses involving workers who were laid off and later recalled to their jobs supported this interpretation, as recalled workers—no longer experiencing the negative consequences of the layoff—reported foreseeability levels similar to survivors, rather than laid-off workers.

The discussion highlights the implications of these findings for understanding hindsight bias and its reduction. While self-relevance may inhibit hindsight bias, it may simultaneously prevent individuals from recognizing their role in negative outcomes or identifying warning signs, thereby hindering effective learning. The authors also note the value of the regression-discontinuity design for future research on hindsight bias and related judgment processes, particularly in applied settings where individuals are assigned to conditions based on cutoff scores (e.g., personnel selection).

Overall, the study provides robust evidence that self-relevance reduces hindsight bias in the context of negative events, challenging some prior theoretical predictions and offering new insights into the mechanisms underlying this cognitive bias.
