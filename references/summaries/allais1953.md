# Le Comportement de l'Homme Rationnel devant le Risque: Critique des Postulats et Axiomes de l'Ecole Américaine

Allais, M.

__This seminal article critiques the foundational axioms of American utility theory, particularly the Bernoulli principle, arguing that rational decision-making under risk must account for psychological values, subjective probabilities, and the dispersion of outcomes, elements neglected by prevailing models.__

Maurice Allais challenges the dominant theories of rational behavior under risk proposed by the American school, particularly the Bernoulli principle, which posits that individuals maximize expected utility. Allais begins by establishing four key psychological elements that any realistic theory of risk must incorporate: the distinction between monetary and psychological values, the distortion of objective probabilities into subjective ones, the mathematical expectation of psychological values, and the dispersion (variance) of these values. He argues that the fourth element—the dispersion of psychological values—is often the most critical yet consistently overlooked by existing theories.

The article systematically critiques the Bernoulli formulation and its axiomatic underpinnings, such as Savage’s independence axiom and Samuelson’s substitutability principle. Allais contends that these axioms, though mathematically elegant, fail to capture the nuanced and context-dependent nature of human decision-making. For instance, he demonstrates through thought experiments and empirical observations that individuals often prefer certainty over probabilistic gains, even when the expected utility suggests otherwise. This behavior contradicts the independence axiom, which assumes that preferences between two gambles should remain unchanged if they share a common outcome.

Allais further distinguishes between abstract and experimental definitions of rationality. Abstract rationality, he argues, requires only internal consistency and the use of objective probabilities, conditions far less restrictive than the axioms of the American school. Experimental rationality, on the other hand, is defined by observing the behavior of individuals deemed rational by societal standards. Through examples, Allais shows that such individuals frequently violate the Bernoulli principle, particularly when faced with choices involving high stakes or near-certain outcomes. For example, many rational individuals prefer a certain gain of 100 million francs over a gamble with a higher expected value but a small chance of losing everything, a preference incompatible with the Bernoulli formulation.

The article also addresses the misconception that the Bernoulli indicator (expected utility) is distinct from cardinal utility (psychological value). Allais argues that if such an indicator exists, it must coincide with psychological value, as the two are inseparable in practice. He critiques the reliance on the law of large numbers to justify the Bernoulli principle, noting that real-world decisions often involve isolated, non-repeatable events where dispersion and psychological factors play a decisive role.

Allais concludes that the American school’s theories fundamentally err by neglecting the dispersion of psychological values, a critical factor in risk perception. He asserts that while the Bernoulli principle may hold in specific cases where individuals are indifferent to risk dispersion, it fails as a general model of rational behavior. The article calls for a more nuanced approach to understanding decision-making under risk, one that integrates psychological realism with economic theory.
