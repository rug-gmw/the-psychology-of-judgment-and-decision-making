# The 'Conjunction Fallacy' Revisited: How Intelligent Inferences Look Like Reasoning Errors

Hertwig, R., Gigerenzer, G.

__This article challenges the notion that violations of the conjunction rule in probability judgments reflect irrationality, arguing instead that such errors arise from semantic and pragmatic inferences about the polysemous term "probability." The authors demonstrate that people infer nonmathematical meanings of "probability" in classic problems like the Linda task, and that using "frequency" instead narrows interpretations to mathematical meanings, reducing conjunction fallacies.__

The article critically examines the "conjunction fallacy," where individuals judge the probability of a combined event (A and B) as higher than one of its constituent events (A or B), violating the fundamental conjunction rule of probability theory. The authors, Hertwig and Gigerenzer, argue that this apparent fallacy stems not from irrationality but from intelligent semantic and pragmatic inferences people make about the term "probability." They contend that the term "probability" is polysemous—having multiple, context-dependent meanings—and that people often infer nonmathematical interpretations (e.g., plausibility, believability) rather than strict mathematical probability.

The introduction frames the conjunction fallacy as a key example of human reasoning errors, with implications for fields like risk assessment and policy-making. The authors critique the assumption that norms of reasoning should be "content-blind," ignoring context and content. They propose that human reasoning is instead shaped by social rationality, particularly conversational maxims like Grice’s relevance maxim, which guides how people interpret ambiguous terms in communication. For instance, in the classic Linda problem, participants infer nonmathematical meanings of "probability" because interpreting it mathematically would render Linda’s description irrelevant to the judgment task.

The article presents a series of empirical studies to test their semantic-inference hypothesis. **Study 1** investigates the meanings participants infer when asked to paraphrase "probability" in the Linda problem. Results show that most participants favor nonmathematical interpretations (e.g., "possibility," "plausibility"), supporting the idea that semantic inferences drive conjunction violations. **Study 2** demonstrates that when participants first make a typicality judgment (e.g., rating how well Linda fits the category of a bank teller), the relevance of Linda’s description is preserved, and subsequent probability judgments align more closely with the conjunction rule. This suggests that conversational context influences semantic inferences.

**Study 3** shifts focus to frequency judgments, showing that participants almost exclusively infer mathematical meanings (e.g., "number," "proportion") when asked to paraphrase "frequency." This aligns with the prediction that "frequency" narrows interpretations to mathematical probability, reducing conjunction violations. **Study 4** tests whether "extensional cues" (e.g., specifying the size of a reference class) account for the frequency effect. The results support the semantic-inference hypothesis: replacing "probability" with "frequency" is the critical factor, not the presence of extensional cues. The authors also address objections to their hypothesis, such as findings of conjunction violations in betting tasks or frequency judgments, and argue that these can be explained by alternative interpretations or design flaws.

The discussion synthesizes the findings, emphasizing that the conjunction fallacy arises from the polysemy of "probability" and the human capacity for semantic inference. The authors argue that frequency representations reduce conjunction violations because "frequency" is less ambiguous than "probability." They also highlight how social rationality—such as adhering to conversational maxims—can lead to responses that appear irrational when judged by content-blind norms. The article concludes by advocating for a broader understanding of rationality that incorporates social and contextual factors, rather than relying solely on formal rules of probability and logic. This perspective challenges the traditional view of human reasoning as inherently flawed and instead positions it as adaptive and context-sensitive.
