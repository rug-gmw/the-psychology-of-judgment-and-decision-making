# Liberals and Conservatives Rely on Different Sets of Moral Foundations

Graham, J., Haidt, J., & Nosek, B. A.

__This article explores how liberals and conservatives in the U.S. differ in their moral foundations, demonstrating that liberals prioritize harm/care and fairness/reciprocity, while conservatives value all five foundations—harm/care, fairness/reciprocity, ingroup/loyalty, authority/respect, and purity/sanctity—more equally.__

The study, grounded in moral foundations theory, investigates the moral differences between political liberals and conservatives in the United States. The authors propose that liberals primarily construct their moral systems around two psychological foundations: Harm/care and Fairness/reciprocity. In contrast, conservatives build their moral systems more evenly across five foundations, including the two liberal foundations plus Ingroup/loyalty, Authority/respect, and Purity/sanctity. This hypothesis was tested through four distinct studies employing various methods to measure moral intuitions and judgments.

The introduction sets the stage by discussing the role of morality in political behavior, highlighting that voters often prioritize moral values over self-interest. The authors argue that the "culture war" in American politics stems from differing moral visions about fundamental issues. They introduce moral foundations theory, which posits that moral intuitions derive from innate psychological mechanisms shaped by cultural practices. The theory identifies five moral foundations: Harm/care, Fairness/reciprocity, Ingroup/loyalty, Authority/respect, and Purity/sanctity.

In Study 1, participants rated the moral relevance of concerns related to the five foundations. Liberals rated Harm/care and Fairness/reciprocity as more relevant, while conservatives rated all five foundations more equally. This pattern persisted even after controlling for demographic variables and was consistent across different national groups. Study 2 expanded on these findings by examining moral judgments of statements and scenarios, using both explicit and implicit measures of political identity. The results confirmed that liberals prioritize individualizing foundations, whereas conservatives value both individualizing and binding foundations.

Study 3 employed a more visceral approach by presenting participants with moral trade-offs, asking how much money they would require to perform actions violating each foundation. Liberals were less willing to violate Harm and Fairness foundations but more open to compromising on binding foundations. Conservatives, however, showed a more uniform unwillingness to violate any of the five foundations. Study 4 analyzed the moral language used in sermons from liberal (Unitarian) and conservative (Southern Baptist) churches. The text analysis revealed that liberal sermons emphasized Harm and Fairness, while conservative sermons highlighted Ingroup, Authority, and Purity.

The discussion integrates these findings, suggesting that the moral differences between liberals and conservatives help explain the intractability of political disagreements. The authors argue that understanding these moral foundations can illuminate why each side views the other's moral stance as incomprehensible or misguided. They also discuss the limitations of their studies, such as the non-representative samples and the reliance on self-reported data, and suggest future directions for research, including exploring implicit moral foundations and expanding the analysis beyond the liberal-conservative spectrum. Overall, the article provides a comprehensive framework for understanding the moral underpinnings of political ideology.
