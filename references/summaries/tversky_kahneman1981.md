# The Framing of Decisions and the Psychology of Choice

Tversky, A., Kahneman, D.

__This seminal article challenges the assumption of human rationality in decision-making, demonstrating how framing the same choice differently leads to predictable preference reversals. The authors introduce prospect theory to explain these inconsistencies, revealing how psychological principles shape risk perception and evaluation.__

The article begins by questioning the foundational assumption of rational choice theory—that human decisions should satisfy basic consistency and coherence requirements. Tversky and Kahneman argue that while rationality demands preferences remain stable across different framings of the same problem, real-world decision-making systematically violates this principle. They define a "decision frame" as the decision-maker's subjective interpretation of the acts, outcomes, and probabilities involved in a choice, shaped by both the problem's formulation and the individual's norms, habits, and personal characteristics. Just as shifting one's vantage point can alter the perceived relative height of mountains, reframing a decision problem can reverse the apparent desirability of options, leading to inconsistent choices.

To illustrate this phenomenon, the authors present a series of empirical demonstrations involving hypothetical and real monetary outcomes, as well as decisions affecting human lives. In one striking example, respondents were asked to choose between two programs to combat an Asian disease expected to kill 600 people. When the outcomes were framed in terms of lives saved (Program A: 200 saved for certain; Program B: 1/3 chance to save 600, 2/3 chance to save none), 72% preferred the risk-averse option A. However, when the same outcomes were framed in terms of lives lost (Program C: 400 die for certain; Program D: 1/3 chance no one dies, 2/3 chance 600 die), 78% preferred the risk-seeking option D. This reversal, observed across multiple groups including physicians, highlights how framing identical outcomes as gains or losses systematically shifts preferences from risk aversion to risk seeking.

To explain these inconsistencies, the authors introduce prospect theory, a descriptive model that modifies expected utility theory to account for observed patterns of choice. Prospect theory proposes a two-phase decision process: an initial framing phase, where acts and outcomes are edited and simplified, followed by an evaluation phase. The theory posits that outcomes are evaluated as gains or losses relative to a neutral reference point, with a value function (ν) that is concave for gains, convex for losses, and steeper for losses than gains. This S-shaped function reflects diminishing sensitivity to both gains and losses, as well as loss aversion—the tendency for losses to loom larger than equivalent gains. Additionally, prospect theory replaces probabilities with decision weights (π), which overweight low probabilities and underweight moderate to high probabilities, leading to phenomena like the certainty effect (where reducing a certain outcome to a probable one has a disproportionate impact).

The article explores three types of framing effects: the framing of acts, contingencies, and outcomes. In the framing of acts, the authors demonstrate how presenting decisions separately versus jointly can lead to violations of dominance. For example, when respondents chose between a sure gain of $240 (preferred by 84%) and a 25% chance to gain $1000 (preferred by 16%), and separately between a sure loss of $750 (preferred by 13%) and a 75% chance to lose $1000 (preferred by 87%), most combined these choices in a way that resulted in a dominated outcome. When the same options were presented jointly, all respondents recognized and avoided the inferior combination. This illustrates how people often fail to integrate concurrent decisions, even when doing so would reveal inconsistencies.

The framing of contingencies is illustrated through problems involving sequential decisions, where the same probabilistic outcomes are evaluated differently depending on whether they are framed as certain or contingent on prior events. For instance, a sure win of $30 was preferred over an 80% chance to win $45 when presented directly, but when the same choice was embedded in a two-stage game with a 25% chance of reaching the second stage, preferences reversed. This "pseudocertainty effect" shows how conditional framing can make probabilistic outcomes appear certain, leading to inconsistent choices. The authors extend this analysis to real-world contexts like insurance, where framing a policy as eliminating a specific risk (e.g., fire) rather than reducing overall risk can make it more attractive, despite both being probabilistic protections.

The framing of outcomes examines how shifts in the reference point—what is considered neutral—can transform gains into losses and vice versa, reversing preferences. For example, a gambler who has already lost $140 at the racetrack may frame a final $10 bet either as a chance to gain $140 (if successful) or as a way to avoid increasing their loss to $150. Prospect theory predicts the latter framing will induce more risk-seeking behavior, consistent with observations that long-shot bets are most popular at the end of the day. The authors also introduce the concept of "psychological accounting," where people evaluate outcomes within mental accounts that group related gains and losses. For instance, losing a $10 ticket to a play feels worse than losing a $10 bill because the former is charged to the same mental account as the ticket purchase, making the total cost seem excessive.

The discussion synthesizes these findings, emphasizing that framing effects are large, systematic, and not eliminated by monetary incentives. The authors compare these effects to perceptual illusions, arguing that just as visual perspective can distort perceived reality, decision frames can distort preferences. They highlight the normative implications of their work, questioning the adequacy of coherence as the sole criterion for rational choice. Instead, they propose that predictive considerations—focusing on future experiences rather than current desires—can help resolve inconsistencies and improve decision quality. The article concludes by noting that framing can also influence the actual experience of outcomes, making the adoption of a particular frame an ethically significant act. Ultimately, Tversky and Kahneman's work reveals the profound impact of psychological factors on decision-making, challenging traditional economic models and offering a richer understanding of human choice.
