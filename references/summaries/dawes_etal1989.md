# Clinical Versus Actuarial Judgment

Dawes, R. M., Faust, D., & Meehl, P. E.

__This article argues that actuarial (statistical) methods consistently outperform clinical judgment in diagnosing and predicting human behavior, despite resistance from professionals who favor intuitive decision-making.__

The article systematically compares clinical and actuarial methods of judgment in psychology, psychiatry, and medicine, demonstrating the superiority of actuarial approaches across a wide range of diagnostic and predictive tasks. The authors begin by defining the two methods: clinical judgment relies on human interpretation of data, while actuarial judgment uses empirically derived formulas or statistical models to reach conclusions. They emphasize that the distinction is not about the setting or the practitioner but about the method of data integration. The introduction highlights real-world scenarios—such as diagnosing psychiatric conditions, detecting brain dysfunction, or predicting survival time in terminal illness—where accurate judgment is critical, and where actuarial methods have proven more reliable.

The methods section outlines the conditions for fair comparisons between the two approaches, stressing that both must use the same data and that actuarial methods must be cross-validated to avoid overfitting. The authors then review a series of landmark studies, including Goldberg’s (1965, 1968) work on distinguishing neurosis from psychosis using the Minnesota Multiphasic Personality Inventory (MMPI). Goldberg’s actuarial rule—a simple linear combination of MMPI scales—outperformed 29 clinicians, including experts, achieving 70% accuracy compared to the clinicians’ 62%. Even after extensive training, clinicians could not match the rule’s performance, and when given the option to override the actuarial conclusion, they still performed worse than the rule alone. Similar findings emerged in studies on detecting brain damage (Leli & Filskov, 1984) and predicting survival time in Hodgkin’s disease (Einhorn, 1972), where actuarial methods consistently surpassed clinical judgment.

The authors synthesize nearly 100 comparative studies, noting that actuarial methods equaled or exceeded clinical judgment in virtually every case. They address common criticisms, such as the claim that clinical judgment excels in complex or theory-mediated tasks, but find little empirical support for these exceptions. For instance, even when clinicians had access to additional information not included in actuarial models, they still failed to outperform the statistical approach. The "broken leg" problem—where rare but critical information (e.g., a patient’s broken leg) might invalidate an actuarial prediction—is acknowledged but dismissed as impractical, as clinicians tend to override actuarial conclusions too frequently, reducing overall accuracy.

The discussion explores why actuarial methods are superior, highlighting key advantages such as consistency, optimal weighting of predictive variables, and resistance to cognitive biases. Clinical judgment is prone to random fluctuations, overconfidence, and illusory correlations, where clinicians perceive patterns that do not exist. Actuarial methods, by contrast, rely on objective, empirically validated relationships between variables and outcomes. The authors also address the limited impact of these findings on clinical practice, attributing resistance to factors like lack of familiarity with the research, misconceptions about probability, and professional identity. Some clinicians argue that actuarial methods dehumanize patients, but the authors counter that the human cost of increased error is far greater.

The article concludes by advocating for greater adoption of actuarial methods, noting their benefits in accuracy, efficiency, and transparency. While actuarial methods are not infallible, they provide a more reliable foundation for decision-making, particularly in high-stakes contexts. The authors call for the development of more actuarial tools and ongoing evaluation of their performance, emphasizing that the scientific evidence overwhelmingly supports their use over unstructured clinical judgment. The accompanying letter by Kleinmuntz (1990) and the authors’ response further clarify that while hybrid approaches (combining clinical and actuarial methods) may be useful in some domains, the evidence in psychology and psychiatry still favors actuarial methods for most tasks.
