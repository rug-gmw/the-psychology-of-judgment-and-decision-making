# Vox Populi: The Wisdom of Crowds in Weight Judging

Galton, F.

__This brief study demonstrates that the median estimate of a crowd—even one with varying expertise—can closely approximate the true value of an uncertain quantity, outperforming most individual guesses.__

In a fascinating exploration of collective judgment, Francis Galton analyzed the results of a weight-guessing competition held at a Plymouth livestock exhibition. Participants—ranging from expert butchers and farmers to laypersons—purchased tickets to estimate the dressed weight of an ox after slaughter. Galton obtained 787 valid entries, which he systematically examined to assess the accuracy of the "vox populi" (voice of the people). The true dressed weight of the ox was later determined to be 1,198 pounds, while the median estimate of the crowd was 1,207 pounds—a mere 9-pound (0.8%) overestimation. This striking accuracy emerged despite the wide dispersion of individual guesses, which clustered around the median but exhibited a curious asymmetry: errors below the median were more tightly grouped than those above it, suggesting a psychological tendency to underestimate rather than overestimate.

Galton’s analysis revealed that the distribution of estimates followed a roughly normal pattern but with notable deviations. The "probable error" of a single guess—defined as the range within which half of all estimates fell—was 37 pounds, or 3.1% of the true weight. However, the lower half of the distribution (estimates below the median) was more compressed, with a probable error of 29 pounds, while the upper half (estimates above the median) was more spread out, with a probable error of 45 pounds. This asymmetry hinted at underlying psychological or methodological biases, though Galton refrained from speculating on their exact cause, noting only that such anomalies might stem from differing judgment strategies or the influence of prior psychophysical research, such as Fechner’s work on perception.

The study’s implications extended beyond the immediate context of weight judging. Galton argued that the crowd’s median estimate was far more reliable than most individual guesses, even those of supposed experts. This finding lent empirical support to the democratic principle of collective decision-making, suggesting that aggregated judgments could mitigate individual errors. Galton concluded that the result was "more creditable to the trustworthiness of a democratic judgment than might have been expected," advocating for the preservation of similar datasets from future competitions to further explore the phenomenon. The work thus bridged psychology, statistics, and social theory, offering an early and influential case for the "wisdom of crowds."
