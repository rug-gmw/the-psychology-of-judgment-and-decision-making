# A Behavioral Model of Rational Choice

Simon, H. A.

__This seminal article challenges classical economic models of "rational man" by proposing a behavioral framework that accounts for human cognitive limitations, introducing concepts like bounded rationality, satisficing, and aspiration levels to better reflect real-world decision-making.__

Herbert A. Simon’s *A Behavioral Model of Rational Choice* critiques the traditional economic assumption of "economic man"—an agent with perfect information, stable preferences, and unlimited computational capacity—arguing that such a model fails to capture how humans actually make decisions. Instead, Simon proposes a theory of *bounded rationality*, where decision-makers operate within cognitive and environmental constraints, simplifying complex problems to make them tractable. The paper systematically dismantles the classical notion of global rationality, replacing it with a more realistic framework that acknowledges the limitations of human information processing and the adaptive strategies people employ to navigate uncertainty.

Simon begins by outlining the core elements of classical rational choice models, which assume that agents maximize utility by evaluating all possible alternatives, outcomes, and their associated payoffs. He formalizes these elements—such as behavior alternatives, payoff functions, and probability distributions—using set theory and mathematical notation, demonstrating how traditional models like the max-min rule, probabilistic rule, and certainty rule rely on unrealistic assumptions about human computational abilities. These models, he argues, demand an "impossibly clear and voluminous" knowledge of the environment, a stable preference system, and an ability to perform complex calculations that far exceed human capacities. Simon’s critique is not merely theoretical; he draws on casual empiricism and psychological insights to suggest that real-world decision-making is far messier, involving heuristics, approximations, and sequential rather than exhaustive evaluation of options.

The heart of Simon’s contribution lies in his proposed simplifications to the rational choice framework, which he argues better align with observed human behavior. First, he introduces the concept of *satisficing*—a decision rule where agents seek alternatives that meet a minimum threshold of acceptability (e.g., "good enough") rather than optimizing. This is operationalized through *simple payoff functions*, where outcomes are categorized into binary or ternary states (e.g., satisfactory/unsatisfactory or win/draw/lose), drastically reducing the computational burden. For example, a chess player might aim to reach a "clearly won" position rather than calculating the optimal move, or a home seller might accept the first offer above a certain price rather than waiting for the highest possible bid. Simon demonstrates how this approach can be formalized mathematically, showing that satisficing can be as effective as optimization in many contexts while requiring far less information and cognitive effort.

Second, Simon addresses the role of *information gathering* in decision-making, noting that humans often refine their understanding of the relationship between actions and outcomes incrementally. Rather than mapping all possible alternatives to outcomes upfront, agents may start with a coarse model and gradually improve it, focusing only on the most relevant information. This dynamic process is illustrated through the chess example, where a player explores potential moves and countermoves to identify a path to a satisfactory outcome, abandoning lines of play that fail to meet the threshold. Simon also highlights how this approach can be applied to economic problems, such as linear programming, where feasibility testing (ensuring a solution meets minimum requirements) can be more practical than optimization.

Third, Simon extends his model to handle *partial ordering of payoffs*, where outcomes are evaluated along multiple, incommensurable dimensions (e.g., salary, job satisfaction, and prestige). Here, he introduces the idea of *aspiration levels*—thresholds for each dimension that define a satisfactory outcome. This allows agents to make decisions without needing to reduce all criteria to a single utility metric, a common but unrealistic assumption in classical economics. For instance, a job seeker might accept a position that meets minimum standards for salary, location, and work-life balance, even if it is not the "best" option along any single dimension. Simon formalizes this using vector payoff functions, showing how the model can accommodate group decision-making, individual multi-criteria choices, and uncertain outcomes.

The paper then addresses the *existence and uniqueness* of solutions in this behavioral framework. Unlike classical models, which often guarantee optimal solutions, Simon’s approach acknowledges that satisfactory solutions may not always exist or may not be unique. To resolve this, he introduces dynamic adjustments, such as *aspiration level adaptation* (raising or lowering thresholds based on ease or difficulty of finding satisfactory options) and *expanding the set of considered alternatives* (searching for new options when existing ones fail to meet aspirations). These mechanisms ensure that the model remains practical even when initial attempts to find a satisfactory solution fail. Simon emphasizes that these adjustments are not merely theoretical but reflect real-world behaviors, such as persistence in searching for better alternatives or lowering expectations when options are scarce.

In the final sections, Simon explores the *dynamic* aspects of decision-making, where outcomes depend not only on current choices but also on past actions. He suggests that agents can simplify dynamic problems by focusing on a "planning horizon" and evaluating outcomes based on both immediate payoffs and future positions (e.g., a chess player considering the board state after a few moves rather than the entire game). He also touches on how experience can refine payoff functions and improve decision-making over time, though he notes that these dynamic extensions require further development. The paper concludes by arguing that this behavioral model of rationality has both *descriptive* and *normative* value. Descriptively, it better explains how humans and organizations make decisions in complex, uncertain environments. Normatively, it offers insights for designing decision-support systems and computing equipment that align with human cognitive limitations, potentially improving outcomes in fields like artificial intelligence and organizational theory.

The appendix provides a mathematical example of how an agent might rationally determine an acceptable payoff, such as setting a minimum price for selling a house. Simon shows that while an optimal solution can be derived using probability distributions and backward induction, real-world agents often rely on simpler, adaptive strategies—such as gradually adjusting the acceptance price based on received offers—without performing complex calculations. This reinforces the paper’s central theme: that bounded rationality, with its focus on satisficing and adaptive heuristics, is not only more realistic but also more practical than the idealized models of classical economics.
