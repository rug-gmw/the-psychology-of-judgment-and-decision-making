# The Curse of Knowledge in Reasoning About False Beliefs

Birch, S. A. J., & Bloom, P.

__This study demonstrates that adults, like children, struggle with false-belief reasoning when their own knowledge of an outcome interferes with their ability to predict another person’s beliefs. The curse of knowledge bias is stronger when adults perceive the outcome as plausible, revealing how perceived plausibility mediates this cognitive limitation.__

The article explores whether adults, who possess a mature understanding of false beliefs, still exhibit difficulties in reasoning about others' false beliefs when influenced by their own knowledge—a phenomenon known as the *curse of knowledge*. While prior research has shown that young children struggle with false-belief tasks (e.g., the classic Sally-Anne task), the source of their difficulty remains debated. Some argue that children lack a conceptual understanding of mental representations, while others suggest their errors stem from cognitive biases, such as the curse of knowledge, which adults also experience but to a lesser degree. The authors hypothesized that if adults were tested with sufficiently sensitive measures, they too would show deficits in false-belief reasoning when their knowledge of an outcome conflicted with another person’s ignorance.

To test this, the researchers designed an experiment using a modified displacement task. Unlike traditional false-belief tasks, which ask participants to make a categorical choice (e.g., "Where will Sally look?"), this study required adults to assign probability estimates to where a protagonist (Vicki) would search for her violin after it was moved in her absence. The task involved four containers, allowing the researchers to manipulate both the participants’ knowledge of the violin’s final location and the plausibility of Vicki’s potential actions. In the *ignorance condition*, participants were told the violin was moved but not where; in the *knowledge-plausible condition*, they were told it was moved to a container now occupying the original location (making it plausible Vicki might look there); and in the *knowledge-implausible condition*, they were told it was moved to a container in a different location (making it unlikely Vicki would search there).

The results revealed that adults’ knowledge of the outcome biased their judgments, but only when the outcome was plausible. Participants in the knowledge-plausible condition assigned significantly higher probabilities to Vicki looking in the correct (but false-belief-violating) container and lower probabilities to her acting on her false belief compared to those in the ignorance condition. However, in the knowledge-implausible condition, participants’ judgments did not differ significantly from the ignorance condition, suggesting that the curse of knowledge only exerted its influence when adults could rationalize why Vicki might act against her false belief. This finding aligns with prior research showing that plausibility mediates the magnitude of the curse of knowledge in other contexts, such as hindsight bias.

The discussion highlights the implications of these findings for both adult social cognition and developmental psychology. For adults, the study suggests that the curse of knowledge is not merely a fleeting bias but can systematically distort reasoning about others’ beliefs when combined with a plausible justification. For children, the results support the idea that their greater susceptibility to the curse of knowledge may exacerbate their difficulties with false-belief tasks. While the study does not rule out the possibility that conceptual limitations also contribute to children’s struggles, it demonstrates that even adults, who lack such deficits, are not immune to knowledge-based biases in mental-state reasoning. The authors conclude that the curse of knowledge is a fundamental constraint on perspective-taking, one that persists across development but may be mitigated by factors like plausibility. Future research could explore whether manipulating plausibility in other false-belief tasks (e.g., unexpected-contents or appearance-reality tasks) similarly reduces children’s errors.
