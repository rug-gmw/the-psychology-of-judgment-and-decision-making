# A Dual-Process Model of Belief and Evidence Interactions in Causal Reasoning

Fugelsang, J. A., & Thompson, V. A.

__This article proposes a dual-process model of causal reasoning, demonstrating that preexisting beliefs—derived from either mechanism-based or covariation-based knowledge—independently influence how individuals evaluate new empirical evidence, with reasoners showing limited awareness of the impact of their prior beliefs.__

The article presents a comprehensive investigation into how individuals integrate preexisting causal beliefs with new empirical evidence when making causal judgments. Fugelsang and Thompson argue that causal reasoning is shaped by two primary sources of information: covariation-based evidence (the observed statistical relationship between a cause and effect) and prior beliefs about causal mechanisms (knowledge of how a cause might produce an effect). The authors critique traditional covariation-based models of causal reasoning, such as Cheng and Novick’s (1990) probabilistic contrast model (PCM), which assume that causal judgments are primarily derived from the computation of contingency (ΔP_c). While these models provide a normative framework for understanding causal inference, they fail to account for the influence of prior knowledge and the limitations of covariation as a sole cue for causality, such as cases where events covary without a causal link (e.g., day and night).

To address these gaps, the authors propose a dual-process model that distinguishes between mechanism-based beliefs (knowledge of causal powers or generative transmission) and covariation-based beliefs (long-term memory representations of statistical associations). They hypothesize that these two types of beliefs are represented independently and interact differently with new empirical evidence. Specifically, mechanism-based beliefs are thought to act as a heuristic filter, restricting the set of causal candidates for which covariation data are considered, while covariation-based beliefs contribute additively to causal judgments.

The article reports three experiments designed to test these hypotheses. In Experiment 1, participants evaluated unfamiliar causal candidates (e.g., "Substance X") under conditions where prior beliefs were manipulated as either mechanism-based or covariation-based. The results showed that covariation-based data were weighted more heavily for believable candidates than for unbelievable ones, but only when beliefs were mechanism-based. This interaction was absent when beliefs were covariation-based, suggesting that mechanism-based beliefs uniquely constrain the evaluation of new evidence. Additionally, participants demonstrated greater accuracy in introspecting about their use of covariation-based data compared to their prior beliefs, indicating that the influence of prior knowledge may operate unconsciously.

Experiment 2 extended these findings to familiar causal scenarios (e.g., insomnia causing fatigue) and compared causal judgments (perceived causal efficacy, or PCE) with covariation estimates (COV). The interaction between belief level and ΔP_c was observed only for mechanism-based beliefs and PCE judgments, reinforcing the idea that causal judgments are uniquely sensitive to mechanistic knowledge. Experiment 3 replicated the introspection findings of Experiment 1 while controlling for the magnitude of belief effects, confirming that participants remained less aware of the influence of prior beliefs even when their effects were comparable to those of covariation-based data.

The discussion synthesizes these findings into a two-stage model of causal reasoning. In the first stage, prior beliefs—whether mechanism- or covariation-based—are automatically recruited and influence the selection of relevant evidence. In the second stage, individuals consciously evaluate covariation-based data, but this evaluation is constrained by the output of the first stage. The authors argue that this model aligns with broader dual-process theories of reasoning (e.g., Evans & Over, 1996), where heuristic processes guide analytic reasoning. They also highlight the adaptive value of this system, as it allows individuals to efficiently narrow down potential causal candidates in complex environments.

The article concludes by emphasizing the need for future research to explore cross-cultural differences in causal reasoning and to develop methods for mitigating the unconscious influence of prior beliefs when they may hinder accurate judgment. Overall, the work advances our understanding of how belief and evidence interact in causal reasoning, challenging purely empiricist models and underscoring the multifaceted nature of human cognition.
