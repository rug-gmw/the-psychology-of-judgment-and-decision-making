# Emotionalized Social Media Environments: How Alternative News Media and Populist Actors Drive Angry Reactions

Humprecht, E., Amsler, M., Esser, F., & Van Aelst, P.

__This study investigates how alternative news media and populist political actors provoke angry reactions on Facebook, emphasizing the role of contextual factors like country, actor type, and message content in shaping emotionalized political discourse.__

The article presents a comparative analysis of emotional responses to political and media posts on Facebook across six countries: Belgium, France, Germany, Switzerland, the United Kingdom, and the United States. The authors examine 175,539 Facebook posts and 360,000 emotional reactions to understand how different actors—populist politicians, traditional politicians, alternative/hyper-partisan media, and traditional media—elicit emotional engagement, particularly anger. The study adopts an opportunity structure framework, considering country characteristics, actor types, media types, and message characteristics to explain variations in emotional responses.

The introduction highlights the growing importance of social media platforms like Facebook in political discussions, where emotions, especially negative ones like anger, play a significant role in shaping public opinion and engagement. The authors argue that populist politicians and alternative media strategically use emotional appeals to attract attention, deepen societal divides, and mobilize supporters. This emotionalization can lead to increased polarization, incivility, and the spread of misinformation, undermining the quality of public discourse.

The methods section details the data collection process, which involved gathering Facebook posts and user reactions from April to June 2020, a period marked by the COVID-19 pandemic. The authors analyzed posts from a diverse set of actors, including traditional and populist politicians, as well as mainstream and alternative media outlets. They normalized emotional reactions (e.g., "angry," "love," "haha") by the number of followers to ensure comparability across accounts. The study employed both automated text analysis and qualitative content analysis to identify themes and communication styles in posts that elicited the highest levels of anger.

Key findings reveal that alternative media and populist politicians consistently provoke more "angry" reactions than traditional media and politicians. This pattern holds across all six countries, though the intensity of emotional reactions varies. For instance, Belgium and the United States exhibit higher levels of both "angry" and "love" reactions, while Switzerland shows more reserved emotional engagement. The content analysis of the most anger-provoking posts reveals a focus on populist rhetoric, including anti-elitism and exclusionary themes, such as blaming immigrants or political opponents for societal issues. Notably, Donald Trump stands out as an exception, receiving predominantly "love" reactions despite using antagonistic messaging, likely due to his position as an incumbent and the loyalty of his followers.

The discussion emphasizes the broader implications of these findings, particularly how the emotionalization of political discourse can contribute to polarization, misinformation, and a decline in the quality of public debate. The authors argue that the prevalence of angry reactions is not solely a product of platform algorithms but is also shaped by cultural and political contexts. They highlight the need for targeted strategies to mitigate the negative effects of emotionalized discourse and promote constructive political engagement.

The study concludes by acknowledging its limitations, such as the inability to distinguish whether user reactions are directed at the content or the author of the post. The authors also note the need for further research to explore the nuances of emotional reactions across different cultural contexts and to develop typologies of alternative media outlets. Despite these limitations, the study provides valuable insights into the mechanisms driving emotional engagement on social media and the role of populist and alternative media actors in shaping public discourse.
