# The Sunk Cost and Concorde Effects: Are Humans Less Rational Than Lower Animals?

Arkes, H. R., Ayton, P.

__This article challenges the assumption that humans are more rational than lower animals by examining the sunk cost effect in humans and the Concorde fallacy in animals. The authors argue that humans, unlike animals and young children, overgeneralize the "Don’t waste" rule, leading to economically irrational decisions.__

The article explores the paradoxical observation that adult humans frequently commit the sunk cost effect—a tendency to continue an endeavor due to prior investments—while lower animals and young children do not exhibit this behavior. The authors begin by defining the sunk cost effect and the Concorde fallacy, illustrating the former with examples from human decision-making, such as the tendency to attend more plays if one has paid a higher price for season tickets. They highlight that this behavior contradicts normative economic principles, which dictate that only incremental costs and benefits should influence decisions.

The discussion then shifts to the Concorde fallacy, initially proposed by Trivers (1972) in the context of parental investment theory, which suggested that animals might favor offspring with greater prior investment. However, Dawkins and Carlisle (1976) critiqued this idea, arguing that animals likely base decisions on future costs and benefits rather than past investments. The authors conduct an exhaustive review of studies on the Concorde fallacy in animals, concluding that there is no unambiguous evidence supporting its existence. For instance, Maestripieri and Alleva’s (1991) study on mice demonstrated that maternal defense behavior was determined by the current number of offspring, not prior investment. The authors reinterpret studies that purportedly found evidence of the Concorde fallacy, such as those involving digger wasps and cichlid fish, arguing that these results can be explained by future-oriented decision-making rather than past investment.

The article then explores why humans might be more susceptible to the sunk cost effect than animals. The authors propose that humans' ability to use and overgeneralize abstract rules, such as "Don’t waste," leads to this irrational behavior. They present evidence from studies showing that young children, who have not yet developed complex rule-based thinking, are less prone to the sunk cost effect than adults. For example, Webley and Plaisier (1997) found that younger children were more likely to make economically rational decisions in scenarios analogous to sunk cost situations, while older children and adults were influenced by prior investments.

The authors also discuss the role of self-justification in exacerbating the sunk cost effect, noting that individuals who feel personally responsible for an initial investment are more likely to continue a failing course of action to avoid admitting mistakes. This social psychological pressure is irrelevant to lower animals, further explaining their immunity to the sunk cost effect.

In their discussion, the authors acknowledge the benefits of rule-based thinking but emphasize the costs of overgeneralization. They suggest that while the sunk cost effect represents a maladaptive overgeneralization of the "Don’t waste" rule, the ability to use such rules is generally advantageous for humans. They conclude by reflecting on the broader implications of their findings, noting that while adherence to normative economic rules leads to better decisions, the computational limits of human cognition may necessitate the use of heuristics that, while not always optimal, are often serviceable in everyday life. The article ultimately highlights the paradox that humans, despite their cognitive sophistication, may sometimes be less rational than lower animals due to their susceptibility to rule overgeneralization.
