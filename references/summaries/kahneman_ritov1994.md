# Determinants of Stated Willingness to Pay for Public Goods: A Study in the Headline Method

Kahneman, D., Ritov, I.

__This article challenges the validity of the contingent valuation method (CVM) for measuring public goods' economic value, proposing instead that willingness-to-pay (WTP) reflects attitudes rather than true economic preferences. Using a "headline method" to elicit quick responses to environmental and public health issues, the authors find high correlations between WTP and other attitude measures, while demonstrating violations of economic logic like embedding effects and preference reversals.__

The article critically examines the contingent valuation method (CVM), which assigns monetary values to public goods like environmental preservation or public health improvements. The authors argue that CVM's theoretical foundation—the "purchase model"—assumes respondents treat public goods similarly to private goods, comparing states of the world with and without the good while accounting for wealth changes. However, this assumption is contested by critics who highlight methodological flaws, such as embedding effects (where WTP does not scale with the scope of the good) and preference reversals (where WTP and direct choices yield inconsistent rankings). The debate is particularly contentious in litigation contexts, where CVM is used to assess damages for non-use or existence values of public goods.

To address these critiques, Kahneman and Ritov introduce the "headline method," a streamlined approach where respondents quickly state their WTP for public goods described in brief, headline-like scenarios. This method allows for efficient data collection across multiple issues, enabling comparisons of WTP with other attitude measures like political support, perceived importance, and personal satisfaction. The authors justify this approach through the principle of "process continuity," which posits that biases observed in quick, intuitive judgments persist even in more deliberative settings. While the headline method differs from CVM in its brevity and lack of detailed information, the authors argue that its findings can serve as plausible hypotheses about CVM's limitations.

The study involved 1,441 participants who responded to 37 environmental and public health issues, each described in a headline paired with a proposed intervention. Four types of responses were collected: WTP (voluntary contributions), political support for government intervention, personal satisfaction from contributing, and perceived importance of the issue. The results revealed several key findings. First, WTP responses were highly correlated with other attitude measures, suggesting that WTP primarily reflects respondents' attitudes toward the problem rather than economic valuation. Second, WTP exhibited limited sensitivity to the scope of problems or interventions, with mean WTP values clustering in a narrow range regardless of the issue's significance. This lack of discrimination raises concerns about WTP's ability to meaningfully differentiate between trivial and important public goods.

The authors also explored specific determinants of WTP through experimental manipulations. For example, they found violations of monotonicity, where WTP for a subset of a problem (e.g., saving turtles) exceeded WTP for the broader problem (e.g., saving reptiles). Similarly, WTP was more sensitive to the severity of the problem than to the scope of the proposed solution, reinforcing the idea that WTP reflects attitudes toward the problem rather than the intervention. Another notable finding was the occurrence of preference reversals: respondents often chose interventions for human health or safety over environmental goods in direct comparisons, even when their WTP suggested the opposite. This challenges the assumption that WTP and choice are interchangeable measures of value.

The discussion critiques the psychometric properties of WTP, noting that it is less reliable than other attitude measures like importance ratings or political support. The authors argue that WTP's limitations stem from its unbounded, unfamiliar scale, which makes it difficult for respondents to express nuanced attitudes. They suggest that CVM's recent shift to referendum-style questions (where respondents vote on hypothetical taxes) may not resolve these issues, as such questions still elicit attitudes rather than economic values.

In conclusion, Kahneman and Ritov propose an alternative approach to valuing public goods: developing a standardized scale linking attitudes to dollar values for a set of hypothetical scenarios. Real-world issues could then be evaluated by comparing them to this scale using more reliable attitude measures. This method avoids the pitfalls of WTP while acknowledging that public attitudes, rather than economic values, are the appropriate input for policy decisions. The authors caution that their findings are based on the headline method, but they argue that CVM practitioners must address the method's demonstrated flaws, particularly its lack of sensitivity to scope and susceptibility to framing effects.
