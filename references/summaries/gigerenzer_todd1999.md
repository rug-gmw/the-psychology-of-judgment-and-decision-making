# Simple heuristics that make us smart

Gigerenzer, P. M., Todd, and the ABC Research Group

__This book challenges traditional models of rationality by proposing that decision-makers use simple, fast, and frugal heuristics to make effective decisions with limited time and knowledge. The authors argue that these heuristics exploit the structure of information in the environment, leading to ecologically rational decisions. They present various heuristics, such as the recognition heuristic and Take The Best, and demonstrate their effectiveness in different decision-making contexts.__

The book begins by critiquing traditional models of unbounded rationality and optimization under constraints, which assume decision-makers have unlimited time, knowledge, and computational power. Instead, the authors advocate for a vision of bounded rationality, where decision-making mechanisms are adapted to the structure of the environment. They introduce the concept of ecological rationality, where heuristics are matched to specific environmental structures to achieve reasonable performance.

The authors describe the building blocks of simple heuristics, including rules for guiding search, stopping search, and making decisions. They present several classes of heuristics, such as ignorance-based decision making, one-reason decision making, elimination heuristics for multiple-option choices, and satisficing heuristics. Each heuristic is designed for specific tasks and environments, demonstrating that simple heuristics can perform comparably to more complex algorithms, particularly when generalizing to new data.

The book also explores the ecological rationality of these heuristics, showing how they can exploit environmental structures like noncompensatory information, scarce information, J-shaped distributions, and decreasing populations. The authors argue that fast and frugal heuristics are robust and can avoid overfitting, making them more accurate in new situations.

Empirical evidence is provided to show when people use simple heuristics, and the authors discuss the challenges of this research program. They relate their work to other notions of heuristics and introduce the metaphor of the adaptive toolbox, which organizes the mind's collection of simple heuristics.

The book concludes with a set of questions remaining to be explored and a summary of the view of bounded rationality presented. The authors emphasize that simple heuristics can make us smart by exploiting the structure of information in the environment, leading to adaptive and robust decision-making.
