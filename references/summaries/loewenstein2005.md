# Projection Bias in Medical Decision Making

Loewenstein, G.

__This article explores *projection bias*—the tendency to overestimate how much our current emotional and physical states will influence future decisions—and its implications for medical decision-making, arguing that rational models fail to account for the powerful role of transient emotions in shaping health-related choices.__

George Loewenstein’s article examines the concept of *projection bias*, a cognitive phenomenon where individuals inaccurately project their present feelings, moods, or physical states onto future decisions, leading to suboptimal choices. The article begins by illustrating projection bias through personal anecdotes, such as how one’s current fatigue or mood influences decisions about future commitments, like accepting a speaking invitation. Loewenstein argues that while rational decision-making models assume people can accurately predict their future preferences, real-world decisions—especially in healthcare—are often distorted by immediate emotional or physical states, such as hunger, pain, or fear. This bias is formalized in a decision-theoretic model, where current affective states (e.g., hunger, craving, or pain) skew predictions of future utility, leading to choices that may not align with long-term well-being.

The article reviews empirical evidence across multiple domains to demonstrate projection bias. For example, studies show that people shop more impulsively when hungry, addicts value drugs more highly when craving them, and individuals misjudge their future tolerance for pain or embarrassment. These findings highlight the *hot-cold empathy gap*, where people in a "cold" (emotionally neutral) state struggle to empathize with their own or others' "hot" (emotionally charged) states. Loewenstein extends this idea to interpersonal contexts, such as physicians underestimating patients' pain or healthy individuals failing to anticipate the preferences of their future sick selves. The article also discusses retrospective projection bias, where people misremember past states (e.g., depression or pain) based on their current feelings, further complicating long-term decision-making.

The implications for medical decision-making are profound. Projection bias can lead to poor adherence to treatment regimens (e.g., patients discontinuing medication when symptoms subside), inadequate pain management (e.g., physicians undermedicating because they cannot empathize with patients' pain), and inconsistent end-of-life decisions (e.g., patients refusing life-sustaining treatment in moments of despair but later regretting it). The article critiques the limitations of patient autonomy in such contexts, as transient emotions can lead to arbitrary or irreversible choices. Loewenstein suggests potential solutions, such as default options informed by decision analysis or policies that account for the instability of preferences over time. However, he acknowledges that even these approaches face challenges, as projection bias undermines the very notion of "informed consent" by distorting patients' understanding of future consequences.

Ultimately, the article calls for a reevaluation of how medical decisions are made, emphasizing the need to incorporate emotional and situational factors into models of rationality. It challenges the Society for Medical Decision-Making to move beyond purely rational frameworks and develop tools that better account for the dynamic, affective nature of human decision-making. The core message is that while rationality is a noble goal, it must be tempered by an understanding of how emotions shape—and often distort—our choices.
