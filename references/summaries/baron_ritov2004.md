# Omission Bias, Individual Differences, and Normality

Baron, J., & Ritov, I.

This article examines the phenomenon of omission bias—the tendency to prefer harm caused by inaction over equal or lesser harm caused by action—and challenges recent criticisms suggesting its limited generality or the existence of an opposing "action bias." The authors argue that omission bias persists even when controlling for normality and individual differences, though its strength varies across contexts and individuals.

The article begins by defining omission bias and reviewing prior research that demonstrates its prevalence in various decision-making contexts, particularly vaccination decisions. Critics have argued that omission bias may be confounded by methodological issues, such as response scale limitations, or by alternative biases like a preference for action in certain scenarios. The authors systematically address these critiques, demonstrating that omission bias remains robust even when these factors are controlled. For instance, they highlight studies where omission bias persisted despite open-ended response scales or when the consequences of action and inaction were equated. They also distinguish omission bias from the status-quo effect, arguing that the former is rooted in the distinction between direct and indirect causation rather than mere preference for the default option.

The authors then explore potential explanations for action bias, where individuals prefer action over inaction. They suggest that action bias may arise in contexts where inaction is perceived as irresponsible or unexpected, such as when a decision-maker is explicitly tasked with taking action (e.g., as an environmental activist). However, they find limited evidence for a pure action bias that cannot be explained by these contextual factors. Another proposed explanation is the "normality bias," where individuals favor what is perceived as standard or normal, regardless of whether it involves action or inaction. While the authors acknowledge this bias, they argue that it does not fully account for omission bias, as their experiments show that omission bias persists even when normality is controlled.

The article presents two new experiments to further investigate these issues. Experiment 1 tests whether omission bias occurs when the risks of action and inaction are equal, while also examining the roles of normality and perceived causation. The results show a small but significant omission bias in choices, particularly when the indirect cause of harm (e.g., treatment failure) is framed as an action rather than an omission. The experiment also reveals substantial individual differences, with some participants exhibiting a consistent action bias even when consequences are equal. Experiment 2 uses a more symmetric response scale to address potential methodological concerns and confirms the presence of omission bias, though its magnitude varies across individuals.

The discussion synthesizes these findings, emphasizing that omission bias is a real and persistent phenomenon, though its strength depends on contextual and individual factors. The authors argue that while some individuals may exhibit action bias, omission bias remains a concern for public policy, particularly in areas like vaccination, where even a minority of non-vaccinators can undermine public health efforts. They conclude by suggesting that omission bias may stem from heuristics that prioritize avoiding direct harm, while action bias could arise from overgeneralized imperatives to "do something." Future research, they propose, should explore the emotional and contextual moderators of these biases.
