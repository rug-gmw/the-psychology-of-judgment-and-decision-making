# Dread Risk, September 11, and Fatal Traffic Accidents

Gigerenzer, G.

__After the 9/11 attacks, Americans' fear of flying led many to drive instead, resulting in an estimated 350 additional traffic fatalities in the following three months—more than the lives lost on the four hijacked flights.__

In this article, Gerd Gigerenzer examines the unintended consequences of psychological reactions to catastrophic events, specifically the terrorist attacks of September 11, 2001. He introduces the concept of "dread risks," which refer to low-probability, high-consequence events that evoke disproportionate fear, such as plane crashes. While millions of Americans reduced or avoided air travel in the months following 9/11—evidenced by a 12–20% decline in airline passenger miles—many likely opted for long-distance driving as an alternative. Gigerenzer hypothesizes that this shift from flying to driving would have led to an increase in traffic fatalities, as driving, while perceived as safer, is statistically riskier for long-distance travel.

To test this hypothesis, Gigerenzer analyzed U.S. Department of Transportation data on traffic fatalities and vehicle miles driven before and after September 2001. He first established a stable baseline by examining fatal crash patterns from 1996 to 2000, which showed consistent monthly trends with minimal variation. In the first eight months of 2001, fatal crashes closely mirrored this historical pattern, deviating by an average of just 0.3% per month. However, in the three months following 9/11, the number of fatal crashes spiked, exceeding the upper range of previous years. This increase was particularly pronounced on rural interstate highways, suggesting a rise in long-distance travel. By comparing the observed fatalities to the expected numbers (based on pre-9/11 trends), Gigerenzer estimated an excess of 353 traffic deaths in the final quarter of 2001—more than the 266 passengers killed on the four hijacked flights.

Gigerenzer argues that this "second toll" of lives was a direct consequence of psychological reactions to the 9/11 attacks, as fear of flying led people to choose a riskier alternative. While preventing terrorist attacks remains a complex challenge, he suggests that educating the public about the psychological biases underlying dread risks could help mitigate such unintended consequences. By fostering a better understanding of how fear influences risk perception, policymakers and public health officials might reduce avoidable fatalities in the aftermath of future catastrophic events.
