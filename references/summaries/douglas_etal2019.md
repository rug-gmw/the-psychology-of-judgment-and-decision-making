# Understanding Conspiracy Theories

Douglas, K. M., Uscinski, J. E., Sutton, R. M., Cichocka, A., Nefes, T., Ang, C. S., & Deravi, F.

__This article provides a comprehensive interdisciplinary review of conspiracy theories, exploring their psychological, political, and social underpinnings, how they are communicated, and their societal impacts. It argues that conspiracy theories are not merely fringe beliefs but have significant consequences for individuals and society, often doing more harm than good.__

The article begins by defining key terms such as "conspiracy," "conspiracy theories," and "conspiracy beliefs," emphasizing the importance of clear definitions to avoid delegitimizing valid concerns. It discusses various methods for measuring conspiracy beliefs, including surveys, scales, and discourse analysis, each with its own strengths and limitations. The authors highlight the interdisciplinary nature of the research, drawing from psychology, political science, sociology, and other fields, and employing a range of methodologies from content analysis to psychological experiments.

The core of the article delves into the factors that lead people to adopt conspiracy theories. Psychologically, conspiracy beliefs are linked to epistemic, existential, and social motives. Epistemic motives involve the desire for understanding and certainty, with conspiracy theories providing broad explanations that preserve beliefs in the face of uncertainty. Existential motives relate to the need for control and security, where conspiracy theories offer a sense of agency in chaotic situations. Social motives encompass the desire to maintain a positive self and group image, often leading to the scapegoating of powerful outgroups. Demographically, lower levels of education and income are associated with higher conspiracy belief, although the causal relationships remain unclear.

Politically, conspiracy theories are often adopted by those on the losing side of power asymmetries, serving as a form of threat perception and a tool for collective action. Ideological factors also play a role, with conspiracy beliefs more prevalent at the political extremes, particularly on the right. However, the relationship between political ideology and conspiracy belief is complex and context-dependent. The article also explores how conspiracy theories are communicated, noting that they are often used to justify political positions, challenge dominant narratives, and cope with collective trauma. The internet and social media play a significant role in spreading conspiracy theories, although their impact is nuanced and often confined within polarized communities.

The societal consequences of conspiracy theories are far-reaching and predominantly negative. While conspiracy theories can sometimes encourage government transparency and reveal inconsistencies in official narratives, they are more often associated with harmful outcomes. These include increased prejudice, health-related risks such as vaccine refusal, science denial (e.g., climate change skepticism), decreased political engagement, and even violence and extremism. The article also discusses the potential for conspiracy theories to undermine workplace engagement and trust.

In conclusion, the authors suggest several avenues for future research, including the need for larger, more representative samples, standardized measurement tools, and experimental and longitudinal designs to better understand causality. They emphasize the importance of studying conspiracy theories across different geographic and cultural contexts and integrating research on the communication and psychological factors driving conspiracy beliefs. The article underscores the necessity of addressing conspiracy theories due to their significant impact on societal attitudes and behaviors.
