# Availability: A Heuristic for Judging Frequency and Probability

Tversky, A., Kahneman, D.

__This seminal paper introduces the *availability heuristic*, demonstrating how people judge the frequency or probability of events based on how easily relevant instances come to mind. While availability often aligns with real-world frequency, it also leads to systematic biases due to factors like memorability, vividness, or ease of imagination. The authors explore these biases across diverse judgment tasks, from word frequency to illusory correlations.__

The paper begins by situating the availability heuristic within a broader framework of judgmental heuristics—mental shortcuts that simplify complex probability or frequency assessments. Earlier work by the authors had focused on *representativeness*, where people judge probability by how well an event matches its parent population (e.g., assuming a random-looking sequence of coin tosses is more likely than an orderly one). Here, they pivot to *availability*, a heuristic where judgments are mediated by the ease with which instances or associations can be retrieved or constructed. For example, people might estimate the likelihood of a politician losing an election by imagining scenarios in which this could happen, or judge the frequency of words starting with "K" by recalling examples. While availability is ecologically valid—frequent events are generally easier to recall—it is also vulnerable to distortions from factors unrelated to actual frequency, such as recency, emotional salience, or imaginability.

The authors first establish that people can assess availability quickly and accurately. In *Study 1*, participants estimated how many words they could construct from a matrix of letters, then attempted to generate those words. The correlation between estimated and actual word counts was remarkably high (0.96), suggesting that people can gauge the ease of construction without exhaustive enumeration. *Study 2* replicated this finding for retrieval tasks, where participants estimated how many instances of a category (e.g., "flowers") they could recall. Again, estimates closely matched actual recall (correlation of 0.93). These studies confirm that availability is a measurable and reliable mediating variable, though the authors note that assessments might rely on partial retrieval (e.g., extrapolating from early recall attempts) or even implicit evaluations of associative strength, as in Hart’s (1967) work on "feelings of knowing."

The core of the paper explores how availability biases judgments of frequency and probability. In *Study 3*, participants judged whether letters like "K" appeared more often in the first or third position of English words. Despite objective evidence that such letters are more frequent in the third position, most participants erroneously favored the first position, likely because words starting with "K" are easier to recall. This bias persisted even when participants were incentivized for accuracy, underscoring the heuristic’s robustness. *Study 4* extended this to combinatorial problems, where participants judged the number of paths in two grid structures. Though both structures contained the same number of paths (512), participants overwhelmingly perceived more paths in the structure where paths were more visually distinct and less confusable. Similarly, *Study 5* showed that people underestimate the number of possible committees of larger sizes (e.g., 8-member committees from a group of 10) because such committees are harder to construct or imagine, despite combinatorial equivalence to smaller committees (e.g., 2-member committees). These studies reveal a consistent pattern: classes whose instances are more available (e.g., easier to construct, retrieve, or visualize) are judged as more numerous, even when this contradicts objective frequency.

The authors also demonstrate how availability interacts with other heuristics, such as representativeness. In *Study 7*, participants judged the frequency of different path types in a binomial distribution, where each path consisted of a sequence of "X"s and "O"s. When the problem was framed as a *path diagram*, participants relied on availability, overestimating the frequency of paths with all "X"s because such paths were easier to visualize. However, when the same problem was framed as a *card game* (where players drew cards marked "X" or "O"), participants shifted to representativeness, judging the most frequent outcome to be the one that matched the population proportion (e.g., 5 "X"s and 1 "O"). This contrast highlights how problem framing can elicit different heuristics, leading to divergent judgments.

The paper then turns to biases in judgments of *retrieved* instances, where availability distorts perceptions of frequency based on prior exposure. In *Study 8*, participants listened to lists of famous and non-famous names, then judged whether the list contained more men or women. Despite equal or near-equal frequencies, participants consistently overestimated the frequency of the gender associated with more famous names, because famous names were easier to recall. This effect mirrors real-world phenomena, such as the overestimation of divorce rates after encountering a divorced acquaintance. The authors also review evidence that availability biases judgments of *repetition frequency*—for example, items that are easier to recall (e.g., due to spacing, vocalization, or distinctiveness) are judged as more frequent, even when actual frequency is held constant. These findings align with associative models of memory, where stronger item-list associations (e.g., from repetition or distinctiveness) increase apparent frequency.

A particularly striking bias is *illusory correlation*, where people perceive a relationship between two variables even when none exists. The authors explain this phenomenon through availability: when two items co-occur, their associative bond strengthens, and people may judge their frequency of co-occurrence by the ease with which the association comes to mind. In *Study 9*, participants judged the frequency of word pairs (e.g., "lion-tiger" vs. "lion-eggs") after hearing them repeated equally often. Highly related pairs (e.g., natural associates or phonetically similar words) were judged as more frequent, even though they were not. This bias persisted in *Study 10*, where participants judged the frequency of personality trait pairs (e.g., "kind-honest" vs. "nervous-gentle"). Highly related pairs were not only judged as more frequent but were also easier to recall, confirming the link between associative strength and perceived frequency. The authors connect these findings to Chapman and Chapman’s (1967, 1969) work on illusory correlations in clinical judgment, where clinicians overestimated the co-occurrence of symptoms and diagnoses that were "natural associates" (e.g., "suspiciousness" and peculiar eye drawings in the Draw-a-Person test). Such biases can have serious consequences, as they may reinforce unvalidated clinical lore and obscure genuine correlations.

The final section of the paper speculates on the role of availability in judgments of *unique events*, where objective frequency is undefined. For example, a clinician assessing a patient’s suicide risk might recall similar cases or construct scenarios leading to suicide. The plausibility or ease of constructing such scenarios could then bias the judgment. The authors suggest that availability may lead to systematic errors in these contexts, such as the *fallacy of initiative*—where people underestimate an opponent’s strategic flexibility because their own plans are more salient—or the tendency to overestimate the likelihood of vivid or emotionally charged events (e.g., airplane crashes after seeing a movie about one). They also note how availability can distort perceptions of risk, as in Kates’ (1962) observation that people’s flood risk assessments are bounded by their most recent experiences, ignoring the possibility of unprecedented events.

The paper concludes with a call for psychological analyses of heuristics to identify and mitigate biases in human judgment. By understanding how availability (or other heuristics) operates, the authors argue, we can anticipate when judgments are likely to be too high or too low and design interventions to reduce errors. This prescient insight laid the groundwork for decades of research on judgment and decision-making, highlighting the dual nature of heuristics as both adaptive tools and sources of systematic bias.
