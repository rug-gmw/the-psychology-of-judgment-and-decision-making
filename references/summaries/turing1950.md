# Computing Machinery and Intelligence

Turing, A. M.

__This seminal paper introduces the "imitation game" (later known as the Turing Test) to evaluate whether machines can exhibit intelligent behaviour, arguing that the question "Can machines think?" is too vague and should be replaced with a testable criterion.__

Alan Turing opens his 1950 paper by rejecting the question "Can machines think?" as inherently ambiguous, proposing instead a more precise formulation: the imitation game. In this game, an interrogator communicates via text with two hidden participants—a human and a machine—and must determine which is which. Turing suggests that if a machine can consistently deceive the interrogator into believing it is human, this would constitute a meaningful operational definition of intelligence. The paper thus shifts the philosophical debate into an empirical framework, offering a concrete test for machine intelligence rather than engaging in abstract definitions of "thinking" or "consciousness."

Turing begins by describing the imitation game in its original form, involving a man, a woman, and an interrogator who must identify their genders based on written responses. He then modifies the game by replacing the man with a machine, asking whether the interrogator’s error rate would remain comparable to the original version. This reframing sidesteps the need for a formal definition of "thinking," instead focusing on observable behaviour. Turing acknowledges potential objections to this approach, such as the argument that the game unfairly disadvantages machines by requiring them to imitate human fallibility. However, he counters that if a machine can nonetheless perform well in the game, such objections lose their force. He also addresses concerns that machines might adopt non-human strategies to succeed, though he considers this unlikely and assumes that the best approach is to mimic human responses.

The paper then narrows its focus to digital computers, which Turing argues are the most promising candidates for passing the imitation game. He provides a detailed explanation of how digital computers function, describing their three core components: the store (memory), the executive unit (processor), and the control (instruction interpreter). Turing emphasizes that digital computers are "universal machines," capable of simulating any discrete-state machine, including other computers, given sufficient storage and programming. This universality means that the question of whether machines can think reduces to whether a suitably programmed digital computer could perform well in the imitation game. Turing speculates that within fifty years, computers with a storage capacity of about 10⁹ (comparable to the human brain) could be programmed to play the game so convincingly that an average interrogator would have no more than a 70% chance of correctly identifying the machine after five minutes of questioning.

Turing systematically addresses nine major objections to the idea of machine intelligence. The theological objection, which claims that thinking is a function of the immortal soul granted only to humans, is dismissed as arbitrary and limiting to divine omnipotence. The "heads in the sand" objection, which fears the consequences of machines thinking, is deemed unworthy of serious refutation, though Turing suggests that such fears stem from a desire to preserve human superiority. The mathematical objection, based on Gödel’s incompleteness theorems, argues that machines are inherently limited in ways that human intellect is not. Turing counters that humans are also subject to logical limitations, and that the apparent superiority of human reasoning may simply reflect our inability to fully understand machine processes. The argument from consciousness, which insists that machines cannot truly think unless they possess subjective experience, is critiqued as solipsistic and impractical. Turing suggests that if a machine can convincingly discuss its own thoughts and emotions (e.g., writing a sonnet or explaining its creative process), this should suffice as evidence of intelligence, even if we cannot verify its internal states.

Other objections, such as the claim that machines cannot exhibit creativity, learn from experience, or display emotions, are addressed by pointing to the limitations of current machines rather than inherent impossibilities. Turing argues that many of these objections rely on the principle of scientific induction—assuming that because existing machines lack certain traits, all future machines must as well. He counters that machines can be programmed to make mistakes, learn, and even surprise their creators, much as humans do. The objection that machines can only do what they are programmed to do (associated with Lady Lovelace) is refuted by Turing’s analogy of the child-machine, which could be educated and shaped through experience, much like a human child. He suggests that learning machines could develop behaviours that even their programmers do not fully understand, blurring the line between programmed and original thought.

Turing also considers objections based on the continuous nature of the nervous system and the informality of human behaviour, arguing that discrete-state machines can approximate these processes closely enough to pass the imitation game. The final objection, based on extra-sensory perception (ESP), is acknowledged as a potential complication but dismissed as requiring further empirical investigation. Turing concludes by outlining a vision for creating intelligent machines through a process akin to education and evolution. He proposes starting with a simple "child-machine" and subjecting it to a learning process involving rewards, punishments, and instruction, much like raising a human child. This approach, he argues, could eventually produce machines capable of competing with humans in all intellectual fields, from abstract reasoning to creative expression.

Ultimately, Turing’s paper is both a philosophical provocation and a technical roadmap, challenging readers to reconsider the boundaries of intelligence while offering a practical framework for achieving machine thought. His enduring contribution lies in transforming an abstract question into a testable hypothesis, laying the groundwork for decades of research in artificial intelligence.
