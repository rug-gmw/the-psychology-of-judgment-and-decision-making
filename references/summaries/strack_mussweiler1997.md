# Explaining the Enigmatic Anchoring Effect: Mechanisms of Selective Accessibility

Strack, F., & Mussweiler, T.

__This article proposes that the anchoring effect—a bias where estimates assimilate toward a previously considered arbitrary value—stems from selective accessibility of anchor-consistent information, akin to semantic priming. Through three studies, the authors demonstrate that anchoring depends on the applicability and representativeness of activated information, challenging traditional adjustment-based explanations.__

The article begins by introducing the anchoring effect, a robust cognitive bias where individuals' numerical estimates are influenced by an initially presented arbitrary value (the "anchor"). Despite its reliability across diverse contexts, the psychological mechanisms underlying anchoring remain poorly understood. Traditional explanations, such as Tversky and Kahneman’s (1974) adjustment heuristic, suggest that individuals start from the anchor and insufficiently adjust their estimates. However, this account struggles to explain anchoring effects when the anchor is a plausible value within the range of acceptable estimates, as adjustment would require knowing how far to move from the anchor—a circular problem.

Strack and Mussweiler propose an alternative framework: the *selective accessibility model*. They argue that anchoring is a special case of semantic priming, where solving a comparative judgment task (e.g., "Is the Mississippi River longer or shorter than 3,000 miles?") activates information consistent with the anchor. This activated information then becomes more accessible and biases subsequent absolute judgments (e.g., "How long is the Mississippi River?"). The authors outline three strategies individuals might use to solve the comparative task, depending on their knowledge: (1) retrieving the true value (if known), (2) applying categorical knowledge (e.g., "rivers are generally shorter than 25,000 miles"), or (3) constructing a mental model of the target to test the anchor’s plausibility. The latter strategy, which involves generating anchor-consistent information, is posited as the primary driver of anchoring effects when the anchor is plausible.

The article then integrates this model with broader findings from priming research, emphasizing two key determinants of how accessible information influences judgments: *applicability* (whether the information is relevant to the judgment) and *representativeness* (whether it is seen as typical of the target). These principles predict that anchoring effects should depend on the alignment between the comparative and absolute judgment tasks. For example, if the comparative task activates information about an object’s height, this information should only bias absolute judgments of height—not width—because it is inapplicable to the latter.

Three empirical studies test these predictions. **Study 1** manipulates the *applicability* of activated information by varying whether the comparative and absolute judgments pertain to the same dimension (e.g., height vs. height) or different dimensions (e.g., height vs. width). Results show stronger anchoring effects when the dimensions match, supporting the idea that anchoring relies on the accessibility of *applicable* information rather than mere numerical priming. **Study 2** examines *representativeness* by varying whether the comparative and absolute judgments target the same object or dissimilar objects (e.g., Antarctic vs. Hawaii temperatures). When the objects differ, the activated information is perceived as unrepresentative of the target, leading to *contrast effects*—judgments that shift *away* from the anchor. This finding aligns with prior priming research showing that unrepresentative primes can produce contrast rather than assimilation. **Study 3** explores the cognitive processes underlying anchoring by comparing plausible and implausible anchors. Plausible anchors (e.g., "Is the Mississippi River longer or shorter than 3,000 miles?") require an elaborate mental model to solve the comparative task, which activates relevant information and facilitates faster absolute judgments. In contrast, implausible anchors (e.g., "Is the Mississippi River longer or shorter than 25,000 miles?") can be dismissed using categorical knowledge, leaving little applicable information for the absolute judgment, which thus takes longer. Response latencies confirm these predictions, with negative correlations between comparative and absolute judgment times for plausible anchors but not implausible ones.

The discussion synthesizes these findings, arguing that anchoring is best understood as a consequence of *selective accessibility* driven by a *positive test strategy*—a tendency to test whether the target matches the anchor by generating anchor-consistent information. This mechanism explains why anchoring is so pervasive: even when individuals recognize the anchor’s arbitrariness, the information they generate to refute it remains accessible and biases their subsequent judgments. The authors also highlight parallels between anchoring and hypothesis-testing research, noting that the comparative task resembles testing a single hypothesis (e.g., "Is the target equal to the anchor?") without considering alternatives, which leads to confirmation bias. They suggest that providing two anchors (e.g., "Is the target between X and Y?") might mitigate anchoring by encouraging consideration of multiple hypotheses.

The article concludes by emphasizing the robustness of anchoring and its resistance to correction, as the biasing information is self-generated and thus less likely to be scrutinized. The selective accessibility model not only unifies anchoring with broader principles of priming and judgment but also offers a nuanced account of when anchoring produces assimilation versus contrast effects. By shifting focus from numerical adjustment to the cognitive processes underlying information activation, Strack and Mussweiler provide a compelling explanation for one of psychology’s most enigmatic biases.
