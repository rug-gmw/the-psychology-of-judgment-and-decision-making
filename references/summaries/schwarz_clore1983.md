# Mood, Misattribution, and Judgments of Well-Being: Informative and Directive Functions of Affective States

Schwarz, N., & Clore, G. L.

__This article explores how momentary moods influence judgments of life satisfaction and happiness, demonstrating that people use their affective states as information for these evaluations. The authors show that negative moods prompt individuals to seek explanations, reducing their impact on well-being judgments when external attributions are available, while positive moods remain unquestioned regardless of situational cues.__

The study, comprising two experiments, investigates the role of affective states in shaping individuals' assessments of their overall well-being. The authors propose that moods serve both informative and directive functions: they provide data for judgments and guide the search for explanations of one's feelings. In the first experiment, participants were asked to vividly describe either a happy or sad life event in a soundproof room, where some were led to believe the room might influence their mood. Those in a negative mood reported lower happiness and life satisfaction, but this effect diminished when they could attribute their feelings to the room rather than their lives. Conversely, participants in a positive mood consistently reported high well-being, unaffected by misattribution manipulations. This suggests that individuals in negative moods actively seek explanations for their feelings, while those in positive moods do not engage in such attributional searches.

The second experiment extended these findings to a naturalistic setting, where participants were interviewed on either sunny or rainy days. Sunny weather induced better moods and higher well-being ratings, but this effect was neutralized when participants were prompted to consider the weather as a potential cause of their mood. Again, the influence of mood on well-being judgments was contingent on whether individuals attributed their feelings to transient external factors. The results underscore the informational role of moods in shaping judgments of happiness and satisfaction, while also highlighting the directive function of negative affective states in prompting explanatory behavior.

The discussion integrates these findings with existing theories on mood and cognition, emphasizing that mood-congruent memory effects alone cannot account for the observed patterns. Instead, the authors argue that individuals actively use their current affective state as a heuristic for evaluating their lives, particularly when no alternative explanations for their mood are salient. The study also touches on the potential motivational biases underlying these processes, suggesting that while negative moods may trigger explanation-seeking to restore well-being, the subsequent processing of information may not always be self-serving. Overall, the research provides compelling evidence for the dual functions of affective states in judgment and decision-making.
