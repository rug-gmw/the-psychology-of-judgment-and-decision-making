# How Safe is Safe Enough?
## A Psychometric Study of Attitudes Towards Technological Risks and Benefits

Fischhoff, B., Slovic, P., Lichtenstein, S., Read, S., Combs, B.

__This study explores public perceptions of risk and benefit for 30 activities and technologies, finding that current risk levels are often deemed unacceptable and that acceptable risk correlates with perceived benefit, particularly when adjusted for qualitative risk dimensions like voluntariness and dread.__

The article investigates an alternative to Chauncey Starr’s "revealed preference" approach for determining acceptable risk levels by using psychometric questionnaires to elicit public judgments. The authors, led by Baruch Fischhoff and Paul Slovic, surveyed 76 members of the League of Women Voters to assess perceived risk, benefit, and acceptable risk levels for 30 activities and technologies, ranging from everyday tasks like swimming to high-stakes technologies like nuclear power. Unlike Starr’s method, which relies on historical economic data to infer societal risk-benefit tradeoffs, this study adopts an "expressed preferences" approach, directly querying individuals about their attitudes. The introduction critiques Starr’s assumptions, particularly the idea that past behavior reflects optimal risk-benefit balances, and highlights the limitations of revealed preferences, such as the lack of public awareness about technological alternatives and the influence of incomplete information on decision-making.

The study’s methodology involved participants rating each activity on perceived benefit, perceived risk, and the acceptability of current risk levels. They also evaluated nine qualitative risk dimensions, including voluntariness, immediacy of effects, knowledge about risks, controllability, and dread. The results revealed little systematic relationship between perceived risks and benefits for current activities, with many high-risk, low-benefit technologies (e.g., smoking, handguns) tolerated despite their perceived unacceptability. However, when participants adjusted risk levels to what they considered acceptable, a positive correlation emerged between acceptable risk and perceived benefit, suggesting that society should tolerate higher risks for more beneficial activities. This finding aligns with Starr’s hypothesis but extends it by showing that other qualitative factors—such as voluntariness, controllability, and familiarity—also influence acceptable risk levels.

A key contribution of the study is the identification of two underlying dimensions of risk through factor analysis: "Technological Risk" (encompassing voluntariness, knowledge, and immediacy) and "Severity" (reflecting the certainty of fatal consequences). These dimensions, combined with perceived benefit, effectively predicted acceptable risk levels, offering a framework for policymakers to anticipate public reactions to new technologies. Notably, nuclear power stood out as uniquely dreaded and catastrophic, underscoring its distinct perception compared to other technologies.

The discussion emphasizes the methodological feasibility of eliciting complex risk judgments and the substantive implications of the findings. The authors argue that neither revealed nor expressed preferences alone can definitively answer "How safe is safe enough?" but that both approaches are necessary for informed policy decisions. They caution that ignoring public attitudes—even if deemed "irrational"—may lead to resistance and regulatory challenges. The study also raises questions about how information presentation affects risk perceptions and the need for further research on the behavioral implications of risk acceptability judgments. Ultimately, the article advocates for a multidisciplinary, multi-method approach to risk assessment that balances expert analysis with public values.
