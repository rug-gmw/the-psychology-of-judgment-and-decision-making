# Moral Grandstanding in Public Discourse: Status-Seeking Motives as a Potential Explanatory Mechanism in Predicting Conflict

Grubbs, J.B., Warmke, B., Tosi, J., James, A.S., Campbell, W.K.

__This article explores the concept of moral grandstanding (MG) as a status-seeking behavior in public moral discourse, demonstrating through six studies that MG motivations are linked to personality traits, political polarization, and increased conflict in moral and political discussions.__

The study introduces the concept of moral grandstanding (MG), defined as the use of moral and political discourse to seek social status, drawing from philosophical literature. The authors conducted six studies involving diverse samples, including undergraduates, Mechanical Turk workers, and nationally representative U.S. populations, to develop and validate the Moral Grandstanding Motivation Scale (MGMS). The research aimed to contextualize MG within the frameworks of status-seeking behaviors—specifically dominance and prestige—and to examine its psychological underpinnings and consequences.

The introduction outlines the increasing volatility and conflict in public discourse, particularly on social media, where discussions about moral and political issues often escalate into polarization and outrage. The authors propose that MG, a phenomenon where individuals use moral talk to enhance their social status, may contribute to these conflicts. They differentiate MG from similar constructs like social vigilantism and virtue signaling, emphasizing that MG is driven by status-seeking motives rather than a desire to correct others or signal virtue.

The methods section details the development of the MGMS, a 10-item scale designed to measure two dimensions of MG motivation: prestige strivings (seeking admiration and respect) and dominance strivings (seeking to shame or silence others). The studies employed factor analysis to refine the scale and confirm its structure across different samples. Participants completed the MGMS alongside measures of personality traits (e.g., narcissism, Big Five factors), political ideology, and experiences of moral or political conflict. Longitudinal studies were also conducted to assess the predictive validity of MG motivations over time.

Results consistently showed that MG motivations are associated with status-seeking personality traits. Prestige strivings were positively correlated with narcissistic extraversion and general extraversion, while dominance strivings were strongly linked to narcissistic antagonism and negatively associated with agreeableness and conscientiousness. The MGMS demonstrated excellent psychometric properties across all studies, confirming its reliability and validity. Importantly, MG motivations were ideologically neutral but associated with political polarization, particularly at the extremes of the ideological spectrum.

The discussion highlights that MG motivations predict greater conflict in moral and political discourse, even after controlling for personality traits and other related constructs. Both prestige and dominance strivings were uniquely associated with increased reports of conflict, suggesting that MG is a significant factor in understanding the caustic nature of public discourse. However, MG motivations were also linked to positive outcomes, such as growing closer to like-minded individuals, indicating that grandstanding may serve social bonding functions within ideological communities.

The authors acknowledge limitations, including the reliance on self-report measures and the focus on U.S. samples, which may limit the generalizability of the findings. They suggest future research should explore the effects of MG on discourse dynamics, its role in polarization, and its impact on well-being and social relationships.

In conclusion, the study provides robust evidence that moral grandstanding is a meaningful psychological construct that contributes to conflict in public discourse. By framing MG as a status-seeking behavior, the research offers a novel lens through which to understand the motivations behind polarized and contentious moral and political discussions.
