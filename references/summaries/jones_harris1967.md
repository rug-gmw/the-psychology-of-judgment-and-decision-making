# The Attribution of Attitudes

Jones, E. E., & Harris, V. A.

__This study explores how people infer others' true attitudes from their expressed opinions, testing whether perceived choice and the expectedness of the opinion influence these attributions. Across three experiments, the authors find that people weigh speech content heavily—even when the speaker had no choice—while also considering situational constraints, though not as much as a rational analysis might predict.__

The article presents three experiments grounded in *correspondent inference theory*, which posits that observers infer a person’s underlying attitudes from their behavior by assessing the degree to which the behavior aligns with internal dispositions rather than external pressures. Jones and Harris investigate whether the perceived freedom of a speaker (choice vs. assignment) interacts with the prior probability of their expressed opinion (expected vs. unexpected) to shape attributions of their true attitudes. The core hypothesis is that choice matters more when the opinion expressed is unexpected—e.g., a Southerner advocating for desegregation—because such behavior provides more diagnostic information about the speaker’s true beliefs.

In **Experiment I**, participants read an essay about Castro’s Cuba written by a student who either chose or was assigned to take a pro- or anti-Castro stance. Results showed that participants attributed attitudes consistent with the essay’s content, even in no-choice conditions, though the effect of choice was stronger for pro-Castro essays (an unexpected position for the predominantly anti-Castro sample). However, the predicted interaction between choice and prior probability was not statistically significant. A notable finding was the high variability in attributions when participants encountered an unexpected opinion in a no-choice context, suggesting ambiguity in how to weigh conflicting cues (speech content vs. situational constraints).

**Experiment II** replicated and extended the first study by introducing a debate context, where participants either wrote their own pro- or anti-Castro speech before evaluating a target’s speech. This manipulation aimed to heighten the salience of the choice variable. The results confirmed the main hypothesis: choice had a stronger effect on attributions when the target expressed an unexpected opinion (e.g., pro-Castro under no-choice conditions). Additionally, when the target delivered an ambivalent speech—contradicting their assigned position—participants inferred attitudes opposite to the assigned stance, though this effect was modest. Correlational analyses revealed that participants’ own attitudes influenced their attributions, particularly in ambiguous conditions, aligning with theories of assimilation and contrast in social judgment.

**Experiment III** shifted the focus to segregation, using audio recordings of a Northerner or Southerner delivering pro- or anti-segregation speeches under conditions of choice or assignment. The goal was to manipulate prior probability by leveraging regional stereotypes (Southerners were assumed to be more pro-segregation). For Northern speakers, the results again supported the hypothesis: choice mattered more for unexpected opinions (e.g., a Northerner advocating segregation). However, for Southern speakers, the pattern was less clear, likely because Southern college students were not uniformly perceived as pro-segregation, reflecting real-world variability in attitudes. The study also explored whether "mavericks"—those who chose opinions contrary to their reference group—would be seen as having more coherent attitude structures. While the data provided suggestive support for this idea, the evidence was not conclusive.

Across all experiments, a consistent finding was the tendency to attribute attitudes in line with the speech’s content, even when the speaker had no choice. This suggests that people overweigh behavioral cues relative to situational constraints, a bias the authors describe as behavior "engulfing the field." They propose that this distortion may stem from the residual ambiguity in how the speech was constructed—even under no-choice conditions, speakers had some latitude in selecting arguments, which participants may have interpreted as revealing their true beliefs. The discussion emphasizes that while people do consider situational constraints, they often fail to discount them sufficiently, leading to overattribution of behavior to internal dispositions. The authors conclude that correspondent inference theory holds merit, but perceivers may not apply its principles as rigorously as a rational model would predict.
