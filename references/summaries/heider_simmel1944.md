# An Experimental Study of Apparent Behavior

Heider, F., Simmel, M.

__This classic study demonstrates how people spontaneously attribute human-like intentions, emotions, and personalities to simple geometric shapes in motion, revealing the perceptual and cognitive mechanisms underlying social perception.__

The article presents an experimental investigation into how individuals perceive and interpret the behavior of others, particularly when minimal visual cues are provided. Heider and Simmel critique prior research in social perception for focusing too narrowly on facial expressions or "correctness" of judgments rather than the underlying perceptual processes that shape interpretations. Their study departs from this tradition by using an abstract, animated film featuring three geometric shapes—a large triangle, a small triangle, and a circle—moving around a rectangular "house" with a door. The movements were carefully choreographed to suggest interactions like chasing, fighting, hiding, and escaping, though no explicit human or emotional cues were present.

The study involved three experiments with undergraduate women as participants. In the first experiment, subjects were simply asked to describe what they saw in the film. Nearly all interpreted the movements as actions of animate beings, often ascribing human-like qualities and narratives to the shapes. For example, the large triangle was frequently described as a bully or villain, while the small triangle and circle were seen as victims or lovers. Only one participant described the film in purely geometric terms. The second experiment explicitly instructed subjects to interpret the movements as human actions and answer a detailed questionnaire about the "personalities" of the shapes and the motivations behind their actions. Results showed remarkable consistency in how participants attributed traits and intentions: the large triangle was overwhelmingly seen as aggressive, dominant, and mean, while the small triangle was often described as brave or clever, and the circle as timid, dependent, or feminine. The third experiment reversed the film’s playback, which led to more varied and idiosyncratic interpretations, suggesting that the temporal sequence of movements plays a critical role in shaping perceptions of causality and intent.

Heider and Simmel analyze how specific movement patterns—such as successive movements with contact (e.g., one shape "hitting" another), simultaneous movements with prolonged contact (e.g., "opening" or "closing" the door), and movements without contact (e.g., "chasing" or "leading")—are interpreted as intentional actions. They argue that these interpretations are not arbitrary but are shaped by perceptual principles, such as temporal succession, spatial proximity, and the broader narrative context. For instance, the large triangle’s relentless pursuit of the other shapes was consistently seen as "chasing" because it followed earlier scenes where the triangle was established as an aggressor. The study also highlights how attributions of causality extend beyond mere movement to include underlying motives, such as hiding, escaping, or seeking protection, which further organize the events into coherent stories.

The authors conclude that social perception is fundamentally a process of unit-formation, where movements and changes are anchored to figural units (the shapes) and imbued with meaning through attributions of personality and intent. This process allows observers to transform arbitrary sequences of motion into structured, meaningful narratives, reflecting deeper cognitive tendencies to seek invariance and coherence in the environment. The study’s innovative method—using abstract stimuli to study social perception—opened new avenues for understanding how people infer mental states and intentions from behavior, laying groundwork for later research in attribution theory and social cognition.
