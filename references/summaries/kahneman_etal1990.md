# Experimental Tests of the Endowment Effect and the Coase Theorem

Kahneman, D., Knetsch, J. L., & Thaler, R. H.

__This study demonstrates that people value goods more highly once they own them, leading to undertrading in experimental markets and contradicting the Coase theorem’s prediction of efficient resource allocation regardless of initial property rights.__

This article presents a series of experiments designed to test the endowment effect—the phenomenon where individuals assign higher value to goods they own compared to identical goods they do not own—and its implications for the Coase theorem. The authors, Daniel Kahneman, Jack Knetsch, and Richard Thaler, challenge the standard economic assumption that willingness to pay (WTP) and willingness to accept (WTA) for a good should be roughly equal when income effects are negligible. Instead, they find that WTA consistently exceeds WTP, even in market settings where participants have opportunities to learn and trade.

The introduction outlines the theoretical backdrop, noting that traditional economic models assume preferences are independent of endowments, implying that the allocation of resources should be unaffected by initial property rights under the Coase theorem. However, empirical evidence, including prior studies summarized in the article, shows large disparities between WTA and WTP, often by orders of magnitude. The authors propose that these discrepancies stem from loss aversion—a psychological tendency to weigh losses more heavily than equivalent gains—rather than strategic bargaining or income effects. They hypothesize that ownership creates a reference point, making the surrender of a good feel like a loss, thereby inflating its perceived value.

The methods involve a series of controlled experiments with undergraduate students, where participants are randomly assigned roles as buyers or sellers of goods (e.g., coffee mugs, pens, or chocolate bars) or induced-value tokens. In induced-value markets, tokens are assigned redemption values, and participants trade them for cash, serving as a control to isolate transaction costs or misunderstandings. The authors compare trading volumes and price disparities between these induced-value markets and markets for consumption goods. Across multiple experiments, they find that induced-value markets achieve near-perfect trading efficiency, with actual trading volumes matching theoretical predictions. In contrast, markets for consumption goods exhibit significant undertrading, with median selling prices often more than double the median buying prices. This undertrading persists even after repeated market trials, suggesting that experience does not eliminate the endowment effect.

The results are robust across variations in experimental design, including random price selection (to eliminate strategic misrepresentation) and bilateral bargaining scenarios. For instance, in one experiment, participants who were endowed with a chocolate bar were far less likely to sell it than buyers were to purchase it, despite identical incentives. The authors also rule out alternative explanations such as income effects, transaction costs, or trophy effects by demonstrating that undertrading occurs even when these factors are controlled for. Notably, experiments comparing sellers, buyers, and "choosers" (who decide between keeping cash or receiving a good) show that the endowment effect is primarily driven by sellers' reluctance to part with their goods, rather than buyers' reluctance to acquire them.

The discussion highlights the broader implications of the endowment effect for economic theory and policy. The findings challenge the Coase theorem’s prediction that initial property rights do not affect final allocations, as the endowment effect introduces inertia into markets, reducing the volume of mutually beneficial trades. The authors argue that this effect is not an artifact of experimental design but a fundamental feature of human preferences, with real-world consequences. For example, endowment effects may explain phenomena such as sticky wages, reluctance to sell assets at a loss, or the persistence of inefficient contractual terms in long-standing firms. The article concludes that loss aversion and the endowment effect are robust, enduring features of decision-making that cannot be dismissed as mere anomalies or strategic errors.
