# Explaining the Evidence: Tests of the Story Model for Juror Decision Making

Pennington, N., Hastie, R.

__This article presents the Story Model, a theory explaining how jurors construct narrative stories from trial evidence to reach verdicts. Through three experiments, the authors demonstrate that story construction mediates juror decisions, influences perceptions of evidence strength and credibility, and varies depending on whether judgments are made globally or incrementally.__

The article introduces the Story Model as an explanation-based theory of juror decision making, contrasting it with traditional algebraic or Bayesian models. The authors propose that jurors organize trial evidence into coherent narrative stories, which then serve as the basis for their verdict decisions. This narrative structure, they argue, is central to understanding how jurors interpret complex and often disjointed evidence presented in trials.

The Story Model consists of three key components: evidence evaluation through story construction, representation of decision alternatives by learning verdict category attributes, and reaching a decision through classification of the constructed story into the best-fitting verdict category. The authors describe how jurors actively construct stories by integrating case-specific information, general world knowledge, and expectations about what constitutes a complete narrative. They introduce four certainty principles—coverage, coherence, uniqueness, and goodness-of-fit—that govern which story jurors accept and how confident they are in their decision.

The article then presents three experiments designed to test and extend the Story Model. In Experiment 1, the authors manipulated the ease of story construction by varying the order of evidence presentation and examined the impact of witness credibility. They found that when evidence was presented in a story order, jurors made stronger decisions in the direction of the preponderant evidence, expressed greater confidence, and were more influenced by credibility information. Importantly, these effects occurred even when memory for the evidence did not differ between conditions, suggesting that story construction itself, rather than differential memorability, mediated the decisions.

Experiments 2 and 3 further explored the role of story completeness and the conditions under which story construction strategies are employed. The authors directly manipulated story completeness by adding story inferences to evidence blocks, making certain interpretations more complete. They also varied the level of aggregation at which judgments were made, comparing global assessments (made after all evidence was presented) with item-by-item judgments (made after each evidence block). The results showed that more complete stories led to stronger judgments in the story direction, and that global assessments were more extreme than item-by-item judgments. This supported the authors' hypothesis that global judgments encourage story construction, whereas item-by-item judgments evoke sequential updating strategies.

The authors also compared subjects' evidence evaluations with predictions from Bayesian and linear updating models. They found that subjects' judgments did not correspond to Bayesian aggregation, but that global assessments were best described by an equal-weight model consistent with the Story Model, while item-by-item judgments followed an anchor-and-adjust strategy. These findings delineate the conditions under which story construction versus sequential updating strategies are likely to be used.

The discussion highlights the implications of the Story Model for understanding juror decision making and its potential applications to legal practice and policy. The authors argue that their research provides a psychologically plausible and empirically supported framework for analyzing juror behavior, which can inform discussions about jury instructions, evidence presentation, and the broader role of juries in the legal system. They also address the limitations of their studies and suggest directions for future research, including the need to explore the generalizability of the Story Model to other decision-making contexts beyond jury trials.
