# An fMRI Investigation of Emotional Engagement in Moral Judgment

Greene, J. D., Sommerville, R. B., Nystrom, L. E., Darley, J. M., Cohen, J. D.

__This study uses fMRI to explore how emotional engagement influences moral judgments, particularly in dilemmas like the trolley and footbridge problems. The authors argue that personal moral dilemmas evoke stronger emotional responses, which in turn shape moral decision-making.__

The article presents an empirical investigation into the neural and psychological mechanisms underlying moral judgment, focusing on the distinction between "personal" and "impersonal" moral dilemmas. The authors begin by introducing classic moral dilemmas, such as the trolley and footbridge problems, which highlight inconsistencies in people's moral intuitions. While most individuals judge it acceptable to sacrifice one life to save five in the trolley scenario, they find it unacceptable to do so in the footbridge scenario, despite the similar outcomes. This discrepancy has long puzzled moral philosophers, who struggle to identify consistent principles that explain these judgments.

To address this psychological puzzle, the researchers hypothesized that emotional engagement plays a critical role in shaping moral judgments. Specifically, they proposed that personal moral dilemmas—such as the footbridge scenario—elicit stronger emotional responses than impersonal dilemmas like the trolley scenario. To test this, they conducted two functional magnetic resonance imaging (fMRI) studies involving participants who were presented with a series of moral and non-moral dilemmas. The moral dilemmas were further categorized into "moral-personal" (e.g., pushing someone off a footbridge) and "moral-impersonal" (e.g., flipping a switch to divert a trolley) based on criteria designed to capture the emotional salience of the actions involved.

In both experiments, the fMRI results revealed that brain regions associated with emotional processing—such as the medial frontal gyrus, posterior cingulate gyrus, and angular gyrus—were significantly more active during the contemplation of moral-personal dilemmas compared to moral-impersonal or non-moral dilemmas. Conversely, areas associated with working memory, such as the middle frontal gyrus and parietal lobe, showed reduced activity during moral-personal dilemmas, suggesting that emotional engagement may suppress cognitive processing in these contexts. These findings were consistent across both experiments, reinforcing the idea that emotional responses are a key differentiator in moral judgment.

The behavioral data from the second experiment provided further support for the emotional interference hypothesis. Participants took significantly longer to respond "appropriate" to moral-personal dilemmas than to respond "inappropriate," a pattern not observed in the other conditions. This delay was interpreted as evidence of emotional conflict, where participants had to override their automatic emotional responses to make a utilitarian judgment. The authors also addressed potential confounds, such as the conventionality of responses, by including dilemmas where the emotionally incongruent response was also the conventional one, and the reaction time effects persisted.

The discussion situates these findings within the broader debate between rationalist and emotivist theories of moral psychology. The authors argue that their results suggest a middle ground, where both emotion and reason interact to shape moral judgments. They emphasize that while emotional responses influence moral decision-making, they do not solely determine it, as evidenced by participants who overrode their emotional inclinations to make utilitarian choices. The study concludes by highlighting the potential implications of these findings for understanding moral behavior in both hypothetical and real-world contexts, though it remains descriptive rather than prescriptive in its conclusions.
