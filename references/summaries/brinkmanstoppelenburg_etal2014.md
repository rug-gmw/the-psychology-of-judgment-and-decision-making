# The effects of advance care planning on end-of-life care: A systematic review

Brinkman-Stoppelenburg, A., Rietjens, J. A. C., & van der Heide, A.

__This systematic review examines the impact of advance care planning (ACP) on end-of-life care, revealing that ACP—particularly complex interventions—can improve compliance with patient wishes, reduce life-sustaining treatments, and increase hospice and palliative care use, though evidence varies by ACP type and setting.__

The article provides a comprehensive overview of studies investigating the effects of advance care planning (ACP) on end-of-life care, synthesizing findings from 113 empirical studies published between 2000 and 2012. ACP is defined as a process of discussing and recording patient preferences for future care, particularly for those who may lose decision-making capacity. The authors highlight the growing importance of ACP in ensuring patient autonomy and aligning care with individual wishes, while noting that its implementation and outcomes remain inconsistent across settings and populations.

The review categorizes ACP into four types: do-not-resuscitate (DNR) orders, do-not-hospitalize (DNH) orders, written advance directives (including living wills and durable powers of attorney), and complex ACP interventions. Most studies were observational (95%), primarily conducted in the United States (81%), and focused on institutional settings such as hospitals (49%) and nursing homes (32%). DNR orders and written advance directives were the most frequently studied forms of ACP.

The findings reveal that DNR orders are consistently associated with reduced use of cardiopulmonary resuscitation (CPR), decreased hospitalizations, and increased enrollment in hospice care. Similarly, DNH orders were linked to fewer hospitalizations and greater use of hospice services, particularly in nursing home settings. However, the effects of written advance directives were more mixed. While they tended to reduce life-sustaining treatments like tube feeding and increase hospice care, their impact on other medical interventions, symptom management, and patient or family satisfaction was inconsistent. Notably, studies in intensive care units (ICUs) often found no association between advance directives and the use of life-sustaining treatments, suggesting that hospital settings may override patient preferences in favor of aggressive care.

Complex ACP interventions, which involve structured discussions and care planning processes (e.g., the Respecting Choices or Let Me Decide programs), showed the most promising results. These interventions were associated with increased compliance with patients' end-of-life wishes, reduced hospitalizations and ICU admissions, and higher satisfaction with care. They also tended to shift care toward comfort-focused, out-of-hospital settings. Unlike simpler forms of ACP, complex interventions demonstrated positive effects on psychosocial outcomes, such as reduced family stress and improved quality of life, without increasing anxiety or depression.

The discussion emphasizes the variability in ACP outcomes, influenced by study design, setting, and the type of ACP used. The authors note that observational studies, while valuable, limit causal inferences, as ACP may reflect pre-existing patient preferences rather than directly altering care. They also highlight the need for more experimental research, particularly outside the U.S. and in community settings, to better understand the effectiveness of ACP. Standardized outcome measures focusing on patient and family experiences are recommended to improve comparability across studies.

In conclusion, the review suggests that ACP, especially when implemented as a comprehensive process, can positively impact end-of-life care by aligning treatments with patient preferences and improving satisfaction. However, the effectiveness of ACP depends on its form and context, with complex interventions showing the greatest potential for meaningful change. Future research should prioritize rigorous study designs and diverse populations to strengthen the evidence base.
