# General Mental Ability in the World of Work: Occupational Attainment and Job Performance

Schmidt, F. L., Hunter, J.

__This article argues that general mental ability (GMA) is the strongest predictor of occupational success and job performance, outperforming other traits like personality, specific aptitudes, and job experience. It synthesizes decades of research to demonstrate GMA’s stability, heritability, and broad relevance across diverse occupations and life outcomes. The authors also propose a theoretical framework explaining why GMA drives performance through job knowledge acquisition.__

The article begins by tracing the resurgence of interest in GMA, a construct first introduced by Charles Spearman in 1904. Despite mid-20th-century skepticism about individual differences, recent research has reaffirmed GMA’s centrality in predicting a wide range of life outcomes, from health behaviors to criminal activity. The authors highlight its stability over time—with individual rankings remaining consistent for over 65 years—and its strong genetic basis, with heritability estimates reaching 80% or higher in older adults. These findings, alongside influential works like *The Bell Curve* and advances in behavior genetics, have reignited debates about GMA’s role in society, particularly in the workplace.

The core of the article examines GMA’s predictive power for two key outcomes: occupational attainment and job performance. Cross-sectional studies reveal a robust correlation between GMA and occupational prestige, with higher-level jobs consistently occupied by individuals with higher GMA scores. For example, data from the U.S. Employment Service’s General Aptitude Test Battery (GATB) show a corrected correlation of .72 between GMA and occupational level. Longitudinal studies further confirm this relationship, demonstrating that GMA measured in childhood or early adulthood predicts later occupational success and income. Notably, Murray (1998) found that siblings with higher GMA earned significantly more than their lower-GMA counterparts, even when controlling for socioeconomic background. These findings challenge the notion that environmental factors alone drive career success, emphasizing GMA’s independent influence.

The article then shifts to GMA’s role in predicting job performance, dispelling the myth that cognitive ability matters only in academic settings. Meta-analyses of thousands of validity studies reveal that GMA consistently predicts performance across all job levels, with correlations ranging from .58 for high-complexity jobs (e.g., professional roles) to .23 for low-complexity jobs (e.g., manual labor). Performance in job training programs is even more strongly predicted by GMA, with average validities around .63. These results contradict the once-dominant "theory of situational specificity," which posited that GMA’s predictive validity varied unpredictably across jobs. Instead, meta-analytic techniques have shown that apparent variability in validity coefficients stems largely from statistical artifacts like sampling error and range restriction, not true differences in job requirements.

The authors also address competing predictors of job performance, such as specific aptitudes, job experience, and personality traits. Specific aptitude theory, which suggests that tailored combinations of narrow cognitive abilities (e.g., verbal or spatial aptitude) should outperform GMA, is thoroughly discredited. Research demonstrates that specific aptitudes primarily reflect GMA, with their unique variance contributing little to performance prediction. Job experience, while initially important, shows declining predictive validity over time, as its correlation with performance drops from .49 in early career stages to .15 after 12 years. In contrast, GMA’s predictive power remains stable or even increases with experience, underscoring its enduring relevance.

Personality traits, particularly those in the Big Five model, are also examined. While Conscientiousness emerges as the most job-relevant personality trait—predicting both occupational attainment and performance—its validity (.31) is substantially lower than GMA’s. Other traits like Openness and Neuroticism show weaker or sporadic relationships with job outcomes. When combined with GMA, Conscientiousness adds incremental validity, but GMA remains the dominant predictor. Integrity tests, which overlap with Conscientiousness, also show modest validity gains over GMA alone.

To explain why GMA is so pivotal, the authors present a theoretical model in which GMA primarily influences job performance through the acquisition of job knowledge. Higher-GMA individuals learn job-related information faster and more thoroughly, leading to superior performance. Path analyses confirm this mediating role, showing that GMA’s indirect effect via job knowledge outweighs its direct effect on performance. This framework provides a plausible mechanism for GMA’s pervasive impact, addressing skepticism about its relevance in real-world settings.

The article concludes by reaffirming Spearman’s century-old proposition that GMA is critical to human affairs, particularly in the world of work. The evidence overwhelmingly supports GMA as the single best predictor of occupational success and job performance, surpassing other traits and variables. While personality and experience contribute to performance, their effects are secondary to GMA’s. The authors call for greater acceptance of these findings, emphasizing their implications for personnel selection, education, and public policy. By integrating empirical research with theoretical explanation, the article offers a compelling case for GMA’s central role in shaping career trajectories and workplace productivity.
