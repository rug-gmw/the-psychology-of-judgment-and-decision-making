# Why Experts Make Errors

Dror, I. E., Charlton, D.

__This study demonstrates that even highly trained fingerprint experts can make inconsistent decisions when influenced by contextual biases, revealing cognitive vulnerabilities in forensic identification. Using real casework, the authors show that two-thirds of experts changed their prior judgments when exposed to misleading information, challenging assumptions about expert objectivity.__

The article explores the sources of errors in expert decision-making, categorizing them into three types: human error (intentional, negligent, or competency-based), technological failures, and inherent methodological flaws tied to cognitive or systemic limitations. The authors focus on the third category, arguing that even when experts are competent and technology functions correctly, psychological and cognitive factors—such as contextual biases—can still lead to errors. This challenges the binary view that errors stem either from practitioner incompetence or fundamental flaws in the scientific domain (e.g., fingerprint uniqueness). Instead, the authors propose that errors may arise from the way the human mind processes information, an epistemological issue rather than an ontological one.

To test this hypothesis, the researchers conducted a covert study with six experienced fingerprint examiners, each with over five years of experience. The experts were unknowingly presented with eight pairs of fingerprints they had previously evaluated—four they had identified as matches ("individualizations") and four they had excluded. Half of these pairs were presented with subtle, routine contextual biases (e.g., a suspect’s confession or alibi), while the other half served as controls with no biasing information. The results were striking: two-thirds of the experts made inconsistent decisions on at least one pair, with 16.6% of biased trials leading to changed judgments. Even more concerning, 8.3% of control trials—where no explicit bias was introduced—also resulted in inconsistent decisions, suggesting that expert judgments may lack reliability over time.

The study found that contextual biases were most influential in difficult comparisons, though they also affected easier cases. Most inconsistencies involved changing prior individualizations to exclusions, but one expert reversed an exclusion to an individualization, indicating that biases can shift decision thresholds or strategies. The authors emphasize the robustness of their findings, given the within-subject design (where experts served as their own controls) and the covert nature of the study, which minimized performance distortions common in laboratory settings.

The discussion highlights the broader implications of these cognitive vulnerabilities. Experts, like all humans, process information dynamically, and their judgments can be swayed by extraneous context, even in routine scenarios. The authors argue that while 100% objectivity is unattainable, systematic research can help minimize errors through improved selection, training, and procedural safeguards. They call for further investigation into the psychological mechanisms underlying forensic decision-making, including how experts perceive, evaluate, and weigh fingerprint features. The study underscores the need for forensic science to acknowledge and address these cognitive pitfalls to enhance the reliability of expert testimony.
