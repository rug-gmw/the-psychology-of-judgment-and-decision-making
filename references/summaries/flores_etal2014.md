# The Influence of Causal Connections Between Symptoms on the Diagnosis of Mental Disorders: Evidence From Online and Offline Measures

Flores, A., Cobos, P. L., López, F. J., Godoy, A., & González-Martín, E.

__This study demonstrates that both experienced clinicians and psychology students engage in causal reasoning during mental disorder diagnosis, influenced by symptom temporal sequences and explicit causal information. Clinicians exhibited fast, online intuitive reasoning processes, while both groups showed offline causal biases in diagnostic judgments.__

The article investigates how causal connections between symptoms affect the diagnosis of mental disorders, focusing on whether these reasoning processes occur online (during information processing) or offline (after reflection). The authors challenge the DSM-IV's atheoretical approach by demonstrating that both clinicians and students rely on causal theories when making diagnostic judgments, even when instructed not to.

The study employed an experimental design with 101 participants: 71 psychology students and 30 experienced clinicians. Participants read clinical reports describing hypothetical clients diagnosed with one of six mental disorders (anorexia nervosa, major depression, specific phobia, obsessive-compulsive disorder, post-traumatic stress disorder, and generalized anxiety disorder). The reports varied in consistency with expected causal theories, manipulating the temporal order of symptoms and explicit information about causal connections between symptoms.

The experiment utilized the inconsistency paradigm from reading comprehension research to detect online reasoning processes. Participants read self-paced clinical reports where target sentences described symptom development in either consistent or inconsistent temporal orders relative to expected causal theories. Reading times (RTs) for these target sentences were recorded, with longer RTs indicating detection of inconsistency. After reading, participants made diagnostic judgments about their agreement with the stated diagnosis and treatment efficacy judgments for symptom removal.

Results revealed significant differences between students and clinicians. While both groups showed offline causal biases in their diagnostic judgments, only clinicians demonstrated online intuitive reasoning processes. Clinicians' RTs were significantly longer when reading inconsistent temporal sequences, suggesting they engaged in fast, automatic causal reasoning during reading. Students, however, showed no significant RT differences between consistent and inconsistent conditions, despite their diagnostic judgments being influenced by causal information.

The treatment efficacy judgment task provided independent evidence that participants' causal theories aligned with the S1→S2→S3 causal chain model used in the experimental manipulation. Both groups rated treatments as more effective for forward indirect effects (down the causal chain) than backward effects (up the causal chain), with this asymmetry being more pronounced in consistent conditions. This pattern persisted even in inconsistent conditions, suggesting participants were reluctant to abandon their causal theories despite contradictory information.

The study's findings have important implications for clinical practice and DSM guidelines. The persistent use of causal reasoning, even when discouraged, suggests that DSM-IV's atheoretical approach may be unrealistic. The authors propose that DSM-5's recommendation to consider diagnosis within case formulation aligns better with these findings. They suggest that clinicians' training should include education about how causal theories influence judgments through intuitive reasoning processes, potentially improving adherence to evidence-based treatments.

The research also raises questions about the source of clinicians' causal theories and whether similar effects would be observed with less familiar disorders. Future research could explore these questions and investigate whether training in causal reasoning could help clinicians better control their intuitive processes during diagnosis and treatment planning.
