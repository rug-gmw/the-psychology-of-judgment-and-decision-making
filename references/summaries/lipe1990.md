# A Lens Model Analysis of Covariation Research

Lipe, M. G.

__This study uses a lens model meta-analysis of published covariation research to demonstrate that all four cells of a 2×2 contingency table influence human judgments of correlation, and that a linear model can effectively approximate the complex Pearson’s phi coefficient.__

The article addresses two key issues in covariation judgment research: the appropriateness of using Pearson’s phi as a normative benchmark and the patterns of cell usage in subjects’ judgments. The introduction critiques prior research suggesting that humans are poor at judging covariation, arguing that traditional statistical models may not adequately capture human cognitive processes. Lipe proposes that a linear approximation of Pearson’s phi could serve as a more cognitively economical yet statistically valid benchmark, aligning with theories that humans often use simplified models to approximate complex judgments.

To explore these questions, Lipe employs a lens model framework, analyzing data from six published studies involving 34 data points. Each data point consists of the four cell frequencies from a 2×2 contingency table and the average judged covariation from subjects. Two regression models are estimated: one predicting the actual correlation (Pearson’s phi) and another predicting subjects’ judgments, both using the four cell frequencies as independent variables. The results reveal that all four cells significantly influence both actual and judged correlations, with the regression models explaining 89% and 74% of the variance, respectively. The lens model statistics further indicate a strong match between the linear models of the environment and subjects’ judgments (G = 0.98), as well as a high degree of nonlinear correspondence (C = 0.68). Notably, aggregated judgments aligned more closely with Pearson’s phi (rₛ = 0.91) than with the linear model (Rₙ = 0.84), though the difference was modest.

Sensitivity analyses test the robustness of these findings. Excluding data points with negative correlations slightly reduces the model’s explanatory power but does not alter the conclusion that all cells are influential. Experimenter emphasis on specific cells appears to affect cell usage, though multicollinearity and confounding in the data limit definitive conclusions. A separate analysis using individual rather than aggregated judgments reveals that while individual judgments are less consistent (Rₛ drops from 0.86 to 0.63), the linear models of subjects and the environment remain well-matched (G = 0.90). However, the nonlinear match (C) and overall achievement (rₛ) decline substantially, suggesting that the high correspondence with Pearson’s phi in the original analysis may partly reflect the use of aggregated data.

The discussion highlights the implications of these findings. Lipe argues that a linear model may be a more appropriate benchmark for assessing human covariation judgments, given its simplicity and close approximation to Pearson’s phi. The study also challenges prior claims that subjects ignore certain cells (e.g., D) or overemphasize others (e.g., A), demonstrating that all cells contribute to judgments when analyzed across a range of tasks. The lens model methodology is presented as a valuable tool for future research on how factors like experimenter instructions or subject expectations influence cell usage. Overall, the article offers a more optimistic view of human covariation judgment abilities, suggesting that people use information in normatively appropriate ways, albeit with some inconsistency at the individual level.
