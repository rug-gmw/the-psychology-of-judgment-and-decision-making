# Prospect Theory: An Analysis of Decision under Risk

Kahneman, D., & Tversky, A.

__This seminal paper critiques expected utility theory, demonstrating systematic violations in human decision-making under risk, and proposes prospect theory as an alternative model that accounts for observed biases such as loss aversion, the certainty effect, and nonlinear weighting of probabilities.__

The article begins by challenging the dominance of expected utility theory as both a normative and descriptive model of decision-making under risk. Kahneman and Tversky argue that while expected utility theory assumes rational choice based on final asset positions and linear weighting of probabilities, real-world decisions frequently violate its core axioms. Through a series of hypothetical choice problems administered to students and faculty, the authors identify consistent patterns of behavior that contradict expected utility theory. For instance, people tend to overweight certain outcomes relative to merely probable ones—a phenomenon labeled the *certainty effect*—leading to risk aversion for gains and risk-seeking for losses. The *reflection effect* further illustrates this asymmetry: preferences reverse when gains are replaced by losses, revealing a steeper value function for losses than for gains.

The paper then introduces *prospect theory* as a descriptive alternative, structured around two phases: editing and evaluation. In the editing phase, individuals simplify prospects by coding outcomes as gains or losses relative to a reference point, combining or segregating probabilities, and canceling shared components. This phase explains inconsistencies like the *isolation effect*, where people ignore common elements of prospects, leading to preference reversals depending on how choices are framed. The evaluation phase formalizes how edited prospects are assessed using a value function and decision weights. The value function, defined over gains and losses rather than final wealth, is concave for gains (reflecting diminishing sensitivity) and convex for losses (reflecting risk-seeking), with losses weighted more heavily than equivalent gains (*loss aversion*). Decision weights, derived from stated probabilities, are nonlinear: small probabilities are overweighted, while moderate to high probabilities are underweighted, violating the expectation principle.

Empirical evidence from the choice problems supports these theoretical claims. For example, the Allais paradox (Problems 1 and 2) demonstrates how the certainty effect leads to inconsistent preferences, while Problems 3–8 show that people’s risk attitudes depend on whether outcomes are framed as gains or losses. The *probabilistic insurance* example (Problem 9) reveals aversion to uncertain coverage, even when actuarially equivalent to traditional insurance, highlighting the role of decision weights. The authors also discuss how shifts in reference points—such as adapting to recent losses or gains—can alter risk preferences, further complicating the application of expected utility theory.

Prospect theory’s implications extend beyond monetary outcomes to broader decision contexts, including policy choices involving lives or quality of life. The paper concludes by acknowledging limitations, such as the need to extend the model to more complex prospects and ambiguous probabilities, while emphasizing its descriptive power in capturing real-world deviations from rationality. By grounding their model in psychological principles like reference dependence and nonlinear weighting, Kahneman and Tversky lay the foundation for behavioral economics, challenging the assumption that human decisions adhere to the axioms of expected utility.
