# Confirmation Bias: A Ubiquitous Phenomenon in Many Guises

Nickerson, R. S.

__This article provides a comprehensive review of confirmation bias, a pervasive cognitive tendency where individuals favor information that confirms their preexisting beliefs or hypotheses while disregarding or undervaluing contradictory evidence. Nickerson explores its manifestations across experimental studies and real-world contexts, potential explanations, and its implications for rationality and decision-making.__

The article begins by defining confirmation bias as the inclination to seek, interpret, and recall information in ways that bolster existing beliefs or hypotheses. Nickerson emphasizes its ubiquity and strength, suggesting it may underlie many disputes and misunderstandings among individuals, groups, and even nations. He distinguishes between deliberate case-building (e.g., by attorneys or debaters) and spontaneous, often unconscious, confirmation bias, which is the focus of the article. The author argues that confirmation bias is not merely about intentional selectivity but reflects an unwitting cognitive tendency that shapes how people process information.

Nickerson systematically reviews experimental evidence demonstrating confirmation bias across various domains. In hypothesis-testing tasks, such as Wason’s number-triplet task, participants often test hypotheses by selecting instances that would confirm rather than disconfirm them. This tendency is linked to a broader pattern of "pseudodiagnosticity," where individuals focus on the probability of data given a hypothesis (p(D|H)) while neglecting the probability of the same data given the hypothesis’s negation (p(D|~H)). This oversight leads to overconfidence in incorrect hypotheses. Similarly, in social judgment tasks, people tend to overweight confirmatory evidence and underweight disconfirming evidence, reinforcing stereotypes, illusory correlations, and self-fulfilling prophecies.

The article also examines how confirmation bias manifests in formal reasoning tasks, such as Wason’s selection task, where participants often fail to select evidence that could falsify a conditional rule. Nickerson critiques the assumption that participants’ behavior reflects a deliberate desire to confirm hypotheses, suggesting instead that cognitive limitations, such as a positivity bias or difficulty processing negative information, may underlie these patterns. He further explores the primacy effect and belief persistence, where initial impressions or beliefs shape the interpretation of subsequent evidence, even when the original basis for the belief is discredited.

Nickerson extends his analysis to real-world contexts, illustrating how confirmation bias influences diverse fields. In medicine, diagnosticians may prematurely settle on a hypothesis and interpret evidence in ways that support it, leading to overconfidence in diagnoses. In judicial reasoning, jurors may form early opinions and selectively interpret evidence to align with their initial verdicts. In science, researchers may cling to favored theories despite contradictory evidence, and scientific institutions may exhibit conservatism that delays paradigm shifts. The article also highlights how confirmation bias contributes to societal phenomena such as witch hunts, policy rationalization, and number mysticism, where individuals selectively interpret evidence to justify preexisting beliefs.

To explain confirmation bias, Nickerson considers both motivational and cognitive factors. Motivational explanations suggest that people seek to protect their self-esteem, maintain cognitive consistency, or avoid dissonance by favoring confirmatory evidence. Cognitive explanations propose that the bias stems from limitations in information processing, such as a tendency to focus on one hypothesis at a time or a positivity bias that makes it easier to process positive rather than negative information. Nickerson also discusses the "positive-test strategy," where individuals test hypotheses by examining instances expected to confirm them, and the role of conditional reference frames, where adopting a hypothesis as true temporarily biases subsequent reasoning.

Despite its apparent irrationality, Nickerson argues that confirmation bias may have adaptive value. In science, it can motivate persistence in pursuing hypotheses, even in the face of initial failures. In everyday life, it may help individuals maintain stable beliefs and avoid frequent, destabilizing changes of opinion. However, he acknowledges that the bias can also lead to overconfidence, belief perseverance, and resistance to change, even when evidence warrants it.

The article concludes by emphasizing the importance of recognizing and mitigating confirmation bias. Nickerson suggests that awareness of the bias, training in considering alternative hypotheses, and efforts to generate counterarguments may help reduce its influence. He also highlights the need for further research into how the bias can be modified, particularly in contexts where objectivity is critical, such as science, medicine, and judicial reasoning. Ultimately, Nickerson’s review underscores the pervasive and multifaceted nature of confirmation bias, its deep roots in human cognition, and its far-reaching implications for rationality and decision-making.
