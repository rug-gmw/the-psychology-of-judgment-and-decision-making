# Do People “Personally Experience” Global Warming, and If So How, and Does It Matter?

Akerlof, K., Maibach, E. W., Fitzgerald, D., Cedeno, A. Y., Neuman, A.

__This study explores whether individuals believe they have personally experienced global warming, the nature of these experiences, their accuracy compared to climatic data, and their impact on local risk perceptions. The research finds that a significant minority of people report personal experiences of global warming, often aligning with actual environmental changes, and these perceptions influence their risk assessments beyond political and cultural factors.__

The article investigates the extent to which residents in Alger County, Michigan, perceive personal experiences of global warming and how these perceptions shape their understanding of local climate risks. The introduction sets the stage by discussing the psychological and sociocultural factors influencing climate change perceptions. It highlights the challenges individuals face in detecting climate change signals amid daily weather variability and the role of experiential versus analytical processing in shaping risk perceptions. The authors emphasize that direct experiences, though often subtle, can significantly impact how people perceive and respond to global warming.

The methods employed in this study include a mail survey of Alger County residents, content analysis of open-ended responses about personal experiences of global warming, and comparison of these self-reported experiences with historical climatic data. Study 1 reveals that 27% of respondents believe they have personally experienced global warming, with most attributing these experiences to local changes. Study 2 delves into the specific ways people feel they have experienced global warming, identifying changes in seasons, weather patterns, lake levels, flora and fauna, and snowfall as the most common perceptions. Study 3 compares these self-reported experiences with NOAA climatic data, finding that many perceived changes—such as shifts in frost dates, increased storm events, and fluctuating lake levels—are corroborated by historical records, though perceptions of reduced snowfall do not align with the data.

Study 4 examines the relationship between perceived personal experiences of global warming and local risk perceptions using hierarchical regression analysis. The results indicate that believing one has personally experienced global warming, particularly within the county, significantly predicts heightened perceptions of local risks, even after controlling for demographics, political affiliation, and cultural beliefs about climate policy outcomes. The study underscores the importance of experiential processing in shaping risk perceptions, suggesting that personal and local experiences of climate change can amplify concerns about its impacts.

The discussion highlights the interplay between direct environmental experiences and sociocultural influences in shaping climate change perceptions. The authors conclude that perceived personal experiences of global warming play a critical role in influencing risk perceptions, potentially offering a pathway to engage the public on climate issues despite political polarization. The findings suggest that experiential information about climate impacts could be an effective tool for communication and policy engagement.
