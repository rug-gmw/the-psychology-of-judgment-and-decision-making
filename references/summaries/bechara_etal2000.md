# Emotion, Decision Making and the Orbitofrontal Cortex

Bechara, A., Damasio, H., Damasio, A. R.

__This article presents the somatic marker hypothesis, which posits that emotions and feelings significantly influence decision-making processes through neural mechanisms involving the orbitofrontal cortex (OFC) and other brain structures. It explores how damage to the ventromedial prefrontal cortex disrupts decision-making despite preserved intellectual abilities, highlighting the critical role of somatic markers in guiding advantageous choices.__

The article begins by introducing the somatic marker hypothesis, which suggests that decision-making is influenced by somatic states—bioregulatory signals that include emotions and feelings. These somatic markers, processed in structures like the orbitofrontal cortex (OFC), amygdala, and somatosensory cortices, help individuals make advantageous decisions by signaling potential outcomes before conscious awareness. The authors emphasize that the OFC is crucial for linking factual knowledge with emotional responses, enabling individuals to anticipate the consequences of their actions. Damage to the ventromedial prefrontal cortex (VM), a specific region within the OFC, disrupts this process, leading to impaired decision-making despite intact cognitive functions like memory and attention.

The authors review clinical observations and experimental studies involving patients with VM lesions to support their hypothesis. These patients exhibit profound deficits in social behavior and decision-making, often failing to adhere to social norms or make personally beneficial choices. Despite their impairments, these individuals retain normal intellectual abilities, performing well on standard cognitive tests. This dissociation suggests that decision-making relies on distinct neural mechanisms separate from general intelligence or memory. The article delves into the anatomical and functional specialization of the prefrontal cortex, noting that different regions, such as the dorsolateral prefrontal cortex, are associated with working memory and other cognitive functions, while the VM cortex is uniquely tied to emotional and decision-making processes.

To empirically test the somatic marker hypothesis, the authors describe the "gambling task," a laboratory tool designed to mimic real-life decision-making under uncertainty. In this task, participants choose cards from decks that offer varying rewards and penalties. Normal individuals gradually learn to favor decks with long-term advantages, while VM patients persistently choose disadvantageous decks, demonstrating a "myopia for the future." Psychophysiological measures, such as skin conductance responses (SCRs), reveal that normal individuals develop anticipatory somatic signals before making risky choices, whereas VM patients lack these signals. This absence of somatic markers correlates with their inability to make advantageous decisions, even when they consciously recognize the risks involved.

The article further explores the distinction between conscious and unconscious biases in decision-making. Normal individuals often develop anticipatory somatic responses before they can consciously articulate the risks of their choices, suggesting that these biases operate at a non-conscious level. VM patients, however, fail to generate these biases, leading to poor decision-making even when they possess explicit knowledge of the consequences. The authors also investigate the relationship between emotion, memory, and decision-making, demonstrating that while VM patients struggle with decision-making, their ability to use emotional content to enhance memory remains intact. This finding suggests that the mechanisms underlying emotional modulation of memory and decision-making are distinct.

The discussion addresses potential reasons for the VM patients' failure to trigger somatic states, including weakened emotional conditioning and difficulties in re-experiencing emotions. While VM patients can still experience certain emotions, their responses are often diminished, which may contribute to their decision-making deficits. The authors also clarify that the impairments observed in VM patients are not due to impulsivity or a lack of response inhibition, as these patients do not exhibit perseverative errors on standard neuropsychological tests. Instead, their deficits stem from an inability to integrate emotional signals into the decision-making process.

In conclusion, the article underscores the critical role of the OFC and somatic markers in guiding decision-making. The somatic marker hypothesis challenges traditional cognitive theories of decision-making by highlighting the primacy of emotion and bioregulatory signals. The findings have significant implications for understanding psychiatric disorders such as substance abuse, psychopathy, and schizophrenia, where decision-making impairments are prominent. The authors suggest that dysfunction in the neural systems involving the VM cortex may underlie these conditions, offering a framework for future research and potential interventions.
