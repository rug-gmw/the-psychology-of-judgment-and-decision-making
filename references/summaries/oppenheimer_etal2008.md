# Anchors Aweigh: A Demonstration of Cross-Modality Anchoring and Magnitude Priming

Oppenheimer, D. M., LeBoeuf, R. A., & Brewer, N. T.

__This article demonstrates that anchoring effects—where irrelevant cues bias judgments—can operate across sensory modalities and dimensions, suggesting that anchors prime a general sense of "largeness" or "smallness" rather than specific numerical or dimensional information.__

The study challenges traditional views of anchoring by showing that physical stimuli, such as line lengths, can influence numerical judgments even when the anchor and target are unrelated in modality or dimension. The introduction reviews the anchoring phenomenon, where irrelevant numerical or contextual cues (e.g., a random number from a wheel spin) bias subsequent estimates (e.g., the percentage of African countries in the UN). While some researchers argue that anchors must be relevant to the target (e.g., sharing the same dimension), others demonstrate that even implausible or unrelated anchors can bias judgments. The authors propose that anchors may prime a general sense of magnitude—such as "largeness" or "smallness"—that transcends specific modalities or dimensions, leading to cross-modal anchoring effects.

Four experiments test this hypothesis. In **Experiment 1**, participants who drew long lines estimated the Mississippi River to be significantly longer than those who drew short lines, despite no explicit link between the anchor and target. **Experiment 2** extended this finding by showing that long lines also increased estimates of Honolulu’s average July temperature, demonstrating cross-modal and cross-dimensional anchoring. **Experiment 3** revealed that the effect depends on the anchor’s *relative* size within its context: when long and short lines were embedded in appropriately scaled maps of the U.S., their anchoring effects disappeared, as the lines no longer appeared differentially large or small. Finally, **Experiment 4** provided direct evidence for the proposed mechanism by showing that long lines primed participants to complete word fragments with synonyms for "large" (e.g., "LONG," "BIG") more often than short lines.

The discussion argues that these findings expand the boundary conditions of anchoring, suggesting that physical anchors (e.g., line lengths) can bias numerical judgments, and vice versa, by activating a general sense of magnitude. The authors rule out alternative mechanisms like numerical priming, insufficient adjustment, or selective knowledge accessibility, as these require the anchor and target to share a dimension or scale. Instead, they propose that anchors prime abstract notions of size, which then influence subsequent judgments. The paper concludes by highlighting the broad implications of cross-modal anchoring, from consumer behavior to political perceptions, and emphasizes the need to understand how irrelevant cues shape judgment in everyday life.
