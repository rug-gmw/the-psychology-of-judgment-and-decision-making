# The Hidden Impact of Conspiracy Theories: Perceived and Actual Influence of Theories Surrounding the Death of Princess Diana

Douglas, K. M., & Sutton, R. M.

__This study explores how exposure to conspiracy theories about Princess Diana’s death influences individuals’ attitudes, revealing that people underestimate their own susceptibility to such theories while accurately judging their impact on others.__

The article investigates the psychological effects of conspiracy theories, focusing on whether people recognize their influence on personal attitudes. The authors, Karen Douglas and Robbie Sutton, build on prior research about the *third-person effect*—the tendency for individuals to believe that persuasive messages affect others more than themselves. They hypothesize that while people may acknowledge some influence of conspiracy theories, they will significantly underestimate their own attitude change compared to that of their peers.

The study employed an experimental design with 96 undergraduate participants, randomly assigned to either a control or experimental group. The control group rated their own and their classmates’ agreement with statements about conspiracy theories surrounding Princess Diana’s death without prior exposure to such theories. The experimental group first read material outlining popular conspiracy theories before rating their current and retrospective (pre-exposure) attitudes, as well as their perceptions of their classmates’ attitudes. This allowed the researchers to compare perceived attitude change (current vs. retrospective ratings) with actual attitude change (current ratings vs. baseline ratings from the control group).

Results confirmed the authors’ predictions: participants in the experimental group showed significant attitude change after exposure to conspiracy theories, yet they underestimated the extent of this change in themselves. While they accurately judged how much their peers’ attitudes shifted, they perceived their own attitudes as far more resistant to influence. This aligns with the *third-person effect*, where individuals assume they are less vulnerable to persuasion than others. Additionally, participants misremembered their pre-exposure attitudes, adjusting them to align more closely with their current views—a phenomenon akin to the *hindsight bias*.

The discussion highlights the broader implications of these findings. Conspiracy theories, though often dismissed as irrational, can subtly shape attitudes without individuals’ awareness. The study extends prior work on persuasive messages to a new domain, suggesting that people may generally underestimate their susceptibility to influence, whether from advertising, political messaging, or conspiracy theories. The authors speculate that this bias may stem from a desire to protect self-esteem or adhere to social norms that discourage admitting gullibility. They also call for further research into the mechanisms behind this underestimation and the potential consequences of conspiracy beliefs, such as their impact on locus of control or just-world beliefs.

Overall, the study reveals a "hidden impact" of conspiracy theories: while people recognize their influence on others, they remain largely unaware of how deeply such theories can shape their own perspectives. This disconnect underscores the need for greater public awareness of the subtle ways in which misinformation can alter attitudes.
