# Exploring the “Planning Fallacy”: Why People Underestimate Their Task Completion Times

Buehler, R., Griffin, D., & Ross, M.

__This article investigates the "planning fallacy," where individuals consistently underestimate the time needed to complete tasks, even when aware of past overruns. The authors explore cognitive mechanisms behind this bias, demonstrating that people focus on optimistic future scenarios rather than past experiences.__

This seminal study examines why people routinely underestimate the time required to complete tasks, a phenomenon termed the "planning fallacy." The authors begin by illustrating this bias through historical examples of major construction projects that significantly exceeded their predicted completion times and budgets. These examples set the stage for a psychological investigation into why individuals—despite knowing that similar tasks have taken longer in the past—remain overly optimistic about their current projects.

The research comprises five empirical studies that collectively explore the prevalence, mechanisms, and potential remedies for the planning fallacy. In the first study, psychology students predicted completion times for their honors theses. Despite their confidence, fewer than one-third finished by their predicted date, with actual completion times averaging 21.6 days longer than estimated. This pattern persisted across various tasks, including academic assignments and personal projects, as demonstrated in Study 2. Here, participants consistently underestimated completion times for both academic and non-academic tasks, regardless of whether they were explicitly informed that their predictions would be evaluated for accuracy.

To uncover the cognitive processes underlying these optimistic predictions, Study 3 employed a "think-aloud" procedure. Participants verbalized their thoughts while estimating completion times for academic tasks. The results revealed a strong focus on future-oriented, plan-based scenarios, with minimal consideration of past experiences or potential obstacles. Participants rarely referenced their own history with similar tasks, even when it suggested a likelihood of delays. When they did recall past experiences, they often attributed previous delays to external, transient, or specific factors—such as unexpected visits or unique circumstances—thereby diminishing the relevance of these experiences to their current predictions.

Study 4 experimentally manipulated participants' focus while predicting completion times for a computer assignment. Some participants were instructed to recall and connect their past experiences with similar tasks to their current prediction. This intervention significantly reduced the optimistic bias, particularly when participants were explicitly guided to link past experiences to the present task. However, simply recalling past experiences without this connection did not improve prediction accuracy. This finding underscores the importance of not just remembering the past but actively integrating it into current planning.

The final study shifted the focus to social prediction, examining how observers estimate task completion times for others. Observers, who were provided with either the actors' future plans or their past completion times, made more conservative predictions than the actors themselves. This actor-observer difference highlights that people are more likely to use distributional information (e.g., past experiences) when predicting for others than for themselves. Observers also considered potential obstacles more frequently than actors, suggesting that their predictions were less influenced by optimistic future scenarios.

The discussion synthesizes these findings, emphasizing that the planning fallacy arises from a cognitive focus on singular, case-based information (e.g., future plans) rather than distributional information (e.g., past experiences). The authors argue that this bias is pervasive and occurs even when individuals are aware of their past prediction failures. They also explore the motivational and attributional processes that contribute to this phenomenon, such as the tendency to explain away past delays as exceptions rather than the norm.

While the study identifies interventions that can reduce the optimistic bias, such as encouraging individuals to connect past experiences to current predictions, it also notes that these interventions do not necessarily improve absolute prediction accuracy. The authors suggest that the relative costs of underestimation versus overestimation may influence the utility of these interventions in real-world contexts. They conclude by highlighting the need for further research into the motivational determinants of prediction accuracy and the consequences of optimistic time estimates for task performance and well-being.
