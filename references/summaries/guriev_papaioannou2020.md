# The Political Economy of Populism

Guriev, S., and Papaioannou, E.

__This article synthesizes the literature on the recent rise of populism, focusing on its definitions, historical evolution, economic and cultural drivers, and implications. It highlights the significant increase in populist support, particularly after the 2008-2009 financial crisis, and examines the roles of trade globalization, automation, economic crises, austerity, identity politics, immigration, and social media in fueling populist movements.__

The article begins by defining populism as a "thin-centered ideology" characterized by anti-elitism and anti-pluralism, with additional features such as nativism and authoritarianism often present. It traces the historical evolution of populism from the late 19th century, noting its resurgence in the 21st century, particularly in Europe and the United States. The authors discuss various methods to quantify populism, including vote shares, survey-based measures, and textual analysis of political speeches.

The core of the article explores the drivers of populism. Economic factors such as globalization, automation, and the 2008-2009 financial crisis are identified as significant contributors. The authors review empirical studies showing that regions affected by trade shocks and automation have seen increased support for populist parties. They also examine the role of the financial crisis and subsequent austerity measures, which have fueled anti-elite sentiment and distrust in political institutions.

Cultural factors, including identity politics, social capital, and moral values, are also discussed. The authors present evidence that cultural backlash against progressive values and perceived status loss among certain groups have contributed to the rise of populism. They highlight the role of social media and the internet in spreading populist narratives and misinformation, which has further polarized societies.

The article then delves into the implications of populism, both economic and non-economic. It reviews studies on the economic performance of populist governments, noting that while some populist regimes have avoided macroeconomic disasters, they often underperform in terms of GDP growth and can weaken political institutions. The authors also discuss the impact of populism on social norms and the increase in hate crimes.

The discussion on immigration reveals mixed findings on its impact on populist support, with factors such as the scale and composition of immigration flows playing crucial roles. The authors emphasize the gap between public perceptions and reality regarding immigration, which populist leaders often exploit.

Finally, the article touches on the role of populism during the COVID-19 pandemic, noting that populist leaders have been slower to respond effectively to the crisis. It concludes by outlining potential solutions to counter populism, such as strengthening social safety nets, fighting corruption, and improving communication strategies, while acknowledging the need for further research in these areas.
