# The Emotional Dog and Its Rational Tail: A Social Intuitionist Approach to Moral Judgment

Haidt, J.

__This article challenges the long-standing rationalist view of moral judgment, arguing that moral decisions are primarily driven by quick, automatic emotional intuitions rather than deliberate reasoning. Haidt proposes the social intuitionist model, where moral reasoning often serves as post-hoc justification for instinctive moral reactions, and social interactions shape these intuitions.__

The article begins by presenting a provocative scenario involving consensual incest between siblings, which most people immediately condemn despite the lack of harmful consequences. This example illustrates how moral judgments often precede and guide reasoning, rather than the reverse. Haidt traces the historical dominance of rationalist models in moral psychology, from Plato's philosophical emphasis on reason to Kohlberg's cognitive-developmental theory, which posits that moral reasoning drives moral judgment. These models assume that moral knowledge is attained through conscious reflection and deliberation, with emotions serving only as potential inputs to this reasoning process.

In contrast, Haidt proposes the social intuitionist model, which posits that moral intuitions—quick, automatic, and affectively charged evaluations—are the primary drivers of moral judgment. This model draws on philosophical intuitionism, which suggests that moral truths are grasped through perception-like processes rather than logical reasoning. The "social" aspect of the model emphasizes that moral judgment is fundamentally interpersonal: people often engage in moral reasoning not to arrive at truth but to justify their intuitions to others or to persuade them. The model outlines six key links between intuition, reasoning, and social influence, with the first four—intuitive judgment, post-hoc reasoning, reasoned persuasion, and social persuasion—forming the core of the process. The latter two links—reasoned judgment and private reflection—are hypothesized to occur less frequently, primarily in situations where initial intuitions are weak or conflicting.

Haidt presents four lines of evidence challenging the causal primacy of reasoning in moral judgment. First, dual-process theories in cognitive psychology demonstrate that intuitive processes are ubiquitous, fast, and effortless, while reasoning is slow and resource-intensive. Research on automatic evaluation and social cognition shows that people form rapid, affectively charged judgments about others, often before conscious reasoning begins. Second, studies on motivated reasoning reveal that people act more like lawyers defending a client than judges seeking truth, using reasoning to justify pre-existing intuitions rather than to discover objective moral truths. Relatedness motives (e.g., the desire for social harmony) and coherence motives (e.g., the need to maintain a consistent worldview) bias reasoning toward confirming initial intuitions. Third, research on post-hoc reasoning demonstrates that people often construct justifications for their judgments after the fact, unaware of the true causes of their intuitions. This leads to two illusions: the "wag-the-dog" illusion, where people believe their reasoning drives their judgments, and the "wag-the-other-dog's-tail" illusion, where they expect others to change their minds in response to logical arguments. Finally, studies on moral action show that moral behavior is more strongly predicted by moral emotions (e.g., empathy, guilt) than by moral reasoning. Psychopaths, for example, possess intact reasoning abilities but lack moral emotions, leading to profoundly immoral behavior. Neuroscientific evidence further supports the link between moral emotions and moral action, with damage to the ventromedial prefrontal cortex impairing emotional responsiveness and moral decision-making.

Haidt elaborates on the mechanisms underlying moral intuitions, drawing on Damasio's somatic marker hypothesis, which suggests that moral judgments are influenced by bodily feelings or "gut reactions" that serve as emotional guides. He also highlights the role of metaphor and embodied cognition, where abstract moral concepts are grounded in physical experiences (e.g., purity as cleanliness). Regarding the origins of moral intuitions, Haidt argues that many moral foundations (e.g., sympathy, reciprocity, loyalty) are innate and shared with other primates, while culture shapes which intuitions are emphasized or suppressed. Developmentally, moral intuitions emerge through immersion in custom complexes—culturally specific practices, beliefs, and values—and peer socialization during sensitive periods in late childhood and adolescence.

The article concludes by addressing the relationship between rationalism and intuitionism, arguing that the social intuitionist model is not anti-rationalist but rather integrates reasoning, intuition, and social influence. While reasoning is often post-hoc, it can still play a causal role in moral judgment, particularly in social contexts where arguments can trigger new intuitions in others. Haidt suggests that improving moral judgment may require fostering cultures that encourage balanced, reflective, and fair-minded reasoning, such as Kohlberg's "just community" schools. He also proposes future research directions to test the social intuitionist model, including studies that interfere with reasoning, examine ecological variations in moral judgment, and explore consilience across disciplines. Ultimately, Haidt contends that the time is ripe to revisit Hume's thesis: moral emotions and intuitions drive moral reasoning, not the other way around.
