# Dysrationalia: A New Specific Learning Disability

Stanovich, K. E.

__This article introduces "dysrationalia" as a new learning disability characterized by an inability to think and behave rationally despite adequate intelligence. Stanovich critiques the overreliance on IQ tests in defining learning disabilities and argues that rationality, often overlooked in psychometric assessments, should be considered a distinct cognitive domain worthy of educational intervention.__

The article begins by examining the foundational concept of selective cognitive deficit in learning disabilities, which has historically been tied to intelligence and operationalized through IQ tests. Stanovich highlights the limitations of IQ tests, which assess only a narrow range of cognitive capacities and ignore broader aspects of intelligence, such as practical problem-solving, creativity, and social skills. This narrow focus, he argues, has led to an incomplete understanding of learning disabilities and has created a precedent for defining disabilities based on discrepancies between IQ and performance in specific domains. To illustrate this point, Stanovich proposes "dysrationalia," a disability defined by a significant discrepancy between an individual’s rational thinking abilities and their measured intelligence.

Stanovich provides examples of dysrationalia, such as individuals with high IQs who hold irrational beliefs (e.g., Holocaust denial or belief in paranormal phenomena) or engage in persistently irrational behaviors. He outlines a model of rational thinking that includes three key components: belief formation, belief/desire consistency assessment, and action determination. These components, he argues, are not adequately captured by traditional IQ tests, which focus primarily on cognitive capacities like memory and processing speed. While some overlap between cognitive capacities and rational thinking may exist, Stanovich contends that the two are largely dissociable, allowing for the possibility of dysrationalia as a distinct disability.

The article addresses potential objections to the concept of dysrationalia, such as the difficulty of measuring rationality or the argument that rationality is not an academic subject. Stanovich counters these objections by emphasizing the real-world consequences of irrational thinking, including poor decision-making in medical, financial, and legal contexts. He also argues that the lack of standardized tests for rationality is not a valid reason to dismiss the concept, as IQ tests themselves have long been criticized for their limitations. Furthermore, he suggests that incorporating rationality into the definition of intelligence could resolve some of the conceptual paradoxes in the learning disabilities field, though this would require significant reforms in how intelligence is measured and valued.

Stanovich concludes by presenting two alternatives for the learning disabilities field: either expand the definition of intelligence to include rationality or recognize dysrationalia as a distinct disability. Both options, he argues, would force the field to confront the limitations of current definitions and encourage a broader focus on skills that have real-world relevance. Ultimately, the article challenges the learning disabilities field to reconsider its reliance on IQ tests and to explore new ways of identifying and addressing cognitive deficits that impact individuals' lives.
