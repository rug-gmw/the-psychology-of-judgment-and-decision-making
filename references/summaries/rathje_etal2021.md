# Out-group animosity drives engagement on social media

Rathje, S., Van Bavel, J. J., & van der Linden, S.

__This study demonstrates that social media posts referencing political out-groups are shared far more frequently than those about in-groups, suggesting platforms may incentivize divisive content.__

The article investigates how out-group animosity influences engagement on social media platforms, specifically Facebook and Twitter. Amid growing concerns about social media's role in political polarization, the authors analyze over 2.7 million posts from news media accounts and U.S. congressional members to determine what drives virality. The introduction highlights debates about social media's impact on polarization, questioning whether "echo chambers" or the nature of viral content itself is more problematic. While prior research has focused on emotional language as a predictor of engagement, this study examines the role of social identity motives, particularly out-group animosity, in driving shares and retweets.

The methods involved collecting posts from liberal and conservative media accounts, as well as Democratic and Republican congressional members, across Facebook and Twitter. The authors used dictionaries to count references to political in-groups and out-groups, as well as emotional language (positive, negative, and moral-emotional). Regression models were employed to assess how these factors predicted engagement, controlling for variables like follower count and post characteristics (e.g., presence of URLs or media).

Results revealed that out-group language was the strongest predictor of engagement across all datasets, with each out-group term increasing the odds of a post being shared by approximately 67%. This effect was significantly larger than that of negative affect language (4.8 times stronger) and moral-emotional language (6.7 times stronger). In contrast, in-group language had a mixed or negligible effect on engagement. The study also found that out-group language strongly predicted "angry" reactions on Facebook, while in-group language predicted "love" reactions, reflecting patterns of out-group derogation and in-group favoritism. These findings were consistent across political orientations and platforms but were more pronounced among political leaders than media accounts.

The discussion contextualizes these results within social identity theory, suggesting that out-group animosity may be a particularly effective strategy for generating engagement in polarized political contexts. The authors argue that while social media platforms may not create echo chambers, they amplify divisive content, which could exacerbate polarization. The study's limitations include its correlational nature and the inability to determine causation or identify who is engaging with out-group content. Additionally, the data reflect a specific timeframe (leading up to the 2020 U.S. election), and it is unclear how these findings generalize to other periods or countries.

Practically, the research highlights the perverse incentives created by social media algorithms, which prioritize engagement over the quality or tone of content. This can lead to the spread of polarizing or false information, as divisive content tends to generate more interaction. The authors suggest that understanding these dynamics is crucial for designing healthier social media environments, whether through algorithmic changes, platform design adjustments, or policy interventions. The study underscores the need for further experimental research to explore the consequences of these trends and develop solutions to mitigate polarization.
