# Helping a Victim or Helping the Victim: Altruism and Identifiability

Small, D. A., & Loewenstein, G.

__This article demonstrates that even minimal identifiability—knowing a victim has been determined, without any personal details—increases altruistic behavior. Through laboratory and field experiments, the authors show that people donate more to victims whose identities are already fixed, challenging prior explanations that attribute the "identifiable victim effect" solely to vividness or reference group size.__

The article revisits the long-debated "identifiable victim effect," which suggests that people respond more strongly to specific, named victims than to abstract or statistical ones. Previous research, such as Jenni and Loewenstein (1997), had attributed this effect primarily to the *reference group effect*—the idea that people care more about victims when the proportion of those affected is larger (e.g., 100 deaths out of 100 feels worse than 100 deaths out of a million). However, the authors argue that this explanation fails to capture the intuitive emotional pull of identifiable victims, as famously described by Schelling (1968), who noted that the death of a particular person evokes guilt, responsibility, and sentiment in ways that statistical deaths do not.

To isolate the effect of identifiability from other confounding factors—such as vividness, certainty, or blame attribution—the authors designed two experiments that manipulated only whether a victim’s identity had been *determined* (i.e., already selected) or was *indeterminate* (i.e., yet to be selected). In both studies, no personalizing information (e.g., names, photos, or backstories) was provided, ensuring that any observed differences in altruism could be attributed solely to the act of determination itself.

The first experiment was a laboratory study using a modified "dictator game," where participants (allocators) could share money with others (recipients) who had "lost" their initial endowment. The key manipulation was whether the recipient’s identity was determined *before* the allocator made their decision or *after*. Results showed that allocators gave significantly more money to determined victims ($3.42 on average) than to indeterminate ones ($2.12), despite the victims being equally deserving and the allocators having no information about them. Interestingly, the effect was driven by the *amount* given rather than the *likelihood* of giving: roughly the same proportion of participants in both conditions donated something, but those in the determined condition gave more.

The second experiment moved into the field, where passersby were asked to donate part of their $5 payment for an unrelated survey to Habitat for Humanity. The charity letter described four needy families but varied whether the recipient family had *already been selected* or *would be selected later*. Again, donations were higher when the family was determined ($2.93 on average) than when indeterminate ($2.33). Unlike the lab study, this effect extended to the *likelihood* of donating: more people gave nothing in the indeterminate condition (43.1%) than in the determined condition (30.5%). A follow-up survey ruled out alternative explanations, such as differences in perceived trustworthiness of the charity or the neediness of the families.

The authors discuss potential psychological mechanisms behind the effect, though their studies did not directly test them. They speculate that determined victims may feel more *tangible* and *emotionally gripping*, triggering deeper cognitive engagement and empathy, as suggested by dual-process models in social psychology (e.g., Petty & Cacioppo, 1986; Chaiken, 1980). Alternatively, determined victims may provide clearer *reasons to act*, as people often rely on justifications rather than pure value calculations when making decisions (Shafir et al., 1993). The authors also propose that the effect might extend beyond victims to other targets—for instance, people might feel *stronger anger* toward a determined wrongdoer than an indeterminate one, suggesting a broader "determined other effect."

The article concludes by emphasizing the real-world implications of their findings. Even a weak form of identifiability—knowing a victim exists, without any details—can significantly boost altruism, which may help explain why charitable appeals often focus on specific individuals rather than abstract statistics. The results also challenge the notion that the identifiable victim effect is merely a byproduct of reference group size or vividness, instead pointing to a fundamental psychological distinction between determined and indeterminate targets. Future research, the authors suggest, could explore the emotional and cognitive processes underlying this effect, as well as its potential applications in policy and advocacy.
