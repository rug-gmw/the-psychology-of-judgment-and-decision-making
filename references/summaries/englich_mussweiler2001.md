# Sentencing Under Uncertainty: Anchoring Effects in the Courtroom

Englich, B., & Mussweiler, T.

__This study demonstrates that judges’ sentencing decisions are significantly influenced by prosecutors’ demands, even when these demands are irrelevant or made by non-experts, and regardless of the judges’ experience.__

The article explores the pervasive issue of sentencing disparities in judicial decision-making, where identical crimes often result in widely varying sentences. The authors propose that these disparities may stem from judgmental anchoring—a cognitive bias where numeric estimates are assimilated to a previously considered standard, such as a prosecutor’s sentencing demand. Drawing on foundational research by Tversky and Kahneman (1974), the authors hypothesize that sentencing decisions, which inherently involve uncertainty and complex information, are susceptible to anchoring effects. This bias, they argue, could explain why sentences differ even when cases are legally identical.

The introduction sets the stage by highlighting the societal importance of justice and the troubling evidence that sentencing outcomes can vary dramatically depending on the judge. Previous research has focused on individual attributes of judges, such as their political affiliation or attitudes toward punishment, but the authors suggest that situational factors—particularly the sentencing demand itself—may play a critical role. They frame sentencing as a decision made under uncertainty, where judges may rely on heuristics like anchoring to simplify the process. The article then reviews the anchoring effect, illustrating how even arbitrary anchors (e.g., a number spun on a wheel of fortune) can influence judgments in domains ranging from real estate pricing to used car valuation. Importantly, the authors note that anchoring effects have been observed even among experts, such as real estate agents and car mechanics, suggesting that experienced judges may not be immune to this bias.

To test their hypothesis, the authors conducted three empirical studies using German trial judges and law students as participants. In Study 1, judges were given identical case materials describing a hypothetical rape case but were exposed to different prosecutorial demands: either 2 months or 34 months of prison confinement. The results revealed a strong anchoring effect: judges who evaluated the high demand (34 months) gave significantly longer sentences (28.70 months on average) than those who evaluated the low demand (18.78 months). This finding provided the first experimental evidence of a direct influence of sentencing demands on judges’ decisions,Study 2 expanded on these findings by examining whether the perceived relevance of the sentencing demand moderated its influence. Participants—senior law students—were given the same case materials but were told that the sentencing demand came either from a prosecutor or from a first-year computer science student with no legal expertise. Despite the majority of participants judging the computer science student’s demand as irrelevant, the anchoring effect persisted: sentences were assimilated to the demand regardless of its source. This study demonstrated that anchoring in sentencing decisions is independent of the perceived relevance of the anchor, a finding that challenges the notion that judges only rely on "informative" anchors.

Study 3 further tested the robustness of the anchoring effect by including highly experienced judges (with an average of 15 years of experience). Even these seasoned professionals were influenced by the sentencing demand of a layperson, with sentences again assimilated to the anchor value. Notably, the size of the anchoring effect was comparable to that observed in less experienced participants, suggesting that experience does not mitigate the bias. However, experienced judges reported higher certainty in their decisions, indicating that confidence in judgment does not necessarily correspond to accuracy or resistance to bias.

The discussion synthesizes these findings, emphasizing that anchoring is a potent and pervasive influence in sentencing decisions. The authors argue that their results extend beyond the legal domain, offering insights into the mechanisms underlying anchoring effects in real-world settings. They propose that the selective accessibility model—a theoretical framework suggesting that anchoring effects arise from the increased accessibility of anchor-consistent knowledge—may explain their findings. According to this model, judges test the plausibility of the anchor by selectively retrieving information consistent with it, which then biases their final decision. This mechanism does not require the anchor to be informative, aligning with the authors’ observation that irrelevant anchors still exert influence.

The article concludes by highlighting the practical and theoretical implications of their research. For legal practice, the findings underscore the need to recognize and potentially mitigate anchoring effects in sentencing to reduce disparities. Theoretically, the study contributes to the broader literature on judgment and decision-making by demonstrating that anchoring is a robust phenomenon that operates even in complex, information-rich environments and among highly experienced individuals. The authors call for further research to explore the psychological processes underlying anchoring in applied settings and to develop strategies for debiasing judicial decisions.
