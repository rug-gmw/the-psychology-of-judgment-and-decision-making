# Experts, Amateurs, and Real Estate: An Anchoring-and-Adjustment Perspective on Property Pricing Decisions

Northcraft, G. B., & Neale, M. A.

__This study demonstrates that both real estate experts and novices are susceptible to the anchoring-and-adjustment heuristic in property pricing decisions, even in information-rich, real-world settings. The authors find that manipulated listing prices significantly bias value estimates, challenging the assumption that decisional biases disappear outside laboratory conditions. Their results suggest that expertise does not fully insulate professionals from cognitive biases.__

The article explores the applicability of the anchoring-and-adjustment heuristic—a cognitive bias where individuals rely too heavily on an initial reference point (anchor) when making judgments—to real-world decision-making, specifically in residential real estate pricing. The authors challenge the prevailing skepticism that laboratory-documented biases may not generalize to complex, information-rich environments, where decision-makers interact with multiple data sources. To test this, Northcraft and Neale conducted two experiments involving both amateur (undergraduate students) and expert (professional real estate agents) participants, who toured actual properties and estimated their fair market value (FMV) under different manipulated listing prices.

The theoretical foundation draws from prior work on anchoring-and-adjustment, particularly Tversky and Kahneman’s (1974) classic "wheel of fortune" study, which showed that arbitrary anchors influence numerical estimates. The authors extend this logic to real estate, where the listing price serves as a natural anchor in negotiations. They hypothesize that (1) listing prices will bias FMV estimates, and (2) this bias will weaken as the listing price deviates further from a credible range. The study also examines whether expertise mitigates susceptibility to anchoring, given that experts operate in familiar, feedback-rich environments.

In Experiment 1, participants evaluated a property with an actual appraised value of $74,900, but were randomly assigned one of four listing prices ranging from $65,900 to $83,900. Both students and agents received a detailed 10-page information packet, including market data, comparable sales, and property features, and toured the house before making their estimates. The results confirmed Hypothesis 1: listing prices significantly influenced all four dependent measures (appraised value, selling price, purchase price, and lowest acceptable offer) for both groups. For example, students exposed to the $65,900 anchor estimated the property’s appraisal value at $63,571, while those exposed to the $83,900 anchor estimated it at $72,196. Experts showed similar patterns, though their estimates were slightly less extreme. Hypothesis 2 received only weak support; while estimates deviated more from distant anchors, the effect was not consistently significant.

Experiment 2 replicated these findings with a different property ($134,900 appraised value) and a larger sample of experts. Again, listing prices significantly biased estimates, but Hypothesis 2 was not supported. Notably, experts were less likely than amateurs to acknowledge using the listing price in their decision-making, despite its clear influence on their judgments. This discrepancy suggests that experts may either lack awareness of their reliance on anchors or are reluctant to admit it.

The discussion highlights three key insights. First, the anchoring effect persists in real-world, information-rich settings, undermining the argument that laboratory biases are artifacts of artificial conditions. Second, expertise does not eliminate susceptibility to anchoring, though experts may rationalize their decisions differently. Third, the study raises questions about the role of feedback in judgmental expertise: if the "true" value of a property is inherently ambiguous, feedback may reinforce conventional practices rather than improve accuracy. The authors conclude that decisional heuristics and biases are not mere laboratory curiosities but fundamental to understanding everyday decision-making, even among professionals. Future research, they suggest, should explore how information richness and decision-maker characteristics modulate these effects.
