# Clinical Judgment and Decision Making

Garb, H. N.

__This article examines the validity, cognitive processes, and biases in clinical judgment, contrasting empiricist and romantic traditions in psychology. It highlights methodological advances in assessing personality and psychopathology, critiques the accuracy of clinicians' diagnoses and predictions, and advocates for evidence-based practices over anecdotal or intuition-driven approaches.__

The article explores the complexities of clinical judgment and decision-making in psychology, emphasizing the tension between empiricist and romantic traditions. The empiricist approach prioritizes scientific validation, rigorous testing, and evidence-based practices, while the romantic tradition relies on clinical intuition, anecdotal success stories, and informal observations. The author, Howard N. Garb, critiques the romantic tradition by illustrating how clinicians like Caldwell and Silver often validate their methods through selective anecdotes rather than systematic research. For example, Caldwell’s interpretation of the Minnesota Multiphasic Personality Inventory (MMPI) as revealing cigarette burn scars on a client’s hands is presented as an impressive but untested claim, underscoring the need for empirical validation.

Garb reviews methodological advancements in evaluating clinical judgments, particularly in assessing personality and psychopathology. Two key approaches are highlighted: daily self-ratings by clients to reduce memory biases and statistical methods like principal components analysis to disentangle trait, context, and perspective influences in multi-informant assessments. These methods aim to improve the validity of clinical descriptions, which often suffer from poor interrater reliability and limited accuracy. For instance, studies on defense mechanisms reveal inconsistent reliability unless clinicians undergo extensive training, and research on detecting deception shows modest success rates even under controlled conditions.

The cognitive processes underlying clinical judgment are also examined, with a focus on heuristics and biases. The affect heuristic, where clinicians rely on emotional responses, is discussed as both a potential source of intuition and a risk for bias, such as racial or gender stereotypes. Clinicians often diagnose disorders by comparing clients to prototypes or forming causal theories, which can lead to inconsistencies if these prototypes diverge from diagnostic criteria like those in the *Diagnostic and Statistical Manual of Mental Disorders* (DSM). For example, Kim and Ahn’s research demonstrates that clinicians weigh symptoms differently based on their perceived causal relationships, sometimes ignoring DSM criteria altogether.

The validity of clinical judgments is scrutinized across several domains. In personality and psychopathology descriptions, clinicians often struggle with reliability and accuracy, particularly when relying on informal observations or implicit models rather than structured assessments. Diagnoses made in routine clinical practice frequently deviate from those based on semistructured interviews, with clinicians underdiagnosing comorbid disorders and exhibiting biases related to race and gender. For example, schizophrenia is often overdiagnosed in African American patients, while disorders like dissociative identity disorder and post-traumatic stress disorder (PTSD) face controversies over potential overdiagnosis.

Case formulation, or explaining the causes of clients’ behaviors, is identified as particularly challenging. Clinicians’ causal judgments often lack reliability and validity, with interrater agreement remaining poor even among experienced professionals. Garb emphasizes the need for systematic history-taking and real-time data collection to reduce reliance on biased retrospective reports. Behavioral predictions, such as violence and sexual recidivism, show mixed progress. While clinicians can predict violence with moderate accuracy, biases persist, and actuarial tools like the Violence Risk Appraisal Guide (VRAG) and Static-99 have proven more reliable than unstructured clinical judgments.

Treatment decisions are another critical area, with research showing that clinicians often deviate from evidence-based recommendations. For example, the Schizophrenia Patient Outcomes Research Team (PORT) guidelines are frequently ignored, particularly for minority patients, who are more likely to receive excessive antipsychotic dosages. The article advocates for greater adherence to standardized, evidence-based practices, noting that tailored treatment plans are not always superior to standardized approaches.

The article concludes by reaffirming the superiority of the empiricist tradition, arguing that clinical judgment is prone to biases and inaccuracies when not grounded in scientific research. Garb calls for reduced reliance on informal observations and clinical lore, urging psychologists to prioritize empirical validation in assessment, diagnosis, and treatment decisions. The overarching message is that while clinical intuition has its place, systematic, evidence-based methods are essential for improving the validity and utility of clinical judgments.
