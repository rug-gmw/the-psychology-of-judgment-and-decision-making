# Actively Open-Minded Thinking and Its Measurement

Stanovich, K. E., & Toplak, M. E.

__This article explores the concept of actively open-minded thinking (AOT), its measurement, and its significance in predicting rational thought and decision-making. The authors review the development of AOT scales over 25 years, address criticisms, and present a refined 13-item scale to mitigate biases related to religiosity and ideology. They also discuss the paradox that AOT predicts many cognitive biases but not myside bias, which is central to the AOT concept.__

The article begins by distinguishing between rationality and intelligence, emphasizing that rationality encompasses both instrumental and epistemic dimensions, often assessed through heuristics and biases tasks. While intelligence facilitates performance on these tasks, thinking dispositions—particularly AOT—play a critical role in rational responding. AOT is characterized by a willingness to consider alternative opinions, sensitivity to contradictory evidence, and a tendency to postpone closure in decision-making. The authors highlight the predictive power of AOT scales in avoiding reasoning traps such as superstitious thinking, belief in conspiracy theories, and pseudoscientific beliefs, despite AOT being primarily measured through questionnaires rather than performance-based indicators.

The authors trace the evolution of their AOT scale over 25 years, detailing refinements made to improve its conceptual coherence. Initially, the scale included items from related constructs like dogmatism and openness, but later versions eliminated these to focus more narrowly on AOT. A significant revision involved addressing the ideological and religious biases introduced by "belief revision" items, which disproportionately affected correlations with religiosity and conservatism. The authors argue that the generic use of the word "belief" in these items allowed respondents to interpret them in ways that unfairly advantaged secular individuals, inflating negative correlations with religiosity. To address this, they propose replacing "belief" with "opinion" in revised items, reducing bias while maintaining the scale's predictive validity.

The article introduces a new 13-item AOT scale, designed to be brief yet comprehensive, and discusses its reliability and factor structure. The authors emphasize the importance of this refined scale for research in politically charged areas, such as fake news, misinformation, and ideological debates, where unbiased measurement is critical. They also explore why AOT scales are such strong predictors of rational thinking, attributing this to their ability to measure cognitive decoupling—the capacity to inhibit intuitive but incorrect responses and simulate alternative possibilities. This ability is increasingly vital in modern societies, where individuals must navigate complex, often deceptive environments that require detachment from immediate, intuitive reactions.

A paradox emerges in the discussion: while AOT scales predict performance on most rational thinking tasks, they do not predict the avoidance of myside bias, despite myside bias being central to the AOT concept. The authors suggest that myside bias, which involves evaluating evidence in a way that favors pre-existing convictions, differs from other cognitive biases because it is tied to deeply held, emotionally charged beliefs. Unlike testable beliefs, which can be verified through evidence, distal beliefs—such as those rooted in ideology or morality—are resistant to change and less amenable to cognitive decoupling. This distinction may explain why AOT scales fail to predict myside bias, as the emotional and ideological commitments involved in such beliefs override the tendencies toward detachment and open-mindedness that AOT measures.

The article concludes by reflecting on the broader implications of AOT research. While AOT is a valuable predictor of rational thinking in many contexts, its inability to address myside bias highlights the limitations of individual-level interventions in combating deeply entrenched beliefs. The authors suggest that societal institutions, such as universities and media, must play a role in fostering environments that encourage open-mindedness and evidence-based reasoning. They caution against overstating the moral virtue of AOT, given its limitations in addressing myside bias, and emphasize the need for continued refinement of AOT measurement tools to ensure they remain unbiased and effective in diverse research contexts.
