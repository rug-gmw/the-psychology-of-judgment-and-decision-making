# Is Overconfidence a Motivated Bias? Experimental Evidence

Logg, J. M., Haran, U., & Moore, D. A.

__This study challenges the assumption that overconfidence is primarily driven by motivation to view oneself positively. Through six experiments, the authors find that motivation influences overconfidence only under limited conditions—specifically when traits and assessment criteria are vague. Cognitive factors, particularly task difficulty, play a more consistent role in shaping self-assessments.__

The article systematically examines whether overconfidence—a tendency to overestimate one’s abilities or performance relative to others—stems from motivational biases (the desire to see oneself positively) or cognitive processes (such as misjudging task difficulty). The authors focus on two forms of overconfidence: *better-than-average (BTA) beliefs* (where individuals rate themselves above the median on vague traits) and *overplacement* (where individuals overestimate their relative performance on measurable tasks). While prior research often conflates these constructs, the authors argue that their distinction is critical for understanding the roots of overconfidence.

The introduction critiques existing literature for relying on correlational evidence to link motivation and overconfidence. For example, studies show that people rate themselves higher on traits they value (e.g., honesty, intelligence), but these findings do not establish causality. The authors also highlight methodological limitations in past work, such as confounding trait importance with trait commonness and using vague performance standards that allow for idiosyncratic interpretations. To address these gaps, the authors conduct six experiments that manipulate motivation and specificity (of traits, measures, and domains) to test their effects on overconfidence.

**Experiment 1** tested whether motivation (the desire to be introverted) influences BTA beliefs for introversion, a trait that can be framed as either desirable or undesirable. Participants were randomly assigned to read about introversion or extroversion as a key to success, then rated themselves and others on introversion using either vague or specific criteria. The results showed that motivation inflated BTA beliefs—but only when the trait was vaguely defined. When introversion was explicitly defined by specific behaviors (e.g., "I work alone when I can"), the effect of motivation disappeared. This suggests that vagueness in trait definitions allows for self-serving interpretations, which motivation can exploit.

**Experiment 2** extended this finding by comparing vague BTA measures with specific overplacement measures. Participants rated their introversion using both a vague scale (e.g., "How introverted are you?") and a specific questionnaire (McCroskey’s introversion scale). Motivation again influenced BTA beliefs more than overplacement, reinforcing the idea that specificity suppresses motivational biases. However, because introversion is a familiar trait, participants may have had preexisting self-assessments that limited the effect of the manipulation.

**Experiment 3** addressed this limitation by introducing an unfamiliar trait, "social responsiveness," to test whether motivation affects overconfidence when participants lack prior beliefs. Participants rated themselves and others on social responsiveness after reading that high or low levels of the trait were desirable. Unlike the previous experiments, motivation influenced both vague (BTA) and specific (overplacement) measures, suggesting that unfamiliarity with a trait increases susceptibility to motivational biases. However, the effect was still stronger for vague measures.

**Experiment 4** compared the effects of motivation and cognitive factors (task difficulty) on overconfidence. Participants rated their social responsiveness after being told the trait was either desirable or undesirable and after completing a questionnaire with either easy or difficult criteria for possessing the trait. Both motivation and difficulty influenced overconfidence, but difficulty had a stronger effect on specific (overplacement) measures, while motivation had a stronger effect on vague (BTA) measures. This finding aligns with cognitive theories of overconfidence, which emphasize that task difficulty shapes self-assessments more consistently than motivation.

**Experiment 5** shifted to an objective domain—performance on math and logic problems—to test whether motivation affects overconfidence when performance is verifiable. Participants took a test and rated their performance before and after receiving feedback. Motivation (manipulated by framing the test as important or trivial) had no effect on either BTA beliefs or overplacement. In fact, participants displayed little overconfidence overall, and some even showed underconfidence. This suggests that objective, verifiable domains suppress motivational biases entirely.

**Experiment 6** explored the psychological mechanism behind motivational effects on BTA beliefs. Participants rated their honesty (a universally desirable trait) using three methods: vague ratings, specific behaviors (e.g., "I do not lie"), and idiosyncratic definitions (where they weighted behaviors according to their personal standards). BTA beliefs were strongest for vague ratings and idiosyncratic definitions, suggesting that people use self-serving interpretations of traits when standards are unclear. The results also showed that participants’ definitions of honesty were highly idiosyncratic, with little agreement on which behaviors were most relevant. This implies that overconfidence on vague traits may stem from rational, conscious adjustments of criteria rather than unconscious self-deception.

The **general discussion** synthesizes these findings, arguing that motivation’s influence on overconfidence is limited to subjective, vaguely defined contexts. When traits, measures, or domains are specific and verifiable, cognitive factors (such as task difficulty) dominate. The authors also challenge the assumption that overconfidence is inherently self-serving, noting that it can lead to negative outcomes (e.g., poor performance, disappointment). They conclude that overconfidence is not a pervasive motivational bias but rather a context-dependent phenomenon that arises when ambiguity allows for idiosyncratic interpretations.

The article has important **theoretical implications** for research on motivated cognition, social comparison, and self-perception. It clarifies that motivation’s role in overconfidence is conditional, not universal, and highlights the need to distinguish between BTA beliefs and overplacement in future studies. **Practical implications** include the potential to reduce overconfidence by providing clear, objective criteria for performance, which could benefit individuals, managers, and organizations.

Overall, the study provides a nuanced understanding of overconfidence, showing that while motivation can inflate self-assessments, its effects are constrained by the specificity of the context. Cognitive explanations, particularly those related to task difficulty, offer a more robust account of overconfidence across diverse settings.
