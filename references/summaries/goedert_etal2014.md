# Differences in the Weighting and Choice of Evidence for Plausible versus Implausible Causes

Goedert, K. M., Ellefson, M. R., & Rehder, B.

__This study investigates how individuals adjust their causal beliefs based on the plausibility of causes and the consideration of alternative explanations, finding that implausible causes lead to differential weighting of evidence and a reduced positive test strategy.__

The article explores how prior beliefs about the plausibility of causal mechanisms influence how people interpret and weigh evidence in causal inference tasks. The authors conducted four experiments to examine whether individuals differentially weight evidence when presented with plausible versus implausible causes, and whether the ability to consider alternative causes affects this process. The core hypothesis was that individuals facing implausible causes would give greater consideration to alternative explanations, leading to a shift in how they weigh different types of contingency evidence.

The introduction establishes the broader context of belief persistence despite contradictory evidence, highlighting concerns from both psychological and philosophical perspectives. The authors review existing literature on how prior beliefs bias causal judgments, noting that plausible causal mechanisms tend to strengthen perceived relationships between variables. They critique current models of causal inference, such as Cheng's (1997) causal power theory and Griffiths and Tenenbaum's (2005) causal support model, for not adequately accounting for the believability of specific causal mechanisms. Fugelsang and Thompson's (2003) dual-process model is discussed as a framework that suggests prior knowledge influences how new evidence is weighted, but the authors propose an extension based on a positive test strategy account.

The positive test strategy hypothesis posits that individuals adopt different focal hypotheses depending on the plausibility of the cause. For plausible causes, individuals are expected to focus on cause-present information (Cells A and B in a 2x2 contingency table), while for implausible causes, they may shift attention to cause-absent information (Cells C and D) to explore alternative explanations. The authors also consider how the number of alternative causes an individual can generate might further influence evidence weighting.

In Experiment 1, participants evaluated plausible or implausible causes for skin rashes and car accidents, receiving complete frequency data for each trial. The results showed that participants given implausible causes weighted Cells A and B less heavily than those given plausible causes, partially supporting the positive test account. However, the effect on Cells C and D was not significant, suggesting that implausible causes did not lead participants to adopt an alternative focal hypothesis.

Experiment 2 expanded the range of outcomes and contingencies, testing whether the effects of plausibility generalized to additional scenarios (plant growth and stress) and whether the number of alternative causes listed by participants influenced cell weights. Participants in the implausible condition gave less weight to all cells compared to those in the plausible condition, but the plausible group showed the typical cell-weight inequality (A > B ≥ C > D). Additionally, participants who listed more alternative causes placed greater negative weight on disconfirming evidence (Cells B and C), supporting the idea that considering alternatives reduces reliance on the focal cause.

Experiments 3 and 4 shifted to an information search paradigm, where participants chose whether to observe cause-present or cause-absent cases before making causal judgments. In Experiment 3, implausible participants chose cause-absent cases more frequently than plausible participants, and this tendency increased as they listed more alternative causes. Experiment 4 replicated this pattern, showing that implausible participants tended to choose cause-absent options more often and weighted Cells A and B less heavily than plausible participants. However, the moderating effect of the number of listed alternatives was inconsistent across experiments.

The discussion synthesizes the findings, noting that the results partially align with both Fugelsang and Thompson's (2003) dual-process model and the positive test account. While implausible causes consistently reduced the weighting of cause-present information (Cells A and B), the predicted increase in weighting of cause-absent information (Cells C and D) was not consistently observed. The authors suggest that a broadly construed positive test account—where implausibility primarily reduces the positive test of the focal cause—may best explain the data. They also acknowledge limitations in their method for assessing the consideration of alternative causes and suggest future research should manipulate the number of alternatives supported by different outcomes more systematically.

Overall, the article contributes to the understanding of how prior beliefs and the consideration of alternatives shape causal inference, highlighting the need for models that account for these factors. The findings have implications for both theoretical models of causal reasoning and practical applications in education and public policy, where entrenched beliefs can hinder the acceptance of new evidence.
