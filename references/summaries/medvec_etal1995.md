# When Less Is More: Counterfactual Thinking and Satisfaction Among Olympic Medalists

Medvec, V. H., Madey, S. F., & Gilovich, T.

__This study reveals that bronze medalists often feel happier than silver medalists due to counterfactual thinking—silver medalists focus on nearly winning gold, while bronze medalists are relieved to have medaled at all.__

This article explores the paradoxical emotional responses of Olympic athletes, demonstrating how counterfactual thinking—imagining "what might have been"—can lead those who are objectively better off (silver medalists) to feel worse than those in an inferior position (bronze medalists). The authors, building on prior research in social psychology, argue that satisfaction is not solely determined by objective outcomes but also by how individuals compare their achievements to imagined alternatives. While social comparison theory and expectations influence happiness, counterfactual thinking plays a unique role in shaping emotional reactions, particularly in high-stakes competitions like the Olympics.

The research consists of three studies. The first study analyzed videotaped reactions of bronze and silver medalists from the 1992 Summer Olympics, both immediately after their events and during the medal ceremony. Participants, who were unaware of the athletes' standings, rated the athletes' happiness on a scale from "agony to ecstasy." The results confirmed the hypothesis: bronze medalists appeared significantly happier than silver medalists in both contexts. This effect persisted even after controlling for potential confounds, such as the structure of certain Olympic events where bronze medalists had just won a match while silver medalists had just lost one. The authors also ruled out the alternative explanation that differences in pre-competition expectations drove the results by statistically controlling for athletes' predicted finishes.

The second study examined the counterfactual thoughts of Olympic medalists by analyzing post-competition interviews. Participants rated the extent to which athletes' comments reflected thoughts of "at least I..." (downward counterfactuals) versus "I almost..." (upward counterfactuals). Silver medalists were more likely to focus on upward counterfactuals, such as nearly winning gold, while bronze medalists concentrated on downward counterfactuals, such as nearly missing a medal. This asymmetry in counterfactual thinking explained the emotional differences observed in the first study.

The third study extended these findings to a different athletic context—the 1994 Empire State Games—by directly surveying bronze and silver medalists about their post-competition thoughts. Consistent with the previous studies, silver medalists reported more upward counterfactual thinking ("I almost won") than bronze medalists, who were more likely to focus on the positive aspects of their achievement ("at least I medaled").

The general discussion highlights the broader implications of these findings, suggesting that the phenomenon extends beyond athletics to other domains where individuals come close to a desired outcome but fall short. The authors argue that counterfactual thoughts are often "imposed" by the nature of the event rather than strategically chosen, as silver medalists cannot help but focus on the gold they nearly won. This automatic counterfactual thinking contrasts with more elaborative counterfactuals, which are influenced by personal motives and intentions. The discussion also draws parallels to Tversky and Griffin’s (1991) concepts of "endowment" and "contrast," where an event’s direct benefits (e.g., winning a medal) are offset by its indirect effects (e.g., the contrast with a nearly achieved outcome). The authors conclude by noting the durability of counterfactual thoughts, as near-misses can linger in the mind long after the event, shaping long-term satisfaction.
