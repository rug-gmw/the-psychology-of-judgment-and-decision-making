# Affective Forecasting: Knowing What to Want

Wilson, T. D., & Gilbert, D. T.

__People routinely mispredict the intensity and duration of their emotional reactions to future events, often overestimating how much pleasure or pain these events will bring. This "impact bias" stems from focalism and a failure to account for unconscious sense-making processes that dampen emotional responses. Understanding these errors could help individuals make better decisions to maximize happiness.__

This article explores the psychological phenomenon of *affective forecasting*—people’s predictions about their future emotional reactions to events—and highlights a pervasive tendency to overestimate both the intensity and duration of these reactions, a bias known as the *impact bias*. Wilson and Gilbert argue that while people are generally accurate at predicting whether an event will feel good or bad, they struggle to anticipate how strongly or for how long they will experience those emotions. For example, college students overestimated how unhappy they would be after being assigned to undesirable dormitories, and professors overestimated their long-term distress after being denied tenure. These mispredictions can lead to suboptimal decisions, such as overspending on material goods in pursuit of fleeting happiness.

The authors identify two primary causes of the impact bias. The first is *focalism*, the tendency to fixate on a single event while neglecting other factors that will influence future emotions. For instance, football fans might overestimate how long a team’s victory will boost their mood, ignoring the competing demands of work, family, or daily stressors. The second cause is people’s failure to recognize how quickly they *make sense* of unexpected events, a process that accelerates emotional recovery. When something surprising happens—whether positive, like an unexpected job offer, or negative, like a romantic breakup—people initially react strongly but then rapidly explain the event, normalizing it and reducing its emotional impact. This sense-making process is often unconscious, so people don’t account for it when forecasting their reactions. For example, students who received an unexpected $1 gift in a library remained happier longer when the reason for the gift was ambiguous, yet they failed to predict this effect, assuming clarity would bring more joy.

The article also delves into how sense-making operates differently for positive and negative events. For positive outcomes, the process of explaining an event can paradoxically diminish its emotional payoff. People work to understand good fortune so they can replicate it, but in doing so, they drain the event of its novelty and excitement. For negative events, people engage in *motivated sense-making*—unconscious coping mechanisms like rationalization, self-affirmation, or dissonance reduction—to mitigate emotional pain. This "psychological immune system" helps people recover from setbacks, but because it operates outside awareness, they neglect to factor it into their forecasts, a phenomenon the authors term *immune neglect*. For instance, people predicted they would feel worse after receiving negative feedback from a clinician than from a computer, but in reality, they recovered more quickly from the clinician’s feedback because it was harder to dismiss, triggering stronger coping mechanisms.

The implications of these forecasting errors are far-reaching. People may overestimate the emotional toll of major traumas while underestimating the lingering pain of minor slights, or they may avoid irreversible decisions (like buying a non-returnable item) out of fear of regret, even though such commitments often lead to greater satisfaction due to forced rationalization. The article also challenges the assumption of *loss aversion*—the idea that people fear losses more than they value gains—by showing that the emotional impact of losses is often no greater than that of equivalent gains, despite people’s predictions to the contrary.

Wilson and Gilbert conclude by noting that while the impact bias may serve as a motivational tool, encouraging people to pursue rewards and avoid risks, it can also lead to unnecessary anxiety and poor decision-making. They suggest that improving affective forecasting—perhaps by encouraging people to consider the broader context of their lives or the inevitability of sense-making—could help individuals make choices that align more closely with their long-term well-being. However, they acknowledge the difficulty of this task, as it requires imagining a future where today’s extraordinary events have become ordinary.
