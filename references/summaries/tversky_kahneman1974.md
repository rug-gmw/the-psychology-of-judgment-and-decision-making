# Judgment under Uncertainty: Heuristics and Biases

Tversky, A., Kahneman, D.

__This seminal article explores how people rely on mental shortcuts—heuristics—to make judgments under uncertainty, often leading to systematic biases and errors in probability assessment.__

The article investigates how individuals assess probabilities and make decisions in uncertain situations, demonstrating that people frequently rely on a limited set of heuristic principles to simplify complex judgment tasks. While these heuristics can be efficient and effective, they also introduce predictable and severe biases. The authors identify three primary heuristics—representativeness, availability, and anchoring—and analyze how each distorts intuitive probability judgments.

The introduction establishes that people often express beliefs about uncertain events through subjective probabilities or qualitative statements. The authors argue that these judgments are shaped by heuristic rules, akin to how physical judgments (e.g., estimating distance based on clarity) can be systematically biased. The core of the article systematically explores three heuristics and their associated biases.

**Representativeness** refers to the tendency to judge the probability that an object or event belongs to a category based on how closely it resembles the prototypical example of that category. For instance, when given a description of a shy, detail-oriented individual named Steve, people are more likely to judge him as a librarian than a farmer, ignoring base-rate probabilities (e.g., that farmers outnumber librarians). The authors demonstrate this bias through experiments where subjects ignored prior probabilities, even when explicitly provided, and instead relied on stereotypic resemblance. This heuristic also leads to insensitivity to sample size, as people expect small samples to reflect population statistics as faithfully as large ones. For example, subjects incorrectly assumed that small and large hospitals would record similar numbers of days with over 60% male births, despite statistical principles dictating otherwise. Additionally, representativeness fosters misconceptions of chance, such as the gambler’s fallacy, where people expect random sequences to "correct" themselves (e.g., believing a roulette wheel is "due" for black after a streak of red). The heuristic also causes insensitivity to predictability, where people make extreme predictions based on unrepresentative or unreliable data, and the "illusion of validity," where confidence in predictions is inflated by the perceived fit between input and output, regardless of predictive accuracy. Finally, the authors discuss how representativeness obscures the phenomenon of regression to the mean, leading people to misattribute fluctuations in performance to causal factors (e.g., assuming punishment improves performance when regression alone explains the pattern).

**Availability** describes how people assess the frequency or probability of an event based on how easily instances come to mind. While this heuristic is often useful, it is prone to biases due to factors like retrievability, imaginability, and illusory correlation. For example, people may overestimate the likelihood of dramatic events (e.g., plane crashes) because they are more vividly recalled. The authors illustrate how availability distorts judgments in tasks like estimating whether more English words begin with "r" or have "r" as the third letter—people incorrectly favor the former because words starting with "r" are easier to retrieve. Imaginability biases arise when people judge the likelihood of events based on how easily they can construct scenarios (e.g., overestimating the number of small committees that can be formed from a group). Illusory correlation occurs when people perceive a relationship between two variables (e.g., suspiciousness and peculiar eyes in drawings) simply because they are readily associated in memory, even when no actual correlation exists.

**Anchoring and adjustment** involve starting from an initial value (the anchor) and making insufficient adjustments to reach a final estimate. This heuristic leads to biases in numerical predictions, such as when people estimate the product of a sequence of numbers by anchoring on the first few calculations and adjusting inadequately. Anchoring also distorts judgments of conjunctive and disjunctive events, causing people to overestimate the probability of conjunctive events (e.g., multiple independent successes) and underestimate disjunctive events (e.g., at least one failure in a series). In assessing subjective probability distributions, anchoring results in overly narrow confidence intervals, reflecting excessive certainty about uncertain quantities.

The discussion highlights that these biases are not due to motivational factors like wishful thinking but stem from the inherent limitations of intuitive judgment. Even experts, including trained statisticians, fall prey to these biases when relying on intuition. The authors argue that while heuristics are indispensable for navigating uncertainty, their pitfalls are often overlooked because people fail to encode relevant statistical information from everyday experience. They emphasize the need for compatibility between subjective probabilities and broader belief systems, rather than mere internal consistency. The article concludes by advocating for a deeper understanding of heuristics and biases to improve decision-making in uncertain contexts.
