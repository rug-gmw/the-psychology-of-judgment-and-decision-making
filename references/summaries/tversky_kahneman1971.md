# Belief in the Law of Small Numbers

Tversky, A., & Kahneman, D.

__This seminal paper exposes a widespread cognitive bias where people—including trained scientists—overestimate the reliability of small samples, mistakenly believing they mirror population characteristics as closely as large samples. The authors demonstrate how this "law of small numbers" leads to flawed research practices, such as underpowered studies and misjudged replications. Their findings challenge statistical intuitions and advocate for computational rigor in scientific inquiry.__

The article begins by illustrating a striking disconnect between statistical reality and human intuition. When psychologists were asked about the probability of replicating a significant result from a small sample (20 subjects) with an additional 10 subjects, most grossly overestimated the likelihood, guessing around .85 instead of the correct .48. This error stems from a fundamental misconception: the belief that small samples are highly representative of their parent populations, a fallacy the authors term the "law of small numbers." This intuition persists despite formal training, as evidenced by similar responses from both mathematical psychologists and general APA members.

Tversky and Kahneman trace this bias to two related cognitive tendencies. First, people expect samples to be *representative*—that is, nearly identical to the population and to each other—even when sample sizes are tiny. For example, when generating random coin-toss sequences, individuals produce strings where short segments closely mirror the 50-50 population proportion, ignoring the natural variability of small samples. Second, they assume sampling is a *self-correcting process*, where deviations from the population mean will be "balanced out" by subsequent observations. This manifests in the gambler’s fallacy, where a run of heads is expected to be followed by tails to restore fairness, or in the expectation that a sample of 50 children with an average IQ of 100 will still average 100 even after the first child tested scores 150. In reality, the correct expected mean is 101, as the initial outlier is merely diluted, not canceled.

The consequences of this bias for scientific research are profound. Researchers who subscribe to the law of small numbers design underpowered studies, overestimating their ability to detect true effects. The authors highlight a hypothetical psychologist who, expecting a correlation of .35, calculates that 33 subjects are sufficient for significance—unaware that the power of such a test is only .50. This aligns with Jacob Cohen’s earlier work, which revealed that the average power of studies in *Journal of Abnormal and Social Psychology* was a dismal .18 for small effects, .48 for medium effects, and .83 for large effects. Such low power leads to a host of problems: valid hypotheses are dismissed as false, researchers grow frustrated with "untrustworthy" nature, and published results are riddled with Type I errors (false positives) due to the file-drawer effect—where nonsignificant findings go unreported.

The bias also distorts judgments about replication. When evaluating a doctoral student’s study with a significant result (*t* = 2.70, *N* = 40), most psychologists recommended a replication with 20 subjects. Yet, as the authors demonstrate, this sample size gives only a ~50% chance of replicating the original significance. Even when the replication yields a *t* of 1.24 (in the same direction), respondents remained skeptical, with many suggesting further replication or seeking spurious explanations for the "discrepancy." This harsh standard reflects the belief that replications should mirror original results almost perfectly, regardless of sample size. The authors expose the irony: pooling the two samples (*t* = 2.70 and *t* = 1.24) would yield a *t* of ~3.00, yet the replication is dismissed as a failure. Similarly, when asked how many of 27 significant correlations from a study with 100 subjects would replicate with 40 subjects, respondents guessed 18—far above the statistically plausible 8–10.

The paper concludes with a call for computational safeguards against these intuitive errors. While experience alone is unlikely to correct the bias (since sampling variability is easily rationalized post hoc), researchers can mitigate it by routinely calculating power, confidence intervals, and effect sizes. The authors argue that significance testing, though imperfect, at least forces scientists to confront sampling variability, whereas intuitive judgments lead to overconfidence. They advocate for a return to reporting confidence intervals alongside significance levels, as this would highlight the uncertainty inherent in small samples. Ultimately, the "law of small numbers" is not a motivational flaw but a cognitive one—a consistent misperception of randomness that can only be countered by replacing intuition with computation. The paper’s enduring influence lies in its demonstration that even experts are not immune to statistical illusions, and that rigorous methods are essential to scientific progress.
