# The Psychology of Sunk Cost

Arkes, H. R., & Blumer, C.

__This article explores the sunk cost effect—where individuals persist in failing endeavors due to prior investments of money, effort, or time—demonstrating its irrationality through experiments and linking it to psychological aversion to wastefulness and prospect theory.__

The paper by Arkes and Blumer investigates the psychological underpinnings of the sunk cost effect, a phenomenon where people continue investing in a project or decision simply because they have already committed resources to it, even when doing so is economically irrational. The authors argue that this behavior stems from a deep-seated desire to avoid appearing wasteful, rather than from logical cost-benefit analysis. The introduction frames the sunk cost effect through real-world examples, such as the Tennessee-Tombigbee Waterway Project, where policymakers justified further spending by citing prior investments, and a hypothetical scenario where a person insists on attending a football game in a blizzard to avoid "wasting" the ticket price.

The article presents a series of experiments to empirically demonstrate the sunk cost effect. In *Experiment 1*, participants chose between two ski trips of unequal cost but equal enjoyment, with many opting for the more expensive trip despite its irrelevance to their actual preference. *Experiment 2* extended this finding to a real-world setting, showing that theater subscribers who paid full price attended more plays than those who received discounts, suggesting that higher sunk costs increased commitment. *Experiments 3 and 4* revealed that individuals who had already invested in a failing project (e.g., a radar-blank plane) were more likely to continue funding it and overestimated its chances of success compared to those evaluating the same project without prior investment. *Experiment 5* ruled out alternative explanations, such as the perceived quality of the final product, by holding the total cost constant across scenarios. *Experiment 6* further illustrated the aversion to wastefulness, as participants preferred to consume a more expensive TV dinner to avoid discarding it, even when the alternatives were identical in utility. *Experiment 7* demonstrated that the sunk cost effect was stronger when additional spending would render a prior investment redundant, reinforcing the idea that wastefulness drives the behavior.

The authors also explored potential moderators of the sunk cost effect. *Experiment 8* found mixed evidence that personal involvement (e.g., being the decision-maker vs. an outside observer) amplified the effect, while *Experiment 9* showed that the effect persisted even when no personal money was at stake. *Experiment 10* revealed that prior economics education did little to mitigate the sunk cost effect, as students with economics training were just as susceptible as those without. The discussion then situates the sunk cost effect within broader psychological theories, such as cognitive dissonance, entrapment, and compliance techniques like the foot-in-the-door and low-ball effects. While these theories share some overlap with sunk cost behavior, the authors argue that none fully capture its unique psychological drivers, particularly the aversion to wastefulness.

The paper concludes by linking the sunk cost effect to prospect theory, which posits that people are more sensitive to losses than gains and are risk-seeking when facing certain losses. The authors suggest that sunk costs create a psychological "loss frame," making individuals more likely to take risks to avoid admitting waste. They also highlight the robustness of the effect, noting that even economically savvy individuals struggle to overcome it. The epilogue contrasts this irrationality with the example of Thomas Edison, who successfully leveraged sunk costs to his advantage by increasing production to maximize efficiency. Overall, the article provides compelling evidence that the sunk cost effect is a pervasive and irrational bias, deeply rooted in human psychology.
