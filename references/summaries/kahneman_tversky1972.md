# Subjective Probability: A Judgment of Representativeness

Kahneman, D., Tversky, A.

This seminal paper introduces the *representativeness heuristic*, demonstrating how people systematically misjudge probabilities by relying on how well an event resembles its parent population or generating process, often ignoring critical statistical principles like sample size.

The article presents a comprehensive exploration of how individuals assess the likelihood of uncertain events through the lens of representativeness—a heuristic where probability judgments are based on the degree to which an event mirrors the essential characteristics of its population or the process that produced it. Kahneman and Tversky argue that this heuristic leads to predictable and systematic errors, as people prioritize similarity over statistical logic. The introduction establishes the heuristic’s central role in decision-making under uncertainty, contrasting it with normative Bayesian approaches that emphasize objective probability calculations. The authors highlight that while representativeness can yield reasonable estimates, it frequently produces biases, such as the neglect of sample size, misconceptions of randomness, and overconfidence in small samples.

The methods employed involve a series of empirical studies, primarily using questionnaires administered to high school students and university undergraduates in Israel. Participants were asked to judge the likelihood of various events, such as birth sequences, distributions of marbles among children, or the probability of samples being drawn from specific populations. The experiments were designed to isolate the effects of representativeness by manipulating factors like sample size, sample proportion, and the apparent randomness of outcomes. For example, in one study, participants judged the likelihood of two birth sequences (e.g., GBGBBG vs. BGBBBB) as equally probable, despite the latter being less representative of the population’s gender ratio. Another experiment demonstrated that people ignore sample size when evaluating probabilities, such as believing that a small hospital and a large hospital are equally likely to record days with more than 60% male births.

The results reveal several key findings. First, subjective probability judgments are heavily influenced by representativeness, often leading to violations of statistical principles. Participants consistently judged more representative events as more likely, even when objective probabilities suggested otherwise. For instance, they perceived sequences with balanced gender ratios as more probable than those with skewed ratios, regardless of sample size. Second, the heuristic leads to the neglect of sample size, as demonstrated by participants’ inability to recognize that extreme outcomes (e.g., a high proportion of boys) are more likely in smaller samples. Third, people’s intuitions about randomness are flawed; they expect random processes to produce locally representative outcomes (e.g., short sequences with balanced heads and tails) and reject sequences with long runs or clusters as non-random, even when such patterns are statistically expected. Finally, the authors show that even sophisticated individuals, such as psychologists, fall prey to these biases, overestimating the replicability of statistically significant results.

The discussion extends these findings to broader implications for human judgment and decision-making. Kahneman and Tversky argue that representativeness is not merely a cognitive shortcut but a fundamental heuristic that shapes how people evaluate uncertainty in everyday life, from political forecasting to clinical diagnoses. They contrast representativeness with another heuristic, *availability*, which relies on the ease of recalling instances to judge probability. While representativeness focuses on the similarity between events and their parent populations, availability emphasizes the accessibility of specific examples. The authors suggest that both heuristics reflect a broader tendency to rely on mental effort—whether in assessing similarity or retrieving instances—to gauge probability. They conclude by emphasizing the need for further research into the conditions under which these heuristics succeed or fail, as well as their potential for improvement through education and training. The paper lays the groundwork for understanding the systematic ways in which human intuition deviates from statistical rationality, offering a critical perspective on the limitations of human judgment.
