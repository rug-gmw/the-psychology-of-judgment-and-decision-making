# Positive Affect Facilitates Integration of Information and Decreases Anchoring in Reasoning among Physicians

Estrada, C. A., Isen, A. M., & Young, M. J.

__This study demonstrates that inducing positive affect in physicians through a small gift of candy leads to earlier consideration of the correct diagnosis and reduces cognitive anchoring, without compromising thoroughness or systematic reasoning.__

The article investigates how positive affect influences clinical reasoning among practicing physicians. The authors, Estrada, Isen, and Young, conducted a randomized controlled trial with 44 internists divided into three groups: a control group, an affect-induction group (who received a small package of candy), and a group asked to read humanistic statements about medicine. The physicians were tasked with diagnosing a simulated patient case involving liver disease while "thinking aloud," with their reasoning processes recorded and later analyzed by independent raters. The study aimed to determine whether positive affect could enhance diagnostic reasoning, particularly in the early hypothesis generation and hypothesis confirmation phases, while also examining its impact on cognitive anchoring—a tendency to overemphasize initial assessments and insufficiently adjust to new information.

The introduction situates the study within a broader body of research showing that positive affect improves cognitive processes such as problem-solving, decision-making, and information integration across various contexts. Positive affect, defined as a pleasant feeling state or good mood, has been induced in prior studies through small gifts, positive feedback, or brief exposure to humorous material. While earlier research demonstrated these effects in medical students and other populations, this study extends the inquiry to experienced physicians, exploring whether positive affect influences expert decision-making in a real-world clinical setting. The authors also address a theoretical debate: whether positive affect facilitates systematic, thorough processing or instead promotes superficial, heuristic thinking. They hypothesized that positive affect would enhance flexibility and integration of information without leading to premature closure or flawed reasoning.

The methods employed a rigorous experimental design to ensure validity. Physicians were randomly assigned to one of the three groups, and the researcher interacting with them was blinded to their condition to prevent bias. The affect-induction group received a small gift of candy, a method validated in prior studies, while the "Statements" group read humanistic phrases about medicine, though this intervention was later found ineffective in inducing positive affect. The clinical case involved a 45-year-old female patient with symptoms suggestive of liver disease, and physicians could request additional information as they worked through the diagnosis. Their verbal protocols were transcribed and analyzed for key dependent measures: the point at which they first considered liver disease as a diagnostic possibility (hypothesis generation), the point at which they confirmed liver disease as the diagnosis (hypothesis confirmation), and the degree of anchoring in their reasoning. Exploratory analyses also examined factors such as the number of tests ordered, premature closure, and the consideration of alternative diagnoses.

The results confirmed the authors' primary hypotheses. Physicians in the affect group considered the liver disease hypothesis significantly earlier in their reasoning process (20% into the protocol) compared to the control group (39%), a difference of 19%. They also exhibited significantly less anchoring, with mean scores of 1.5 versus 3.9 on a 10-point scale. However, both groups reached the confirmation of liver disease at similar points in the protocol, suggesting that positive affect accelerated the initial integration of information without hastening the final decision. Exploratory analyses revealed no differences between the affect and control groups in premature closure, the number of diagnoses considered, or the thoroughness of information gathering, indicating that the affect group's earlier hypothesis generation was not due to superficial or hasty processing. The "Statements" group showed no significant differences from the control group on any measures, reinforcing that the observed effects were specific to the positive affect induction.

The discussion interprets these findings as evidence that positive affect enhances cognitive flexibility and integration without compromising systematic reasoning. The authors argue that the earlier consideration of the correct diagnosis by the affect group reflects improved efficiency and openness to new information, rather than premature closure or heuristic processing. They also address theoretical implications, countering claims that positive affect impairs systematic thinking by demonstrating that it fosters both thoroughness and adaptability. The study's strengths include its use of practicing physicians in a realistic setting, blinded raters, and a design that closely mirrors actual clinical reasoning. Limitations include the use of a single simulated case and the uncertainty of how these findings translate to real-world clinical practice.

The article concludes by highlighting the practical and theoretical significance of the results. Practically, the study suggests that simple interventions to induce positive affect—such as small gifts or supportive environments—could enhance diagnostic reasoning and potentially improve doctor-patient interactions. Theoretically, the findings support the view that positive affect facilitates systematic, flexible thinking, rather than superficial processing. The authors call for further research to explore the broader implications of these effects in medical education and clinical practice.
