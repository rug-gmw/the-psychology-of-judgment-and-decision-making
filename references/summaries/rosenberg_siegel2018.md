# A 50-year review of psychological reactance theory: Do not read this article

Rosenberg, B., Siegel, J. T.

This article provides a comprehensive 50-year review of psychological reactance theory (PRT), tracing its evolution across multiple disciplines and outlining five key waves of research. It synthesizes findings from social psychology, clinical psychology, and communication studies while critically evaluating measurement tools and proposing future directions for the field. The authors emphasize PRT’s motivational roots and suggest new avenues for expanding its applicability.

The review begins by introducing PRT, originally proposed by Brehm (1966), which posits that individuals experience psychological reactance—a motivational state aimed at restoring freedom—when their behavioral freedoms are threatened or eliminated. The article distinguishes PRT from cognitive dissonance theory, highlighting its focus on freedom restoration rather than cognitive inconsistency. The first wave of research (Wave 1) established the theory’s foundational assumptions: that people possess a set of free behaviors they believe they can enact, and that threats to these freedoms trigger reactance. The authors detail the core components of PRT, including the nature of freedoms, the types of threats (elimination vs. threat), the arousal of reactance, and the subsequent restoration of freedom through behavioral or subjective outcomes. Early studies demonstrated that reactance could lead to boomerang effects, increased attractiveness of restricted options, and hostility toward the source of the threat. The authors also discuss individual differences in reactance arousal, such as personality traits (e.g., Type A vs. Type B) and perceived coping ability, which influence how people respond to freedom threats.

Wave 2 explores the contributions of clinical psychology to PRT, where reactance was reconceptualized as a trait rather than a momentary state. Clinicians identified three approaches to addressing reactance: viewing it as a moderator of therapeutic success, attempting to overcome it through interventions like paradoxical therapy, and tailoring therapy based on clients’ trait reactance levels. However, the validity of trait reactance measures, such as the Hong Psychological Reactance Scale (HPRS), has been questioned, with critics arguing that reactance is better understood as a motivational state. The authors suggest future research should resolve measurement issues and explore how therapists’ communication styles (e.g., autonomy-supportive vs. controlling) influence patient reactance, particularly among those with heightened depressive symptomatology.

Wave 3 examines the role of communication research in advancing PRT, particularly through studies on message features that arouse or reduce reactance. Controlling language (e.g., "must," "should") was found to increase reactance, while autonomy-supportive language (e.g., "perhaps," "maybe") diminished it. Other strategies for reducing reactance included restoration postscripts (reminders of freedom), message novelty, narratives, and empathy-inducing content. The authors also discuss how reactance can be harnessed as a persuasive tool, such as in anti-tobacco campaigns that frame smoking as a loss of freedom. Future directions for communication scholars include investigating the relationship between the proportion of freedoms threatened and reactance arousal, as well as applying PRT to interpersonal communication contexts.

Wave 4 addresses the measurement of reactance, a long-standing challenge in PRT research. Early studies relied on indirect measures, such as behavioral outcomes, but later work introduced self-report and physiological measures. Trait reactance measures, like the HPRS and Therapeutic Reactance Scale (TRS), have faced psychometric criticisms, while state reactance measures, such as Dillard and Shen’s (2005) intertwined model (combining anger and negative cognitions), have gained broader acceptance. The Salzburg State Reactance Scale (SSR Scale) and Reactance to Health Warnings Scale (RHWS) offer additional tools, though concerns about construct validity remain. Physiological measures, such as heart rate variability, provide promising avenues for directly assessing reactance arousal. The authors recommend further refinement of these measures to ensure their validity and reliability.

Wave 5 marks a return to PRT’s motivational foundations, proposing three key areas for future research. First, scholars should explore how states of mind (e.g., uncertainty, depressive symptomatology) influence perceptions of freedom threats and the desire for freedom restoration. For example, uncertainty may reduce reactance by diminishing the perceived importance of threatened freedoms, while depressive symptoms may amplify reactance by narrowing cognitive focus. Second, researchers should investigate catalysts of reactance beyond direct freedom threats, such as identity threats or implicit social pressures (e.g., group categorization). Third, the authors suggest expanding the outcomes of reactance to include broader cognitive and behavioral effects, such as social dominance orientation or goal shielding. By revisiting PRT’s motivational roots, the article argues that the theory’s explanatory power can be significantly enhanced, benefiting research in clinical psychology, communication, and measurement.

The conclusion synthesizes the five waves of PRT research and emphasizes the importance of interdisciplinary collaboration to advance the field. The authors highlight unresolved questions, such as the validity of trait reactance measures and the need for further exploration of implicit freedom threats. They also underscore the potential for PRT to address socially relevant phenomena, from therapeutic resistance to intergroup conflict. By integrating motivation science into PRT, scholars can continue to refine the theory’s accuracy and applicability, ensuring its relevance for another 50 years.
