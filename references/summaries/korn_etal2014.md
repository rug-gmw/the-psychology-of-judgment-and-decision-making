# Depression is related to an absence of optimistically biased belief updating about future life events

Korn, C. W., Sharot, T., Walter, H., Heekeren, H. R., & Dolan, R. J.

__This study demonstrates that individuals with major depressive disorder (MDD) lack the optimistic bias in belief updating seen in healthy individuals, with symptom severity correlating with a more pessimistic pattern of updating expectations about future life events.__

The article investigates how individuals with major depressive disorder (MDD) update their beliefs about future adverse life events compared to healthy individuals. The core premise is rooted in cognitive theories of depression, which suggest that maladaptive cognitive biases play a central role in the development and maintenance of MDD. While healthy individuals exhibit an optimistic bias—adjusting their beliefs more in response to desirable information than undesirable information—this study hypothesizes that such a bias is absent in MDD patients.

The research involved 18 MDD patients (13 of whom were medicated, and eight with comorbid anxiety disorders) and 19 healthy controls. Participants were asked to estimate their likelihood of experiencing 70 different adverse life events, such as Alzheimer’s disease or robbery. After providing their initial estimates, they were shown the average probability of these events occurring to someone in a similar sociocultural environment. Participants then re-estimated their likelihood of experiencing these events to assess how the new information influenced their beliefs.

The results revealed a striking difference between the two groups. Healthy controls exhibited an optimistic bias, updating their beliefs more significantly when presented with desirable information (i.e., when the average probability was lower than their initial estimate) compared to undesirable information (i.e., when the average probability was higher). In contrast, MDD patients showed no such bias; their belief updates were similar for both desirable and undesirable information. Furthermore, the severity of depressive symptoms correlated with the degree of biased updating: more severely depressed individuals exhibited a more pessimistic updating pattern, adjusting their beliefs more in response to undesirable information.

The study also found that MDD patients initially estimated their probability of experiencing adverse events as higher than both the healthy controls and the average population probabilities, reflecting their pervasive pessimism. Despite receiving more desirable information (due to their initial overestimations), MDD patients did not exhibit the optimistic bias seen in healthy individuals. Additional analyses ruled out potential confounding factors such as differences in memory for the presented probabilities, subjective ratings of the events, or framing effects.

The discussion highlights the potential implications of these findings for understanding mental health. The absence of an optimistic bias in MDD patients may contribute to the maintenance of depressive symptoms, as it prevents them from incorporating positive information about their future. The authors suggest that this lack of biased updating could be a target for therapeutic interventions, such as cognitive bias modification or positive psychology techniques, which aim to enhance optimistic thinking. However, they also caution that the study does not establish causality and that longitudinal research is needed to determine whether altered belief updating precedes or follows depressive episodes. Overall, the study provides compelling evidence that optimistically biased belief updating may be a key factor associated with mental health.
