# Cognitive Reflection and Decision Making

Frederick, S.

__This article introduces the Cognitive Reflection Test (CRT) as a simple measure of cognitive ability and demonstrates its strong predictive power for decision-making behaviors, particularly in time and risk preferences, while also highlighting significant gender differences in CRT performance and decision patterns.__

The paper begins by critiquing the neglect of cognitive ability in decision-making research, despite its known influence on various life outcomes. Frederick argues that while individual differences in intelligence are often dismissed as noise in experimental settings, they may fundamentally shape decision-making processes. To address this gap, he introduces the three-item Cognitive Reflection Test (CRT), designed to measure the ability to override intuitive but incorrect responses in favor of reflective, correct answers. The CRT’s problems—such as the "bat and ball" question—are deceptively simple, with intuitive but wrong answers (e.g., 10 cents) that must be suppressed to arrive at the correct solution (5 cents). The test’s validity is supported by its correlation with other cognitive measures, such as the Wonderlic Personnel Test (WPT) and SAT scores, though it uniquely captures a disposition toward cognitive reflection rather than raw intelligence or academic achievement.

Frederick then explores the CRT’s relationship with two key decision-making domains: time preferences and risk preferences. In time preferences, higher CRT scores consistently predict greater patience, particularly for short-term monetary choices (e.g., preferring $3,800 next month over $3,400 now). However, this relationship weakens for longer-term decisions, suggesting that cognitive reflection helps individuals recognize the irrationality of extreme discounting but may not influence "pure" time preference (e.g., preferences for non-monetary rewards like massages). The data also reveal that high-CRT individuals are less impulsive and more attuned to economic factors like inflation, further supporting the idea that cognitive reflection enhances the consideration of rational justifications for patience.

In the domain of risk preferences, the CRT reveals striking patterns. High-CRT individuals are more risk-seeking for gains, even when the gamble has a lower expected value, and more risk-averse for losses. This challenges prospect theory’s reflection effect, which predicts uniform risk aversion for gains and risk-seeking for losses. For low-CRT individuals, the reflection effect holds strongly, but high-CRT individuals exhibit a more consistent risk profile across domains. Frederick suggests that cognitive reflection may lead to a more deliberate evaluation of gambles, overriding intuitive biases like loss aversion. The CRT’s predictive power for risk preferences is particularly strong for men, whereas its influence on time preferences is more pronounced for women—a gender interaction that remains unexplained but aligns with prior findings on patience and cognitive ability.

The paper also compares the CRT to other cognitive measures, such as the Need for Cognition (NFC) scale and standardized test scores. While all measures correlate with decision-making tendencies, the CRT emerges as the most efficient and predictive tool, requiring only three items to match or exceed the validity of longer tests. This efficiency makes it an attractive option for researchers studying individual differences in decision-making. Additionally, Frederick addresses the CRT’s gender gap, with men scoring significantly higher than women, even after controlling for math ability. This difference is not merely a matter of mathematical skill but reflects a broader tendency for men to engage in more reflective problem-solving, while women are more likely to rely on intuitive responses.

In the discussion, Frederick grapples with the normative implications of his findings. If cognitive ability predicts "better" decisions—such as choosing a gamble with a higher expected value—does this imply that such choices are objectively superior? He argues that while preferences are often treated as subjective, the strong link between CRT scores and rational decision-making suggests that some choices may indeed be more normatively defensible. For example, rejecting a 15% chance of $1,000,000 in favor of a sure $500 is difficult to justify on rational grounds, much like the intuitive but incorrect answer to the CRT’s "bat and ball" problem. However, he acknowledges that cognitive ability is not the sole determinant of good decision-making and that individual differences in utility or context may warrant different choices.

Ultimately, the paper underscores the importance of cognitive ability in shaping decision-making and introduces the CRT as a powerful, concise tool for measuring it. The findings challenge the assumption that preferences are purely subjective and highlight the need for decision-making theories to account for individual differences in cognitive reflection. The gender differences observed further complicate the picture, suggesting that the interplay between cognition and decision-making is nuanced and context-dependent. Frederick’s work calls for greater integration of cognitive ability into behavioral research, offering a path toward more comprehensive models of human judgment and choice.
