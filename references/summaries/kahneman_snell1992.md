# Predicting a Changing Taste: Do People Know What They Will Like?

Kahneman, D., Snell, J.

__This article explores whether people can accurately predict their future preferences, introducing distinctions between decision utility, experienced utility, and predicted utility. Through experiments involving repeated exposure to foods and music, the authors find that individuals generally fail to predict changes in their tastes, often expecting declines in enjoyment even when actual preferences improve or remain stable.__

The article begins by critiquing the narrow economic conception of utility, which equates it solely with revealed preferences rather than the hedonic quality of experiences. Kahneman and Snell propose a framework distinguishing *decision utility* (derived from choices), *experienced utility* (the actual hedonic quality of outcomes), and *predicted utility* (beliefs about future experiences). This distinction raises critical questions about the rationality and efficiency of decision-making: Do people accurately predict what they will enjoy? Do they use these predictions when making choices? And do their choices align with what they ultimately experience?

To investigate the accuracy of predicted utility, the authors conducted two experiments. In a pilot study, participants consumed ice cream while listening to unfamiliar music daily for a week, rating their enjoyment and predicting their future liking. Most predicted a decline in enjoyment, which aligned with the average trend for the music but not for the ice cream, where individual changes varied widely. Crucially, there was little correlation between predicted and actual changes, suggesting participants lacked insight into how their tastes would evolve. A main study expanded this design, using plain yogurt (an initially aversive stimulus) and self-selected music, with participants making predictions and ratings over eight days. Again, predictions were often inaccurate: participants overestimated their initial dislike for yogurt and failed to anticipate their growing preference for it, while also overestimating the decline in enjoyment for music. Control groups confirmed that the prediction task itself did not influence outcomes.

The results highlight a striking inability to forecast changing tastes, even under favorable conditions. Participants relied heavily on their current hedonic responses as anchors for predictions, leading to systematic errors. For instance, they assumed their liking for yogurt would remain stable after the first taste, ignoring the possibility of adaptation. While group-level trends (e.g., declining enjoyment for familiar stimuli) were sometimes correctly anticipated, individual deviations from these trends were not. The authors speculate that people hold implicit theories about taste dynamics—such as the belief that repetition reduces liking—but these theories are often incorrect or inapplicable to specific contexts.

The discussion underscores the broader implications of these findings. If people cannot predict their future preferences, decisions based on such predictions—from mundane choices like food to life-altering ones like medical procedures—may be flawed. The article challenges the assumption of rational decision-making, suggesting that hedonic ignorance could lead to suboptimal choices and undermine concepts like "informed consent." It also raises ethical questions about whether others might sometimes be justified in overriding an individual’s preferences if they believe those preferences are based on inaccurate forecasts. The authors call for further research into the mechanisms of hedonic prediction and the conditions under which accuracy might improve. Ultimately, the study reveals a fundamental gap in self-knowledge, with significant consequences for both individual well-being and theories of human decision-making.
