# Hindsight Bias and Causal Reasoning: A Minimalist Approach

Yopchick, J. E., & Kim, N. S.

__This study investigates the role of causal reasoning in hindsight bias, demonstrating that the bias occurs only when a causal link can be drawn between event information and the outcome, and that outcome knowledge alone is insufficient to elicit the effect.__

The article explores the Causal Model Theory (CMT) of hindsight bias, which posits that the bias arises when individuals identify and evaluate potential causal antecedents that could explain an event’s outcome. The authors argue that previous research using complex, detailed scenarios has obscured the precise factors contributing to hindsight bias, prompting them to adopt a minimalist approach with highly controlled, sparse materials. In two experiments, they tested whether hindsight bias emerges when participants are given only outcome knowledge or when they are also provided with causally relevant information.

The introduction reviews foundational work on hindsight bias, distinguishing between motivational and cognitive explanations. While motivational accounts emphasize self-image preservation (e.g., individuals overestimating their predictive abilities to maintain a positive self-view), the current study focuses on cognitive processes, particularly causal reasoning. The authors highlight Fischhoff’s (1975) seminal work, which demonstrated that people assimilate outcome knowledge into their understanding of an event, making the outcome seem inevitable—a phenomenon termed "creeping determinism." They also discuss the CMT, which builds on this idea by proposing that hindsight bias occurs when individuals engage in a "sense-making" process, searching for and evaluating causal antecedents to explain an outcome.

In Experiment 1, the authors used minimalist scenarios (e.g., a battle between two tribes, a gold mining expedition) to manipulate the causal relevance of additional information provided to participants. They found that hindsight bias occurred only when the additional information was highly relevant to the actual outcome, supporting the CMT’s prediction that causal reasoning is necessary for the bias to emerge. When the information was of low relevance or absent altogether, hindsight bias did not appear, suggesting that outcome knowledge alone is insufficient to elicit the effect. These findings were corroborated by pilot studies confirming that the high-relevance statements were perceived as more causally relevant than the low-relevance statements.

Experiment 2 extended these findings to reverse hindsight bias, a phenomenon where individuals judge an unexpected outcome as less likely than they would have without outcome knowledge. Using the same minimalist approach, the authors demonstrated that reverse hindsight bias occurred when the causal information better explained an alternative outcome than the actual one. This result further supported the CMT, showing that the theory’s predictions hold for both standard and reverse hindsight bias.

The general discussion synthesizes the findings, emphasizing that causal reasoning is a critical mechanism underlying hindsight bias. The authors acknowledge the trade-off between experimental control and ecological validity in their minimalist approach but argue that their results align with those from more complex scenarios, reinforcing the robustness of the CMT. They also speculate on the broader implications of their work, suggesting that causal reasoning may underlie other cognitive phenomena, such as theory of mind judgments and epistemic egocentrism. Additionally, they propose that motivational factors in hindsight bias, such as personal relevance, may interact with causal reasoning, offering avenues for future research. Overall, the study provides compelling evidence for the CMT and highlights the importance of causal reasoning in shaping retrospective judgments.
