# Contextual Prerequisites for Understanding: Some Investigations of Comprehension and Recall

Bransford, J. D., Johnson, M. K.

__This study demonstrates that providing relevant contextual knowledge before exposure to prose passages significantly enhances comprehension and recall, whereas the same information given afterward has minimal effect. The authors argue that prior knowledge must be activated as a semantic context during processing to facilitate understanding.__

This article challenges the prevailing linguistic theories of comprehension, which emphasize the role of deep structural relations in sentence meaning, by highlighting the critical role of prior knowledge in interpreting and remembering linguistic information. Bransford and Johnson present four experiments to show that comprehension and recall of prose passages depend heavily on the availability of appropriate contextual information before exposure to the material. In Experiment I, participants who viewed a contextual picture (depicting a man serenading a woman with a guitar via balloons and a wire) before hearing an ambiguous passage reported higher comprehension and recalled more ideas than those who saw the picture afterward, received a partial context, or no context at all. The results suggest that the *timing* of contextual information is crucial—only when it is available during the initial processing of the passage does it enable meaningful interpretation.

The subsequent experiments (II, III, and IV) extended this finding by manipulating the provision of a *topic* (e.g., "washing clothes" or "making and flying a kite") rather than a visual context. Again, participants who received the topic before hearing the passage demonstrated superior comprehension and recall compared to those who received it afterward or not at all. The authors argue that topics function not merely as mnemonic devices for generating associations but as tools for activating semantic frameworks that guide the interpretation of incoming information. Notably, even when participants were familiar with the topic, its presentation after exposure to the passage failed to improve performance, reinforcing the idea that comprehension is an *ongoing process* that integrates prior knowledge with new input in real time.

The discussion situates these findings within broader cognitive theories, emphasizing that comprehension is not a passive extraction of meaning from linguistic structures but an active, constructive process that relies on pre-existing knowledge. The authors critique the limitations of deep structure theories, which overlook how context and prior experience shape understanding. They also address alternative explanations for their results, such as the role of retrieval cues, and conclude that while such cues may aid recall, they are ineffective without prior comprehension. The paper closes by underscoring the need for further research into how cognitive contexts are activated and how they interact with linguistic input, offering a framework for studying the dynamic interplay between knowledge and comprehension.
