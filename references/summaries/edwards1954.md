# The Theory of Decision Making

Edwards, W.

__This foundational article reviews economic and psychological theories of decision-making, critiquing the assumptions of "economic man" and exploring models of riskless and risky choices, transitivity, and game theory.__

Ward Edwards’ 1954 paper synthesizes decades of economic and psychological thought on decision-making, offering a comprehensive critique of existing theories while proposing new experimental directions. The article begins by introducing the concept of "economic man," a hypothetical decision-maker assumed to be fully informed, infinitely sensitive, and rational—meaning they can weakly order preferences and maximize utility. Edwards acknowledges that while these assumptions are unrealistic, they form the backbone of economic theories of consumer choice, which rely on utility maximization and indifference curves to predict behavior. He traces the evolution of utility theory from Jeremy Bentham’s hedonistic framework to the more sophisticated indifference curve analysis developed by Edgeworth, Pareto, and later economists like Hicks and Allen, who sought to eliminate cardinal utility in favor of ordinal preferences. Despite the elegance of these models, Edwards highlights their empirical limitations, noting that experiments attempting to measure indifference curves or utility functions often rely on overly simplistic assumptions or fail to account for real-world complexities.

The article then shifts to risky decision-making, where Edwards examines the von Neumann-Morgenstern utility theory, which posits that individuals maximize expected utility when faced with uncertain outcomes. He reviews experiments, such as Mosteller and Nogee’s study, that attempt to measure utility curves for money, but also critiques the theory’s limitations, including the existence of probability preferences—where individuals favor certain probabilities over others regardless of expected value. Edwards’ own experiments demonstrate that such preferences complicate the measurement of utility and subjective probability, suggesting that these constructs may not be independent. He explores alternative models, including Coombs’ ordered metric scaling and Shackle’s potential surprise theory, while cautioning against the overreliance on small, poorly controlled experiments in this emerging field.

Edwards also addresses the transitivity of choices, a cornerstone of rational decision-making. While economic theory assumes transitive preferences, experiments by Papandreou and May reveal that intransitivities occur, particularly when choices involve multiple dimensions. Edwards proposes stochastic models to account for these inconsistencies, arguing that intransitivities may reflect natural variability in human judgment rather than irrationality.

The final section of the article introduces the theory of games, summarizing von Neumann and Morgenstern’s minimax principle, which suggests that players should minimize their maximum possible loss in strategic interactions. Edwards notes the theory’s abstract nature and its limited empirical applications but highlights its potential to inspire experimental research on bargaining, coalition formation, and strategic behavior. He concludes by emphasizing the need for psychologists to engage with these theories, as they offer rich opportunities for experimental validation and refinement. Throughout the paper, Edwards advocates for a more empirical and interdisciplinary approach to decision-making, bridging the gap between economic theory and psychological reality.
