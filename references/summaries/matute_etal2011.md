# Illusions of causality at the heart of pseudoscience

Matute, H., Yarritu, I., & Vadillo, M. A.

__This article argues that pseudoscientific beliefs stem from systematic cognitive illusions in causal reasoning, which arise from the brain’s normal functioning rather than intellectual deficits. Through a laboratory experiment, the authors demonstrate how these illusions can be reduced by exposing individuals to counterfactual evidence and prompting causal thinking.__

The article explores how pseudoscience, quackery, and superstitions persist despite efforts to debunk them, posing significant risks to public health. The authors propose that these beliefs are not due to flawed intelligence or psychological deficits but rather result from the brain’s natural tendency to perceive illusory causal relationships under certain conditions. Drawing from research on the *illusion of control*—where individuals believe they influence uncontrollable outcomes—and *causal learning*—how people infer cause-and-effect relationships—they argue that these cognitive processes, while adaptive in many contexts, can lead to systematic errors when applied to pseudoscientific claims.

To test this hypothesis, the authors conducted an experiment using a fictional medical scenario where participants observed the outcomes of a fictitious treatment for a made-up disease. The study manipulated two key variables: the frequency with which patients took the treatment (high vs. low exposure) and the framing of the assessment question (causal vs. effectiveness-based). Participants were shown records of 100 fictitious patients, with 80% reporting improvement regardless of whether they took the treatment, meaning the treatment had no actual causal effect. Despite this, participants in the high-exposure group (where 80% of patients took the treatment) developed stronger illusions of causality than those in the low-exposure group (where only 20% took it). Additionally, participants who were asked to judge the treatment’s *effectiveness* reported stronger illusions than those asked to assess its *causal* role, suggesting that the wording of questions significantly influences perceptions of causality.

The results confirm that pseudoscientific beliefs can arise vicariously—through testimony or advertising—without direct personal experience. The authors highlight that reducing exposure to confirmatory cases (e.g., showing patients who improved without the treatment) and encouraging causal reasoning (e.g., asking whether the treatment *caused* the improvement) can effectively diminish these illusions. They argue that these findings have practical implications for public education and policy, suggesting that simple interventions, such as presenting balanced evidence or framing questions in causal terms, could help combat pseudoscientific thinking. The study also bridges gaps between the illusion of control and causal learning research, demonstrating how integrating these fields can provide actionable strategies to address pseudoscience in society.
