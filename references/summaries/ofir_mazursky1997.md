# Does a Surprising Outcome Reinforce or Reverse the Hindsight Bias?

Ofir, C., & Mazursky, D.

__This study challenges the conventional understanding of hindsight bias by demonstrating that highly surprising outcomes can eliminate or even reverse the bias, whereas moderately surprising or unsurprising outcomes reinforce it.__

Hindsight bias—the tendency to perceive past events as more predictable than they actually were—has been widely documented across various domains, from medical decisions to historical judgments. Fischhoff’s (1975) "effortless assimilation" theory, the dominant explanation for this bias, posits that individuals naturally integrate outcome knowledge into their existing mental schemas, leading to the "I knew it all along" effect. However, this study introduces a critical moderating factor: the level of surprise experienced upon learning the outcome. The authors argue that while unsurprising outcomes reinforce hindsight bias, highly surprising outcomes disrupt this process, prompting individuals to reflect on their inability to foresee the event, thereby eliminating or reversing the bias.

The paper presents three experiments to test this hypothesis. In Experiment 1, participants were given a scenario about a patient with a heart condition who underwent a bypass operation with a 2% mortality rate. Those informed of the patient’s death (a surprising outcome) were asked to estimate the probability of success *as if they had not known the outcome*. Results showed that highly surprised participants provided *higher* probability estimates for success than a control group without outcome knowledge, contradicting the classical hindsight bias (which would predict lower estimates). This reversal effect was attributed to participants’ reaction: "I could not have expected this to happen." Experiment 2 replicated this finding in a consumer context, where dental patients were told that a mouthwash brand was ineffective in removing plaque. Again, surprised participants judged the brand’s effectiveness as *higher* in hindsight than control subjects, reinforcing the reversal hypothesis.

Experiment 3 further dissected the role of surprise by manipulating outcome information in an art judgment task. Participants were shown an undistinguished painting and asked to estimate the probability it was painted by Picasso, Renoir, a 12-year-old boy, or "Other." Those told the painter was Picasso (a surprising outcome) exhibited *no hindsight bias*, with their probability estimates for Picasso being *lower* than those of a no-outcome control group. When participants were split by surprise level, highly surprised individuals showed a *reversal* of the bias (lower Picasso estimates than controls), while less surprised individuals exhibited the classical bias (higher estimates). Conversely, participants told the painter was "Other" (an unsurprising outcome) displayed the traditional hindsight bias, with higher estimates for "Other" than controls.

The discussion addresses potential methodological concerns, such as the use of subjective probability scales versus rating scales, and critiques prior studies (e.g., Schkade & Kilbourne, 1991) for failing to measure surprise levels, which may explain their reinforcement of hindsight bias. The authors argue that surprising outcomes trigger "special processing," including causal searches and justification efforts, which disrupt the effortless assimilation of outcome information. This leads to the "I could not have expected it" reaction, reversing or eliminating the bias. The study concludes that hindsight bias is not a uniform phenomenon but depends on the surprise level: unsurprising outcomes reinforce the bias, while highly surprising outcomes reverse it. These findings extend the theoretical framework of hindsight bias and highlight the importance of considering emotional and cognitive responses to outcomes in retrospective judgments.
