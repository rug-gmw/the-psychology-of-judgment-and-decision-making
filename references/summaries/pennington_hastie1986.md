# Evidence Evaluation in Complex Decision Making

Pennington, N., Hastie, R.

__This article introduces the Story Model, proposing that jurors construct narrative stories from trial evidence to interpret events and match them to verdict categories, with story differences significantly influencing verdict choices.__

The study challenges traditional decision-making models by arguing that jurors do not merely combine or weigh evidence in a linear or probabilistic manner but instead engage in a cognitively rich process of evidence evaluation. The authors, Nancy Pennington and Reid Hastie, present the Story Model, which posits that jurors organize trial evidence into coherent narrative structures—stories—based on causal and intentional relationships. This narrative construction allows jurors to assign meaning to disjointed testimony and fill in gaps with inferences drawn from general world knowledge. The model consists of three stages: *story construction* (organizing evidence into a narrative), *verdict category establishment* (understanding legal definitions of verdicts), and *story classification* (matching the constructed story to the most fitting verdict category).

The research involved 26 jurors from Massachusetts jury pools who watched a filmed murder trial and were interviewed about their decision-making processes. The study found that jurors’ mental representations of the evidence consistently took the form of stories, with events organized into episode schemas—structures that include initiating events, psychological states, goals, actions, and consequences. Notably, jurors who reached different verdicts constructed distinct stories, with variations in causal chains, inferred motivations, and interpretations of key events. For example, jurors who voted for first-degree murder emphasized premeditation and intent, while those who chose self-defense focused on immediate threats and the defendant’s lack of escape options. These differences mapped directly onto the legal elements of each verdict category, suggesting that story construction plays a functional role in shaping decisions.

The authors also examined whether variations in jurors’ understanding of verdict categories or their application of legal standards (e.g., "beyond a reasonable doubt") influenced verdict choices. However, no systematic differences were found in these areas across verdict groups, further supporting the primacy of story construction in the decision-making process. The study concludes that traditional models of juror decision-making, which focus on information integration or Bayesian probability, fail to account for the interpretive and narrative dimensions of evidence evaluation. Instead, the Story Model highlights the importance of cognitive representations in complex decision-making tasks, offering a framework that may extend to other domains where individuals must evaluate interdependent evidence to reach a judgment.
