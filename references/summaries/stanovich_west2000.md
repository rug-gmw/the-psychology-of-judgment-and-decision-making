# Individual differences in reasoning: Implications for the rationality debate?

**Keith E. Stanovich and Richard F. West**

The article begins by outlining the long-standing debate on human rationality, particularly focusing on the gap between normative models of reasoning and actual human performance. This debate has been contentious, with some researchers (Meliorists) arguing that human cognition is systematically irrational, while others (Panglossians) suggest alternative explanations such as performance errors, computational limitations, incorrect normative models, or different task interpretations by subjects.

The authors introduce the concept of individual differences in reasoning performance as a tool to better understand this normative/descriptive gap. They discuss four primary explanations for this gap: performance errors, computational limitations, incorrect normative models, and alternative task construals. The article emphasizes the importance of principled constraints on these explanations.

In the methods section, the authors describe a series of experiments involving various reasoning tasks from the heuristics and biases literature. They measure individual differences using Scholastic Assessment Test (SAT) scores and other cognitive ability tests. The tasks include syllogistic reasoning, statistical reasoning, covariation detection, hypothesis testing, outcome bias, if/only thinking bias, and argument evaluation.

Results from these experiments show that performance on these reasoning tasks often correlates with cognitive ability measures. For instance, individuals with higher SAT scores tend to perform better on tasks requiring adherence to normative models. The authors also find that some tasks, such as nondeontic versions of Wason's selection task, show significant correlations with cognitive ability, while others, like the false consensus effect, do not.

The discussion delves into the implications of these findings. The authors argue that computational limitations and performance errors can explain some discrepancies, but not all. They introduce the understanding/acceptance principle, suggesting that more reflective and intelligent individuals are more likely to accept appropriate normative principles. This principle is used to evaluate the appropriateness of normative models and task construals.

The article also explores the idea of dual-process theories of reasoning, distinguishing between System 1 (contextualized, interactional intelligence) and System 2 (decontextualized, analytic intelligence). System 1 is characterized by automatic, unconscious processes, while System 2 involves controlled, conscious reasoning. The authors suggest that conflicts between these systems can explain some of the discrepancies in reasoning performance.

Finally, the authors discuss the implications of their findings for the rationality debate, arguing that individual differences in reasoning can provide valuable insights into the nature of human rationality and the factors contributing to the normative/descriptive gap. They conclude that while computational limitations and performance errors play a role, there is also evidence of systematic irrationalities in intentional-level psychology.
