# When More Pain Is Preferred to Less: Adding a Better End

Kahneman, D., Fredrickson, B. L., Schreiber, C. A., & Redelmeier, D. A.

__This study demonstrates that people often prefer to repeat a longer, more painful experience over a shorter one if it ends on a less painful note, revealing that retrospective evaluations of pain are shaped more by peak and final moments than by total duration.__

This article explores how people evaluate and remember painful experiences, challenging the assumption that longer durations of pain are inherently worse. The authors, led by Daniel Kahneman, build on prior research showing that retrospective assessments of aversive episodes are disproportionately influenced by the most intense moment (the "peak") and the final moment (the "end"), while the total duration of the experience is largely ignored. To test this phenomenon in a controlled setting, the researchers conducted an experiment where participants submerged one hand in cold water—first for 60 seconds at 14°C (the "short trial"), and then for 60 seconds at the same temperature followed by an additional 30 seconds during which the water temperature was slightly raised to 15°C (the "long trial"). Despite the long trial involving more total pain, a significant majority of participants (69%) chose to repeat it when given the option, suggesting they retained a more favorable memory of it due to its less painful ending.

The study’s methods were carefully designed to isolate the effects of peak and end experiences. Participants, all healthy male university students, were screened for health conditions and drug use to ensure consistency in pain perception. The cold-water apparatus maintained precise temperature control, and participants used a discomfort meter to report their pain levels in real time. After experiencing both trials, they were asked which one they would prefer to repeat, along with additional questions comparing the two experiences. The results confirmed the researchers’ hypothesis: participants who experienced a noticeable reduction in pain during the final 30 seconds of the long trial were far more likely to choose it again, while those who felt little or no relief were split in their preferences. This aligns with the "peak-and-end rule," which posits that retrospective evaluations are shaped by an average of the worst and final moments of an experience, rather than its total duration.

The discussion expands on the implications of these findings, emphasizing that this cognitive bias can lead to irrational decision-making. For example, participants who chose the long trial were not doing so because they valued the extra pain but because their memory of the experience was skewed by its more pleasant ending. The authors note that this phenomenon extends beyond laboratory settings, citing studies of colonoscopy patients and film viewers, where similar patterns emerged. They also acknowledge that duration is not always irrelevant—it may matter in cases where an experience is much longer or shorter than expected—but argue that the peak-and-end rule dominates in most evaluations. The article raises ethical questions about informed consent in medical contexts, where patients might unknowingly prefer treatments that leave them with better memories but involve more actual pain. Ultimately, the study underscores a fundamental disconnect between the experience of pain and its memory, suggesting that people often make choices based on flawed recollections rather than objective reality.
