# Creating False Memories: Remembering Words Not Presented in Lists

Roediger III, H. L., & McDermott, K. B.

__This study demonstrates how people can vividly recall or recognize words that were never presented in word lists, revealing a powerful illusion of memory. Using a paradigm inspired by Deese (1959), the authors show that false memories arise from associative processes and can feel as real as true memories.__

The article explores the phenomenon of false memories through two experiments that build on Deese’s (1959) work, which had been largely overlooked in the decades since its publication. The authors sought to replicate and extend Deese’s findings by examining false recall and false recognition in a list-learning paradigm. In Experiment 1, participants studied lists of 12 words (e.g., *bed, rest, awake*), each composed of associates of a nonpresented critical word (e.g., *sleep*). On immediate free recall tests, participants falsely recalled the nonpresented word 40% of the time and later recognized it with high confidence. The results showed that false memories were not due to wild guessing, as other intrusions were rare, but rather reflected a compelling illusion where nonpresented words were remembered as vividly as presented ones.

Experiment 2 expanded the scope by using 24 lists of 15 words each, further increasing the false recall rate to 55%. The study also introduced the "remember-know" procedure, where participants distinguished between items they vividly remembered encountering and those they simply knew were familiar. Strikingly, participants often reported *remembering* the nonpresented critical words, indicating that these false memories were not just feelings of familiarity but involved detailed, conscious recollection. The act of recalling the lists enhanced both accurate and false memories, with falsely recalled items later recognized at rates comparable to or even exceeding those of studied items.

The authors discuss these findings in the context of prior research on false memories, which had primarily focused on prose, sentences, or visual materials rather than word lists. They argue that associative processes—where activation spreads through a network of related concepts—play a central role in generating false memories. For example, hearing words like *bed* and *rest* may implicitly activate the concept of *sleep*, leading participants to later "remember" encountering it. The results challenge the traditional distinction between reproductive memory (accurate recall) and reconstructive memory (error-prone recall), suggesting that all remembering is inherently constructive and prone to distortion.

The study also has broader implications for debates about false memories in real-world contexts, such as recovered memories of childhood abuse. While the experiments were conducted in a controlled laboratory setting, the authors note that the ease with which false memories were created under optimal conditions underscores the fragility of human memory. They caution that vivid recollections, even those accompanied by high confidence, cannot be taken as definitive proof that an event occurred. The findings align with theories of source monitoring, where individuals confuse internally generated thoughts (e.g., associations) with externally presented information, and with attributional models of memory, where the fluency of recalling an event can lead to false feelings of remembering.

Overall, the article highlights the robustness of false memories and their potential to arise from everyday cognitive processes, offering a paradigm for further study of memory distortions.
