# Causes and Conditions

Mackie, J. L.

__This article challenges the traditional view that a cause is a necessary and sufficient preceding condition for an effect, proposing instead that causes are often "INUS conditions"—insufficient but necessary parts of unnecessary but sufficient conditions. Mackie develops a formal analysis of causation, distinguishing between singular and general causal statements, and explores how such claims can be empirically supported or falsified.__

The article begins by critiquing the conventional definition of a cause as a necessary and sufficient preceding condition for an effect, using the example of a house fire caused by an electrical short-circuit. Mackie argues that the short-circuit is neither necessary (since other events could have caused the fire) nor sufficient (since additional conditions, like the presence of inflammable material, were required). Instead, he introduces the concept of an *INUS condition*—an "Insufficient but Necessary part of an Unnecessary but Sufficient" condition. In the fire example, the short-circuit is an indispensable part of a complex set of conditions that, together, were sufficient to cause the fire, even though other sets of conditions could have produced the same result. This framework allows for a more nuanced understanding of causation, where causes are often embedded within broader, contingent sets of conditions.

Mackie formalizes this idea by defining an INUS condition as a factor *A* that is part of a minimal sufficient condition *AX* (where *X* represents other necessary conditions) for an effect *P*, such that the disjunction of all such minimal sufficient conditions (*AX or Y*) constitutes a necessary and sufficient condition for *P*. He argues that singular causal statements, such as "A caused P," often implicitly claim that *A* is at least an INUS condition of *P*, that *A* and its accompanying conditions were present, and that no other sufficient conditions for *P* were present. This account aligns with, but refines, earlier analyses by philosophers like Konrad Marc-Wogau and Michael Scriven, who similarly emphasized that causes are non-redundant parts of sufficient conditions.

The article then introduces the notion of a *causal field*—the broader context or domain within which a cause operates. For example, the cause of a fire in a house is sought within the history of that house, not the entire universe. This concept helps address the difficulty of identifying genuinely sufficient conditions, as it allows for the exclusion of irrelevant or overly broad factors (e.g., cosmic events) from the causal analysis. Mackie also discusses complications, such as cases where multiple sufficient conditions are present simultaneously (e.g., two bullets striking a target at once), or where a cause is redundant because an alternative sufficient condition was also present. In such cases, the ordinary concept of causation becomes ambiguous, and Mackie suggests that additional rules may be needed to clarify usage.

Turning to general causal statements (e.g., "smoking causes lung cancer"), Mackie argues that these often assert that a factor is an INUS condition within a specified field. For instance, smoking is neither necessary nor sufficient for lung cancer, but it is an indispensable part of a sufficient condition (e.g., smoking combined with genetic predisposition and environmental factors). He notes that some causal statements refer to *necessary* conditions (e.g., "the yellow fever virus is the cause of yellow fever") or to *functional dependence*, where the magnitude of an effect varies with the magnitude of the cause. However, the INUS framework remains central to his analysis.

Mackie then explores how causal claims can be empirically supported, focusing on the *Method of Difference*—a form of eliminative induction where an effect is observed in one instance (*I₁*) but not in a comparable negative case (*N₁*), with the only difference being the presence of the putative cause *A*. He formalizes this method, showing how it can establish that *A* is at least an INUS condition for *P* within a given field. For example, dipping litmus paper in acid turns it red, and this observation, combined with the assumption that the color change has a necessary and sufficient condition, supports the conclusion that the acid is an INUS condition for the reddening. Mackie argues that this method is widely used in science and everyday reasoning, even if the assumption of determinism (that every event has a cause) is not universally justified. He also addresses the *progressive localization* of causes, where initial rough causal claims (e.g., "drinking wine causes intoxication") are refined through further observation (e.g., "alcohol in wine causes intoxication").

The article acknowledges that INUS-based causal statements are not conclusively falsifiable, as they are often incomplete or "gappy." However, Mackie argues that this is not a flaw but a reflection of how causal knowledge is actually acquired and used. Evidence can still count against such statements—for example, if a previously unnoticed factor is found to account for the effect, the original causal claim may be abandoned. He also briefly discusses *functional dependence*, where causes and effects vary in magnitude (e.g., temperature and metal expansion), and shows how the Method of Concomitant Variation can support such claims.

Finally, Mackie addresses the *direction of causation*—the distinction between *A* causing *P* and *P* causing *A*. He argues that causal priority is not identical to temporal priority, though the two are often aligned. He suggests that the direction of causation may be linked to *explanatory priority*, where a single event (*A*) can explain a complex, coherent effect (*P*) more naturally than the reverse. The article concludes by highlighting the broader implications of Mackie’s analysis, including its potential to reconcile competing views on historical explanation and to clarify the nature of mental causation. By grounding causation in observable regularities while allowing for incomplete or elliptical laws, Mackie’s account bridges the gap between singular causal claims and general scientific principles.
