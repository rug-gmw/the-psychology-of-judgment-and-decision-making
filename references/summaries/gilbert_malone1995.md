# The Correspondence Bias

Gilbert, D. T., & Malone, P. S.

__This article explores the *correspondence bias*—the tendency to infer a person’s enduring dispositions from behaviors that are actually explained by situational factors. The authors argue that this bias arises from four distinct psychological mechanisms and discuss its persistence despite logical flaws.__

The *correspondence bias* refers to the human tendency to attribute behaviors to an individual’s stable personality traits or dispositions, even when those behaviors are clearly shaped by situational constraints. Gilbert and Malone challenge the notion that this bias stems from a single psychological process, instead proposing that it emerges from four distinct mechanisms: *lack of awareness* of situational forces, *unrealistic expectations* about how people behave in certain contexts, *inflated categorizations* of behavior due to situational knowledge, and *incomplete corrections* of initial dispositional inferences. The article traces the intellectual history of this phenomenon in social psychology, situating it within broader debates about attribution theory and the person-situation distinction.

The authors begin by defining the correspondence bias as a violation of the *discounting principle*—a foundational rule in attribution theory stating that observers should not infer dispositions when a behavior is fully explained by situational demands. For example, if a politician delivers a pro-choice speech to a liberal audience, observers should not assume the politician personally holds pro-choice views, as the situation (audience pressure) sufficiently explains the behavior. Yet, decades of research demonstrate that people routinely ignore this principle, making dispositional inferences even when situational explanations are obvious. The article critiques the field’s fragmented approach to studying this bias, arguing that its persistence as an unsolved puzzle stems from a failure to recognize that it is not a single phenomenon but a constellation of related processes.

A brief historical overview highlights key milestones in the study of correspondence bias. Kurt Lewin’s 1931 essay critiqued psychology’s Aristotelian tendency to explain behavior through internal traits rather than situational interactions, laying the groundwork for later research. Gustav Ichheiser’s 1949 work further emphasized how people overlook situational constraints, a theme later echoed by Fritz Heider. The 1960s saw the first empirical tests of this bias, notably Jones and Harris’s (1967) *attitude attribution paradigm*, where participants inferred pro-Castro attitudes from essays written under coercion. Lee Ross’s 1977 concept of the *fundamental attribution error* (a broader term for the bias) reframed it as central to social psychology, linking it to underestimations of situational power in classic experiments like Milgram’s obedience studies.

Gilbert and Malone then dissect the four mechanisms underlying the correspondence bias. The first, *lack of awareness*, occurs when observers fail to recognize situational constraints due to their invisibility (e.g., unseen social pressures) or misconstrual (e.g., assuming an actor perceives a situation the same way the observer does). The second, *unrealistic expectations*, involves observers misjudging how strongly a situation influences behavior, often due to the *availability heuristic*—assuming others would act as they themselves would. The third, *inflated categorizations*, describes how situational knowledge can distort perceptions of behavior, making it seem more extreme than it is (e.g., interpreting a nervous interviewee’s behavior as highly anxious because the topic is known to be stressful). The fourth, *incomplete corrections*, reflects a two-step attributional process: observers first make spontaneous dispositional inferences and then—if cognitively able—adjust them based on situational information. However, cognitive load or distraction can impair this correction, leaving the initial bias intact.

The authors also address the *salience effect*, a commonly cited explanation for the bias, arguing that it lacks empirical support. While salient behaviors may draw attention, they do not inherently lead to dispositional inferences unless they obscure situational factors. This critique underscores their broader claim that the correspondence bias is not a monolithic error but a product of multiple, context-dependent mechanisms.

Finally, the article explores why the correspondence bias persists despite its logical flaws. The authors propose that its consequences are often benign or even beneficial. For instance, when situational constraints are *self-induced* (e.g., a conservative person choosing a banking career), *omnipresent* (e.g., a night manager’s distrustful behavior in a high-crime environment), or *superfluous* (e.g., a child rewarded for an activity they already enjoy), ignoring them may lead to accurate or pragmatically useful inferences. Moreover, dispositional attributions satisfy a cultural preference in Western societies for individual agency and control, reinforcing a sense of predictability in social interactions. While the bias can lead to errors—such as misjudging a hostage’s anti-American remarks as genuine—the authors argue that its adaptive value in many contexts explains its endurance.

In closing, Gilbert and Malone acknowledge that social psychology experiments are ill-suited to measure the prevalence of the correspondence bias in everyday life. However, they caution against dismissing its significance, noting that real-world misjudgments—from personal relationships to political conflicts—often stem from this fundamental tendency to overlook situational forces. The article thus reframes the correspondence bias not as a mere cognitive error but as a complex, multifaceted phenomenon with deep roots in human psychology and culture.
