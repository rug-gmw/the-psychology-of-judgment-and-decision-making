# Confirmation bias: why psychiatrists stick to wrong preliminary diagnoses

Mendel, R., Traut-Mattausch, E., Jonas, E., Leucht, S., Kane, J. M., Maino, K., Kissling, W., & Hamann, J.

__This study demonstrates that confirmation bias—seeking information that aligns with a preliminary diagnosis while ignoring contradictory evidence—significantly impairs diagnostic accuracy in psychiatry, leading to incorrect diagnoses and treatment decisions in both psychiatrists and medical students.__

The article investigates whether psychiatrists and medical students are susceptible to confirmation bias during diagnostic decision-making and whether this bias negatively affects diagnostic accuracy. The authors begin by defining confirmation bias as the tendency to favor information that supports an initial hypothesis while neglecting disconfirming evidence, a phenomenon well-documented in psychology but understudied in clinical medicine. They argue that such bias can lead to a cascade of errors, including incorrect diagnoses and inappropriate treatments, with potentially severe consequences for patients. Despite warnings in medical literature about cognitive biases, empirical research on confirmation bias in clinical settings—particularly in psychiatry—remains scarce.

To address this gap, the researchers designed an experimental study involving 75 psychiatrists and 75 medical students. Participants were presented with a case vignette of a 65-year-old male patient exhibiting depressive symptoms but with subtle indicators of Alzheimer’s disease. The vignette was constructed to lead most participants toward an incorrect preliminary diagnosis of major depression. After forming their initial hypothesis, participants could request additional information from a set of 12 items—six supporting depression and six supporting Alzheimer’s disease—to refine their diagnosis. The study measured whether participants engaged in a confirmatory (favoring information aligned with their preliminary diagnosis), disconfirmatory (seeking contradictory evidence), or balanced (equal consideration of both) information search.

Results revealed that 13% of psychiatrists and 25% of medical students exhibited confirmation bias, requesting more information that aligned with their initial diagnosis. Psychiatrists who engaged in a confirmatory search were significantly more likely to make an incorrect final diagnosis (70% error rate) compared to those who conducted a balanced (47% error rate) or disconfirmatory (27% error rate) search. Medical students showed similar patterns, with a 63% error rate for confirmatory searchers. A logistic regression analysis confirmed that confirmatory information search was a strong predictor of diagnostic inaccuracy, even after controlling for variables like professional experience and the number of information items requested. Additionally, incorrect diagnoses led to inappropriate treatment recommendations, such as prescribing antidepressants instead of anti-dementia drugs.

The discussion highlights the clinical implications of these findings, emphasizing that confirmation bias poses a tangible risk to patient care. The authors note that while only a minority of participants exhibited the bias, its impact is magnified when combined with other cognitive biases, potentially affecting a substantial proportion of medical decisions. They also explore potential explanations for confirmation bias, including cognitive dissonance theory (where individuals seek to reduce mental discomfort by confirming their initial beliefs) and cognitive limitations (where simpler decision-making strategies are favored over more complex ones).

To mitigate confirmation bias, the authors suggest several debiasing strategies, such as meta-cognitive training, encouraging systematic consideration of alternative diagnoses, and using decision-support tools. However, they acknowledge that many of these strategies lack robust validation or practical implementation in clinical settings. The study’s limitations, including its experimental design and the artificial nature of the diagnostic task, are also addressed, though the authors argue that the controlled setting allowed for a clear examination of the bias’s effects.

In conclusion, the study provides empirical evidence that confirmation bias negatively impacts diagnostic accuracy in psychiatry and underscores the need for greater awareness and targeted interventions to reduce its influence in clinical practice.
