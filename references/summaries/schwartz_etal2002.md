# Maximizing Versus Satisficing: Happiness Is a Matter of Choice

Schwartz, B., Ward, A., Lyubomirsky, S., Monterosso, J., White, K., & Lehman, D. R.

__This study explores how individual differences in decision-making strategies—maximizing (seeking the best possible outcome) versus satisficing (settling for "good enough")—impact well-being, finding that maximizers tend to experience lower happiness, higher regret, and greater susceptibility to social comparison than satisficers.__

The article challenges the assumptions of rational choice theory, which posits that individuals aim to maximize utility in decision-making. Instead, the authors draw on Herbert Simon’s concept of "satisficing," where individuals seek outcomes that meet a threshold of acceptability rather than the absolute best. The study introduces two scales—the Maximization Scale and the Regret Scale—to measure individual differences in these decision-making tendencies. Across seven diverse samples (totaling 1,747 participants), the authors found consistent negative correlations between maximization and measures of well-being, including happiness, life satisfaction, optimism, and self-esteem. Conversely, maximization was positively correlated with depression, perfectionism, and regret. These findings suggest that while maximizers may achieve objectively better outcomes due to their exhaustive search for the best option, they often experience worse subjective outcomes.

Study 1 established the validity and reliability of the Maximization and Regret Scales through factor analysis and demonstrated their associations with well-being measures. The results indicated that maximizers reported lower life satisfaction, happiness, and self-esteem, while also experiencing higher levels of regret and depression. The authors proposed that regret might partially mediate the relationship between maximization and well-being, as maximizers’ tendency to second-guess their choices and consider foregone alternatives likely contributes to their dissatisfaction.

Study 2 examined the role of social comparison in consumer decision-making, hypothesizing that maximizers would engage in more social comparison and experience greater regret in their purchasing decisions. Participants completed questionnaires assessing their general social comparison tendencies and specific consumer behaviors, such as product comparison and post-purchase satisfaction. The results confirmed that maximizers reported more frequent social comparisons, greater regret, and lower satisfaction with their purchases. This effect persisted even after controlling for dispositional happiness, suggesting that maximization itself, rather than underlying unhappiness, drives these behaviors.

Study 3 experimentally tested the hypothesis that maximizers are more sensitive to social comparison than satisficers. Participants solved anagrams alongside a confederate who either outperformed or underperformed them. Maximizers who witnessed a faster peer reported greater declines in self-assessed ability and increases in negative mood compared to those who saw a slower peer. Satisficers, in contrast, were less affected by the social comparison manipulation. These findings supported the idea that maximizers rely more heavily on relative performance to evaluate their outcomes, which can amplify dissatisfaction when they perceive themselves as falling short.

Study 4 investigated whether maximizers exhibit different decision-making behaviors and experience greater regret in an ultimatum bargaining game. Participants played two versions of the game: a standard version and a modified version where the minimum acceptable offer (reservation price) was revealed after acceptance. While maximizers did not consistently make lower offers, they were less satisfied with their outcomes overall, regardless of whether their offers were accepted or rejected. The study provided partial support for the hypothesis that maximizers are more sensitive to regret, though the results were nuanced and suggested that gender and social motivations might also play a role in decision-making.

The discussion synthesizes these findings, proposing several mechanisms to explain why maximizers experience lower well-being. First, the exhaustive search for the best option often leads to anticipated and experienced regret, as maximizers are more likely to dwell on foregone alternatives. Second, adaptation processes diminish the satisfaction derived from chosen outcomes, and maximizers, with their higher expectations, may be more vulnerable to this effect. Third, maximizers rely heavily on social comparison to evaluate their choices, which can exacerbate dissatisfaction when they perceive others as doing better. Finally, the proliferation of options in modern life may lead maximizers to blame themselves for suboptimal outcomes, fostering a depressogenic attributional style.

The authors also address potential caveats and unanswered questions. For instance, they acknowledge that the causal direction between maximization and unhappiness remains unclear—whether being a maximizer leads to unhappiness or whether unhappy individuals are more likely to adopt a maximizing strategy. They also highlight the need for further research on the domain specificity of maximization, the role of other types of comparisons (e.g., temporal or counterfactual), and the behavioral differences between maximizers and satisficers in real-world decision-making contexts.

Overall, the article presents a compelling case that the pursuit of the "best" outcome, while logically sound, may come at a psychological cost. The findings underscore the paradox that more choices and higher standards, often seen as markers of freedom and progress, can undermine well-being for those who strive to maximize their outcomes.
