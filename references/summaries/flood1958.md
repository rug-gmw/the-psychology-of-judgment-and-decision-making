# Some Experimental Games

Flood, M. M.

__This paper presents experimental findings on human behavior in non-constant-sum games, testing the applicability of game theory to real-world bargaining. It proposes a "split-the-difference principle" to refine theoretical predictions and explores how players adapt strategies over repeated trials, often deviating from optimal outcomes.__

Flood’s paper investigates the practical relevance of non-constant-sum game theory—particularly the von Neumann-Morgenstern framework—by reporting six experiments designed to mimic real-world bargaining scenarios. The introduction highlights the unresolved nature of non-constant-sum games in classical game theory, noting that while theoretical advancements (e.g., Nash’s bargaining solution) have addressed some cases, no universally accepted set of axioms exists to predict player behavior in all situations. Flood’s work is exploratory, aiming to bridge the gap between abstract theory and observed human decision-making, despite its limited scope and tentative conclusions.

The experiments focus on two-person bargaining situations, such as the sale of a used car, where the "split-the-difference principle" is introduced as a heuristic to determine final payments. This principle, which suggests dividing the surplus between players based on their relative bargaining positions, appears to align well with experimental outcomes. One key finding is that players often begin near an equilibrium point in repeated trials but then adjust their strategies to seek more favorable equilibria if available. However, the experiments also reveal that when payoff calculations are ambiguous or complex, players frequently deviate from optimal behavior, leading to suboptimal outcomes.

Beyond two-person interactions, Flood examines a scenario involving five players dividing five parcels of objects, each with distinct valuations. The analysis outlines how fair divisions can be computed and discusses the role of coalitions in shaping outcomes. While the experiments are modest in scale, they underscore the challenges of applying game theory to real-world bargaining, where psychological factors, uncertainty, and strategic adaptation play significant roles. The paper concludes by suggesting that game theory may need further refinement—such as incorporating behavioral principles like the split-the-difference rule—to better predict and explain human interactions in non-constant-sum settings.
