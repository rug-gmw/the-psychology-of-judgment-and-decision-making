# On the Psychology of Prediction

Kahneman, D., & Tversky, A.

__This seminal paper demonstrates that people rely on the *representativeness heuristic*—judging likelihood based on how well evidence matches stereotypes—while ignoring statistical principles like base rates and regression, leading to systematic errors in prediction.__

The article explores how intuitive predictions deviate from normative statistical models, focusing on the role of the *representativeness heuristic*. Kahneman and Tversky argue that people predict outcomes by assessing how representative the evidence is of a given category or value, rather than incorporating prior probabilities or the reliability of the evidence. This leads to predictions that are overly extreme and insensitive to statistical constraints.

The paper is structured around two main types of prediction: categorical (e.g., diagnosing a profession based on a personality sketch) and numerical (e.g., forecasting academic performance). In categorical prediction, the authors show that judgments of likelihood closely align with judgments of similarity, while base rates are largely ignored. For example, in the "Tom W." study, subjects ranked the likelihood of Tom’s graduate specialization based on a personality description, and their predictions mirrored similarity rankings rather than the actual distribution of fields. Even when explicitly told that projective tests (the source of the description) were unreliable, subjects still disregarded base rates, violating Bayesian principles. A follow-up study manipulated expected accuracy, but subjects’ predictions remained unaffected, further confirming the dominance of representativeness over statistical reasoning.

In numerical prediction, the authors demonstrate that people fail to regress predictions toward the mean, even when predictive accuracy is low. For instance, when evaluating a student’s academic potential based on a brief description, subjects’ predictions of future GPA were just as extreme as their evaluations of the description’s impressiveness, despite the inherent uncertainty. Similarly, when predicting GPA from percentile scores on tests of varying validity (e.g., mental concentration vs. sense of humor), subjects’ predictions were non-regressive for inputs perceived as relevant (e.g., mental concentration) but regressive for irrelevant ones (e.g., sense of humor). This pattern held even in real-world settings, such as military officer selection, where predictions of future performance matched the distribution of past outcomes despite known moderate predictive validity.

The paper also addresses methodological considerations, showing that predictions are no more regressive than evaluations or translations of inputs, contrary to statistical theory. The authors link overconfidence in predictions to the *illusion of validity*, where consistency in input information (e.g., correlated test scores) inflates confidence despite reducing predictive accuracy. Finally, they highlight how people misinterpret regression effects, often inventing spurious explanations (e.g., attributing performance declines after praise to psychological factors) rather than recognizing the statistical inevitability of regression toward the mean.

Overall, the article reveals a fundamental disconnect between human intuition and statistical principles, with the representativeness heuristic driving predictions that are systematically biased and overconfident. These findings laid the groundwork for the authors’ later work on heuristics and biases in judgment and decision-making.
