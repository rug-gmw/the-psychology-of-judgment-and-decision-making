# Equilibrium Points in N-Person Games

Nash, J. F., Jr.

__This seminal paper introduces the concept of equilibrium points in n-person games, proving their existence using Kakutani’s fixed-point theorem and demonstrating their foundational role in game theory.__

John Nash’s article presents a groundbreaking formalization of equilibrium in non-cooperative games with an arbitrary number of players. The paper begins by defining an *n-person game* in which each player selects from a finite set of pure strategies, and a corresponding set of payoffs is determined by the combination of strategies chosen. When players adopt *mixed strategies*—probability distributions over their pure strategies—the payoffs become expected values, expressed as polylinear forms in the probabilities assigned to each strategy. Nash then introduces the critical concept of an *equilibrium point*: an n-tuple of strategies (one per player) where no player can unilaterally improve their expected payoff by deviating from their chosen strategy. Such an equilibrium is described as a "self-countering" n-tuple, where each player’s strategy is optimal against the strategies of the others.

To prove the existence of equilibrium points, Nash constructs a mapping from the product space of all possible strategy combinations to itself, where each point is associated with its set of countering (or best-response) strategies. He demonstrates that this mapping has two key properties: the image of any point is convex, and the graph of the mapping is closed. These properties allow him to invoke Kakutani’s fixed-point theorem, which guarantees the existence of a fixed point—a strategy combination contained within its own image. This fixed point corresponds precisely to an equilibrium point in the game. Nash further notes that in the special case of two-person zero-sum games, the existence of an equilibrium point is equivalent to von Neumann and Morgenstern’s "main theorem," though in general, multiple equilibrium points may yield different payoffs for the players. The paper thus establishes a general and rigorous foundation for equilibrium analysis in game theory, extending beyond the zero-sum framework and paving the way for modern economic and strategic modeling.
