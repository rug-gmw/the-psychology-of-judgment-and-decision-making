# Cognitive Development: Foundational Theories of Core Domains

Wellman, H. M., Gelman, S. A.

__This article argues that cognitive development in children is best understood through the acquisition of foundational theories in core domains such as naive physics, naive psychology, and naive biology, challenging earlier domain-general approaches like Piaget’s stages of development.__

The article explores the shift in cognitive development research from domain-general theories, such as Piaget’s stages of cognitive development, to domain-specific frameworks that emphasize foundational theories in distinct areas of knowledge. Wellman and Gelman propose that children develop early, specialized understandings in three core domains: naive physics, naive psychology, and naive biology. These domains are characterized by unique ontologies and causal reasoning frameworks that guide further conceptual development.

The introduction critiques earlier domain-general approaches, such as Piaget’s theory of cognitive stages and information-processing models, which suggest that cognitive development is driven by broad, content-independent processes. The authors argue that recent research demonstrates children’s cognition is highly domain-specific, with distinct systems of knowledge emerging early in development. For instance, children’s memory skills are tied to specific content domains, and their understanding of concepts like classification and conservation varies across different types of knowledge.

The concept of "framework theories" is introduced as a way to understand how children organize their knowledge. Framework theories are broad, foundational understandings that define the ontology and causal principles of a domain, guiding the development of more specific theories. The authors draw parallels between these framework theories in children and paradigms in scientific communities, which similarly provide foundational assumptions for further inquiry.

The article then delves into three core domains of foundational knowledge: naive physics, naive psychology, and naive biology. In naive physics, infants as young as 3-4 months demonstrate an understanding of object permanence, solidity, and basic mechanical causality. For example, infants expect objects to continue existing even when hidden and understand simple physical interactions like collisions. As children grow, their understanding of physical objects and causality becomes more sophisticated, encompassing concepts like the insides of objects and the dynamics of mechanical forces.

In naive psychology, children develop a "theory of mind," which involves understanding mental states such as beliefs and desires. By age 3 or 4, children distinguish between mental and physical entities, recognize the subjectivity of thoughts, and understand false beliefs. This understanding is preceded by earlier phases of social interaction, where infants begin to recognize intentionality in others’ actions. The authors also discuss how impairments in this domain, such as in autism, highlight the distinctiveness of naive psychology as a cognitive framework.

Naive biology involves children’s understanding of living things and biological processes. The article reviews evidence that children distinguish between animate and inanimate objects early in development, though their understanding of plants as living things develops later. Children’s reasoning about biological phenomena, such as inheritance, growth, and illness, suggests they possess biologically specific causal beliefs that are distinct from psychological or physical explanations. For example, preschoolers understand that animals inherit traits from their parents and that biological processes like growth are intrinsic rather than driven by human intentions.

The conclusion emphasizes that these foundational theories provide a framework for children’s further cognitive development, enabling them to acquire more detailed and specific knowledge within each domain. The authors argue that these early understandings are not merely simplifications of adult knowledge but represent coherent, theory-like systems that guide children’s reasoning and learning. They also highlight the need for further research to explore how these foundational theories interact with cultural and scientific knowledge systems, as well as how they develop and differentiate over time.
