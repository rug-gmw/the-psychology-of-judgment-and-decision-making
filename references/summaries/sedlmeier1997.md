# BasicBayes: A tutor system for simple Bayesian inference

Peter S. Sedlmeier

__This article introduces *BasicBayes*, a computerized tutor designed to teach Bayesian inference by leveraging frequency formats instead of probability formats, based on Gigerenzer and Hoffrage’s (1995) theoretical framework. The system demonstrates significant success in improving users' Bayesian reasoning skills, outperforming traditional rule-based training methods. Its flexible design also allows it to function as a research tool for studying statistical reasoning.__

The article begins by highlighting the persistent difficulty people face in applying Bayesian inference, a framework for updating the probability of a hypothesis in light of new evidence. Despite its practical importance—such as in medical diagnostics—most individuals, including experts like physicians, struggle with problems like the "mammography problem," often neglecting base rates and producing wildly inaccurate estimates. Previous attempts to teach Bayesian reasoning, such as rule-based training or corrective feedback, have yielded limited success, with effects that are neither substantial nor long-lasting.

The theoretical foundation of *BasicBayes* stems from Gigerenzer and Hoffrage’s (1995) argument that human cognitive algorithms for statistical reasoning are evolutionarily tuned to *frequency formats* rather than probabilities or percentages. This perspective suggests that the widespread failure to solve Bayesian problems arises not from inherent cognitive limitations but from the mismatch between the information format (probabilities) and the mind’s natural processing mechanisms. Empirical studies support this claim, showing that presenting problems in frequency formats—such as imagining 100 women and tracking how many test positive for cancer—dramatically improves performance. For example, Gigerenzer and Hoffrage found that 46% of participants solved Bayesian problems correctly when presented in frequency formats, compared to just 16% with probability formats.

*BasicBayes* operationalizes this insight by teaching users how to translate probability-based problems into frequency formats using two visual tools: the *frequency grid* and the *frequency tree*. The frequency grid represents cases (e.g., women) as squares in a grid, where users can mark those with the hypothesis (e.g., cancer) and the data (e.g., positive test results). The frequency tree, meanwhile, organizes information hierarchically, allowing users to input numbers at each node to reflect the relationships between hypotheses and evidence. The tutor operates in two modes: a *fixed mode*, where the system demonstrates step-by-step solutions to problems like the mammography example, and a *flexible mode*, where users actively manipulate the frequency formats and receive immediate feedback on their inputs. Both modes emphasize the importance of reference class size (e.g., 100 vs. 1,000 cases) in achieving accurate posterior probability estimates.

The effectiveness of *BasicBayes* was empirically validated through a study comparing its frequency-based training to rule-based training and a no-training control group. Before training, participants in all groups performed poorly, with median correct solution rates near 0%. Immediately after training, the frequency-based groups (grid and tree) achieved median correct rates of 70–80%, while the rule-training group reached only 35%. Even more strikingly, five weeks later, the frequency groups’ performance improved to 90%, whereas the rule-training group’s performance dropped to 20%. These results underscore the durability and transferability of the frequency-based approach, as users retained and applied the skill to novel problems long after training.

Beyond its educational purpose, *BasicBayes* is designed as a flexible research tool. It includes 38 preloaded Bayesian problems and allows researchers to add custom problems via a template. The system can administer tests without feedback, saving results for later analysis, making it useful for studying factors influencing Bayesian reasoning. The article concludes by emphasizing the broader implications of *BasicBayes*: it offers a practical, theory-driven solution to a long-standing challenge in statistical education, demonstrating that teaching people to *represent* information in evolutionarily aligned formats can unlock their latent reasoning abilities. The tutor is available free for noncommercial use, positioning it as a valuable resource for classrooms and research settings alike.
