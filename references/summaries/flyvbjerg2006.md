# From Nobel Prize to Project Management: Getting Risks Right

Flyvbjerg, B., & Techn., D.

__This article argues that inaccurate forecasts in project management—driven by optimism bias and strategic misrepresentation—can be mitigated using "reference class forecasting," a method rooted in Nobel Prize-winning behavioral economics. The authors demonstrate its first practical application in transportation infrastructure, showing how an "outside view" improves accuracy by grounding estimates in historical data rather than subjective projections.__

The article begins by documenting the persistent and severe inaccuracy of cost, demand, and impact forecasts in project management, particularly in transportation infrastructure. For decades, forecasts for rail, bridges, tunnels, and roads have consistently overestimated benefits and underestimated costs, with average cost overruns of 44.7% for rail, 33.8% for bridges and tunnels, and 20.4% for roads. Demand forecasts fare no better, with rail passenger projections off by an average of -51.4% and road traffic forecasts wrong by ±20% in half of all cases. These inaccuracies distort cost-benefit analyses, leading to misleading viability assessments and poor decision-making. The authors emphasize that this problem is not unique to transportation but extends to a wide range of projects, from IT systems to megaprojects like dams and sports arenas.

To explain this inaccuracy, the article contrasts technical, psychological, and political-economic explanations. Technical explanations—such as poor data or flawed models—fail to account for the observed bias, as inaccuracies are not randomly distributed but systematically skewed. Psychological explanations, particularly *optimism bias*, highlight how individuals and organizations tend to overestimate benefits and underestimate risks due to overconfidence and a focus on the unique aspects of their project (the "inside view"). Political-economic explanations, termed *strategic misrepresentation*, attribute inaccuracy to deliberate deception, where forecasters and managers inflate benefits and downplay costs to secure funding or approval. The authors argue that these two explanations are complementary: optimism bias dominates in low-pressure environments, while strategic misrepresentation thrives under high political or organizational pressure.

The article then introduces *reference class forecasting* as a solution, drawing on the work of Daniel Kahneman and Amos Tversky, whose theories on decision-making under uncertainty earned Kahneman the 2002 Nobel Prize in Economics. Reference class forecasting counters the "planning fallacy"—the tendency to ignore historical precedents—by adopting an "outside view." Instead of focusing on the specifics of a single project, this method bases forecasts on the actual outcomes of a *reference class* of comparable past projects. The process involves three steps: (1) identifying a relevant reference class of similar projects, (2) establishing a probability distribution of outcomes for that class, and (3) positioning the current project within that distribution to derive a more realistic forecast. The authors illustrate this with Kahneman’s anecdote about a curriculum development project, where an inside view led to wildly optimistic estimates, while an outside view—grounded in the outcomes of similar projects—predicted a far more accurate (and pessimistic) timeline.

The article presents the first practical application of reference class forecasting in the UK’s transportation sector. In response to HM Treasury’s 2003 *Green Book* guidelines, which acknowledged systematic optimism bias in public procurement, the UK Department for Transport commissioned Flyvbjerg and Cowi to develop empirically based "optimism bias uplifts" for cost forecasts. These uplifts adjust initial budget estimates based on historical cost overruns for comparable projects. For example, road projects require a 15% uplift to achieve a 50% probability of staying within budget, while rail projects need a 40% uplift. The methodology was first applied to the Edinburgh Tram Line 2, where initial cost estimates were deemed overly optimistic; reference class forecasting revealed a far higher likely cost, prompting a more realistic budget allocation.

Finally, the article assesses the potentials and barriers to widespread adoption of reference class forecasting. In contexts dominated by optimism bias—where forecasters are genuinely trying to improve accuracy—the method is welcomed and faces few barriers. However, in environments where strategic misrepresentation prevails, such as competitive funding scenarios, forecasters may resist the method because it undermines their ability to manipulate estimates. To overcome this, the authors advocate for accountability measures, such as independent reviews, penalties for misleading forecasts, and tying funding approvals to the use of reference class forecasting. They also caution that while the method improves accuracy, it must be paired with strong cost-control mechanisms to prevent inefficiencies, such as contractors spending reserves simply because they are available. The article concludes that reference class forecasting, combined with institutional reforms, offers a promising path to more realistic and reliable project planning.
