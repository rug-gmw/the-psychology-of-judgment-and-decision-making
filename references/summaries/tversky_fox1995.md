# Weighing Risk and Uncertainty

Tversky, A., & Fox, C. R.

This article extends prospect theory to decision-making under uncertainty, introducing the principle of *bounded subadditivity*, which posits that events have greater psychological impact when they shift outcomes from impossibility to possibility or possibility to certainty than when they merely adjust likelihoods. Through experimental studies, the authors demonstrate that people exhibit this subadditivity in both risky and uncertain contexts, with stronger effects under uncertainty, and argue that this challenges classical expected utility theory.

The article begins by distinguishing between *risk*—where probabilities are known—and *uncertainty*, where they are not. Classical decision theory, such as expected utility theory, assumes that people weight outcomes by their probabilities, but empirical research (e.g., the *fourfold pattern of risk attitudes*) shows systematic deviations from this model. Prospect theory addresses these deviations by introducing a nonlinear weighting function that overweights low probabilities and underweights moderate to high probabilities, explaining phenomena like risk-seeking for low-probability gains (e.g., lottery tickets) and risk aversion for high-probability gains. However, prospect theory was originally formulated for risky prospects, where probabilities are explicitly defined. The authors extend this framework to uncertainty by proposing *bounded subadditivity*, a principle stating that the impact of an event is disproportionately large when it transforms impossibility into possibility (e.g., a near-impossible outcome becoming slightly possible) or possibility into certainty (e.g., a near-certain outcome becoming guaranteed). This principle generalizes the idea of diminishing sensitivity to probability changes, which is already evident in prospect theory’s S-shaped weighting function.

To formalize this, the authors define a *weighting function* for uncertain events, which assigns decision weights to events rather than probabilities. Unlike in risky contexts, where weights are a direct transformation of known probabilities, uncertain weights reflect subjective evaluations of likelihood. The weighting function is assumed to satisfy two key properties: *lower subadditivity* (the weight of an event is greater when added to impossibility than to another non-null event) and *upper subadditivity* (the weight of an event is greater when subtracted from certainty than from another uncertain event). Together, these properties constitute *bounded subadditivity*, which implies that people are more sensitive to changes at the extremes of the probability scale (impossibility and certainty) than in the middle. The authors provide an illustrative example involving a football game, where the impact of a tie is greater when it is the sole possible outcome than when it is one of several possibilities.

The empirical portion of the article consists of three studies testing these hypotheses. Participants—recruited for their expertise in specific domains (e.g., basketball fans, football fans, or students familiar with local weather)—made choices between uncertain prospects (e.g., betting on a sports game or a future temperature) and sure monetary amounts. By inferring certainty equivalents from these choices, the authors derived decision weights for various events and tested for subadditivity. The results consistently supported bounded subadditivity: decision weights were more subadditive under uncertainty than under risk, and this effect was robust across different domains (sports, financial markets, weather). The authors also introduced a measure of *relative sensitivity*, showing that people are less sensitive to changes in uncertainty than to changes in risk, a finding that held even when controlling for the curvature of the value function.

The discussion explores the relationship between decision weights and judged probabilities, suggesting a two-stage process where people first assess probabilities and then transform them via the weighting function. This model explains why decision weights exhibit greater subadditivity than probability judgments. The authors also address *source preference*, showing that people prefer to bet on domains where they feel competent (e.g., sports fans betting on games) rather than on chance events, challenging the notion of universal ambiguity aversion. Finally, they argue that bounded subadditivity provides a unifying framework for understanding decision-making across risk, uncertainty, and even ignorance, highlighting the psychological mechanisms—such as affective responses to hope and fear—that underlie these patterns. The article concludes by emphasizing the normative and descriptive implications of these findings, suggesting that traditional models like expected utility theory underestimate the complexity of human decision-making under uncertainty.
