# Incorporating the Irrelevant: Anchors in Judgments of Belief and Value

Chapman, G. B., & Johnson, E. J.

__This article explores the anchoring effect—a cognitive bias where irrelevant numerical information (anchors) influences judgments—reviewing its definitions, necessary conditions, psychological mechanisms, related phenomena, and real-world applications. The authors argue that anchoring is pervasive, often unconscious, and resistant to debiasing efforts, while proposing that it arises primarily from selective activation of anchor-consistent information rather than insufficient adjustment.__

The article begins by illustrating the anchoring effect through everyday examples, such as supermarket signs limiting purchases ("Limit 12 per customer") or a wheel-of-fortune spin influencing estimates of African countries in the UN. These examples underscore how seemingly irrelevant numbers can skew judgments, challenging the intuition that people remain unaffected by such cues. The authors trace the concept’s origins to Tversky and Kahneman’s (1974) seminal work on heuristics, which framed anchoring as a useful but error-prone shortcut. While anchoring can aid estimation (e.g., pricing an antique by referencing a similar item), it often leads to bias when adjustments from the anchor are insufficient or when the anchor itself is misleading.

Chapman and Johnson systematically define anchoring across three dimensions: as a *procedure* (presenting an uninformative number before a judgment), as an *outcome* (the resulting bias toward the anchor), and as a *process* (the cognitive mechanism driving the effect). They emphasize the importance of studying *uninformative* anchors to isolate the bias, as informative anchors might reflect rational weighting of relevant information rather than a true heuristic failure. The review then outlines the necessary conditions for anchoring to occur, including attention to the anchor, compatibility between the anchor and target (e.g., same scale or dimension), and the anchor’s extremity. Notably, anchoring persists even with implausible anchors (e.g., estimating Einstein’s first U.S. visit after seeing the year 1215), and it remains robust despite warnings, incentives for accuracy, or lack of awareness—suggesting it is deeply ingrained in cognitive processing.

The core of the article examines two competing explanations for anchoring’s psychological mechanisms. The traditional *anchoring-and-adjustment* model posits that people start with the anchor and adjust insufficiently due to uncertainty or cognitive effort. However, the authors critique this account, citing mixed evidence from studies on incentives, cognitive load, and process-tracing methods (e.g., no consistent link between adjustment magnitude and bias). Instead, they favor an *activation* model, where anchors prime or selectively retrieve anchor-consistent information from memory, biasing the judgment. For example, a high anchor for the height of the Brandenburg Gate makes "tall" features more accessible, while a low anchor primes "short" features. This model is supported by experiments showing that anchoring is reduced when participants are prompted to consider anchor-inconsistent information or when the primed information is irrelevant to the target judgment.

The article also situates anchoring within a broader landscape of cognitive biases, drawing parallels to confirmation bias (seeking hypothesis-consistent evidence), overconfidence (failing to consider counter-evidence), hindsight bias (outcome knowledge priming consistent information), and preference reversals (response scales acting as anchors). These connections highlight anchoring as a fundamental associative error, where irrelevant cues shape judgment by altering the accessibility of information. The authors distinguish anchoring from *reference point effects*, which involve psychophysical shifts in evaluation (e.g., framing outcomes as gains or losses), noting that anchoring typically produces assimilation (judgments pulled toward the anchor) rather than contrast.

Finally, the article explores anchoring’s real-world implications, demonstrating its influence across domains such as real estate appraisals, legal verdicts, consumer behavior, and survey responses. For instance, listing prices anchor home appraisals, plaintiffs’ damage requests anchor jury awards, and initial offers anchor negotiation outcomes. The authors conclude by likening anchoring to the Stroop effect—a pervasive, automatic bias where irrelevant information intrudes on judgment—underscoring the need to understand its mechanisms to develop effective debiasing strategies. While anchoring is difficult to eliminate, interventions like prompting counter-evidence or increasing awareness of its influence may mitigate its impact.
