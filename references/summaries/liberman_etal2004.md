# The Name of the Game: Predictive Power of Reputations versus Situational Labels in Determining Prisoner's Dilemma Game Moves

Liberman, V., Samuels, S. M., & Ross, L.

__This study demonstrates that situational labels (e.g., "Wall Street Game" vs. "Community Game") significantly influence cooperation in the Prisoner’s Dilemma, far more than reputation-based predictions by peers or trait assessments. Laypeople consistently underestimate the power of framing, instead overestimating the role of individual dispositions in shaping behavior.__

This article presents three studies exploring how situational framing—specifically, the name given to a Prisoner’s Dilemma (PD) game—shapes cooperative behavior, while challenging the predictive power of reputation-based assessments. The authors argue that people’s construal of a situation, influenced by subtle cues like labels, plays a decisive role in their decisions, often overriding personality-based expectations. The first two studies, conducted with American college students and Israeli pilots, respectively, compared the impact of labeling the game as either the "Wall Street Game" (or "Bursa Game" in Hebrew, evoking competitive norms) or the "Community Game" (or "Kommuna Game," evoking cooperative norms) against predictions made by individuals who knew the participants well. In both studies, the name of the game had a profound effect on cooperation rates: participants were far more likely to cooperate in the Community Game than in the Wall Street Game, regardless of whether they were nominated as "likely cooperators" or "likely defectors." Reputation-based predictions, including those made by dormitory resident assistants or flight instructors, failed to accurately forecast behavior, as did self-reported trait assessments. The third study further revealed that laypeople systematically underestimate the influence of situational labels, even when explicitly asked to consider their impact.

The methods employed in the studies were carefully designed to isolate the effects of framing. In Study 1, resident assistants nominated students they believed were most likely to cooperate or defect in the PD game, and these nominees then played either the Wall Street or Community Game. The results showed that the game’s label, rather than the participants’ reputations, determined their choices. Study 2 replicated this finding with Israeli pilots and their instructors, demonstrating that even in a high-stakes, elite population, situational framing outweighed trait-based predictions. The third study surveyed students’ expectations about how others—and themselves—would behave under the two game labels, confirming that people consistently underestimate the power of framing. Across all studies, participants’ expectations about their counterparts’ moves also played a mediating role: those who expected cooperation were more likely to cooperate themselves, particularly in the Community Game, while expectations of defection led to reciprocal defection.

The discussion highlights the broader implications of these findings for understanding human behavior. The authors argue that the results underscore the limitations of dispositional explanations in social psychology, emphasizing instead the power of situational construal. They also draw parallels to classic demonstration experiments in social psychology, such as Milgram’s obedience studies or Asch’s conformity experiments, which similarly revealed the surprising influence of situational factors. The article suggests that these findings have practical applications, such as in negotiation and mediation, where reframing a situation could shift perceived norms and foster cooperation. Ultimately, the research challenges both laypeople and social scientists to reconsider the weight they assign to situational versus dispositional factors in predicting behavior.
