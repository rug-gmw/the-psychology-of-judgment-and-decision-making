# Using the president's tweets to understand political diversion in the age of social media

Lewandowsky, S., Jetter, M., & Ecker, U. K. H.

__This study examines whether former U.S. President Donald Trump strategically used Twitter to divert media attention from politically damaging topics, particularly the Mueller investigation, by flooding the platform with unrelated tweets.__

The article presents a comprehensive empirical analysis of how Trump’s Twitter activity may have influenced media coverage during his first two years in office. The authors begin by contrasting traditional media-driven agenda-setting in Western democracies with authoritarian practices, such as China’s "50-cent army," which floods social media to distract from unfavorable news. While Western politicians lack direct control over media narratives, the rise of social media—especially Twitter—has given leaders like Trump unprecedented direct access to the public, potentially reshaping how political agendas are set.

The core of the study focuses on whether Trump’s tweets diverted attention from the Mueller investigation, a politically sensitive topic that posed significant risks to his presidency. The authors hypothesize that increased media coverage of the investigation would trigger Trump to tweet more about unrelated issues (e.g., China, jobs, immigration), which in turn would reduce subsequent coverage of the investigation. To test this, they analyzed daily data from two major U.S. media outlets—*The New York Times* and *ABC World News Tonight*—alongside Trump’s tweets from January 2017 to January 2019. They employed both ordinary least squares (OLS) regression and three-stage least squares (3SLS) models to assess the relationships between media coverage, Trump’s tweets, and potential suppression of harmful topics.

The results support the diversion hypothesis. When media coverage of the Mueller investigation increased, Trump’s tweets about unrelated topics—particularly those aligned with his political priorities, such as China, jobs, and immigration—also rose. This surge in diversionary tweets was followed by a measurable decline in media coverage of the Mueller investigation the next day. The pattern was robust across multiple analytical approaches and persisted even after controlling for long-term trends, weekly fluctuations, and autocorrelation in the data. Notably, the effect was absent when analyzing placebo topics like Brexit or neutral subjects (e.g., skiing, gardening), which posed no political threat to Trump, suggesting the diversion was specific to harmful coverage.

The expanded analysis, which examined all possible word pairs in Trump’s Twitter vocabulary, further confirmed the findings. Many word pairs associated with diversionary tweets were linked to a subsequent reduction in Mueller-related coverage, reinforcing the idea that Trump’s tweeting strategy may have systematically influenced media narratives. The authors acknowledge limitations, including the observational nature of the data, which precludes definitive causal claims, and the focus on only two media outlets. However, they argue that the consistency of the results across multiple analyses and the absence of similar patterns for non-threatening topics strengthen the case for a deliberate or intuitive diversion strategy.

The discussion situates these findings within broader debates about media influence, political communication, and the role of social media in shaping public discourse. The authors draw parallels to the "diversionary theory of war," where leaders use foreign conflicts to distract from domestic issues, and highlight how Trump’s use of Twitter may represent a modern, digital adaptation of this tactic. They also note that while the suppression effect was less statistically robust than the diversion effect, the overall pattern suggests a dynamic interplay between Trump’s tweets and media coverage that warrants further investigation.

Ultimately, the study underscores the transformative impact of social media on political communication, raising critical questions about how journalists and media organizations can adapt to avoid being manipulated by diversionary tactics. The authors conclude that Trump’s use of Twitter may have set a precedent for future leaders, making it essential to understand and counter such strategies to preserve the integrity of public discourse.
