# Prisoners and their dilemma

Khadjavi, M., Lange, A.

__This study compares cooperation rates in simultaneous and sequential Prisoner's Dilemma games between female inmates and university students, revealing that inmates cooperate more in simultaneous games while students exhibit higher cooperation as first movers in sequential games.__

The article addresses a notable gap in the literature by examining how actual prisoners behave in the Prisoner's Dilemma, a game famously named after their predicament but never tested with them. The authors, Menusch Khadjavi and Andreas Lange, conducted experiments with female inmates from a German prison and compared their behavior to that of female university students in identical game settings. The study employed both simultaneous and sequential versions of the Prisoner's Dilemma to explore unconditional and conditional cooperation, respectively.

The introduction highlights the historical significance of the Prisoner's Dilemma, tracing its origins to experiments by Flood and Dresher in 1950 and its popularization by Tucker. Despite its prominence in game theory, no prior research had tested the game with actual prisoners. The authors frame their study as both a methodological contribution—extending lab experiments to a unique subject pool—and a substantive inquiry into the social preferences of inmates, which has implications for behavioral law and economics. Previous studies, such as those by Chmura et al. (2010) and Birkeland et al. (2011), found no significant differences in altruism between inmates and other groups in dictator games, but this study delves deeper into conditional cooperation.

The experimental design involved a 2x2 framework, comparing inmates and students in simultaneous and sequential Prisoner's Dilemma games. In the simultaneous version, players chose to cooperate or defect without knowing the other's choice, while in the sequential version, the second mover responded to the first mover's decision. The payoff structure was standard, with mutual cooperation yielding higher collective payoffs than mutual defection, but defection being the dominant strategy for self-interested players. The authors derived predictions based on theories of social preferences, such as Fehr and Schmidt's (1999) model of inequality aversion, which suggest that cooperation may arise if players believe others will cooperate or if they hold pro-social preferences.

The experiments were conducted in two settings: a prison in Vechta, Germany, with female inmates, and a university lab in Hamburg with female students. Inmates were paid in coffee, tobacco, or phone credits, while students received cash. The results revealed striking differences between the two groups. In the simultaneous Prisoner's Dilemma, 56% of inmates cooperated, compared to only 37% of students, a statistically significant difference. This led to higher rates of mutual cooperation among inmates (30%) than students (13%). However, in the sequential Prisoner's Dilemma, students were more likely to cooperate as first movers (63%) than inmates (46%), though second-mover cooperation rates were nearly identical between the groups (62% for students and 60% for inmates). This suggests that inmates and students exhibit similar levels of conditional cooperation but differ in their strategic anticipation of others' behavior.

The discussion explores potential explanations for these findings. The authors suggest that inmates may be more cooperative in simultaneous games due to social norms within prison or a greater willingness to take risks in cooperative strategies. Conversely, students may be more strategic in sequential games, anticipating conditional cooperation from second movers. The study also controls for demographic differences, such as age, education, and marital status, but finds that these factors do not fully explain the behavioral disparities. The authors conclude that while inmates and students share similar social preferences, their decision-making processes differ in ways that reflect their distinct environments and experiences.

Overall, the article provides a nuanced picture of cooperation among prisoners and students, challenging assumptions about the selfishness of inmates and highlighting the importance of context in experimental game theory. The findings have implications for behavioral law and economics, particularly in designing policies for crime prevention and prisoner reintegration.
