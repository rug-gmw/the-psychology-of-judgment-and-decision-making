# Misinformation and Its Correction: Continued Influence and Successful Debiasing

Lewandowsky, S., Ecker, U. K. H., Seifert, C. M., Schwarz, N., & Cook, J.

__This article examines how misinformation spreads and persists in society, exploring cognitive mechanisms that make misinformation resistant to correction and proposing strategies to effectively debias false beliefs.__

The article investigates the widespread prevalence and persistence of misinformation, such as the debunked link between childhood vaccinations and autism, which has led to increased vaccine-preventable diseases and unnecessary public expenditure. The authors first analyze societal mechanisms that facilitate the dissemination of misinformation, including rumors, fiction, governments, politicians, vested interests, and the media, particularly the internet. They highlight how emotional content and perceived relevance drive the spread of misinformation, often irrespective of its truth value.

At the individual level, the article reviews cognitive factors that render misinformation resistant to correction. People assess the truth of statements based on compatibility with existing beliefs, internal coherence, source credibility, and social consensus. Misinformation often persists because retractions fail to update memory effectively, leading to the "continued influence effect," where retracted information continues to influence reasoning. This effect is attributed to mental models, retrieval failures, fluency and familiarity, and reactance. The authors discuss how corrections can sometimes backfire, reinforcing misinformation by increasing its familiarity or triggering psychological reactance.

To mitigate the impact of misinformation, the article suggests several strategies. Pre-exposure warnings can reduce misinformation effects by priming individuals to critically assess incoming information. Repeated retractions can also help, though they risk reinforcing misinformation if not carefully managed. Providing an alternative narrative that fills the coherence gap left by the retraction is particularly effective. However, these strategies can be complicated by individuals' worldviews, which may lead to resistance or backfire effects when corrections challenge deeply held beliefs.

The article also explores how skepticism and distrust can enhance the detection of misinformation, while worldview-affirming approaches and self-affirmation techniques can make corrections more palatable. It concludes with practical recommendations for designing effective corrections, emphasizing the importance of simplicity, clarity, and worldview alignment. The authors also discuss the ethical implications of debiasing and suggest behavioral interventions, such as "nudging," as alternative approaches to counteract misinformation when debiasing is ineffective or impractical. Future research directions include examining the roles of emotion, individual differences, and social networks in misinformation effects.
