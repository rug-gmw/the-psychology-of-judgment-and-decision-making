# On the Failure to Eliminate Hypotheses in a Conceptual Task

Wason, P. C.

__This study investigates how intelligent young adults test hypotheses in a simple conceptual task, revealing a strong tendency to seek confirming evidence rather than disconfirming evidence, often leading to incorrect conclusions.__

Peter C. Wason’s 1960 experiment explores the cognitive strategies individuals employ when attempting to discover an abstract rule governing a sequence of numbers. The study centers on a task where participants are given the triplet "2, 4, 6" and told it conforms to a hidden relational rule. Their goal is to deduce this rule by generating their own triplets and receiving feedback on whether each conforms to the rule. The correct rule—"three numbers in increasing order of magnitude"—is deceptively simple, yet many participants struggle to identify it because they fixate on more specific, incorrect hypotheses that also fit the initial example, such as "numbers increasing by intervals of two" or "consecutive even numbers."

Wason designed the experiment to highlight the pitfalls of *enumerative induction*—the tendency to seek only confirming evidence—by ensuring that the correct rule was entailed by many plausible but narrower alternatives. Unlike prior concept-attainment studies, which used finite sets of pre-defined instances, this task allowed participants to generate an infinite number of triplets, freeing them from memory constraints and enabling them to test hypotheses without external prompts. The procedure involved 29 psychology undergraduates, who recorded their triplets, the reasoning behind each choice, and the experimenter’s feedback. Participants were instructed to announce the rule only when highly confident, and the experiment continued until they either arrived at the correct rule, gave up, or exceeded 45 minutes.

The results revealed striking patterns in hypothesis-testing behavior. Only six participants correctly identified the rule on their first attempt, while 13 announced one incorrect rule before eventually arriving at the correct one, nine announced two or more incorrect rules, and one failed to reach any conclusion. Quantitative analysis demonstrated that those who immediately arrived at the correct rule generated significantly more *incompatible* instances (triplets that contradicted their hypotheses) and *negative* instances (triplets that did not conform to the correct rule) than those who announced incorrect rules first. This suggested a reliance on *eliminative induction*—actively seeking disconfirming evidence to refine or reject hypotheses. In contrast, participants who announced incorrect rules tended to rely on confirming evidence, often persisting with their initial hypotheses even after being told they were wrong. For example, some reformulated their incorrect rules in slightly different terms rather than abandoning them, a behavior Wason likened to "magical thinking," where verbal adjustments substitute for genuine conceptual change.

Qualitative analysis of participant protocols further illuminated these tendencies. Some individuals repeated or rephrased their incorrect rules after receiving negative feedback, as if the act of restating them might somehow validate their hypotheses. Others generated instances that tested only one aspect of their rule while ignoring broader possibilities. For instance, a participant might test whether the rule required even numbers but fail to consider whether the sequence needed to be strictly increasing. The most common incorrect rules—such as "increasing by intervals of two" or "arithmetic progression"—were all sufficient conditions for the correct rule but not necessary-and-sufficient ones, meaning they could not account for all possible valid triplets.

Wason’s discussion frames these findings within the broader context of scientific reasoning. He argues that the experiment simulates the challenges of hypothesis testing in science, where uncontrolled data and confirmation bias can lead to erroneous conclusions. The reluctance or inability to seek disconfirming evidence, he suggests, reflects a deeper cognitive preference for certainty and simplicity, even at the cost of accuracy. While some participants eventually adopted eliminative strategies after their initial hypotheses were disproven, others persisted in enumerative thinking, either due to a lack of understanding of how to falsify hypotheses or a preference for direct, confirmatory tests. Wason concludes by emphasizing the importance of cultivating an attitude of skepticism and falsification in both scientific and everyday reasoning, as the readiness to challenge one’s own beliefs is as critical as the capacity for logical deduction.
