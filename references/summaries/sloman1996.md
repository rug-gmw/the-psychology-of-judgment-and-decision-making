# The Empirical Case for Two Systems of Reasoning

Sloman, S. A.

__This article argues for a dual-process theory of human reasoning, distinguishing between an associative system that relies on similarity and temporal contiguity, and a rule-based system that operates on symbolic, logical structures. Sloman reviews empirical evidence demonstrating that these systems often generate conflicting responses to the same problem, with the rule-based system capable of suppressing—but not entirely inhibiting—the associative system.__

The article begins by tracing the historical debate between associative and rule-based models of cognition, noting that while both perspectives have deep philosophical roots, neither alone fully captures human reasoning. Sloman proposes that the mind operates through two distinct computational systems: an associative system that encodes statistical regularities and processes information based on similarity and temporal structure, and a rule-based system that manipulates symbolic representations with logical content and variables. The associative system is described as fast, automatic, and capable of handling novel stimuli through similarity-based generalization, while the rule-based system is deliberate, strategic, and productive, enabling abstract reasoning and systematic inference.

Sloman characterizes the associative system as operating on principles of similarity and contiguity, drawing inferences from statistical patterns in the environment. This system is likened to a sophisticated statistician, making predictions based on observed correlations rather than causal models. In contrast, the rule-based system is defined by its adherence to hard constraints, logical structure, and variables, allowing for productivity (generating an unbounded number of propositions) and systematicity (the ability to encode related facts). The article emphasizes that these systems are not mutually exclusive but often interact, with the rule-based system capable of overriding associative responses when necessary.

Empirical evidence for this distinction is drawn from multiple domains, including categorization, judgment, argument strength, syllogistic reasoning, and conditional reasoning tasks. For example, in categorization, people often rely on similarity (e.g., judging a 3-inch circular object as more similar to a quarter than a pizza) but can also apply rule-based logic (e.g., recognizing that pizzas vary in size, making the object more likely to be a pizza). Similarly, in judgment tasks like the conjunction fallacy (e.g., the Linda problem), people simultaneously hold contradictory beliefs—intuitively favoring a less probable conjunction (e.g., "bank teller and feminist") over a more probable single event (e.g., "bank teller") due to representativeness, while also acknowledging the logical error when prompted.

The article also explores the interaction between the two systems, noting that associative responses are often automatic and persistent, even when overridden by rule-based reasoning. This dynamic is illustrated through phenomena like the inclusion fallacy, where people judge arguments based on similarity (e.g., inferring properties from robins to birds more readily than from robins to ostriches) despite recognizing the logical flaw in such reasoning. Sloman argues that these conflicts demonstrate the functional independence of the two systems, with the rule-based system acting as a "suppressor" of associative responses rather than eliminating them entirely.

The discussion extends to the developmental and functional implications of the dual-system framework. Sloman suggests that rule-based reasoning may develop ontogenetically before associative reasoning, as children internalize cultural and linguistic rules, but that associative processes remain critical for efficient, flexible reasoning in novel or complex situations. The article concludes by highlighting the broader implications of this distinction for education, everyday decision-making, and cognitive science, emphasizing that both systems are essential for adaptive reasoning, with their interplay shaping human thought and behavior.
