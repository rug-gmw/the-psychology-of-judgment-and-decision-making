# On the Origin of Personal Causal Theories

Young, M. E.

__This article explores the parallels between human causal attribution and animal learning, arguing that personal causal theories emerge from basic learning principles rooted in neo-Humean cues such as temporal priority, contiguity, covariation, and prior experience. Young integrates empirical findings from both domains to propose that associative models, particularly those inspired by conditioning paradigms, can explain the development and influence of personal causal theories.__

The article begins by highlighting the centrality of causality in human cognition, enabling prediction and control in social, scientific, and everyday contexts. Young posits that personal causal theories—learned through direct observation—are shaped by a dynamic interplay between data-driven processes (bottom-up) and theory-driven influences (top-down). In contrast, public causal theories are acquired through cultural transmission or analytical reasoning. The core of the argument rests on a neo-Humean framework, which treats causality as a psychological construct inferred from environmental cues rather than directly observed. These cues, including temporal and spatial contiguity, temporal priority, covariation, and prior experience, guide causal judgments and form the basis of personal theories that later influence future attributions.

Young systematically examines each neo-Humean cue, drawing parallels between human causal attribution and animal learning research. Temporal contiguity, for instance, is critical in both domains; delays between cause and effect weaken causal judgments in humans and reduce conditioned responses in animals. However, temporal gaps can be bridged by intervening events, a phenomenon observed in both serial conditioning in animals and causal chain reasoning in humans. Spatial contiguity also plays a facilitatory role, though its importance varies with task specificity and prior experience. For example, humans rely on spatial proximity in collision tasks, while animals show similar dependencies in conditioning experiments where spatial separation between stimuli impairs learning.

Temporal priority—the requirement that causes precede effects—is another key cue. While young children initially struggle with this concept, older children and adults consistently prioritize temporally prior events in causal judgments. Animal studies similarly demonstrate the necessity of forward conditioning (where the conditioned stimulus precedes the unconditioned stimulus) for effective learning, though backward conditioning (where the effect precedes the cause) can occur under specific conditions, particularly with aversive stimuli. This suggests that temporal priority is more a performance variable than a strict learning constraint.

Covariation and contingency, often conflated in the literature, are dissected to reveal their distinct roles. Covariation refers to the bidirectional association between events, while contingency implies a unidirectional, predictive relationship. Young argues that humans and animals alike rely on contingency rather than mere covariation when making causal inferences. However, the mode of information presentation—such as summary tables versus trial-by-trial exposure—can bias judgments toward covariation. Animal learning studies, particularly those involving the Rescorla-Wagner model, demonstrate that contiguity-based mechanisms can account for contingency-like effects, challenging the necessity of explicit contingency calculations. The article also addresses critiques of covariation, such as the ability to make causal judgments after a single observation, by emphasizing the role of prior experience and generalization in such cases.

The interaction of multiple cues is another focal point. Blocking effects, where prior learning about one cue inhibits learning about another, are observed in both human causal judgments and animal conditioning. Forward blocking (where pretraining of one cue impairs learning of a second) is robust in both domains, while backward blocking (where later training of one cue retroactively weakens another) is less consistent. Young suggests that these interactions reflect competitive learning processes, where cues vie for associative strength. Other paradigms, such as occasion setting and serial conditioning, further illustrate how intervening events or contextual cues modulate causal attributions, akin to how conditions (e.g., oxygen for fire) set the stage for causal mechanisms.

Prior experience and knowledge emerge as overarching influences on causal attribution. Latent inhibition (where preexposure to a stimulus alone impairs later learning) and learned irrelevance (where prior noncontingent exposure weakens subsequent associations) demonstrate how past experiences shape current judgments. These phenomena underscore the adaptive nature of learning, where irrelevant or nonpredictive cues are deprioritized. Young reconciles generative theories of causality, which emphasize knowledge of causal mechanisms, with neo-Humean approaches by proposing that mechanisms themselves arise from prior experience distilled into personal theories. This integration is further supported by connectionist models, which can encode experiential traces without relying on explicit symbolic rules.

The article concludes by advocating for expanded research into the conditioning-causal attribution analogy. Young recommends studying real-time causal attributions, leveraging methodologies from animal learning (e.g., occasion setting, serial conditioning), and developing richer learning models that incorporate temporal dynamics. While acknowledging the limitations of current associative theories, he suggests that connectionist frameworks offer promising avenues for capturing the complexity of causal learning. Ultimately, the article presents a compelling case for the shared mechanisms underlying human and animal causal reasoning, rooted in fundamental learning principles.
