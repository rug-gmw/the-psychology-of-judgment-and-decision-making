# Pooling of Unshared Information in Group Decision Making: Biased Information Sampling During Discussion

Garold Stasser, William Titus

__This study demonstrates that group discussions often fail to effectively pool unshared information, instead reinforcing initial biases by focusing on shared information and preference-consistent arguments. In a political caucus simulation, groups frequently chose suboptimal candidates despite collectively possessing enough information to make better decisions. The findings highlight how discussion dynamics can perpetuate rather than correct individual biases.__

The article introduces a *biased sampling model* of group discussion, which posits that group decision-making processes are inherently flawed when members hold partial or biased information. The authors argue that while groups *could* theoretically combine unshared information to form a more accurate collective judgment, real-world discussions tend to be dominated by (1) information already known to all members (*shared information*) and (2) arguments that align with the group’s preexisting preferences. This bias undermines the potential advantages of group decision-making, particularly in judgmental tasks where no objectively correct answer exists.

To test their model, Stasser and Titus conducted an experiment in which university students evaluated three hypothetical candidates for student body president. The candidates’ profiles were constructed with varying numbers of positive, neutral, and negative attributes, with Candidate A objectively the strongest choice based on the total available information. Participants were assigned to one of three conditions: a *shared* condition (all members received identical, complete information about the candidates) or one of two *unshared* conditions, where individuals received partial, biased information. In the *unshared/consensus* condition, the partial information was designed to create a pre-discussion preference for Candidate B, while in the *unshared/conflict* condition, the bias was split between Candidates B and C to reduce initial consensus.

The results confirmed the biased sampling model’s predictions. In the shared condition, groups overwhelmingly chose Candidate A, as expected. However, in the unshared conditions—where groups *could* have reconstructed the full profiles through discussion—most groups failed to do so. Instead, they favored the candidate initially preferred by a plurality of members (usually B or C), even though this choice contradicted the total available information. Post-discussion individual preferences further reinforced these biases, with members recalling more information that supported the group’s eventual choice rather than correcting their initial misconceptions. Notably, the *unshared/conflict* condition, which was intended to encourage more balanced discussion, did not significantly improve outcomes compared to the *unshared/consensus* condition.

The study also examined how initial consensus shaped group decisions. Groups tended to follow a "plurality-supported wins" rule, where the candidate with the most initial support almost always became the group’s choice. This pattern persisted even when unshared information could have shifted preferences toward the objectively stronger candidate. Recall data further illustrated the discussion’s bias: members remembered more positive information about the winning candidate after discussion, even if that information was already shared beforehand. Conversely, negative or contradictory information was less likely to be recalled post-discussion, suggesting that discussion amplified rather than challenged existing biases.

The authors frame their findings within broader theories of group decision-making, such as *persuasive arguments theory* (Burnstein & Vinokur, 1977) and *social decision schemes* (Davis, 1973). They argue that while these theories emphasize the role of information exchange in shaping group outcomes, they often overlook the structural biases that prevent unshared information from being effectively pooled. The study’s implications extend to real-world settings, such as juries, hiring committees, or expert panels, where diverse perspectives and unshared expertise are critical. The results suggest that unstructured group discussions may systematically undervalue unique information, particularly when it contradicts the group’s initial leanings.

In conclusion, Stasser and Titus demonstrate that group decision-making is prone to *process loss*—a failure to fully utilize the collective knowledge of its members. Their biased sampling model highlights how shared information and preference-consistent arguments dominate discussions, often at the expense of more accurate or nuanced judgments. The study underscores the need for structured interventions, such as devil’s advocacy or systematic information-sharing protocols, to help groups overcome these inherent biases.
