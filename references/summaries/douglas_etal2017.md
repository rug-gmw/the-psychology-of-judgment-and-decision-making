# The Psychology of Conspiracy Theories

Douglas, K. M., Sutton, R. M., & Cichocka, A.

__This article reviews the psychological motivations behind belief in conspiracy theories and their consequences. It argues that while conspiracy theories appeal to epistemic, existential, and social motives, they often fail to fulfill these needs and may even exacerbate the very insecurities they aim to address.__

The article synthesizes research on why people endorse conspiracy theories—explanations of significant events as secret plots by powerful, malevolent groups—and explores their psychological effects. The authors organize their review around three core motives: epistemic (the desire for understanding and certainty), existential (the need for control and security), and social (the drive to maintain a positive self or group image). Conspiracy theories, they argue, are uniquely appealing because they offer broad, internally consistent explanations that resist falsification, providing a sense of coherence in uncertain or contradictory situations. For instance, they allow individuals to dismiss disconfirming evidence (e.g., scientific consensus) as part of the conspiracy itself, thereby protecting cherished beliefs. Research supports this, showing that conspiracy belief is stronger among those who seek patterns in randomness, experience uncertainty, or lack cognitive closure. However, these theories often come at a cost: they are typically speculative, complex, and resistant to evidence, making them less likely to be accurate. People with lower analytic thinking skills or education are more prone to endorsing them, and such beliefs correlate with tendencies to overestimate coincidences or perceive intentionality where none exists.

Despite their appeal, the article highlights a paradox: conspiracy theories may not actually satisfy the motives that draw people to them. While they can temporarily shield beliefs from disconfirmation, experiments show they also increase uncertainty and undermine trust in institutions. Existentially, conspiracy theories may initially promise a sense of control, especially for those feeling powerless or anxious, but research suggests they ultimately erode autonomy. For example, exposure to conspiracy theories reduces people’s willingness to engage in political processes or organizational commitments, leaving them feeling more disempowered. Socially, conspiracy theories can bolster self-esteem by scapegoating others, but they also foster distrust and alienation. Studies show that exposure to such theories decreases trust in government, scientists, and even unrelated institutions, potentially worsening social fragmentation.

The authors acknowledge that most research has focused on relatively privileged populations (e.g., students), where conspiracy theories tend to have destabilizing effects. However, they suggest that for marginalized or threatened groups, these theories might serve adaptive functions, such as fostering solidarity or challenging oppressive systems. For instance, conspiracy theories could help disempowered groups reclaim agency by constructing alternative narratives. The article calls for more research on these populations to determine whether conspiracy beliefs genuinely fulfill psychological needs or remain self-defeating. In conclusion, while conspiracy theories are a compelling form of motivated social cognition, their long-term consequences often contradict the very motives they aim to address, raising questions about their psychological utility.
