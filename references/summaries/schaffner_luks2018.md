# Misinformation or Expressive Responding? What an Inauguration Crowd Can Tell Us About the Source of Political Misinformation in Surveys

Schaffner, B. F., Luks, S.

__This study examines whether political misinformation in surveys stems from genuine belief or intentional "expressive responding" to signal partisan loyalty. Using a clear-cut case—comparing crowd sizes at Trump’s and Obama’s inaugurations—the authors find strong evidence that politically engaged Trump supporters often provided incorrect answers to support their side, suggesting misinformation may sometimes reflect partisan cheerleading rather than true misperceptions.__

The article addresses a longstanding debate in political science: whether survey respondents who provide factually incorrect answers about politics do so because they genuinely hold misperceptions or because they intentionally misreport to express partisan allegiance. Previous research has largely attributed misinformation to motivated reasoning, where individuals process information in a way that aligns with their political identity. However, an alternative explanation—expressive responding—posits that some respondents knowingly provide false answers to signal support for their party, even when they recognize the truth. Distinguishing between these explanations has proven difficult, as most survey questions leave room for ambiguity or genuine uncertainty.

To resolve this, Schaffner and Luks designed a novel experiment leveraging a high-profile controversy: the dispute over whether Donald Trump’s 2017 inauguration drew a larger crowd than Barack Obama’s 2009 inauguration. The authors presented a nationally representative sample of U.S. adults with side-by-side aerial photographs of the two events, where the difference in crowd size was visually unambiguous. Respondents were randomly assigned to one of two conditions. In the first, they were simply asked which photo showed more people—a question with an obvious correct answer. In the second, they were asked to identify which photo corresponded to Trump’s inauguration and which to Obama’s, a question where the answer was less immediately apparent.

The results revealed striking partisan differences in responses to the first question. While only 2–3% of nonvoters and Clinton voters incorrectly identified the photo with fewer people as the larger crowd, 15% of Trump voters did so. This disparity was even more pronounced among highly educated Trump supporters, 26% of whom chose the incorrect photo, compared to just 11% of less educated Trump voters. Since the question was designed to eliminate genuine misperception or information bias as explanations, the authors interpret these findings as clear evidence of expressive responding. Politically engaged Trump supporters, who were more likely to be aware of the crowd-size controversy, appeared to intentionally provide the wrong answer to align with their partisan identity.

The second condition, where respondents matched photos to inaugurations, provided a useful contrast. Here, misidentifications were more common overall, but the pattern reversed: less educated Trump voters were twice as likely as college-educated Trump voters to incorrectly assign the larger crowd to Trump’s inauguration. This suggests that when the answer was less obvious, less engaged partisans may have guessed in a way that favored their side, while highly engaged partisans continued to engage in expressive responding at similar rates regardless of question difficulty.

The study’s implications are significant. It demonstrates that at least some political misinformation in surveys is not the result of genuine belief but rather partisan signaling, particularly among the most politically engaged individuals. This challenges the assumption that misperceptions are primarily driven by motivated reasoning and suggests that survey responses may sometimes reflect performative loyalty rather than deeply held (if incorrect) convictions. The authors acknowledge that their findings focus on Trump supporters and call for future research to test whether Democrats exhibit similar behavior. Nonetheless, the results underscore the need to reconsider how misinformation is measured and interpreted in political surveys, as well as the potential limitations of relying on self-reported factual beliefs in partisan contexts.
