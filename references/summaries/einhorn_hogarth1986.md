# Judging Probable Cause

Einhorn, H. J., & Hogarth, R. M.

__This article argues that people use systematic, probabilistic rules to judge causation in both scientific and everyday contexts, integrating philosophical, psychological, and statistical theories. It introduces a four-component framework—causal field, cues-to-causality, judgmental strategies, and discounting of alternatives—to explain how individuals assess probable cause despite the lack of a formal definition of causality.__

The article begins by highlighting the fragmented and often isolated approaches to understanding causality across disciplines such as psychology, philosophy, statistics, and law. Despite the absence of a unified definition, the authors contend that people intuitively and systematically assess causation using judgment processes that extend beyond available information, inherently involving uncertainty. This perspective bridges the gap between theories of causation and the literature on judgment under uncertainty, emphasizing the probabilistic nature of causal reasoning.

The core of the article is structured around a four-component framework for understanding how people judge probable cause. The first component, the *causal field*, refers to the contextual background against which causal relevance is determined. The causal field helps distinguish causes from mere conditions, influences the salience of alternative explanations, and shapes whether explanations are considered at a broad (molar) or detailed (molecular) level. For example, an event like a watch breaking when struck by a hammer may be attributed to the hammer in a neutral context but to a defect in the watch glass in a factory testing context. The causal field also explains why unusual or unexpected events trigger causal reasoning, as people seek explanations for deviations from the norm.

The second component, *cues-to-causality*, includes probabilistic indicators such as covariation, temporal order, contiguity in time and space, and similarity between cause and effect. Covariation, for instance, is a key cue but is often imperfect, as people infer causation even when events do not perfectly covary. The authors introduce the concept of "INUS conditions" (Insufficient but Necessary parts of an Unnecessary but Sufficient scenario) to explain how people interpret imperfect covariation. Temporal order is another critical cue, as causes must precede effects, though this can conflict with probabilistic reasoning, leading to errors like the confusion of inverse probabilities. Contiguity and similarity also play roles, with contiguity directing attention to potential causal links and similarity influencing judgments based on the congruity of cause and effect.

The third component involves *judgmental strategies* for combining the causal field with cues-to-causality. The authors propose a model where gross causal strength is determined by the interaction of these factors. For example, temporal order, the causal field, and the existence of a plausible causal chain are necessary conditions for perceiving causation, while other cues like covariation and similarity can compensate for one another. The strength of a causal chain, which links cause and effect, depends on the number and strength of its individual links, with longer chains generally resulting in weaker perceived causality. The article also discusses how conflicts between cues can lead to phenomena like spurious correlations or "causalations," where true causal relations are masked by low covariation.

The final component addresses the *discounting of causal strength* by alternative explanations. The authors frame this process as a sequential updating of beliefs, where the net strength of a causal explanation is reduced by the presence of plausible alternatives. This model aligns with anchoring-and-adjustment processes observed in other areas of judgment and decision-making. The article also explores how multiple causation and conjunction fallacies—where people judge conjunctive explanations as more probable than their individual components—can be understood within this framework. The authors argue that the judged probability of a conjunctive explanation is influenced by the trade-off between the sufficiency and necessity of the explanation, leading to a single-peaked function over the number of reasons included.

In conclusion, the article synthesizes diverse theories of causation into a cohesive framework that emphasizes the probabilistic, context-dependent, and dynamic nature of causal judgment. By integrating the causal field, cues-to-causality, judgmental strategies, and the discounting of alternatives, the authors provide a comprehensive model for understanding how people navigate the complexities of judging probable cause in both scientific and everyday contexts. The framework not only bridges gaps between disparate literatures but also highlights areas for future research, such as the role of causal chains in memory and the interplay between theory-driven and data-driven judgments.
