# Genetics and intelligence differences: five special findings

Plomin, R., Deary, I. J.

__This expert review highlights five unique genetic findings about intelligence, emphasizing its high heritability, genetic stability across the lifespan, and significant implications for cognitive neuroscience and social epidemiology.__

The article synthesizes decades of research on the genetics of intelligence, beginning with a defense of intelligence as a measurable and meaningful construct that predicts life outcomes such as education, occupation, and health. The authors first outline three "laws" of behavioral genetics that apply broadly to complex traits: (1) all traits show significant genetic influence, (2) no traits are 100% heritable, and (3) heritability is caused by many genes of small effect. These principles set the stage for the five findings specific to intelligence,The first special finding is that the heritability of intelligence increases dramatically from about 20% in infancy to as much as 80% in later adulthood, despite strong genetic stability across ages. This paradox—where the same genes influence intelligence throughout life, yet their contribution to variance grows—suggests a process of "genetic amplification," where small genetic differences are magnified through gene-environment correlations as individuals select, modify, and create environments aligned with their genetic propensities. Twin and adoption studies consistently support this trend, though newer Genome-wide Complex Trait Analysis (GCTA) methods, which estimate genetic influence using DNA from unrelated individuals, have yet to fully replicate these findings due to limited statistical power.

The second finding is that intelligence captures general genetic effects across diverse cognitive and learning abilities. While phenotypic correlations among cognitive tests average around 0.30, genetic correlations are typically 0.60 or higher, indicating that the same genes influence a wide range of abilities, from spatial reasoning to vocabulary. This "Generalist Genes Hypothesis" is supported by both twin studies and GCTA, which show high genetic correlations between intelligence and learning abilities like reading and mathematics. The authors argue that this pleiotropy—where genes affect multiple traits—challenges modular views of brain function and instead supports a network-based model of cognitive abilities.

The third finding is that assortative mating (the tendency for individuals to pair with partners of similar traits) is greater for intelligence (~0.40) than for other behavioral or physical traits. This high level of assortative mating increases additive genetic variance in the population, as offspring inherit a more extreme combination of their parents' genes. The implications are significant: it suggests that genetic predictions from parents to offspring may be stronger for intelligence, and that genome-wide association studies (GWAS) and GCTA, which focus on additive genetic effects, may be more successful in identifying genes for intelligence than for traits with lower assortative mating.

The fourth finding shifts focus to the "positive genetics" of high intelligence. Unlike psychiatric disorders, which are often conceptualized as deviations from a norm, intelligence is normally distributed, with a positive tail of exceptional performance. Genetic research shows that high intelligence is not qualitatively different from the rest of the distribution; it is just as heritable and influenced by the same genes. This challenges the "Genetic Discontinuity Hypothesis," which posits that extreme intelligence arises from unique, non-additive genetic interactions. Instead, the authors propose that high intelligence requires the accumulation of many positive alleles and few negative ones, making it a model for studying the genetic basis of exceptional performance.

The fifth finding explores the role of intelligence in "social epidemiology," the study of how social factors like education and social class influence health and mortality. Intelligence, education, and social class are highly correlated, but the causes of these associations are disputed. Twin and GCTA studies reveal that these variables share genetic causes, with genetic correlations as high as 0.95 between intelligence and education. This suggests that some of the variance in traditionally "environmental" variables like education and social class is actually influenced by DNA, complicating causal interpretations in social mobility and health inequalities research.

The article concludes by discussing the future of genetic research on intelligence, particularly the potential of polygenic scores—aggregated measures of genetic risk or propensity derived from GWAS. While current polygenic scores explain only a small fraction of the variance in intelligence, the authors predict that larger samples and whole-genome sequencing will improve their predictive power. They propose several hypotheses for testing with polygenic scores, such as whether a score derived from one cognitive ability can predict others, or whether assortative mating for intelligence is mediated genetically.

Throughout, the authors emphasize the importance of integrating genetic findings into broader scientific and societal discussions, from cognitive neuroscience to public health. They argue that intelligence, as one of the most heritable and stable behavioral traits, offers unique insights into the genetic architecture of complex traits and the interplay between genes and environments.
