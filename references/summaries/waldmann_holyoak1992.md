# Predictive and Diagnostic Learning Within Causal Models: Asymmetries in Cue Competition

Waldmann, M. R., & Holyoak, K. J.

__This article challenges the reduction of higher-order learning (e.g., causal induction) to associative learning by demonstrating that predictive and diagnostic reasoning are not symmetrical. Through three experiments, the authors show that cue competition occurs among causes but not effects, supporting a causal-model theory over associative frameworks.__

The article critiques the prevailing view that higher-order cognitive processes like causal induction and categorization can be explained by lower-order associative learning mechanisms, such as those observed in classical conditioning. The authors argue that associative models, which emphasize cue competition (e.g., blocking effects), fail to account for the directional and structural nuances of human causal reasoning. To test this, Waldmann and Holyoak conducted three experiments comparing predictive learning (where cues are interpreted as causes of a common effect) and diagnostic learning (where cues are interpreted as effects of a common cause). Despite identical associative structures in both tasks, the results revealed a fundamental asymmetry: cue competition was robust in predictive learning but absent in diagnostic learning, where multiple effects of a common cause did not compete.

The introduction lays the groundwork by reviewing associative learning theories, particularly the Rescorla-Wagner model, which explains blocking as a failure of redundant cues to acquire associative strength. The authors note that researchers like Gluck and Bower (1988) and Shanks and Dickinson (1987) have extended these principles to human learning, arguing that causal induction and categorization can be reduced to associative processes. However, Waldmann and Holyoak propose an alternative: a causal-model theory that posits people construct mental models with directed links from causes to effects, even when information is presented in reverse temporal order. This theory assumes that (1) causal links are inherently directional, (2) causal strength is tied to contingency, and (3) predictive and diagnostic inferences are structurally distinct.

The methods involved three experiments using a blocking paradigm, where subjects learned to predict outcomes based on cues framed as either causes (predictive condition) or effects (diagnostic condition). In Experiment 1, subjects rated the causal strength of cues in either a predictive (e.g., features causing an emotional response) or diagnostic (e.g., symptoms indicating a disease) context. The results showed blocking only in the predictive condition, where redundant causes competed, while redundant effects in the diagnostic condition did not. Experiment 2 replicated the design but asked subjects to rate the *predictiveness* of cues, revealing apparent blocking in both conditions. The authors attribute this to theory competition in diagnostic reasoning, where prior knowledge of alternative causes may have influenced ratings. Experiment 3 controlled for prior knowledge by using unfamiliar stimuli (e.g., buttons signaling an alarm), restoring the asymmetry: blocking occurred only in the predictive condition.

The discussion interprets these findings as strong evidence for causal-model theory. The authors argue that associative models cannot explain the asymmetry because they treat cues and outcomes symmetrically, ignoring the semantic distinction between causes and effects. In contrast, causal-model theory predicts that causes compete for explanatory power, while effects collaborate to provide evidence for a common cause. The article also explores implications for categorization, suggesting that cue competition or facilitation depends on whether features are interpreted as causes or effects of a category. Finally, the authors speculate about the role of expertise in diagnostic reasoning and question whether even classical conditioning might involve higher-order causal inference.

Overall, the article makes a compelling case that human learning is guided by abstract causal models, not just associative mechanisms, and that predictive and diagnostic reasoning are fundamentally distinct processes.
