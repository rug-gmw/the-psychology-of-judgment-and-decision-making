# Dead and Alive: Beliefs in Contradictory Conspiracy Theories

Wood, M. J., Douglas, K. M., & Sutton, R. M.

__This article explores how individuals who believe in conspiracy theories often endorse contradictory explanations for the same event, driven by a broader distrust of authority rather than logical consistency among the theories themselves.__

The study investigates the phenomenon of monological belief systems in conspiracy theories, where individuals simultaneously endorse mutually incompatible explanations for events like the deaths of Princess Diana and Osama bin Laden. The authors argue that conspiracy theories form a self-sustaining worldview, where belief in one theory predicts belief in others, even when they contradict each other. In the introduction, they highlight that while some conspiracy theories may logically support one another, others provide directly opposing explanations for the same event, raising questions about how believers reconcile these contradictions.

Two studies were conducted to test this hypothesis. In Study 1, participants who believed Princess Diana faked her own death were also more likely to believe she was murdered, despite these theories being mutually exclusive. The results showed significant positive correlations between contradictory theories, challenging the assumption that belief in one theory would negate belief in another. Study 2 replicated these findings with the death of Osama bin Laden, where participants who believed he was already dead when U.S. forces raided his compound were also more likely to believe he is still alive. The authors used hierarchical regression models to demonstrate that this positive correlation was driven by a shared belief in a cover-up by authorities, rather than direct support between the theories themselves.

The discussion emphasizes that the coherence of conspiracy belief systems stems from higher-order beliefs—such as the idea that authorities engage in systematic deception—rather than logical consistency among individual theories. The authors draw parallels to Adorno et al.’s (1950) work on prejudice, where contradictory stereotypes were endorsed because they aligned with a broader negative worldview. They suggest that conspiracism functions similarly, as an ideological system where any theory opposing the official narrative gains credibility, regardless of internal contradictions. The article also explores the implications of this phenomenon for understanding the psychological and social dynamics of conspiracy belief, including its potential role in opinion-based groups and its modeling through connectionist frameworks like Thagard’s (1989) explanatory coherence theory. Ultimately, the research underscores that conspiracism is less about specific explanations and more about a generalized distrust of authority.
