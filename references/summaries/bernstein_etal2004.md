# We Saw It All Along: Visual Hindsight Bias in Children and Adults

Bernstein, D. M., Atance, C., Loftus, G. R., & Meltzoff, A.

__This study demonstrates that hindsight bias—a tendency to overestimate what others know after learning the outcome—emerges early in childhood and persists into adulthood, though its magnitude may decline with age. Using a visual identification task, researchers found that both children and adults overestimated peers' ability to recognize degraded images when they themselves knew the object's identity in advance.__

This study explores the developmental origins of hindsight bias by examining whether young children, like adults, exhibit this cognitive phenomenon. Traditionally, hindsight bias research has relied on verbal or introspective methods, which are unsuitable for young children. To address this limitation, the authors adapted a computer-based visual identification task originally designed for adults, making it accessible to preschoolers. Participants—ranging from 3-year-olds to adults—viewed gradually clarifying images of common objects under two conditions: a baseline condition, where they identified the object without prior knowledge, and a hindsight condition, where they knew the object's identity in advance and estimated when a naive same-age peer would recognize it.

In Experiment 1, observers identified objects degraded by either blurring or pixelation. The results revealed that all age groups exhibited hindsight bias, overestimating their peers' ability to identify objects at more degraded stages than they themselves could in the baseline condition. Notably, the magnitude of this bias declined with age, suggesting that older individuals are somewhat better at suppressing their knowledge when judging others' perspectives. Experiment 2 replicated these findings using a cropping degradation instead of pixelation and presented the same objects in both baseline and hindsight conditions to rule out potential confounds. While hindsight bias was again observed across all ages, the age-related decline in bias was less pronounced, indicating that the effect may vary depending on task specifics.

The study's findings highlight that hindsight bias is a robust and early-developing cognitive phenomenon, present even in preschoolers. The authors discuss the implications of these results for theories of cognitive development, particularly theory of mind, which involves understanding others' perspectives. Unlike theory-of-mind tasks, which show marked improvement around age 4, hindsight bias remains relatively stable across early childhood, suggesting distinct developmental trajectories for these related abilities. The research also has practical implications, such as in eyewitness testimony, where hindsight bias could lead individuals to overestimate the clarity of past visual experiences. By bridging developmental psychology and cognitive science, this study provides a novel approach to studying perception and cognition across the lifespan.
