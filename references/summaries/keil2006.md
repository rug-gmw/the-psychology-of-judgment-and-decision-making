# Explanation and Understanding

Keil, F. C.

__This article explores the nature of explanations, their types, developmental trajectories, and cognitive limitations, arguing that humans rely on incomplete yet functional explanatory frameworks that are socially and culturally shaped.__

Frank Keil’s review examines how humans construct, evaluate, and utilize explanations across domains and developmental stages. Explanations, he argues, are not merely deductive or law-based but often implicit, mechanistic, and socially transactional, serving purposes beyond prediction—such as diagnosis, justification, and aesthetic appreciation. Keil distinguishes between different kinds of explanations, including those based on causal patterns (e.g., common-cause, common-effect, or homeostatic explanations), explanatory stances (mechanical, design, or intentional), and domain-specific frameworks (e.g., biology, physics, or psychology). These distinctions highlight how explanations vary in structure and function depending on the context, yet all share a reliance on causal reasoning as a central organizing principle.

The article delves into the cognitive processes underlying explanation, noting that people often operate with skeletal understandings of causal structures rather than exhaustive knowledge. This incompleteness is compounded by the "illusion of explanatory depth" (IOED), where individuals overestimate their grasp of how things work—particularly for complex systems like zippers or helicopters—until forced to articulate details. Despite these gaps, people compensate by outsourcing knowledge to experts, leveraging fragmentary understandings to identify reliable sources of information. Keil emphasizes that explanations are not isolated cognitive acts but are deeply embedded in social and cultural contexts, with developmental research showing that children as young as preschoolers differentiate between domains (e.g., biological vs. psychological explanations) and refine their explanatory skills over time.

Culturally, while explanatory styles may vary in prominence (e.g., collectivist vs. individualist frameworks), no culture entirely lacks certain explanatory schemes. Instead, differences lie in default hierarchies and the mechanistic details of shared core schemas. Keil concludes that explanations are fundamental to human cognition, enabling us to navigate a complex world despite inherent limitations in our understanding. The review underscores the interplay between individual cognition, social interaction, and cultural frameworks in shaping how we seek and provide explanations.
