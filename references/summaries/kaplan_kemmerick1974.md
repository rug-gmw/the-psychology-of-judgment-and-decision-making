# Juror Judgment as Information Integration: Combining Evidential and Nonevidential Information

Kaplan, M. F., & Kemmerick, G. D.

__This study demonstrates that juror judgments of guilt and punishment follow an information integration model, where both legally relevant evidence and irrelevant defendant characteristics are combined additively. The findings suggest that jurors weigh and average evidential and nonevidential information, with defendant traits biasing judgments regardless of legal instructions.__

The article explores how jurors integrate different types of information—evidential (directly related to the crime) and nonevidential (such as defendant character traits)—into judgments of guilt and punishment. Drawing from information integration theory, the authors propose that juror decision-making mirrors the processes observed in impression formation tasks, where individuals combine multiple pieces of information into a unitary judgment. According to this framework, each piece of information has both a *scale value* (its position on the judgment dimension, e.g., guiltiness) and a *weight* (its relative importance in the final judgment), which are then averaged to produce an overall evaluation. The study tests whether this model applies to juror judgments by systematically varying the incriminating value of evidence (high or low) and defendant characterizations (positive, neutral, negative, or none) in simulated traffic felony cases.

The experiment involved 96 participants from an introductory psychology class, who acted as simulated jurors. Each participant evaluated eight case summaries, which varied in evidential incrimination and defendant traits. The cases were constructed to ensure plausibility and were pretested to confirm that high-incrimination versions elicited significantly higher guilt and punishment ratings than low-incrimination versions. Defendant traits were selected from established likability norms and carefully screened to avoid direct relevance to the crime. Participants rated guilt on a 0–20 scale and punishment on a 1–7 scale. To isolate the effects of evidence and character traits, the study employed a factorial design, with instructions about the utility of character traits (emphasizing their relevance, questioning their usefulness, or providing no guidance) as an additional variable.

Results supported the information integration model. Guilt and punishment ratings increased with the incriminating value of the evidence and became more severe for defendants with negative traits. Critically, the effects of evidence and defendant traits combined *additively*—that is, the impact of character traits on judgments was consistent regardless of whether the evidence was highly incriminating or exonerating. This was visually and statistically confirmed by the parallelism of the data in graphs of guilt and punishment ratings, indicating no interaction between evidence and traits. Neutral traits appeared to reduce guilt ratings compared to cases with no character information, suggesting that even neutral information may subtly bias jurors toward leniency. However, instructions about the utility of character traits had little effect on judgments, implying that jurors may hold strong preconceptions about the relevance of personal characteristics that are resistant to external guidance.

The discussion highlights the broader implications of these findings. By applying information integration theory to juror decision-making, the study provides a unifying framework for understanding how jurors process diverse types of information, including legally irrelevant factors like defendant traits. The results align with prior research showing that defendant characteristics influence judicial outcomes, but they extend this work by demonstrating how such biases operate within a formal model of information processing. The authors note that while the model explains how information is combined, further research is needed to understand how jurors assign scale values and weights to different pieces of information. They also express concern about the ineffectiveness of instructions in mitigating the influence of irrelevant traits, suggesting that legal directives may need to focus more on the *legal relevance* of information rather than its predictive utility. Overall, the study underscores the challenges of ensuring impartiality in juror judgments, particularly when extralegal factors systematically bias outcomes.
