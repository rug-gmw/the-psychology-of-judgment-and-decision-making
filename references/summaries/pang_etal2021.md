# The effectiveness of graphic health warnings on tobacco products: a systematic review on perceived harm and quit intentions

Pang, B., Saleme, P., Seydel, T., Kim, J., Knox, K., Rundle-Thiele, S.

__This systematic review examines longitudinal studies to assess how different formats of graphic health warnings (GHWs) on tobacco products influence perceived harm and quit intentions among smokers and non-smokers. The findings highlight that GHWs covering more than 50% of the pack and featuring negatively framed messages are most effective in increasing perceived risks and quit intentions, though wear-out effects over time are evident.__

This comprehensive review synthesizes evidence from 35 longitudinal studies to evaluate the impact of various graphic health warning (GHW) formats on perceived harm and quit intentions related to tobacco use. The introduction underscores the importance of understanding cognitive reactions to GHWs, which are critical in designing effective health warnings. The World Health Organization's Framework Convention on Tobacco Control (FCTC) emphasizes the necessity of health warnings, but leaves specifics of design and format open to interpretation, leading to varied effectiveness.

The methods involved a systematic search across ten databases, adhering to PRISMA guidelines, to identify relevant longitudinal studies published between 2008 and 2019. The review included studies that reported on GHW formats and their impact on perceived risk and quit intentions. Data extraction focused on bibliographic information, study characteristics, GHW formats, and outcome evaluations. Quality assessments were conducted using the ROBINS-I tool for non-randomized studies and the RoB2 tool for randomized controlled trials.

Results revealed that GHWs covering more than 50% of the pack were most common and generally more effective. Most studies featured warnings on both the front and back of packs, with a predominant use of negatively framed messages. The review found that perceived harm and quit intentions increased with the implementation of GHWs, though wear-out effects were observed over time regardless of the format. Specifically, larger GHWs with credible sources were associated with increased perceptions of harm. However, some studies noted a decline in perceived harm and quit intentions over extended periods, indicating potential desensitization to the warnings.

The discussion highlights several key contributions: pictorial GHWs are more effective than text-only warnings, negatively framed messages are prevalent but may induce reactance, and source attribution is infrequent despite its importance for credibility. The review also calls for further research on positively framed messages and the use of objective measures beyond self-reporting to better understand the impact of GHWs.

In conclusion, GHWs are effective in increasing awareness of health risks and quit intentions, but their design and message framing need continuous evaluation to sustain their impact. The review recommends testing alternative GHW formats, such as those communicating the benefits of quitting, and emphasizes the need for ongoing research to identify wear-out points and optimize GHW efficacy.
