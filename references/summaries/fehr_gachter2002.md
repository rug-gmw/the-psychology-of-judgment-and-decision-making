# Altruistic punishment in humans

Fehr, E., Gächter, S.

__This study demonstrates that altruistic punishment—where individuals incur personal costs to penalize free-riders—is a key driver of human cooperation, even among unrelated strangers in one-time interactions. Experimental evidence shows that cooperation thrives when punishment is possible but collapses without it, with negative emotions toward defectors serving as the proximate mechanism.__

The article addresses the evolutionary puzzle of human cooperation, which frequently occurs among genetically unrelated individuals in large groups, even in the absence of repeated interactions or reputation benefits. Traditional theories—such as kin selection, direct and indirect reciprocity, and costly signaling—fail to fully explain this phenomenon. The authors propose that altruistic punishment, where individuals punish free-riders at a personal cost without material gain, may resolve this puzzle. To test this hypothesis, they conducted a controlled experiment with 240 participants in a public goods game, comparing two conditions: one where punishment was possible and another where it was not.

In the experiment, participants were anonymously grouped and given an endowment to either keep or contribute to a group project. Contributions benefited all group members, but free-riding (contributing little or nothing) was individually rational, as keeping money yielded higher personal returns. In the punishment condition, participants could assign costly punishment points to others after observing their contributions, reducing the punished individual’s earnings. The experiment was repeated over six periods with randomized group compositions to eliminate reciprocity or reputation effects.

The results revealed that altruistic punishment was widespread: 84.3% of participants punished at least once, with most punishment directed at below-average contributors by above-average contributors. The severity of punishment increased with the degree of free-riding, creating a strong incentive for cooperation. As a result, cooperation levels were significantly higher in the punishment condition, with contributions rising over time, whereas cooperation collapsed in the no-punishment condition. The introduction or removal of punishment opportunities led to immediate shifts in cooperation, suggesting that the mere threat of punishment was sufficient to sustain high contribution levels.

The authors also explored the emotional underpinnings of punishment, finding that free-riding triggered strong negative emotions like anger and annoyance, particularly when the free-rider’s contribution deviated sharply from the group norm. These emotions appeared to motivate punishment, as participants anticipated the anger of others when they themselves free-rode. The consistency between emotional responses and punishment behavior supports the idea that emotions are a proximate mechanism driving altruistic punishment.

The study concludes that altruistic punishment is a critical factor in explaining human cooperation, particularly in contexts where traditional theories fall short. Unlike kin selection or reciprocity, altruistic punishment does not rely on genetic relatedness or repeated interactions, making it uniquely suited to large, anonymous groups. The findings suggest that future research on the evolution of human cooperation should prioritize understanding the origins and mechanisms of altruistic punishment.
