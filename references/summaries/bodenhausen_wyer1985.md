# Effects of Stereotypes on Decision Making and Information-Processing Strategies

Bodenhausen, G. V., & Wyer, R. S., Jr.

__This article examines how stereotypes influence judgment and information processing in decision-making, demonstrating that stereotypes act as heuristics, leading to harsher punishments for stereotype-consistent transgressions and shaping recall of case details based on stereotype alignment.__

The study, comprising two experiments, investigates how stereotypes affect evaluations of behavioral transgressions and the recall of related information. The authors argue that stereotypes function as cognitive shortcuts, simplifying complex social judgments by providing ready-made explanations for behavior. In the first experiment, participants reviewed case files of employees who committed job-related infractions, with the transgressor’s ethnicity (American or Arab) and the nature of the offense (stereotypically American or Arab) manipulated. Results showed that stereotype-consistent transgressions were perceived as more likely to recur and were punished more severely. Additionally, participants recalled less background information when the offense aligned with the stereotype, suggesting reduced processing of other details once a stereotype-based explanation was formed.

The second experiment extended these findings by introducing a parole decision-making task, where participants evaluated prisoners with varying ethnic names (Hispanic, upper-class WASP, or nondescript) and crimes (stereotypically Hispanic or WASP). The case files included background information, decision-relevant details (e.g., prison behavior), and life circumstances that either explained or did not explain the crime. The study tested three hypotheses about stereotype use: the *heuristic* hypothesis (stereotypes override other information), the *default* hypothesis (stereotypes are used only when no other explanations exist), and the *integration* hypothesis (both stereotypic and nonstereotypic information are considered). Results strongly supported the heuristic hypothesis, as stereotypes dominated judgments regardless of alternative explanations. For example, when a stereotype was activated, participants ignored crime-relevant life circumstances that would have otherwise led to more lenient parole recommendations. Recall data further revealed that participants processed information selectively to confirm their stereotype-based impressions, recalling details more effectively when they aligned with the stereotype’s implications.

The discussion highlights that stereotypes simplify decision-making by providing immediate explanations for behavior, often leading to biased judgments. The authors emphasize that stereotypes are not merely faulty cognitive processes but functional tools that reduce cognitive load in complex situations. However, this efficiency comes at the cost of overlooking nuanced or contradictory information. The study also clarifies that recall patterns reflect post-judgment processing, where individuals seek confirmation for their initial stereotype-based impressions rather than engaging in unbiased evaluation. The findings underscore the need to understand the conditions under which stereotypes are applied and how they interact with other cognitive processes in social judgment.
