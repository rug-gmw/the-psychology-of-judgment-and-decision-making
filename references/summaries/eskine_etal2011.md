# A Bad Taste in the Mouth: Gustatory Disgust Influences Moral Judgment

Eskine, K. J., Kacinik, N. A., & Prinz, J. J.

__This study demonstrates that bitter tastes can heighten moral disgust, leading to harsher moral judgments, with politically conservative individuals showing greater sensitivity to this effect than liberals.__

The article explores the relationship between physical disgust—specifically induced through taste—and moral judgment, building on David Hume’s 18th-century suggestion that moral assessments are rooted in emotion rather than pure reason. The authors argue that moral judgments may be influenced by embodied sensory experiences, such as taste, which can evoke emotional responses that shape perceptions of right and wrong. Prior research has established links between physical disgust (e.g., foul odors, dirty environments) and moral condemnation, as well as overlapping neural activations in response to both types of disgust. However, no studies had yet examined how taste perception—particularly bitter and sweet tastes—might affect moral reasoning.

To investigate this, the researchers conducted an experiment with 57 undergraduate participants, randomly assigning them to one of three beverage conditions: sweet (berry punch), bitter (Swedish Bitters), or neutral (water). Participants were deceived into believing the study concerned motor interference on cognition, with the beverage serving as a prop to simulate natural hand movements. They consumed two small doses of their assigned drink while evaluating six moral vignettes depicting transgressions such as incest, bribery, and animal cruelty. Afterward, participants rated the beverages on dimensions like sweetness, bitterness, and disgust, and disclosed their political orientation.

The results confirmed the primary hypothesis: participants who consumed the bitter beverage made significantly harsher moral judgments than those in the sweet or control conditions. Self-reported disgust ratings accounted for 27.5% of the variance in moral judgments, reinforcing the link between physical and moral disgust. A secondary analysis revealed that political orientation moderated this effect. Conservatives exhibited a stronger sensitivity to the bitter taste, showing markedly harsher judgments in the bitter condition compared to the sweet or control conditions, whereas liberals’ judgments remained relatively unaffected by taste. This aligns with prior findings that conservatives are more responsive to disgust cues and rely more on sensory information in moral decision-making.

The discussion situates these findings within broader theories of embodied cognition, suggesting that abstract concepts like morality may originate from sensory experiences. The authors propose that while both liberals and conservatives may use emotions as information in moral judgments, conservatives may be more attuned to disgust due to their greater emphasis on purity norms. The study opens avenues for future research, such as exploring the duration of taste-induced effects, the influence of other tastes (e.g., sour, savory), and practical implications, like whether jurors’ diets might bias their verdicts or whether food preferences shape moral development. Overall, the research underscores the profound and often unconscious ways in which sensory experiences can shape higher-order cognitive processes like moral reasoning.
