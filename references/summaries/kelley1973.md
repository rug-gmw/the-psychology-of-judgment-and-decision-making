# The Processes of Causal Attribution

Kelley, H. H.

__This article synthesizes attribution theory, focusing on how individuals infer causes for behaviors or events by analyzing patterns of covariation and employing causal schemata. Kelley outlines key principles like the discounting and augmentation principles, emphasizing the role of prior beliefs and information in shaping attributions.__

Harold H. Kelley’s article provides a comprehensive overview of attribution theory, which explores how people explain the causes of behaviors and events. The theory, rooted in social psychology, addresses questions of social perception—such as whether a person’s behavior reflects their disposition or situational pressures—and self-perception, where individuals judge their own abilities or emotions. Kelley situates attribution theory within a broader framework of "psychological epistemology," examining how people develop a sense of certainty about their judgments.

The article distinguishes between two primary cases of attribution: one where the attributor has information from multiple observations (Case I) and another where only a single observation is available (Case II). For multiple observations, Kelley introduces the *covariation principle*, which posits that an effect is attributed to the cause with which it consistently varies over time. This principle is illustrated through experiments, such as the Prisoner’s Dilemma Game, where shifts in behavior are interpreted based on their alignment with external factors. Kelley also draws an analogy to the analysis of variance (ANOVA) framework, suggesting that laypeople use a simplified version of this statistical method to infer causation. For example, if a person consistently reacts positively to a specific stimulus while others do not, the effect is attributed to the person’s disposition. Conversely, if most people react similarly to the stimulus, the effect is attributed to the stimulus itself.

For single observations, Kelley introduces *causal schemata*—mental models that help individuals interpret limited information by considering the configuration of plausible causes. The *discounting principle* states that the role of a cause is diminished if other plausible causes are present. For instance, if a person complies with a request under external pressure, their internal motivation is discounted. Conversely, the *augmentation principle* suggests that if an effect occurs despite inhibitory causes, the internal cause is inferred to be stronger. Kelley elaborates on various types of causal schemata, such as multiple sufficient causes (where any one cause can produce the effect) and multiple necessary causes (where all causes must be present). These schemata help explain how people make attributions even with incomplete information.

The article also explores the interplay between preexisting beliefs and new information, highlighting how prior causal assumptions can distort the perception of covariation. Kelley cites studies by Chapman and Chapman, which demonstrate that individuals often perceive illusory correlations between diagnostic signs and psychological traits due to associative connections. This tendency to rely on simple, correspondence-based explanations—such as assuming that "good people cause good effects"—is discussed as a persistent bias, even among adults. Kelley suggests that while complex schemata develop with age, simple attributional patterns remain influential, particularly when subtlety is not explicitly required.

Kelley further examines how attributors sometimes assume interdependence among causes, complicating the attribution process. For example, if ability and task difficulty are assumed to covary, inferences about one may influence judgments about the other. The article also addresses errors and biases in attribution, such as the actor-observer discrepancy, where actors attribute their behavior to situational factors while observers attribute it to dispositional traits. This bias is explained by differences in the salience and availability of information for actors versus observers.

Finally, Kelley emphasizes the practical consequences of attributions, noting that causal explanations influence emotions, behaviors, and social interactions. For instance, attributions of responsibility affect aggression, reciprocation of benefits, and the effectiveness of persuasive communications. The article concludes by underscoring the importance of attribution theory in understanding how people navigate their social worlds, make decisions, and interact with others. Kelley’s synthesis not only consolidates existing research but also identifies key areas for future exploration, such as the conditions under which different causal schemata are employed and the impact of attributional biases on real-world behavior.
