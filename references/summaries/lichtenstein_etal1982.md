# Calibration of Probabilities: The State of the Art to 1980

Lichtenstein, S., Fischhoff, B., & Phillips, L. D.

__This article reviews the concept of calibration in probability assessment, highlighting that people often exhibit overconfidence in their judgments, especially for difficult tasks, and explores methods, findings, and theories related to improving calibration in both discrete and continuous probability assessments.__

The article begins by defining calibration within the subjectivist view of probability, where a probability reflects an individual’s degree of belief in a proposition. Calibration refers to the alignment between assessed probabilities and the actual frequency of outcomes—well-calibrated individuals assign probabilities that match the proportion of correct predictions over time. The authors distinguish calibration from other forms of "goodness" in probability assessments, such as normative goodness (adherence to probability axioms) and substantive goodness (knowledge of the subject matter). The review synthesizes research on calibration for both discrete propositions (e.g., true/false questions) and continuous uncertain quantities (e.g., estimating numerical values).

For discrete propositions, the article outlines various experimental designs, including tasks with no alternatives, one alternative, two alternatives, or multiple alternatives, and describes how calibration is measured using calibration curves and numerical indices like the Brier score. The Brier score is decomposed into components reflecting calibration, resolution, and knowledge, providing a nuanced understanding of assessors' performance. The authors summarize findings from meteorological research, where weather forecasters often demonstrate excellent calibration, likely due to extensive feedback and experience. Early laboratory studies, however, reveal consistent overconfidence, particularly among inexperienced assessors, though training and feedback can modestly improve calibration.

The article delves into more recent laboratory research, identifying overconfidence as a pervasive issue, especially for tasks of moderate or extreme difficulty. For example, individuals tend to assign high probabilities to incorrect answers in general-knowledge questions, and this overconfidence is exacerbated when tasks are difficult. The "hard-easy effect" is discussed, where assessors are overconfident on hard items and underconfident on easy ones, likely due to an inability to adjust their confidence levels appropriately. The authors also explore the influence of base rates, individual differences, and expertise on calibration. While personality traits show limited correlation with calibration, expertise in specific domains (e.g., students taking exams in their field) can lead to better calibration, though experts in other fields (e.g., physicians diagnosing illnesses) often exhibit poor calibration due to the complexity and variability of their tasks.

Corrective efforts to improve calibration are reviewed, including training with feedback, task restructuring, and encouraging assessors to consider contradictory evidence. While training can lead to modest improvements, its effectiveness varies, and generalization to new tasks is limited. The article also examines calibration for continuous propositions, where assessors typically provide fractiles (e.g., median, quartiles) to express uncertainty about numerical quantities. Research consistently shows that people tend to provide overly narrow confidence intervals, resulting in high "surprise indices" (i.e., true values frequently fall outside the assessed ranges). Training and feedback can reduce this overconfidence, but significant biases often remain.

The discussion addresses the importance of calibration in real-world decision-making, particularly in high-stakes contexts like medical diagnoses or nuclear safety assessments, where poorly calibrated probabilities can lead to substantial losses. The authors critique external recalibration methods, which adjust assessors' probabilities post hoc, due to theoretical and practical limitations. Instead, they advocate for training assessors to improve their calibration, though they acknowledge challenges such as the need for immediate feedback and the difficulty of predicting task difficulty in real-world settings.

Finally, the article situates calibration research within cognitive psychology, critiquing the field's empirical focus and calling for more theoretical development. Theories such as anchoring and adjustment, information-processing limitations, and serial models of probability assessment are discussed as potential frameworks for understanding calibration biases. The authors conclude by emphasizing the need for further theoretical integration to advance the field beyond descriptive studies and toward a deeper understanding of how people develop and express uncertainty.
