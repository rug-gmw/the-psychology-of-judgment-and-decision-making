# How Mental Systems Believe

Gilbert, D. T.

__This article challenges Descartes’ view that belief requires effortful assessment after comprehension, proposing instead Spinoza’s model: people automatically accept ideas upon understanding them and must later exert effort to reject false ones. Drawing on psychology research, Gilbert argues that belief is an involuntary part of comprehension, making humans prone to credulity under cognitive strain.__

The article begins by contrasting two historical perspectives on belief: Descartes’ Cartesian model, which posits that comprehension and assessment are distinct, sequential processes, and Spinoza’s alternative, which argues that comprehension inherently includes acceptance, with rejection requiring additional cognitive effort. Gilbert frames this debate as central to understanding how humans process ideas, noting that while Descartes’ view aligns with intuitive notions of rational assessment, Spinoza’s model better explains why people often believe what they hear or see without deliberate evaluation.

To explore these competing models, Gilbert dissects the mechanics of belief, likening the mind to a library where ideas are "tagged" as true or false. In Descartes’ system, new ideas remain unassessed until consciously evaluated, while in Spinoza’s, ideas are automatically accepted upon comprehension and only later "untagged" if proven false. This distinction becomes critical under cognitive stress, where limited resources force mental systems to output premature results. Gilbert introduces the "principle of premature output," which predicts that stressed systems will default to the output of early processing stages—meaning Cartesian systems would remain uncertain, while Spinozan systems would retain their initial acceptance of ideas.

Empirical evidence from developmental psychology, persuasion research, and psycholinguistics supports Spinoza’s model. Children, for instance, exhibit "primitive credulity," accepting ideas effortlessly before developing the ability to doubt—a pattern inconsistent with Cartesian symmetry but aligned with Spinoza’s asymmetry hypothesis. Similarly, studies on persuasion show that cognitively depleted individuals (e.g., those distracted or fatigued) are more likely to accept doubtful propositions, suggesting that rejection is a resource-intensive process that follows automatic acceptance. Even in everyday interactions, people tend to believe others’ self-descriptions (e.g., smiles, statements) unless they have the capacity to scrutinize them, a phenomenon exacerbated by cognitive load.

Linguistic patterns further reinforce Spinoza’s view. Words denoting acceptance (e.g., "true," "correct") are often unmarked compared to their opposites (e.g., "false," "incorrect"), implying that acceptance is the default state. Sentence verification research shows that people initially code all assertions as true, only revising this if evidence contradicts them—a process that mirrors Spinoza’s claim that disbelief is a secondary act. Even denials, which require comprehending and then rejecting a core assertion, can momentarily lead listeners to believe the very idea being negated, especially under cognitive strain.

Gilbert also addresses the "unity hypothesis," which posits that acceptance is inseparable from comprehension. While harder to prove, evidence suggests people struggle to withhold belief even when motivated to do so. For example, individuals exposed to invalid information (e.g., false feedback) often act as if it were true, even when forewarned of its falsity. Similarly, when instructed to merely read statements without assessing them, people later treat those statements as true, implying that comprehension inherently entails belief.

The article concludes by speculating on the evolutionary origins of this belief system. Gilbert suggests that the cognitive process of accepting ideas may have evolved from perceptual systems, which automatically treat sensory input as true to enable swift action. Since most socially communicated propositions are true, the efficiency of immediate acceptance may outweigh the costs of occasional errors. Thus, the Spinozan model—though procedurally redundant and error-prone—may be a "jury-rigged" adaptation of older perceptual mechanisms, retaining the automaticity of belief while adding a secondary rejection process.

Ultimately, Gilbert argues that Spinoza’s model offers a more accurate account of human belief, revealing why people are so prone to credulity. Just as we trust our eyes before questioning what we see, we trust our comprehension of ideas before assessing their truth—a tendency that shapes everything from childhood gullibility to the persistence of misinformation.
