# Trust, Reciprocity, and Social History

Berg, J., Dickhaut, J., and McCabe, K.

__This experimental study challenges the economic assumption of pure self-interest by demonstrating that trust and reciprocity emerge even in one-shot, anonymous interactions. The authors show that social history—knowledge of others' past cooperative behavior—strengthens these tendencies, suggesting trust is a fundamental behavioral primitive rather than merely a product of repeated interactions or reputational concerns.__

The article presents an experimental investigation into trust and reciprocity using a two-player "investment game" designed to isolate these behaviors from alternative explanations like reputation-building or contractual obligations. In the game, Player A (the "trustor") decides how much of a $10 endowment to send to an anonymous Player B (the "trustee"), with the amount tripling in transit. Player B then chooses how much to return to Player A. Standard economic theory predicts Player A should send nothing, as Player B has no incentive to reciprocate. However, in the "no history" treatment, 30 of 32 Player A participants sent money (averaging $5.16), and 11 of these decisions resulted in Player B returning more than the original amount, yielding positive net returns. This finding contradicts the subgame perfect Nash equilibrium and suggests that trust and reciprocity operate as intrinsic behavioral tendencies, even in one-shot, double-blind interactions where reputational or punitive mechanisms are absent.

The authors then introduce a "social history" treatment, where new participants are informed of the aggregate results from the no-history sessions. This manipulation tests whether shared knowledge of others' cooperative behavior reinforces trust and reciprocity. The results show that social history increases both the average amount sent by Player A ($5.36) and the average amount returned by Player B ($6.46), with a statistically significant rise in reciprocity. Notably, Player A decisions became more systematic, with 50% of participants sending either $5 or $10, and the correlation between amounts sent and returned strengthened. These findings suggest that social history enhances the interpretation of trust as a norm, making reciprocity more likely when players perceive a shared expectation of cooperation.

Methodologically, the study employs a rigorous double-blind procedure to ensure anonymity and eliminate confounding factors like reputation or punishment. Participants were recruited from a university population and played the game only once, with no opportunity for repeated interactions. The experimental design also controlled for framing effects by presenting the game neutrally, without invoking moral or social language. Data were analyzed using non-parametric tests, including randomization tests and Spearman’s rank correlation, to assess the significance of behavioral patterns.

The discussion situates the results within broader debates about the role of trust in economic and social systems. The authors argue that trust is not merely a byproduct of repeated interactions or institutional mechanisms but a fundamental behavioral primitive that can emerge spontaneously. They connect their findings to evolutionary theories of cooperation, which posit that trust and reciprocity may have developed as strategies to maximize fitness, even if they appear irrational from a myopic self-interest perspective. The study also highlights the role of social norms in reinforcing cooperative behavior, as social history appears to strengthen the internalization of reciprocity as a prescriptive norm.

The implications of the study extend to institutional design, suggesting that property rights and social norms may evolve to support trust and reciprocity. The authors note that their results align with other experimental findings, such as those from ultimatum games and repeated prisoner’s dilemma games, where participants frequently deviate from self-interested predictions to reward cooperation or punish defection. They conclude by emphasizing the need to incorporate trust and reciprocity into economic models, as these behaviors are essential for explaining real-world phenomena like organizational cooperation, market exchanges, and the development of social institutions.
