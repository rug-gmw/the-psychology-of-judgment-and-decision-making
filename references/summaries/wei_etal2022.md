# Chain-of-Thought Prompting Elicits Reasoning in Large Language Models

Wei, J., Wang, X., Schuurmans, D., Bosma, M., Ichter, B., Xia, F., Chi, E. H., Le, Q. V., & Zhou, D.

__This article introduces *chain-of-thought prompting*, a method that enables large language models (LLMs) to solve complex reasoning tasks by generating intermediate reasoning steps. The authors demonstrate that this approach significantly improves performance on arithmetic, commonsense, and symbolic reasoning benchmarks, particularly in models with over 100 billion parameters.__

The paper presents a novel prompting technique called *chain-of-thought prompting*, which enhances the reasoning capabilities of large language models by encouraging them to generate a series of intermediate steps before arriving at a final answer. Unlike traditional few-shot prompting, which relies on input-output pairs, chain-of-thought prompting provides exemplars that include both the reasoning process and the answer. This method is motivated by the observation that humans often break down complex problems into smaller, manageable steps, and the authors hypothesize that LLMs can similarly benefit from this structured approach.

The introduction highlights the limitations of scaling model size alone for improving reasoning performance, as prior work has shown that even large models struggle with tasks requiring multi-step reasoning. The authors propose chain-of-thought prompting as a solution that combines the strengths of rationale-augmented training (which uses intermediate steps) and few-shot prompting (which leverages in-context learning). By including chains of thought in the exemplars, the method avoids the need for costly finetuning while still enabling models to tackle complex reasoning tasks.

The core of the paper is divided into three experimental sections: arithmetic reasoning, commonsense reasoning, and symbolic reasoning. For arithmetic reasoning, the authors evaluate the method on five benchmarks, including GSM8K and SVAMP, and demonstrate that chain-of-thought prompting leads to dramatic performance improvements, particularly in the largest models (e.g., PaLM 540B). The results show that this approach achieves state-of-the-art accuracy on GSM8K, surpassing even finetuned models. An ablation study further confirms that the success of chain-of-thought prompting is not merely due to increased computation or the inclusion of equations but rather the structured reasoning process itself.

In the commonsense reasoning section, the authors apply chain-of-thought prompting to tasks like CSQA and StrategyQA, showing that it enhances performance on tasks requiring background knowledge and multi-hop reasoning. While the gains are less pronounced than in arithmetic reasoning, the method still outperforms standard prompting, particularly in larger models. For symbolic reasoning, the authors test the method on tasks like last-letter concatenation and coin-flip problems, demonstrating that chain-of-thought prompting enables models to generalize to longer sequences than those seen in the exemplars.

The discussion section reflects on the broader implications of the findings, emphasizing that chain-of-thought prompting is an emergent ability of sufficiently large models. The authors also acknowledge limitations, such as the potential for incorrect reasoning paths and the high computational cost of large models. Despite these challenges, the paper underscores the potential of chain-of-thought prompting to expand the range of tasks that LLMs can perform, suggesting that standard prompting may only scratch the surface of their capabilities. The work concludes by calling for further research into other prompting methods that could unlock additional reasoning abilities in LLMs.
