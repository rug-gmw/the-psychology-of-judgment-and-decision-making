# Increasing Saving Behavior Through Age-Progressed Renderings of the Future Self

Hershfield, H. E., Goldstein, D. G., Sharpe, W. F., Fox, J., Yeykelis, L., Carstensen, L. L., & Bailenson, J. N.

__This article explores how interacting with age-progressed digital renderings of one’s future self can increase individuals' tendency to save for retirement, demonstrating that such visualizations foster a stronger connection with future selves and promote more future-oriented financial decisions.__

The article addresses a critical economic issue: many people fail to save adequately for retirement, risking financial instability in later life. The authors propose that this shortfall is partly due to individuals' difficulty in identifying with their future selves, leading to excessive discounting of future rewards. Traditional interventions to improve saving behavior focus on reducing the allure of immediate rewards through precommitment strategies or enhancing the appeal of future rewards by encouraging imagination of future benefits. The authors introduce a third approach—strengthening the psychological connection between present and future selves—by leveraging immersive virtual reality and age-progression technology.

The theoretical foundation of the study draws on research suggesting that people often perceive their future selves as strangers, leading to decisions that prioritize immediate gratification over long-term well-being. Philosophers and psychologists argue that a lack of psychological connectedness with one’s future self contributes to poor intertemporal choices, such as insufficient retirement savings. The authors hypothesize that interacting with vivid, age-progressed renderings of one’s future self can bridge this psychological gap, fostering a sense of continuity and encouraging more prudent financial decisions.

The article presents four empirical studies to test this hypothesis. In Study 1, participants interacted with either a digital representation of their current self or an age-progressed version of their future self in an immersive virtual reality environment. Those who saw their future selves allocated significantly more money to a hypothetical retirement fund compared to those who saw their current selves. Study 2 aimed to rule out demand characteristics and priming effects by separating the virtual reality experience from the decision-making task and comparing the effects of interacting with one’s own future self versus another person’s aged avatar. The results confirmed that exposure to one’s future self, rather than aging in general, led to increased saving behavior across multiple intertemporal choice tasks.

Studies 3a and 3b extended these findings to more practical, field-like conditions. Study 3a used an online decision aid that displayed emotional responses of the future self to changes in retirement savings, demonstrating that participants who interacted with their future selves allocated a higher percentage of their income to retirement. Study 3b replicated these results with a broader, non-student sample and neutral facial expressions, further confirming that the effect was not driven by emotional valence but by the connection to the future self. Additionally, Study 3b found that future self-continuity mediated the relationship between exposure to the future self and increased retirement savings.

The discussion highlights the potential of age-progressed renderings as a novel intervention to improve saving behavior, particularly in an era of increasing life expectancy and financial uncertainty. The authors acknowledge limitations, such as the hypothetical nature of the tasks and the use of relatively young participants, but argue that the findings provide a promising foundation for future research and practical applications. They suggest that as technology advances, such interventions could become more accessible and widely implemented, helping individuals make more future-oriented financial decisions. The article concludes by emphasizing the importance of fostering a stronger psychological connection with one’s future self to address the growing challenge of retirement preparedness.
