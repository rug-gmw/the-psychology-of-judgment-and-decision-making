# Outcome Bias in Decision Evaluation

Baron, J., & Hershey, J. C.

__This article demonstrates that people often judge the quality of decisions based on their outcomes rather than the decision-making process itself, even when they recognize that outcomes should not influence such evaluations. Through five experiments, the authors show that favorable outcomes lead to higher ratings of decision quality, competence, and willingness to defer to the decision maker, despite instructions to ignore outcomes.__

The article explores the *outcome bias*—a tendency to evaluate decisions based on their results rather than the reasoning behind them—even when the outcome is irrelevant to assessing decision quality. The authors argue that while outcomes can sometimes provide useful information about decision-making processes, they should not directly influence evaluations when all relevant information (e.g., probabilities, utilities) is already known. The distinction between a "good decision" and a "good outcome" is central to decision analysis, yet the authors hypothesize that people outside this field often conflate the two.

The introduction establishes the theoretical foundation, citing historical and contemporary sources that critique the tendency to judge decisions by their outcomes. The authors define three types of information in decision evaluation: *actor information* (known only to the decision maker), *judge information* (known only to the evaluator, including outcomes), and *joint information* (known to both). They argue that outcome information should only indirectly affect evaluations by informing beliefs about actor information, but they predict that people will overuse it even when it is irrelevant.

Five experiments test this hypothesis using undergraduate subjects who evaluated medical decisions or monetary gambles. In Experiment 1, subjects rated the quality of medical decisions (e.g., whether to perform surgery) and showed a clear outcome bias: decisions with favorable outcomes were rated higher, even when subjects acknowledged that outcomes should not matter. Experiment 2 expanded this by asking subjects to rate the importance of various arguments for or against decisions, revealing that outcomes influenced the salience of these arguments. Experiment 3 used monetary gambles to test whether foregone outcomes (what *could* have happened) also affected evaluations, finding that decisions were rated more favorably when the foregone outcome was worse.

Experiment 4 addressed potential criticisms by using a between-subjects design and asking subjects to predict the future competence of decision makers. Even when subjects explicitly stated that outcomes should not influence their judgments, they still showed an outcome bias. Experiment 5 further tested this by measuring subjects' willingness to let a decision maker choose on their behalf, again finding that favorable outcomes increased trust in the decision maker, even when the decision was objectively worse.

The discussion highlights the practical implications of the outcome bias, particularly in contexts like law, medicine, and politics, where decisions are often judged retrospectively. The authors suggest that understanding this bias could help mitigate unfair evaluations of decision makers. They also speculate that the bias may stem from an overgeneralization of the heuristic that bad outcomes signal bad decisions, a belief in luck as a consistent trait, or the naturalistic fallacy that what feels natural must be rational. While the salience of arguments partly explains the bias, it does not fully account for the effect, suggesting other cognitive mechanisms are at play.

Overall, the article provides robust evidence for the outcome bias and underscores the importance of distinguishing between decision quality and outcomes in both theoretical and practical contexts.
