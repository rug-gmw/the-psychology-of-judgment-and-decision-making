# The Base-Rate Fallacy in Probability Judgments

Bar-Hillel, M.

__This article examines why people often ignore base-rate information in favor of individuating or diagnostic information when making probability judgments, proposing that perceived relevance—particularly through specificity or causality—determines whether base rates are integrated or dismissed.__

The article explores the *base-rate fallacy*, a cognitive bias where individuals prioritize specific, often individuating information over general statistical base rates when assessing probabilities. Bar-Hillel argues that this tendency is not merely a failure to understand Bayesian reasoning but stems from how people perceive the *relevance* of different types of information. The core thesis is that information is ordered by its perceived relevance to a judgment, with more specific or causally linked data dominating less relevant, general base rates.

The introduction presents a classic example—the "Cab Problem"—where subjects estimate the probability that a cab involved in a hit-and-run accident was Green, given that 85% of cabs are Blue and a witness identifies it as Green with 80% accuracy. Most subjects ignore the base rate (85% Blue cabs) and rely solely on the witness’s testimony, yielding an incorrect probability of 80% instead of the Bayesian answer of 41%. This fallacy is shown to persist across various real-world contexts, from clinical diagnostics to legal judgments, where base rates are routinely overlooked in favor of case-specific details.

Bar-Hillel critiques existing explanations for the fallacy, such as Kahneman and Tversky’s *representativeness heuristic* (where judgments are based on how well evidence matches a stereotype) and Nisbett et al.’s *vividness hypothesis* (where concrete details overshadow abstract statistics). She demonstrates that the fallacy occurs even when both base rates and individuating information are presented numerically, undermining these accounts. Instead, she proposes that *relevance*—mediated by specificity or causality—determines whether information is integrated. For instance, base rates framed as causally linked to an outcome (e.g., "85% of cab accidents involve Blue cabs") are more likely to be considered than coincidental base rates (e.g., "85% of cabs are Blue").

The empirical section presents a series of experiments testing this relevance-based account. In the *Suicide Problem*, subjects ignore the base rate of marital status (80% married) when given differential suicide rates between single and married individuals, treating the latter as more relevant. The *Dream Problem* shows that subjects prioritize matching statistics for married couples over general dreaming rates, even when the former are normatively irrelevant. The *Urn and Beads Problem* reveals that sample-specific information (e.g., bead colors drawn) dominates general population base rates, despite both being statistical. These results confirm that specificity—whether through subset membership or causal framing—enhances perceived relevance.

To test the limits of the fallacy, Bar-Hillel designs problems where base rates and individuating information are equally relevant. In the *Intercom Problem*, where both base rates and a non-causal indicator (e.g., intercom presence in cabs) are presented symmetrically, subjects integrate both sources, reducing the fallacy’s prevalence. Similarly, in the *Motor Problem*, where base rates are causally linked to mechanical failures, subjects combine them with diagnostic test results, yielding more accurate judgments. These findings support the claim that the fallacy arises from a *relative* lack of relevance, not an inherent inability to integrate information.

The discussion synthesizes these results, emphasizing that the base-rate fallacy is not a universal error but context-dependent. When base rates are perceived as equally relevant—through specificity, causality, or symmetry—they are incorporated into judgments. Bar-Hillel also highlights the broader implications: in domains like medicine or law, where base rates are often dismissed, this bias can lead to overconfidence in diagnostic tests or miscarriages of justice. She concludes by calling for further research into how people integrate multiple sources of uncertainty and how to mitigate the fallacy in real-world decision-making. The study thus reframes the base-rate fallacy as a dynamic cognitive process shaped by perceived relevance, rather than a static error in probabilistic reasoning.
