# Analytic thinking reduces belief in conspiracy theories

Swami, V., Voracek, M., Stieger, S., Tran, U. S., & Furnham, A.

__This article investigates how individual differences in thinking dispositions, particularly analytic thinking, influence belief in conspiracy theories. Through correlational and experimental studies, the authors demonstrate that promoting analytic thinking reduces conspiracist ideation, suggesting potential strategies for countering the widespread acceptance of conspiracy theories.__

The article begins by defining conspiracy theories as false beliefs attributing events to secretive plots by multiple actors and highlights their prevalence and negative societal impacts, such as reduced political engagement, vaccine hesitancy, and risky health behaviors. The authors argue that understanding the psychological origins of these beliefs is crucial, noting that prior research has linked conspiracist ideation to traits like distrust in authority, political cynicism, and paranormal beliefs. However, the role of thinking dispositions—particularly analytic versus experiential (intuitive) thinking—remains underexplored. The authors hypothesize that analytic thinking, which involves deliberate and evidence-based reasoning, may reduce belief in conspiracy theories, while intuitive thinking, which relies on affect and heuristics, may increase it.

In **Study 1**, a correlational study with 990 British participants, the authors examined associations between belief in conspiracy theories and various thinking dispositions, including analytic and intuitive thinking styles, open-mindedness, need for cognition, and need for closure. Results showed that stronger belief in conspiracy theories was significantly associated with lower analytic thinking, lower open-mindedness, and greater intuitive thinking. These findings suggest that individuals who rely more on intuitive, affect-driven reasoning are more prone to conspiracist beliefs, while those who engage in analytic thinking are less likely to endorse such theories.

To establish causation, the authors conducted three experimental studies. **Study 2** used a verbal fluency task to implicitly prime analytic thinking in 112 undergraduates. Participants who completed the analytic priming task showed a significant reduction in belief in conspiracy theories compared to a control group, supporting the hypothesis that analytic thinking causally reduces conspiracist ideation. **Study 3** employed an alternative method to elicit analytic thinking by manipulating cognitive disfluency—presenting information in a difficult-to-read font. Among 189 undergraduates, those in the disfluent condition reported lower belief in conspiracy theories than those in the control group, further confirming the role of analytic thinking in reducing conspiracist beliefs.

**Study 4** extended these findings to a general population sample (N = 140) and examined both generic conspiracist ideation and belief in a specific conspiracy theory (the 7/7 London bombings). Participants in the disfluent condition again showed reduced belief in both generic and specific conspiracy theories, demonstrating the robustness and generalizability of the effect. The authors conclude that promoting analytic thinking may be an effective strategy for countering the negative consequences of conspiracy theories.

The discussion explores potential mechanisms underlying the relationship between analytic thinking and reduced conspiracist beliefs, such as increased scrutiny of logical fallacies and factual inaccuracies in conspiracy theories. The authors also acknowledge limitations, including the reliance on undergraduate samples and the need for further research on long-term effects and cultural generalizability. Overall, the article highlights the importance of cognitive processes in shaping belief systems and suggests practical implications for addressing the societal challenges posed by conspiracy theories.
