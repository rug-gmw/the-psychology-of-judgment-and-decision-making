# The Essential Moral Self

Strohminger, N., Nichols, S.

__This article argues that moral traits are considered the most essential part of personal identity, more so than memory, desires, or cognitive abilities, based on five empirical studies examining lay intuitions about identity persistence across various scenarios.__

The paper explores how different aspects of the mind contribute to perceptions of personal identity, challenging the notion that all mental faculties are equally important. The authors introduce the "essential moral self hypothesis," proposing that moral traits—such as empathy, honesty, and conscientiousness—are central to how people judge whether someone remains the same person after cognitive or physical changes. This hypothesis is tested through five studies, each employing distinct scenarios to assess which mental traits people deem most critical to identity.

The introduction situates the research within philosophical and psychological debates about personal identity, noting that while psychological continuity is often prioritized over physical continuity, little work has examined which specific mental faculties matter most. The authors highlight the historical emphasis on memory in philosophical accounts of identity (e.g., Locke’s theory) but suggest that morality may play an even more foundational role. They also discuss the role of distinctiveness in identity, noting that while unique traits (like preferences or memories) might seem important, they may not be as central as universally valued traits like moral character.

Study 1 investigates how different neuropsychological impairments—such as loss of object recognition, autobiographical memory, desires, or moral conscience—affect judgments of identity persistence. Participants read a scenario about a man named Jim who undergoes a brain transplant and subsequently loses one of these faculties. Results showed that loss of moral conscience led to the greatest perceived disruption of identity, followed by memory and desire loss, while impairments to low-level cognition (e.g., object recognition) had minimal impact. Qualitative responses revealed that participants often explicitly linked morality to the core of the self.

Study 2 expands this inquiry by examining how voluntary pharmaceutical interventions—imagined "magic bullet" drugs that alter specific traits—affect identity. Participants rated how much a person would change if a drug removed one of 62 traits, spanning morality, memory, desires, personality, and perception. Moral traits again emerged as the most identity-conferring, with personality traits ranking second. Memory and desires were moderately important, but not all memories were valued equally; for example, traumatic or cherished memories were seen as more central to identity than procedural memories (e.g., knowing how to ride a bike). Perceptual traits, such as vision or hearing, were considered the least important to identity.

Study 3 shifts to a supernatural context, asking participants which traits they believe would persist if a soul moved from one body to another. Moral traits were most strongly associated with the soul, followed by desires, memories, and personality traits. Physical traits, such as fingerprints or medical conditions, were deemed least likely to transfer with the soul. Notably, these results held regardless of participants' explicit beliefs in souls or dualism, suggesting that intuitions about the soul draw on implicit, widely shared assumptions about identity.

Study 4 further tests the moral self hypothesis in the context of reincarnation. Participants chose which of two traits—one moral (e.g., honesty) and one non-moral (e.g., creativity)—was more likely to persist after reincarnation. Moral traits were overwhelmingly selected as more essential to the "true self," even when compared to highly valued traits like intelligence or extroversion. This pattern persisted across participants, regardless of their religious beliefs or belief in reincarnation.

Study 5 grounds the findings in a more everyday context: age-related cognitive changes. Participants rated how much various changes (e.g., becoming more selfish, forgetful, or colorblind) would alter an old friend’s identity. Moral changes were again judged as the most disruptive, followed by personality traits. Basic cognitive abilities (e.g., attention span) and memories were moderately important, while perceptual changes and preferences were least impactful. The study also found that the perceived frequency of a change did not correlate with its perceived impact on identity, countering the idea that rare changes are seen as more identity-disrupting.

The discussion synthesizes these findings, emphasizing that moral traits are consistently viewed as the most essential to personal identity across diverse scenarios. The authors suggest that this may reflect the social function of identity: moral traits are critical for predicting behavior in cooperative relationships, which are central to human survival and well-being. They also note that while memory has been historically emphasized in philosophical accounts of identity, its importance may be overstated or contingent on its social relevance. The paper concludes by highlighting the implications of these findings for understanding identity disruptions in real-world contexts, such as neurodegenerative diseases or psychopharmaceutical interventions, where moral faculties may be particularly vulnerable.
