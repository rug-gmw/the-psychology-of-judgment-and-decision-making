# Representativeness Revisited: Attribute Substitution in Intuitive Judgment

Kahneman, D., Frederick, S.

__This article revisits the concept of representativeness in intuitive judgment, proposing a unified model of attribute substitution where difficult judgment tasks are simplified by substituting easier, heuristic attributes. It introduces the dual-process theory of cognition (System 1 and System 2) and explores how biases arise when System 2 fails to correct System 1’s intuitive but flawed judgments.__

The article begins by tracing the origins of the heuristics and biases research program, which initially demonstrated how even experts fall prey to intuitive errors, such as overestimating the reliability of small sample sizes. Kahneman and Frederick argue that these errors stem from the use of heuristics—mental shortcuts that substitute complex judgments with simpler, more accessible attributes. The authors expand on earlier work by formalizing the concept of *attribute substitution*, where individuals unconsciously replace a difficult target attribute (e.g., probability) with a heuristic attribute (e.g., representativeness or similarity) that comes more readily to mind. This process is central to the operation of System 1, the fast, automatic, and intuitive cognitive system, while System 2, the slower, deliberative system, is responsible for monitoring and correcting these intuitive judgments.

The authors introduce a dual-process framework to explain how intuitive judgments are formed and why they often deviate from normative standards. System 1 operates automatically, effortlessly, and associatively, generating intuitive responses based on natural assessments such as similarity, affective valence, and causal propensities. System 2, in contrast, is effortful, rule-governed, and capable of overriding or refining System 1’s outputs. The interplay between these systems determines whether intuitive judgments are expressed or corrected. For example, in the well-known "Linda problem," individuals judge the likelihood that Linda is a feminist bank teller as higher than the likelihood that she is a bank teller, violating the rules of probability. This error occurs because System 1 substitutes the target attribute (probability) with the heuristic attribute (representativeness), and System 2 fails to intervene despite the logical inconsistency.

The article then delves into the mechanisms of attribute substitution, illustrating how it operates across various judgment tasks. The authors describe a heuristic elicitation design, where separate groups of participants judge either the target attribute or the heuristic attribute for the same set of objects. For instance, in the Tom W. study, participants ranked fields of graduate specialization either by how representative Tom W. was of each field or by the probability that he belonged to each field. The near-perfect correlation between these rankings provided strong evidence for attribute substitution. Similarly, the authors discuss how affective responses can serve as heuristic attributes, as seen in studies of willingness to pay for public goods, where emotional reactions to prototypical instances (e.g., a bird drowning in oil) drive judgments of economic value, often neglecting the scope of the problem (e.g., the number of birds affected).

A significant portion of the article addresses the controversy surrounding the representativeness heuristic, particularly the criticisms that base-rate neglect and conjunction errors are artifacts of experimental design or misinterpretation. Kahneman and Frederick counter these critiques by emphasizing that the heuristics and biases program was never intended to claim that individuals are incapable of logical reasoning. Instead, they argue that intuitive judgments are prone to systematic biases unless System 2 actively intervenes. They highlight conditions under which biases can be reduced or eliminated, such as statistical sophistication, intelligence, frequency formats, and manipulations of attention. For example, statistically trained individuals are more likely to avoid conjunction errors when the logical relationship between categories is made explicit, demonstrating the corrective role of System 2.

The article also introduces the concept of *prototype heuristics*, where categories are represented by prototypical exemplars, leading to *extension neglect*—the tendency to ignore extensional attributes such as base-rates, scope, or duration. This phenomenon is illustrated through studies of willingness to pay for public goods, where individuals’ valuations are insensitive to the scale of the problem (e.g., saving 2,000 vs. 200,000 birds), and through studies of retrospective evaluations of experiences, where global judgments are dominated by peak and end moments rather than the total duration of the experience. The authors argue that these biases arise because System 1 relies on prototypes and heuristic attributes, while System 2 fails to incorporate extensional information unless explicitly prompted.

Finally, the article situates the representativeness heuristic within a broader family of prototype heuristics, showing how similar processes underlie diverse judgment tasks, from categorical prediction to economic valuation. The authors conclude by reflecting on the evolution of the heuristics and biases program, noting that while the original focus on uncertainty was somewhat arbitrary, it provided a compelling framework for understanding intuitive judgment. They argue that the attribute substitution model offers a more general and unified account of heuristics, applicable beyond the domain of probability judgments to areas such as affective evaluation and decision-making. The article underscores the importance of studying the boundary conditions under which intuitive judgments are corrected or overridden, highlighting the interplay between System 1 and System 2 as central to understanding human rationality.
