# Feeling “Hangry”: When Hunger is Conceptualized as Emotion

MacCormack, J.K.

__This thesis explores how hunger influences emotional experiences, proposing that "hanger" arises when individuals interpret their bodily sensations of hunger as negative emotions in specific contexts. The research challenges the traditional view that hunger depletes self-regulation, instead suggesting that hunger-induced affective states are constructed as emotions through psychological processes.__

The thesis investigates the psychological mechanisms underlying the phenomenon of feeling "hangry"—a state of irritability or anger due to hunger. Jennifer Kay MacCormack hypothesizes that "hanger" occurs when individuals associate their bodily sensations of hunger with contextual cues, interpreting these sensations as high-arousal, negative emotions. The research comprises three studies that collectively examine how hunger interacts with context and attentional focus to shape emotional experiences and interpersonal perceptions.

In the introduction, MacCormack outlines the neurobiological basis of hunger, emphasizing its role in maintaining homeostasis through biochemical processes such as the release of hormones like ghrelin and cortisol. She highlights the overlap between brain regions involved in hunger and those associated with affective states, suggesting that hunger induces unpleasant, high-arousal bodily sensations that can be conceptualized as emotions. The thesis critiques the regulatory depletion hypothesis, which posits that hunger impairs self-regulation, leading to emotional outbursts and aggression. Instead, MacCormack proposes a psychological constructionist hypothesis, where emotions are constructed from core affective states (valence and arousal) and conceptual knowledge in context.

Study 1 employed an Affect Misattribution Procedure (AMP) to test whether hungry participants were more likely to perceive ambiguous stimuli as negative in a negative context. The results showed that hunger amplified negative perceptions of ambiguous stimuli when presented in a negative, high-arousal context, but not in a neutral context. Study 2 replicated these findings and extended them by demonstrating that the effect was specific to negative contexts, as hunger did not increase positive perceptions in positive, high-arousal contexts. These studies suggest that hunger-induced affect is interpreted as negative emotion only when the context affords such interpretations.

Study 3 experimentally manipulated hunger and participants' attentional focus on emotions to test whether hunger leads to emotional experiences and interpersonal judgments in a frustrating situation. Participants who were hungry and not explicitly focused on emotions reported greater negative, high-arousal emotions and viewed the experimenter as more judgmental. Importantly, hunger did not deplete self-regulation, as measured by perseverance on a tedious task, challenging the regulatory depletion hypothesis. Instead, the findings support the idea that hunger influences emotions through situated conceptualization—when individuals are unaware of attributing their affective state to emotions.

The discussion integrates these findings into broader psychological theories, emphasizing the role of core affect and conceptual knowledge in constructing emotional experiences. MacCormack argues that the psychological constructionist approach provides a more nuanced understanding of how hunger impacts emotions, perceptions, and behaviors. She also highlights the implications of these findings for theories of affect misattribution and situated cognition, suggesting that bodily states like hunger can shape emotional experiences in context-dependent ways. The thesis concludes by calling for further research into how homeostatic processes influence social and emotional experiences, underscoring the interconnectedness of body and mind.
