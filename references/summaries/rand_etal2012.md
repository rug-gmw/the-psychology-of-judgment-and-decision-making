# Spontaneous giving and calculated greed

Rand, D. G., Greene, J. D., & Nowak, M. A.

__This study demonstrates that humans are intuitively cooperative, with faster decisions leading to greater generosity in economic games, while reflection and deliberation increase selfishness. The authors propose that cooperative heuristics develop from daily life experiences where cooperation is advantageous, and these intuitions carry over into laboratory settings.__

This article explores the cognitive mechanisms underlying human cooperation using a dual-process framework, which distinguishes between intuitive (fast, automatic) and reflective (slow, deliberative) decision-making. The authors challenge the assumption that self-interest is the default human response, instead arguing that cooperation is often the intuitive choice. Through ten studies involving economic games—such as the public goods game (PGG) and prisoner’s dilemma—they consistently find that faster decisions correlate with higher levels of cooperation. For example, in a one-shot PGG with 212 participants, those who decided more quickly contributed significantly more to the common pool than those who deliberated longer. This pattern held across multiple experimental designs, including both online and in-person studies with varying stakes.

To establish causality, the researchers manipulated decision speed: participants forced to decide within 10 seconds contributed more than those instructed to reflect and delay their choices. Similarly, priming participants to trust their intuitions increased cooperation compared to those encouraged to rely on careful reasoning. These findings suggest that intuition fosters prosocial behavior, while reflection can suppress it. The authors propose that cooperative intuitions arise from real-world social interactions, where repeated interactions, reputation, and sanctions make cooperation advantageous. They validate this mechanism by showing that the link between intuition and cooperation weakens among participants familiar with laboratory experiments or those who report interacting with less cooperative peers in daily life.

The discussion highlights the implications of these results for economic and evolutionary models of cooperation, emphasizing the need for more cognitively nuanced frameworks. The authors also caution against overinterpreting cooperation as purely innate, noting that cultural evolution and social learning likely shape intuitive responses. The study concludes that while rational self-interest is compelling, humans’ first impulse is often to cooperate, a tendency that could inform policies and interventions aimed at promoting prosocial behavior.
