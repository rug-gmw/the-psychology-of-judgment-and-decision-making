# The Simulation Heuristic

Kahneman, D., & Tversky, A.

__This article introduces the *simulation heuristic*, a cognitive process where people mentally construct scenarios to assess probabilities, causality, and counterfactual outcomes. Kahneman and Tversky argue that mental simulations shape judgments, emotions, and decision-making, often following predictable but biased rules.__

The article builds on the authors’ earlier work on the *availability heuristic*, distinguishing between two ways people "bring things to mind": retrieving past instances and constructing hypothetical scenarios. While retrieval has been extensively studied, the process of mental construction—termed the *simulation heuristic*—has received less attention. Kahneman and Tversky propose that mental simulation operates like running a model, where individuals assess the ease with which a system (e.g., a real-world situation) could produce different outcomes. This ease, akin to Monte Carlo simulations in statistics, influences judgments of probability, causality, and emotional reactions to events.

The authors outline several key applications of the simulation heuristic. In *prediction*, people simulate interactions (e.g., two acquaintances meeting) to forecast outcomes. When *assessing probabilities*, they construct scenarios to gauge how easily a target event (e.g., U.S. military intervention in Saudi Arabia) could occur. *Conditional probabilities* involve simulating outcomes given a specific initial state (e.g., consequences of a Saudi civil war), though this raises questions about how much to adjust one’s mental model to accommodate the condition. *Counterfactual assessments* rely on simulation to imagine alternative realities (e.g., "What if Hitler had developed the atomic bomb earlier?"). Finally, *causality* is tested by mentally undoing events to see if outcomes change (e.g., "Would they have quarreled even without her comment?").

To explore these ideas empirically, the authors focus on *counterfactual undoing*—how people mentally revise past events to imagine alternatives. In one study, participants read about two travelers, Mr. Crane and Mr. Tees, who both missed their flights but differed in how close they came to making them. Despite identical outcomes, 96% of participants judged Mr. Tees (who missed his flight by 5 minutes) as more upset, illustrating how the ease of simulating an alternative scenario shapes emotional responses. This finding underscores the psychological reality of "near misses" and their outsized impact on regret.

A deeper investigation into undoing involved a tragic accident scenario where Mr. Jones, a banker, is killed in a collision with a drugged driver. Participants were given two versions of the story: one where Mr. Jones took an unusual route home (the "route" version) and another where he left work early (the "time" version). When asked to complete the phrase "If only...," participants overwhelmingly undid the *exceptional* aspect of the story—changing the route in the "route" version or the departure time in the "time" version—rather than introducing arbitrary or unlikely alterations. This preference for *downhill changes* (reverting to normal or expected states) over *uphill changes* (introducing exceptions) reveals a core constraint on mental simulation: people favor alterations that restore coherence and reduce surprise. Horizontal changes (e.g., tweaking continuous variables like timing by seconds) were notably absent, suggesting that undoing is not a random process but follows structured, intuitive rules.

The authors also identify the *focus rule*, where people modify the most salient element in a scenario. In the accident study, participants undid Mr. Jones’s actions when empathizing with his family but focused on the drugged driver when empathizing with his family. This highlights how attention shapes counterfactual thinking. Together, these findings demonstrate that mental simulations are governed by shared norms, such as the preference for downhill changes and the focus on central actors, which lend a sense of realism to counterfactual fantasies.

Beyond counterfactuals, the article discusses how the simulation heuristic influences *emotions* like regret, frustration, and grief, which arise from comparing reality to easily imagined alternatives. It also explores *scenario construction* in prediction and planning, where people build narratives to assess probabilities. A "good" scenario, they argue, is one with a downhill trajectory—no implausible leaps—and where each link is causally significant yet non-redundant. However, this approach introduces biases: scenarios with dramatic turning points are overvalued, while slow, incremental changes are underestimated. Similarly, plans often seem more likely to succeed because they consist of plausible links, ignoring the cumulative risk of multiple small failures.

In conclusion, Kahneman and Tversky present the simulation heuristic as a fundamental cognitive tool, shaping how people judge probabilities, assign causality, and experience emotions. While powerful, it is prone to systematic biases, such as overestimating the likelihood of scenarios with clear, downhill paths and underestimating the role of rare, uphill events. These insights not only advance the study of judgment and decision-making but also offer a framework for understanding the psychological distance between reality and the "possible worlds" we imagine.
