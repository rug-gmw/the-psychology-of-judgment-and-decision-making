# The Tragedy of the Commons

Hardin, G.

__This seminal article argues that certain societal problems, like overpopulation and environmental degradation, lack technical solutions and instead require moral and political changes, particularly the abandonment of the "commons" system in favor of regulated or privatized resources.__

Garrett Hardin’s 1968 essay challenges the widespread assumption that all human problems can be solved through technological or scientific advancements. Drawing on the earlier work of Wiesner and York, who concluded that the nuclear arms race had no technical solution, Hardin expands this idea to a broader class of "no technical solution problems," of which overpopulation is a prime example. He illustrates this concept through the metaphor of a game of tick-tack-toe, where no technical strategy can guarantee victory without fundamentally altering the rules or abandoning the game entirely. Similarly, Hardin contends that the population problem cannot be resolved through technological fixes like agricultural innovations or space colonization, as these merely delay the inevitable consequences of exponential growth in a finite world.

Hardin then explores the philosophical and biological underpinnings of the population dilemma. He critiques Bentham’s utilitarian goal of "the greatest good for the greatest number," demonstrating its mathematical and biological impossibility. Maximizing population would require minimizing per-capita resource consumption to near-zero, eliminating all luxuries and cultural pursuits, which is neither desirable nor sustainable. Even with infinite energy, the problem shifts from resource acquisition to waste dissipation, leaving Bentham’s ideal unattainable. Hardin argues that the optimal population is necessarily smaller than the maximum, but defining this optimum is fraught with difficulty due to the incommensurability of human values. While nature resolves such conflicts through natural selection, humans must consciously devise systems of weighting and judgment to balance competing interests—a task no society has yet mastered.

The core of Hardin’s argument lies in his exposition of the "tragedy of the commons," a concept first articulated by William Forster Lloyd in 1833. Hardin describes a scenario where herders share a common pasture: each rational herder seeks to maximize their gain by adding more cattle, but the collective overgrazing leads to the ruin of the commons. This tragedy arises because the benefits of overuse are privatized (the herder gains from additional cattle), while the costs are shared (the land degrades for all). Hardin extends this metaphor to modern environmental and social issues, such as pollution, overfishing, and the degradation of public spaces like national parks. He argues that the commons system, which may function under low population densities, becomes untenable as populations grow, necessitating the enclosure or regulation of shared resources.

Pollution, Hardin notes, presents a reverse tragedy of the commons: instead of taking from the commons, individuals and industries deposit waste into it. The rational actor finds it cheaper to pollute than to treat waste, leading to collective environmental harm. Hardin introduces the idea that morality is system-sensitive—actions deemed acceptable in a sparsely populated frontier become intolerable in a crowded metropolis. This challenges traditional ethical frameworks, which often rely on absolute prohibitions rather than context-dependent judgments. Hardin critiques the inadequacy of administrative law in addressing these nuanced moral dilemmas, highlighting the risks of corruption and the difficulty of balancing individual freedoms with collective well-being.

Hardin then turns to the population problem, arguing that the "freedom to breed" is incompatible with a finite world, particularly in a welfare state where the costs of overbreeding are socialized. He condemns the United Nations’ Universal Declaration of Human Rights for enshrining the right of families to determine their size, calling it a tragic ideal that locks humanity into a path of ruin. He further dismantles the notion that appeals to conscience can solve the population problem, citing Charles Galton Darwin’s argument that such appeals are self-eliminating. Those who heed calls for reproductive restraint will produce fewer offspring, while those who ignore them will dominate future generations, leading to the erosion of conscience itself. Hardin also warns of the psychological harm caused by appeals to conscience, which create "double binds" that can damage mental health and foster anxiety.

The solution, Hardin argues, lies in "mutual coercion, mutually agreed upon." He advocates for coercive measures—such as taxes, regulations, or other restrictions—to prevent the tragedy of the commons, drawing parallels to laws against bank robbery. While coercion is often viewed negatively, Hardin contends that it is necessary to preserve broader freedoms, as unchecked individual actions in a commons lead to collective ruin. He acknowledges that no system is perfectly just, but argues that the alternative—total ruin—is far worse. Hardin concludes by emphasizing the necessity of abandoning the freedom to breed, urging education to reveal this necessity and mutual coercion to enforce it. Only by recognizing and acting upon this necessity, he asserts, can humanity avert the tragedy of the commons and preserve its most precious freedoms.
