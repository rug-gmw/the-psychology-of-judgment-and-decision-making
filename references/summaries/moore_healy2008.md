# The Trouble With Overconfidence

Moore, D. A., & Healy, P. J.

__This article reconciles three distinct definitions of overconfidence—overestimation, overplacement, and overprecision—by proposing a Bayesian theory that explains their inconsistent empirical findings, particularly the negative relationship between overestimation and overplacement across tasks.__

The article critically examines the fragmented literature on overconfidence, identifying three primary definitions: *overestimation* (overrating one’s actual performance), *overplacement* (believing one is better than others), and *overprecision* (excessive certainty in one’s beliefs). The authors argue that prior research has often conflated these constructs, leading to contradictory findings. For instance, overestimation is most pronounced on difficult tasks, while overplacement is strongest on easy tasks, creating an inverse relationship between the two. Overprecision, meanwhile, appears more persistent but moderates the other two forms of overconfidence.

The core of the article introduces a Bayesian theory to explain these inconsistencies. The theory posits that people have imperfect information about their own performance and even less about others’, leading to regressive estimates. Specifically, when tasks are easy, individuals underestimate their own performance but believe they outperform others (overplacement). Conversely, on difficult tasks, they overestimate their performance but believe others outperform them (underplacement). This regressive updating occurs because people anchor their judgments on prior expectations and adjust insufficiently based on new, imperfect information. The theory formalizes this process, showing how beliefs about one’s own and others’ performances are updated based on signals of varying reliability.

To test their theory, the authors conducted an experiment with 82 participants who completed 18 rounds of trivia quizzes varying in difficulty. Participants estimated their own scores and those of a randomly selected previous participant at three stages: before taking the quiz, after taking it but before receiving feedback, and after learning the correct answers. The results confirmed the theory’s predictions: participants overestimated their performance on difficult quizzes and underestimated it on easy ones, while overplacement occurred on easy quizzes and underplacement on difficult ones. Overprecision was consistently observed across all conditions, though its presence reduced the magnitude of overestimation and overplacement. The study also found that greater precision in estimates was associated with less bias in both overestimation and overplacement.

The discussion highlights the broader implications of the theory, extending it to domains like risk perception, entrepreneurial decision-making, and social comparisons. The authors argue that their framework can reconcile seemingly contradictory findings in prior research, such as smokers overestimating their risk of lung cancer while simultaneously believing they are at lower risk than other smokers. The article concludes by emphasizing the distinct nature of the three forms of overconfidence and the importance of distinguishing them in future research. While the theory does not account for motivational or individual differences in overconfidence, it provides a parsimonious explanation for the observed patterns in empirical data.
