# "General Intelligence," Objectively Determined and Measured

Spearman, C.

__This seminal article introduces the concept of "general intelligence" (g) and establishes a foundational methodology for measuring correlations between sensory discrimination and intellectual abilities, demonstrating a strong, consistent relationship between them.__

Charles Spearman presents a comprehensive investigation into the relationship between sensory discrimination and general intelligence, challenging previous inconclusive and contradictory findings in experimental psychology. The article begins by critiquing the methodological weaknesses of earlier studies, which failed to provide precise quantitative measures of correlation, ignored probable errors, and did not account for irrelevant factors or observational errors. Spearman argues that these flaws rendered prior conclusions unreliable, necessitating a new, rigorous approach.

Spearman defines the problem clearly, focusing on the correspondence between sensory discrimination (in hearing, sight, and touch) and various forms of intelligence, while controlling for irrelevant factors such as practice, age, and sex. He conducts experiments across different populations, including village school children, high-class preparatory school boys, and adults, using carefully designed tests to measure sensory thresholds and intellectual rankings.

The results reveal a significant correlation between sensory discrimination and intelligence, with the most striking finding being the near-perfect correlation (approximately 1.00) between "General Discrimination" and "General Intelligence." This suggests that a common underlying factor—termed "General Intelligence" (g)—permeates all intellectual activities, from sensory discrimination to academic performance. Spearman further demonstrates that specific intellectual abilities form a hierarchical structure based on their saturation with this general factor, with classical studies and common sense ranking highest, and sensory discrimination, particularly in less practiced individuals, ranking lower.

The article also addresses potential criticisms and conflicting findings from prior research, such as those by Gilbert, Seashore, and Wissler, arguing that their methodological shortcomings explain their negative or inconclusive results. Spearman concludes by summarizing the key findings: the existence of a general intelligence factor, its universal presence across diverse intellectual activities, and the hierarchical organization of specific intelligences based on their correlation with this general factor. The work lays the groundwork for future research in psychometrics and the study of intelligence, emphasizing the importance of rigorous methodology in psychological experimentation.
