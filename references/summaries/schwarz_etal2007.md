# Metacognitive Experiences and the Intricacies of Setting People Straight: Implications for Debiasing and Public Information Campaigns

Schwarz, N., Sanna, L. J., Skurnik, I., & Yoon, C.

__This article explores how metacognitive experiences—such as the ease or difficulty of recalling information—significantly influence judgment and decision-making, often undermining traditional debiasing strategies and public information campaigns. The authors argue that ignoring these subjective experiences can lead to unintended consequences, including reinforcing biases rather than correcting them.__

The article begins by highlighting the persistent challenge of human judgment biases, which have been extensively documented in psychological research. Traditional debiasing strategies, such as encouraging individuals to "consider the opposite" or providing incentives for careful reasoning, often fail because they overlook the role of metacognitive experiences—subjective feelings that accompany cognitive processes. The authors introduce two key metacognitive experiences: *accessibility experiences* (the ease or difficulty of recalling information) and *processing fluency* (the ease or difficulty of processing new information). These experiences interact with declarative information (what people think about) to shape judgments, often in ways that contradict the predictions of content-focused models.

The first section of the article delves into the principles of metacognitive experiences, emphasizing that judgments are not solely based on the content of thoughts but also on how easily those thoughts come to mind. For example, people may infer that they are more assertive if they can easily recall instances of assertive behavior, but if recalling such instances is difficult, they may conclude the opposite. Similarly, processing fluency—the ease with which information is processed—can lead people to perceive information as more familiar or true, even when it is not. The authors present empirical evidence demonstrating that these experiences systematically influence judgments, often overriding the content of the information itself.

The second section critiques the assumption underlying many debiasing strategies: that encouraging people to think more deeply about an issue will lead to less biased judgments. The authors argue that this assumption fails when generating alternative thoughts is difficult, as the effort itself can reinforce the initial judgment. For instance, asking people to "consider the opposite" may backfire if they struggle to generate counterarguments, leading them to believe their initial judgment was correct. The authors present a metacognitive model that predicts when debiasing strategies will succeed or fail, depending on whether the generation of thoughts is experienced as easy or difficult. Empirical studies, such as those involving students' predictions about exam outcomes, support this model, showing that debiasing strategies only work when generating alternative thoughts is easy.

The third section examines the implications of metacognitive experiences for public information campaigns. The authors argue that campaigns designed to correct misinformation by repeating false claims (e.g., "myth vs. fact" formats) can inadvertently increase the acceptance of those myths over time. This occurs because repeated exposure to false information increases its processing fluency, making it seem more familiar and thus more likely to be accepted as true. Studies involving flu vaccination myths and health product claims demonstrate that such campaigns can backfire, particularly among older adults, whose memory for details fades more quickly than their sense of familiarity. The authors conclude that public information campaigns should avoid repeating myths and instead focus on presenting facts in a way that enhances their fluency and familiarity.

The article concludes with a discussion of open questions and future research directions. The authors highlight the need to explore how naive theories about mental processes influence the interpretation of metacognitive experiences, how these experiences change over time, and how they operate in group decision-making contexts. They also emphasize the importance of considering temporal changes in metacognitive experiences, as initial surprise or difficulty may give way to familiarity and confidence over time. Overall, the article underscores the critical role of metacognitive experiences in shaping judgment and decision-making, offering a nuanced perspective on why traditional debiasing and public information strategies often fail and how they might be improved.
