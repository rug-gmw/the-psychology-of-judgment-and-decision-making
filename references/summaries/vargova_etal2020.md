# The way of making choices: Maximizing and satisficing and its relationship to well-being, personality, and self-rumination

Vargová, L., Zibrínová, Ł., Baník, G.

__This study examines the relationship between decision-making tendencies—maximizing (as a goal and strategy) and satisficing—and well-being, personality traits, and self-rumination in a Slovak sample. It finds that maximizing as a strategy is maladaptive, correlating with lower happiness and higher depression, while maximizing as a goal shows no such effects, and satisficing remains unrelated to well-being.__

The article explores the longstanding debate over whether maximizing (seeking the best possible option) or satisficing (settling for "good enough") tendencies are adaptive or maladaptive for well-being. Previous research has yielded contradictory findings, partly due to inconsistencies in how these constructs are conceptualized and measured. The authors address this by adopting a two-component model of maximizing—distinguishing between *maximizing as a goal* (high standards) and *maximizing as a strategy* (exhaustive alternative search)—while measuring satisficing as a separate construct. The study investigates how these tendencies relate to well-being (happiness and depression), personality traits (neuroticism and extraversion), and self-rumination in a sample of 514 Slovak participants.

The introduction highlights the theoretical divide: early work (e.g., Schwartz et al., 2002) framed maximizing as maladaptive, associating it with lower happiness and higher depression, while later studies suggested that maximizing could be adaptive if operationalized differently (e.g., as high standards rather than decision difficulty). The authors argue that measurement inconsistencies—such as treating maximizing and satisficing as a continuum or conflating their subcomponents—have obscured their true relationships with well-being. They propose that a two-component model of maximizing (goal vs. strategy) and a separate measure of satisficing could clarify these dynamics, particularly in understudied cultural contexts like Eastern Europe.

The methods section details the study’s design, using validated scales to measure the key constructs. Maximizing as a goal was assessed with the 7-item Maximizing Tendency Scale (MTS-7), which captures high standards, while maximizing as a strategy was measured via the alternative search subscale of the Maximization Inventory (MI). Satisficing was evaluated using the "less ambitious satisficing" subscale of the Decision Making Tendency Inventory (DMTI). Well-being was gauged through the Subjective Happiness Scale (SHS) and Beck Depression Inventory (BDI-II), while personality traits (neuroticism and extraversion) and self-rumination were measured using the Big Five Inventory (BFI-44) and Rumination-Reflection Questionnaire (RRQ), respectively. Data were collected both online and in-person, with analyses conducted using Pearson’s correlations and Bayesian factors to assess the strength of relationships.

Results revealed that maximizing as a strategy was maladaptive, showing positive correlations with depression and neuroticism and negative correlations with happiness and extraversion. In contrast, maximizing as a goal was unrelated to well-being but positively associated with extraversion and self-rumination. Satisficing, however, showed no significant relationships with any variables, raising questions about its conceptual validity. The findings support the utility of the two-component model of maximizing, demonstrating that its subcomponents interact differently with well-being and personality. Notably, the study also highlights potential cultural differences: while maximizing as a strategy was maladaptive in this Slovak sample, similar studies in Western contexts (e.g., the U.S.) have found weaker or even positive associations with well-being.

The discussion interprets these results within the broader literature, emphasizing that the maladaptive effects of maximizing depend on how it is measured and conceptualized. The authors argue that distinguishing between maximizing as a goal and strategy resolves some inconsistencies in prior research, as the latter’s exhaustive search for alternatives appears to drive negative outcomes. The lack of relationships for satisficing suggests that its measurement may need refinement or that its adaptive benefits are context-dependent. The study also underscores the role of personality traits—particularly neuroticism—in moderating the effects of decision-making tendencies on well-being, aligning with prior work showing that neuroticism amplifies maladaptive outcomes.

Limitations include the cross-sectional design, which precludes causal inferences, and the predominantly female, young, and educated sample, which may limit generalizability. The authors call for longitudinal and experimental studies to further explore these dynamics, as well as cross-cultural comparisons to test the robustness of their findings. Despite these constraints, the study contributes to the literature by validating the two-component model of maximizing in a new cultural context and challenging assumptions about the adaptiveness of satisficing. It concludes that the relationship between decision-making tendencies and well-being is nuanced, shaped by measurement choices, personality factors, and cultural values.
