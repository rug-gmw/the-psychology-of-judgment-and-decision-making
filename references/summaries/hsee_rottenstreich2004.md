# Music, Pandas, and Muggers: On the Affective Psychology of Value

Hsee, C. K., & Rottenstreich, Y.

__This article explores how people assess value through two distinct psychological processes—valuation by feeling and valuation by calculation—and demonstrates that these processes yield different sensitivities to the scope of a stimulus, influencing real-world decision-making.__

The study investigates how individuals determine the subjective value of stimuli by contrasting two psychological processes: *valuation by feeling* and *valuation by calculation*. The authors argue that when people rely on feelings to assess value, they are highly sensitive to the presence or absence of a stimulus but largely indifferent to variations in its magnitude or scope. In contrast, when people engage in deliberate calculation, they exhibit a more consistent sensitivity to changes in scope, resulting in a roughly linear relationship between scope and value. This distinction helps explain why real-world value functions often appear concave, as most decisions involve a mix of both processes.

The introduction sets the stage by posing questions about how people’s willingness to work or donate changes with varying rewards or quantities (e.g., working for $30 vs. $60 or donating to save one vs. four endangered animals). These questions highlight the broader theoretical interest in the relationship between the quantitative scope of a stimulus and its subjective value. The authors build on dual-process models of cognition, which distinguish between associative, affect-based thinking and deliberate, rule-based reasoning. They propose that *valuation by feeling* is driven by the emotional reactions a stimulus evokes, while *valuation by calculation* involves algorithmic assessments that explicitly account for scope. For example, someone deciding how much to pay for a bundle of Madonna CDs might either calculate a price based on the number of discs or rely on their emotional response to Madonna’s music, leading to different valuations.

The empirical portion of the article consists of four studies designed to test the hypothesis that affect-rich stimuli encourage valuation by feeling (resulting in scope-insensitivity), while affect-poor stimuli encourage valuation by calculation (resulting in scope-sensitivity). In *Study 1 (Madonna)*, participants were primed to either calculate or feel before indicating their willingness to pay for a bundle of 5 or 10 Madonna CDs. Those primed to calculate showed greater sensitivity to the number of CDs, while those primed to feel were largely indifferent to scope. A crossover effect—where feeling yielded higher valuations at low scope but lower valuations at high scope—suggested that the affective response to Madonna was of moderate intensity.

*Study 2 (Music Book Versus Cash)* compared participants’ willingness to work for either a music book (affect-rich) or cash (affect-poor). Participants were more sensitive to the scope of cash rewards ($30 vs. $60) than to the scope of the music book’s list price, again demonstrating scope-insensitivity for affect-rich stimuli. *Study 3 (Saving Pandas)* manipulated the presentation of endangered pandas using either affect-poor dots or affect-rich pictures. Donations were scope-sensitive in the dot condition but scope-insensitive in the picture condition, with participants donating similar amounts regardless of whether one or four pandas were depicted. Finally, *Study 4 (Sentencing a Mugger)* extended the findings to a negative domain by examining how empathy instructions influenced recommended prison sentences for a mugger with zero or four prior offenses. Participants in the empathy (affect-rich) condition were less sensitive to the number of prior offenses than those in the no-empathy (affect-poor) condition.

The discussion synthesizes these findings, emphasizing that valuation by feeling leads to scope-insensitivity, while valuation by calculation leads to scope-sensitivity. The authors connect their work to prior research on *scope neglect*, where people often fail to adjust their valuations in response to changes in the quantity of a good (e.g., donating similar amounts to save 2,000 vs. 200,000 birds). They argue that such neglect arises when people rely on affective reactions to prototypical images rather than quantitative assessments. The article also explores the implications of these processes for *probability weighting*, suggesting that affect-rich prospects may lead to hypersensitivity to changes in probability near the extremes (0% or 100%) but insensitivity to intermediate variations, a pattern consistent with observed probability weighting functions in decision-making under risk.

The authors propose a mathematical model to account for the concavity of real-world value functions, suggesting that concavity arises from a mix of valuation by feeling and calculation. They conclude by highlighting the complexity of valuation processes, noting that while feeling and calculation are key drivers, other factors—such as joint versus separate evaluations or social norms—can also influence preferences. The article offers a novel, process-based explanation for why people’s valuations often deviate from rational economic models, with important implications for understanding decision-making in both positive and negative domains.
