# Myside Bias, Rational Thinking, and Intelligence

Stanovich, K. E., West, R. F., & Toplak, M. E.

__This article challenges the assumption that intelligence equates to rational thinking by demonstrating that myside bias—the tendency to evaluate evidence in a way that favors one’s own beliefs—is largely independent of cognitive ability. The authors argue that rationality is a distinct construct from intelligence, with myside bias serving as a key example of a rational thinking skill not captured by IQ tests.__

The article synthesizes research on myside bias, a form of confirmation bias where individuals assess evidence, generate arguments, and test hypotheses in ways that align with their preexisting opinions. Despite intelligence tests being widely regarded as measures of good thinking, the authors highlight a surprising finding: myside bias shows little to no correlation with intelligence. This dissociation is evident across multiple studies, including those by Perkins and colleagues, who found that while intelligence predicted the *quantity* of arguments generated in reasoning tasks, it did not predict the ability to produce counterarguments opposing one’s own position. Similarly, Klaczynski and colleagues demonstrated that verbal ability improved overall reasoning quality but did not reduce the tendency to critique opinion-inconsistent evidence more harshly than opinion-consistent evidence.

The authors’ own research further reinforces this pattern. In one study, participants generated arguments for and against controversial issues (e.g., organ sales), revealing a strong myside bias—more arguments were produced in favor of their own stance—but no relationship between the degree of bias and cognitive ability. Another study examined causal reasoning about social phenomena (e.g., criminal recidivism), where participants provided evidence for and against their personal theories. Even here, the quality and distribution of arguments were similar across high- and low-intelligence individuals. Naturalistic paradigms, such as evaluating arguments about abortion or assessing policy positions (e.g., banning a dangerous car), also failed to show any attenuation of myside bias in higher-intelligence groups. In one striking example, participants were more likely to support banning a "dangerous German car" in the U.S. than a "dangerous American car" in Germany, yet this bias was equally prevalent among high- and low-IQ individuals.

The article explains this dissociation by distinguishing between tasks that *explicitly* instruct participants to decouple prior beliefs (e.g., formal syllogistic reasoning) and those that do not (e.g., informal reasoning tasks). In formal reasoning, where participants are warned to ignore real-world knowledge (e.g., "Assume all things smoked are good for health"), intelligence correlates with the ability to override prior beliefs, as higher cognitive ability provides the computational power for such inhibition. However, in informal or naturalistic tasks—where no such warning is given—intelligence offers no protection against myside bias. The authors argue that real-world reasoning rarely comes with explicit instructions to set aside personal opinions, making myside bias a pervasive and intelligence-independent phenomenon.

The discussion emphasizes that rationality is a broader construct than intelligence, encompassing skills like open-minded thinking, resistance to cognitive miserliness, and the ability to align beliefs with evidence. Myside bias, as a component of rational thought, is particularly resistant to intelligence, highlighting the need for assessment tools that measure rational thinking independently of IQ. The authors propose a model for evaluating rational thinking, including dimensions like scientific thinking, probabilistic reasoning, and the absence of myside bias, which have been linked to real-world outcomes such as medical decision-making and financial literacy. Ultimately, the article underscores that high intelligence does not inoculate against biased reasoning, challenging the assumption that smarter individuals are inherently better thinkers.
