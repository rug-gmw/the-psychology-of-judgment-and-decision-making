# The Behavioral Life-Cycle Hypothesis

Shefrin, H. M., and Thaler, R. H.

__This article introduces the Behavioral Life-Cycle (BLC) hypothesis, which integrates self-control, mental accounting, and framing into the traditional life-cycle theory of saving. The authors argue that households treat wealth as nonfungible, dividing it into mental accounts that influence spending behavior, and present empirical evidence supporting their behavioral model over the standard life-cycle hypothesis.__

The Behavioral Life-Cycle (BLC) hypothesis, proposed by Hersh Shefrin and Richard Thaler, represents a significant departure from the traditional life-cycle (LC) theory of saving developed by Modigliani and Brumberg. While the LC model assumes rational, forward-looking behavior where households smooth consumption over their lifetime, Shefrin and Thaler argue that empirical evidence consistently contradicts this idealized framework. Their BLC hypothesis seeks to explain these discrepancies by incorporating behavioral concepts such as self-control, mental accounting, and framing into the economic analysis of saving behavior.

The foundation of the BLC model rests on three key behavioral features. First, the authors recognize that self-control is costly and that individuals employ various strategies—such as pension plans and rules of thumb—to manage the challenge of postponing consumption until retirement. This introduces the concept of internal conflict, where individuals behave as if they possess two sets of preferences: a "planner" focused on long-term goals and a "doer" driven by short-term gratification. Second, the model incorporates mental accounting, whereby households categorize wealth into distinct mental accounts (current income, current assets, and future income) that violate the principle of fungibility. This segmentation influences spending decisions, as some accounts are more tempting to invade than others. Third, framing effects play a crucial role, as the way wealth increments are described or perceived can significantly impact saving rates. For example, a lump-sum bonus may be treated differently from regular income, even if both are fully anticipated.

The theoretical framework of the BLC model is built around the interplay between the planner and the doer. The planner, representing the rational, long-term aspect of the individual, seeks to maximize lifetime utility, while the doer, embodying the emotional, short-term aspect, is myopically focused on current consumption. This dual-preference structure creates a dynamic where the planner must exert willpower to constrain the doer's impulses, incurring psychic costs in the process. To mitigate these costs, the planner may employ precommitment devices, such as pension plans or self-imposed rules, to restrict future consumption choices. However, these strategies are inherently second-best, as they do not fully align with the idealized consumption path predicted by the LC model. The BLC model formalizes this tension by introducing a willpower effort variable, which captures the psychic costs of resisting temptation and varies depending on the mental account being accessed.

The empirical portion of the paper tests several predictions derived from the BLC model, each of which contrasts sharply with the LC hypothesis. One of the most compelling predictions is that the marginal propensity to consume (MPC) differs across mental accounts. Specifically, the MPC is highest for current income, lower for current assets, and lowest for future income. This prediction is supported by a survey of MBA students, who reported significantly higher MPCs for regular income compared to lump-sum bonuses or future inheritances. Additional evidence from econometric studies, such as those by Courant et al. and Holbrook and Stafford, further corroborates the differential MPC hypothesis, showing that households consume a larger fraction of labor income than capital income or transfer payments.

Another critical prediction of the BLC model concerns the effect of pension savings on discretionary saving. The LC model predicts a one-to-one offset, where increases in pension savings are fully offset by decreases in discretionary saving. In contrast, the BLC model predicts that the offset will be less than one, particularly for younger individuals, because pension contributions reduce disposable income and are framed as future wealth, which is less tempting to spend. This prediction is strongly supported by a range of studies spanning multiple decades and countries, including work by Cagan, Katona, Green, King and Dicks-Mireaux, Kurz, and Diamond and Hausman. These studies consistently find offsets close to zero, rather than the -1.0 predicted by the LC model, suggesting that pension plans effectively increase total saving.

The BLC model also offers novel insights into the adequacy of retirement saving. The LC model predicts smooth consumption profiles, where retirement consumption equals preretirement consumption. However, the BLC model predicts that, in the absence of sufficient pension and Social Security programs, retirement consumption will fall short of preretirement levels due to self-control challenges. Empirical evidence on this point is mixed, with some studies (e.g., Blinder et al. and Hamermesh) suggesting inadequate saving, while others (e.g., Kotlikoff et al.) argue that saving is adequate, largely due to the role of pensions and Social Security. The BLC model reconciles these findings by emphasizing the importance of institutionalized saving mechanisms in overcoming self-control problems, while also noting that consumption often declines during retirement, consistent with the idea that households gradually adapt to inadequate resources.

The paper also addresses the proportionality principle, which posits that saving rates are independent of income levels. The BLC model rejects this principle, predicting instead that saving rates increase with permanent income, as the marginal cost of exercising willpower declines at higher consumption levels. This prediction is strongly supported by empirical evidence, including studies by Mayer and Diamond and Hausman, which show that higher-income households save a larger fraction of their income. The BLC model attributes this pattern to the reduced temptation costs associated with higher income, rather than measurement errors or transitory income effects.

Additional predictions of the BLC model concern the sensitivity of consumption to income fluctuations, bonuses, and windfalls. The model predicts that consumption tracks income more closely than the LC model would suggest, particularly for transitory income changes. This "hypersensitivity" is attributed to self-imposed borrowing constraints and the framing of income as current and thus more tempting to spend. Evidence from studies by Hall and Mishkin and others supports this prediction, showing that households respond strongly to transitory income changes, even when such behavior is inconsistent with the LC model's predictions. The BLC model also predicts that bonuses and windfalls will have lower MPCs than regular income, as these forms of income are more likely to be framed as wealth and thus saved. Studies of Japanese bonus systems and Israeli windfall recipients provide strong support for these predictions, demonstrating that the form and timing of income receipt significantly influence consumption behavior.

The policy implications of the BLC model are substantial. The authors suggest that governments seeking to increase saving rates should consider seemingly irrelevant changes, such as tax cuts accompanied by underwithholding (to generate refunds framed as windfalls) or the promotion of Japanese-style bonus systems. These strategies leverage the behavioral insights of the BLC model to encourage saving without altering income levels or after-tax rates of return. The model also underscores the importance of institutionalized saving mechanisms, such as pensions, in helping individuals overcome self-control problems.

In conclusion, the Behavioral Life-Cycle hypothesis offers a compelling alternative to the traditional life-cycle model by incorporating behavioral realism into the analysis of saving behavior. The BLC model's predictions are consistently supported by empirical evidence, and its insights have important implications for both economic theory and policy. While the LC model remains a valuable normative benchmark, the BLC model provides a more accurate descriptive framework for understanding how households actually save and consume over their lifetimes.
