# Sympathy and Callousness: The Impact of Deliberative Thought on Donations to Identifiable and Statistical Victims

Small, D. A., Loewenstein, G., Slovic, P.

__This article explores how encouraging people to think analytically about charitable donations reduces generosity toward identifiable victims without increasing donations to statistical victims, leading to an overall decline in giving. The authors demonstrate that deliberative thought diminishes emotional responses to individual cases but fails to generate sympathy for broader, abstract suffering.__

The study investigates the "identifiable victim effect," where people donate more to specific, named individuals (e.g., "Baby Jessica") than to statistical victims (e.g., thousands of starving children). The authors argue that this discrepancy arises from dual-process thinking: affective reactions dominate when individuals are vivid and personal, while deliberative reasoning prevails for abstract, impersonal cases. Prior research shows that identifiable victims evoke stronger emotional responses, leading to greater generosity, whereas statistical victims fail to trigger the same sympathy. The authors hypothesize that teaching people about this bias or priming deliberative thought would reduce donations to identifiable victims but not increase giving to statistical victims, as the latter lacks an inherent emotional pull.

The paper presents four studies to test these hypotheses. In Study 1, participants were either informed about the identifiable victim effect or not before being asked to donate to either an identifiable or statistical victim. Results showed that the intervention reduced donations to identifiable victims but had no effect on giving to statistical victims, confirming the asymmetric impact of deliberative thought. Study 2 ruled out framing effects by showing that alternative descriptions of the bias (e.g., "people give more to identifiable victims" vs. "people give less to statistical victims") did not alter the results. Study 3 demonstrated that presenting statistical information alongside an identifiable victim reduced donations to the latter, suggesting that deliberative comparisons dampen emotional responses. Finally, Study 4 primed participants to think either analytically or emotionally before making donation decisions. Analytic priming reduced giving to identifiable victims, while emotional priming had no effect on donations to statistical victims, further supporting the idea that deliberation suppresses affective responses without generating new sympathy.

The discussion highlights the implications of these findings for charitable giving and social welfare. While interventions to reduce the identifiable victim effect may increase consistency in valuing lives, they also risk reducing overall generosity. The authors suggest that deliberative thought can sometimes increase giving—such as when it helps people recognize the deservingness of victims from different backgrounds—but in the case of identifiable versus statistical victims, it primarily undermines emotional responses. They conclude that while the identifiable victim effect may distort resource allocation, it also drives more donations than would otherwise occur, making it a "second best" optimum for charitable organizations. Future research could explore conditions under which deliberation enhances rather than diminishes generosity.
