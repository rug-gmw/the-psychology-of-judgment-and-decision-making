# Mispredicting distress following romantic breakup: Revealing the time course of the affective forecasting error

Eastwick, P. W., Finkel, E. J., Krishnamurti, T., & Loewenstein, G.

__This study examines why people overestimate their emotional distress after a romantic breakup, finding that errors stem from misjudging initial reaction intensity rather than recovery speed. The research reveals that individuals more deeply in love, less likely to start a new relationship, or less involved in initiating the breakup make the largest forecasting errors.__

This article investigates the psychological phenomenon of affective forecasting—people’s tendency to mispredict their emotional responses to future events—by focusing on romantic breakups, a deeply distressing yet common life experience. The authors argue that while prior research has established that people consistently overestimate their emotional reactions to negative events, the precise nature of this error remains unclear. Specifically, it was unknown whether forecasting inaccuracies arise from misjudging the *initial intensity* of emotional distress or the *rate of emotional decay* over time. To address this gap, the study employed a longitudinal design tracking college freshmen in romantic relationships over nine months, with 38% experiencing a breakup during the study period.

Participants provided both predicted and actual distress ratings at multiple time points—two weeks before the breakup and at intervals up to ten weeks afterward. The results demonstrated a clear initial intensity bias: participants significantly overestimated how upset they would be immediately after the breakup, but their predictions about how quickly their distress would fade (the decay bias) were surprisingly accurate. In other words, while people expected to feel worse than they actually did, they correctly anticipated the trajectory of their emotional recovery. This finding challenges the assumption that forecasting errors stem from a failure to account for emotional resilience or adaptation over time.

The study also identified key moderators of the forecasting error. Participants who reported being more in love with their partners, those who believed it was unlikely they would soon enter a new relationship, and those who played a lesser role in initiating the breakup exhibited the largest discrepancies between predicted and actual distress. These moderators align with theories of interdependence and attachment, suggesting that forecasting errors are amplified when the loss of a relationship feels more costly or when individuals are more emotionally invested. Notably, the study found that while participants’ mean predictions were inaccurate, their *relative* predictions—how their distress compared to others’—were often correct, highlighting a nuanced form of forecasting accuracy.

Methodologically, the research stands out for its rigorous within-subjects design, which allowed for direct comparisons between predicted and actual emotional responses over time. This approach overcame limitations of prior studies that relied on between-subjects comparisons or short timeframes. The use of growth curve modeling further enabled the authors to disentangle the contributions of initial intensity and decay biases, providing a clearer picture of the affective forecasting error’s time course.

In discussing the implications, the authors suggest that the initial intensity bias may reflect psychological mechanisms like focalism (overemphasizing the breakup while ignoring other life events) or the empathy gap (failing to account for how one’s emotional state might shift). The findings also underscore the resilience of the "psychological immune system," which helps individuals adapt to negative events more quickly than anticipated. While the study focuses on romantic breakups, the authors acknowledge that the time course of forecasting errors may differ for other types of emotional events, leaving room for future research.

Ultimately, the article contributes to a growing body of literature on affective forecasting by demonstrating that people’s mispredictions are not uniform but are shaped by both situational and individual factors. The takeaway is both sobering and reassuring: while breakups may feel devastating in prospect, the emotional fallout is often less severe and shorter-lived than expected.
