# Using Natural Frequencies to Improve Diagnostic Inferences

Hoffrage, U., Gigerenzer, G.

__This study demonstrates that physicians’ ability to accurately interpret diagnostic test results improves dramatically when statistical information is presented in natural frequencies rather than probabilities. The findings suggest a simple, training-free method to enhance medical decision-making and patient communication.__

The article explores how physicians struggle to correctly estimate the positive predictive value (PPV) of diagnostic tests—a critical measure of how likely a patient is to have a disease given a positive test result—when information is presented in traditional probability formats. Building on earlier research, such as David Eddy’s seminal work, the authors highlight a persistent problem: physicians often confuse test sensitivity (the probability of a positive result given the disease) with PPV, leading to gross overestimations of a test’s accuracy. For example, in Eddy’s mammography problem, most physicians estimated a PPV of 70–80% when the correct value was only 7.5%. This error stems from the cognitive challenge of applying Bayes’ rule to probabilities, which requires integrating disease prevalence, test sensitivity, and false-positive rates into a single calculation.

The authors propose that natural frequencies—a format that presents absolute numbers of cases (e.g., "8 out of 10 women with breast cancer test positive") rather than normalized probabilities—can simplify this reasoning. Unlike probabilities, natural frequencies preserve the base rates of disease and non-disease, making it easier to visualize the relationships between true positives, false positives, and overall prevalence. For instance, in the mammography example, natural frequencies clarify that out of 1,000 women, 8 with cancer and 99 without cancer test positive, yielding a PPV of 8 out of 107 (7.5%). This approach aligns with evolutionary theories of cognition, which suggest that human minds are better adapted to processing raw, observed frequencies than abstract probabilities.

To test this hypothesis, the authors conducted a study with 48 physicians in Germany, presenting them with four diagnostic problems (e.g., mammography, colorectal cancer screening) in either probability or natural frequency formats. The results were striking: only 10% of physicians correctly estimated PPVs when given probabilities, but this rose to 46% when the same information was presented as natural frequencies. Performance improved across all four problems, with the most dramatic gains seen in colorectal cancer screening (from 4% to 67% accuracy). Physicians also spent less time and reported less frustration with natural frequency problems, describing them as more "visual" and intuitive. Notably, even when physicians did not use Bayesian reasoning, their non-Bayesian strategies (e.g., focusing on prevalence) yielded better estimates with natural frequencies than with probabilities.

The discussion emphasizes two key implications. First, medical education should incorporate training on translating probabilities into natural frequencies, as prior research shows this skill leads to lasting improvements in Bayesian reasoning. Second, natural frequencies can enhance physician-patient communication by making risks more transparent and understandable. The authors argue that this simple representational change—requiring no additional training—can significantly improve diagnostic accuracy and informed decision-making in clinical practice. The study’s findings underscore the power of cognitive tools that align with human reasoning strengths, offering a practical solution to a long-standing challenge in medical judgment.
