# An Experimental Analysis of Ultimatum Bargaining

Güth, W., Schmittberger, R., & Schwarze, B.

__This seminal study experimentally investigates ultimatum bargaining, revealing that players often deviate from the subgame perfect equilibrium by prioritizing fairness and punishing perceived unfairness, even at personal cost.__

The article presents one of the first experimental analyses of ultimatum bargaining, a two-player game where one player proposes a division of a fixed sum, and the other either accepts or rejects it—resulting in zero payoffs for both if rejected. The authors, Güth, Schmittberger, and Schwarze, designed experiments to test whether real-world behavior aligns with the game-theoretic prediction of subgame perfect equilibrium, where the proposer (Player 1) should demand nearly the entire sum, leaving only a minimal amount for the responder (Player 2), who should accept any positive offer. The study introduces two types of games: "easy games," where players divide a fixed monetary amount, and "complicated games," where they allocate bundles of black and white chips with differing values for each player.

The introduction frames ultimatum bargaining as a special case of perfect-information bargaining games, where strategic interaction occurs solely through anticipation of future moves. The authors highlight the theoretical simplicity of the subgame perfect equilibrium but question whether real players—particularly those without formal game-theoretic training—would adhere to it. They contrast their work with earlier studies, such as Fouraker and Siegel’s (1963) bilateral monopoly experiments, which found greater alignment with equilibrium predictions, possibly due to the experimental context or lack of face-to-face interaction.

The methods section details the experimental design, conducted with graduate economics students unfamiliar with game theory. In "easy games," Player 1 proposes a split of a fixed sum (e.g., 10 DM), and Player 2 decides whether to accept or reject it. The "complicated games" involve dividing chips with asymmetric values, where Player 1 partitions the chips into two bundles, and Player 2 chooses one, leaving the other for Player 1. The experiments were repeated with variations in stakes and player experience to assess consistency and learning effects.

Results reveal striking deviations from equilibrium predictions. In easy games, proposers rarely demanded the entire sum, instead offering substantial shares to responders (e.g., 30–50% of the total). Responders frequently rejected offers they deemed unfair, even when doing so incurred a personal cost. For example, in the pilot study, three of nine offers were rejected, despite the equilibrium prediction that responders should accept any positive amount. The main experiments showed that proposers’ demands increased slightly with experience, but so did rejections, suggesting a trade-off between ambition and fairness. A "consistency test" further demonstrated that many proposers’ offers as Player 1 were more generous than their minimum acceptable offers as Player 2, indicating a preference for fairness over pure self-interest.

In complicated games, proposers more often proposed the equilibrium division (e.g., 18 DM for Player 1 and 10 DM for Player 2), but responders still occasionally rejected it, opting for a less favorable outcome for both players to punish perceived unfairness. The authors note that the equilibrium payoff in complicated games is less extreme than in easy games, which may explain the higher acceptance rates. However, even here, fairness considerations influenced behavior, with some proposers opting for egalitarian splits.

The discussion interprets these findings as evidence that fairness and reciprocity play a central role in bargaining behavior, challenging the assumption that players act purely as rational, self-interested maximizers. The authors suggest that proposers anticipate responders’ willingness to reject unfair offers, leading them to propose more balanced divisions. They also highlight the role of social norms, noting that players may view extreme demands as unjust and thus unacceptable. The study’s limitations, such as the use of student subjects and abstract scenarios, are acknowledged, but the results are positioned as a foundation for understanding more complex bargaining situations.

The article concludes by emphasizing the importance of fairness in ultimatum bargaining and the need for further research, including experiments with higher stakes or auction-based role assignments to mitigate egalitarian biases. The authors argue that cooperative game theory, which often assumes efficiency or equal splits, fails to capture the nuances of ultimatum bargaining, where strategic power and fairness considerations interact. Their work laid the groundwork for subsequent research on social preferences, fairness, and the limits of rational choice theory in bargaining.
