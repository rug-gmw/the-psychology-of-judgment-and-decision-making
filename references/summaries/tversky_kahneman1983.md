# Extensional Versus Intuitive Reasoning: The Conjunction Fallacy in Probability Judgment

Tversky, A., Kahneman, D.

__This seminal article explores how people systematically violate the conjunction rule of probability, favoring intuitive heuristics like representativeness and availability over formal logic. Through various experiments, the authors demonstrate that individuals often judge a conjunction of events as more probable than one of its constituents, challenging traditional probabilistic reasoning.__

The article begins by introducing the fundamental conjunction rule in probability theory, which states that the probability of a conjunction of two events (A and B) cannot exceed the probability of either event alone. Despite this logical principle, the authors argue that human judgments under uncertainty are often guided by intuitive heuristics—mental shortcuts such as representativeness and availability—that can lead to systematic biases. Representativeness refers to the tendency to judge probability based on how well an outcome matches a mental prototype, while availability involves assessing probability based on how easily instances come to mind.

Tversky and Kahneman present a series of experiments to illustrate how these heuristics lead to the conjunction fallacy, where individuals incorrectly judge a conjunction (e.g., "Linda is a bank teller and a feminist") as more probable than one of its constituents (e.g., "Linda is a bank teller"). The authors test this phenomenon across diverse contexts, including personality judgments, medical prognoses, sports forecasting, and political predictions. For example, in the famous "Linda problem," participants consistently ranked the conjunction of "bank teller and feminist" as more likely than "bank teller" alone, despite the logical impossibility of this judgment. This pattern persisted even among statistically sophisticated participants, such as graduate students and medical professionals, though the latter group showed slightly fewer errors when the logical structure of the problem was made transparent.

The methods employed in the study involve both indirect and direct tests of the conjunction rule. Indirect tests compare judgments of a conjunction and its constituents across different groups of participants, while direct tests ask the same participants to compare the probabilities of a conjunction and its constituents explicitly. The results reveal that violations of the conjunction rule are widespread, occurring in both indirect and direct tests, and are resistant to simple interventions like providing logical arguments or financial incentives. The authors also explore the role of causal reasoning in exacerbating the conjunction fallacy, showing that adding a plausible cause to an event (e.g., "an earthquake causing a flood") can make the conjunction appear more probable than the event alone.

In the discussion, Tversky and Kahneman reflect on the implications of their findings for understanding human reasoning. They argue that the conjunction fallacy is not merely a misunderstanding but a fundamental feature of how people assess probability in everyday life. The authors suggest that intuitive heuristics, while often useful, can lead to systematic errors when they conflict with formal probabilistic principles. They also discuss the challenges of correcting these biases, noting that even when individuals recognize the logical validity of the conjunction rule, they may still rely on intuitive judgments in practice.

The article concludes by emphasizing the tension between extensional logic and intuitive reasoning. While formal probability theory provides a normative framework for assessing uncertainty, human judgment is often guided by heuristics that prioritize coherence, representativeness, and causal plausibility over strict logical consistency. This tension highlights the need for a deeper understanding of how people make decisions under uncertainty and the potential limitations of relying solely on formal models of probability.
