# Clinical Versus Mechanical Prediction: A Meta-Analysis

Grove, W. M., Zald, D. H., Lebow, B. S., Snitz, B. E., & Nelson, C.

__This meta-analysis systematically compares the accuracy of clinical (human) versus mechanical (statistical or algorithmic) prediction methods across 136 studies, finding that mechanical prediction is, on average, about 10% more accurate, with no consistent exceptions favoring clinical judgment.__

This landmark meta-analysis addresses a long-standing debate in psychology and medicine: whether human clinicians or formal, statistical methods produce more accurate predictions about human behavior, diagnoses, or outcomes. The authors, building on decades of prior research—most notably Paul Meehl’s seminal 1954 work—conducted the first quantitative meta-analysis to rigorously compare these two approaches. Their study synthesizes data from 136 empirical studies spanning psychology, medicine, education, and forensic settings, where clinicians (e.g., psychologists, physicians) and mechanical methods (e.g., regression equations, actuarial tables, or computer algorithms) predicted outcomes such as academic performance, medical diagnoses, criminal recidivism, or psychiatric prognosis.

The introduction traces the historical context of the debate, noting that while earlier reviews (e.g., Sawyer, 1966) suggested mechanical prediction was often superior or at least equivalent to clinical judgment, these conclusions were limited by methodological flaws, including low statistical power and subjective interpretations of study results. The authors sought to overcome these limitations by employing meta-analytic techniques, which allowed them to quantitatively aggregate effect sizes across studies while accounting for variability in study design, sample size, and prediction tasks. Their central question was not just whether mechanical prediction outperforms clinical judgment, but under what conditions and to what extent.

The methods employed were meticulous and comprehensive. The authors conducted an exhaustive literature search, including keyword searches in PsycLit and MedLine, hand-searching relevant journals, and consulting reference lists from prior reviews and prominent researchers. Studies were included only if they directly compared clinical and mechanical predictions using the same predictor variables and outcome criteria, ensuring a fair comparison. Each study was coded for numerous variables, including the type of prediction task, the professional background and experience of the clinicians, the nature of the predictors (e.g., interview data, psychological tests, medical exams), and the accuracy metrics used (e.g., hit rates, correlation coefficients). To ensure reliability, a subset of studies was independently coded by multiple raters, yielding high inter-rater agreement. Effect sizes were standardized across studies to allow direct comparison, with positive values indicating superiority for mechanical prediction.

The results overwhelmingly favored mechanical prediction. Across all studies, mechanical methods were, on average, about 10% more accurate than clinical judgment. In 33%–47% of studies, mechanical prediction substantially outperformed clinical prediction, while clinical judgment was notably superior in only 6%–16% of cases. The distribution of effect sizes revealed that mechanical prediction was either equal to or better than clinical prediction in the vast majority of studies, with no systematic exceptions based on the type of prediction task, the clinicians’ experience, or the setting (e.g., medical vs. psychological). One notable finding was that clinical predictions performed *worse* when clinicians had access to interview data, suggesting that subjective impressions from interviews may introduce noise or bias rather than improve accuracy. Conversely, the use of medical data (e.g., lab tests) reduced the advantage of mechanical prediction, likely because such data are more objective and easier to integrate statistically.

The authors explored potential moderators of prediction accuracy, such as the clinicians’ training, the amount of data available to each method, and whether the mechanical algorithm was cross-validated. Surprisingly, none of these factors significantly influenced the relative superiority of mechanical prediction. For instance, experienced clinicians did not perform better than novices, and mechanical methods retained their advantage even when clinicians had access to more data. The only consistent exception was the aforementioned negative impact of interview data on clinical prediction. The authors also tested alternative definitions of effect size (e.g., comparing the best-performing clinician to the best-performing mechanical method) and found that their conclusions held regardless of how effect sizes were calculated.

In the discussion, the authors contextualize their findings within the broader literature on human judgment and decision-making. They attribute the superiority of mechanical prediction to well-documented cognitive biases and heuristics that plague human judgment, such as ignoring base rates, overweighing vivid or recent information, and failing to account for regression to the mean. Clinicians also often lack timely feedback on the accuracy of their predictions, which limits their ability to improve over time. The authors address potential objections to their meta-analysis, such as the inclusion of methodologically weak studies, by demonstrating that study quality (e.g., peer-reviewed vs. unpublished, sample size) did not significantly alter the results. They also dismiss the "garbage in, garbage out" critique, arguing that their findings are robust across diverse study designs and that no subset of studies—except those explicitly favoring clinical judgment—would yield a contrary conclusion.

Practically, the authors argue that the near-equivalence of mechanical and clinical prediction in some studies should still be considered a win for mechanical methods, given their lower cost and greater reproducibility. While mechanical prediction is not always cheaper (e.g., proprietary algorithms may incur costs), it generally requires fewer resources than assembling teams of highly paid professionals. The authors acknowledge that a full cost-benefit analysis—including the costs of prediction errors—has not been conducted, but they place the burden on advocates of clinical prediction to demonstrate that its higher costs are justified by superior outcomes. They conclude with a call for greater adoption of mechanical prediction methods where feasible, while recognizing that clinical judgment may still play a role in cases where mechanical algorithms are unavailable or where human intuition is needed to interpret complex, idiosyncratic data. Ultimately, the study provides compelling evidence that, in most circumstances, mechanical prediction is the more reliable and efficient choice.
