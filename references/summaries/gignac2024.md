# Rethinking the Dunning-Kruger effect: Negligible influence on a limited segment of the population

Gignac, G. E.

__This article challenges the Dunning-Kruger (DK) effect, arguing that when analyzed with appropriate statistical methods—such as polynomial and LOESS regression—it appears as a minor phenomenon affecting only a tiny fraction of the population, primarily those with very low cognitive abilities. The study finds that the effect is often overstated due to methodological flaws and low test reliability at distribution extremes.__

The article critically reexamines the Dunning-Kruger (DK) effect, a widely cited psychological phenomenon suggesting that individuals with low ability tend to overestimate their competence while high-ability individuals may underestimate theirs. The author, building on prior work by Gignac and Zajenkowski (2020), introduces a refined method to assess the magnitude and prevalence of the DK effect by comparing linear and LOESS regression models. The study begins by simulating data designed to reflect a plausible DK effect, incorporating known patterns such as a 0.30 correlation between objective and subjective intelligence measures and a better-than-average effect. The simulation reveals that while a DK effect can theoretically exist, its impact is far smaller and more limited than previously reported. Specifically, the effect peaks at a 20-point relative overestimation at an IQ of 55, affecting only 0.14% of the population, and diminishes to a 7-point overestimation at an IQ of 70, impacting just 2.3% of individuals.

The second part of the study applies this methodology to two large field datasets (N ≈ 3500 each) involving grammar and logical reasoning tests. The results show that the DK effect, when detectable, accounts for a maximum relative overestimation of 7 to 9 percentile points, confined almost exclusively to participants scoring at or near chance levels (IQ ≈ 55). This finding suggests that the effect is not a broad psychological phenomenon but rather a minor anomaly affecting a negligible portion of the population. The study also highlights a critical methodological issue: the low conditional reliability of test scores at the extremes of the ability distribution (≈ 0.40), which complicates the interpretation of results that superficially support the DK hypothesis. The use of Expected a Posteriori (EAP) factor scores, which account for varying measurement precision across the ability spectrum, does little to alter these findings, reinforcing the conclusion that the DK effect is not a meaningful or widespread phenomenon.

The discussion contextualizes these results within the broader literature, noting that prior studies often relied on flawed statistical methods, such as quartile splits, which conflate the DK effect with regression to the mean and the better-than-average effect. The author argues that the DK effect’s prominence in psychological discourse is largely an artifact of these methodological shortcomings. Furthermore, the study questions the validity of interpreting effects observed at the very low end of the ability spectrum, where test performance may reflect random guessing or disengagement rather than genuine cognitive ability. The article concludes by advocating for a shift in focus toward understanding individual differences in meta-cognition through more robust predictors, such as narcissism or self-efficacy, rather than perpetuating the DK effect as a significant psychological phenomenon. Ultimately, the findings suggest that the DK effect, as traditionally understood, is unlikely to be a meaningful or generalizable explanation for self-assessment inaccuracies.
