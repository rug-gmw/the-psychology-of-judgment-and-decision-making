# Evaluating the Factor Structure of the Wechsler Adult Intelligence Scale, Fourth Edition (WAIS-IV) in Adults with Congenital Heart Disease

Semmel, E. S., Dawkins-Henry, O. S., Bernstein, J. H., Wypij, D., Calderon, J., Bellinger, D. C., Newburger, J. W., Gurvitz, M. Z., & Cassidy, A. R.

__This study confirms that the Wechsler Adult Intelligence Scale, Fourth Edition (WAIS-IV) retains its standard four-factor structure in adults with dextro-transposition of the great arteries (d-TGA), a congenital heart disease (CHD), supporting its use in this population. The findings suggest that despite early neurodevelopmental differences, the WAIS-IV functions similarly in individuals with d-TGA as in the general population.__

This study addresses a critical gap in neuropsychological assessment by examining whether the WAIS-IV, a widely used measure of cognitive ability, maintains its psychometric properties in adults with congenital heart disease (CHD), specifically those with d-transposition of the great arteries (d-TGA). Given the growing population of CHD survivors—now exceeding 2.4 million in the U.S. alone—understanding the long-term neurodevelopmental outcomes of these individuals has become a priority. Prior research has documented early brain dysmaturation and persistent cognitive challenges in CHD populations, raising questions about whether standard assessment tools like the WAIS-IV are appropriate for this group. The authors hypothesized that the WAIS-IV’s four-factor model (Verbal Comprehension, Perceptual Reasoning, Working Memory, and Processing Speed) would fit well in adults with d-TGA, mirroring its performance in the normative population.

The study utilized data from the Boston Circulatory Arrest Study (BCAS), a longitudinal cohort of individuals with d-TGA who underwent corrective surgery in infancy. At the 28-year follow-up, 87 adults with d-TGA completed the WAIS-IV as part of a broader neuropsychological assessment. Confirmatory factor analysis (CFA) was employed to evaluate the fit of one-, two-, and four-factor models, as well as a second-order model incorporating a Full-Scale IQ (FSIQ) factor, as outlined in the WAIS-IV manual. The sample was predominantly male (74%) and white (92%), with a median age of 29 years at assessment. Most participants had completed college or graduate school (62%) and were employed or in school (95%), though the sample’s representativeness was limited by lower participation rates during the COVID-19 pandemic.

Results indicated that the four-factor model provided the best fit to the data, with all subtests loading significantly onto their respective latent factors in expected directions. The four-factor model achieved strong fit indices (CFI = 0.988, RMSEA = 0.042), outperforming the one- and two-factor models. The second-order model, which included an FSIQ factor, also fit well (CFI = 0.985, RMSEA = 0.045) but did not demonstrate superior fit compared to the first-order four-factor model. Notably, the factor loadings in the d-TGA sample closely mirrored those of the WAIS-IV’s normative sample, suggesting that the measure functions similarly in this clinical population. The authors also observed that the sample’s mean Full-Scale IQ (102.4) and index scores were broadly average, though this may reflect limited statistical power to detect subtle differences.

The discussion highlights the significance of these findings, as they provide the first empirical validation of the WAIS-IV’s factor structure in adults with CHD. Despite known differences in brain development and neurodevelopmental trajectories in this population, the WAIS-IV appears to measure cognitive abilities in a manner consistent with its design. This reassures clinicians and researchers that the WAIS-IV can be used confidently in adults with d-TGA and potentially other forms of CHD. However, the authors acknowledge several limitations, including the study’s modest sample size, lack of a control group for direct measurement invariance testing, and limited demographic diversity. Additionally, the exclusion of individuals with genetic syndromes or extracardiac anomalies may restrict the generalizability of the findings to the broader CHD population. The study also did not explore alternative factor structures, such as a five-factor model aligned with the Cattell-Horn-Carroll theory of intelligence, due to power constraints.

Future research is encouraged to validate these findings in larger, more diverse CHD samples and to examine the psychometric properties of other commonly used neuropsychological measures in this population. The authors also suggest exploring network models of the WAIS-IV and investigating cognitive profiles across different CHD subtypes, particularly those with more complex or severe conditions. Overall, this study represents an important step toward ensuring that neuropsychological assessments are appropriate and valid for individuals with CHD, ultimately supporting better clinical care and research outcomes.
